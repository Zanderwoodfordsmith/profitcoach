# Decision Tracking System

## What to Do

Create a consistent, simple system for documenting decisions, assigning owners, setting deadlines, and following up on progress. Every decision your business makes should be captured somewhere accessible so there is a single source of truth about what was agreed, who is responsible, and when it should be done. No more "I thought we decided..." conversations. No more actions that fall through the cracks because nobody wrote them down.

## Why It Matters

The cost of undocumented decisions is invisible but enormous. Teams re-discuss decisions that were already made because nobody recorded the outcome. Actions get dropped because nobody was clearly assigned as owner. Deadlines pass without anyone noticing because there is no tracking system. In a business with 5-20 people, even a few lost decisions per week compounds into significant waste. Ray Dalio's "believability-weighted decision making" at Bridgewater only works because every decision is meticulously documented. You do not need that level of rigour, but you do need a system.

## How to Use This

Set up a simple tracking system, embed it into your existing meeting rhythms, and review it weekly. The system only works if it is used consistently, so simplicity is essential.

---

## System Setup

- [ ] **Choose your tracking tool** - Spreadsheet, project management tool, shared document, or even a physical notebook. The best tool is the one your team will actually use. Do not buy new software for this. Use what you already have.
- [ ] **Define the standard fields** - Every tracked decision needs: (1) Decision made (one sentence), (2) Owner (one person), (3) Deadline, (4) Status (Open/In Progress/Complete/Overdue), (5) Source (which meeting or conversation).
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Create the tracker now and add your last 5 unresolved decisions** - You already have decisions floating around without a home. Capture them immediately.
- [ ] **Set access permissions** - Everyone who attends meetings should be able to view the tracker. Only meeting organisers or the owner should be able to add or modify entries. This prevents the tracker from becoming cluttered with informal notes.

## Capture Process

- [ ] **Assign a "decision scribe" for each meeting** - One person is responsible for capturing decisions and actions during the meeting. Rotate if you want, but someone must own it every time. This is not full minute-taking. It is specifically decisions made and actions assigned.
- [ ] **Capture in real time, not after** - Write decisions down as they happen, not from memory after the meeting. Memory is unreliable, especially for the details that matter most: exact wording, specific deadlines, and who volunteered versus who was assigned.
- [ ] **Read decisions back at the end of the meeting** - Before closing any meeting, the scribe reads out: "Here is what we decided, here is who owns what, and here are the deadlines. Any corrections?" This 2-minute step prevents most follow-up confusion.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Add a "decisions and actions" section to the bottom of every meeting agenda template** - This visual prompt reminds people that every meeting should produce documented decisions.

## Follow-Up Process

- [ ] **Review overdue items at the start of each weekly meeting** - Open the tracker, filter for overdue items, and address them. No judgement, just accountability. "This was due Thursday. What happened and when will it be done?"
- [ ] **Send a weekly summary of open actions** - A brief automated or manual email listing all open items with their owners and deadlines. Visibility creates accountability.
- [ ] **Escalate items overdue by more than one week** - If something has been overdue for more than 7 days without a revised deadline, it needs a direct conversation. Either the deadline was unrealistic, the owner needs help, or the decision is being avoided.
- [ ] **Close completed items promptly** - When an action is done, mark it complete with the date. A tracker full of completed items builds a record of execution that is valuable for performance reviews and team morale.

## System Maintenance

- [ ] **Archive completed items monthly** - Move completed decisions to an archive so the active tracker stays clean and focused. Keep the archive searchable for when you need to reference past decisions.
- [ ] **Review tracker health quarterly** - How many decisions are being captured? How many are completed on time? What percentage are overdue? These meta-metrics tell you whether your decision culture is healthy.
- [ ] **Improve based on feedback** - Ask your team every quarter: "Is the tracking system helping or creating busywork?" If it feels like overhead, simplify it. If decisions are still falling through, tighten it.
- [ ] **Connect to your meeting rhythms** - The decision tracker should be a standing input to weekly operations meetings, monthly reviews, and quarterly strategy sessions. If it is separate from your meeting rhythms, it will be forgotten.

---

## Expected Outcome

- Every significant decision has a documented owner, deadline, and status
- Decisions stop being re-discussed because there is a clear record of what was agreed
- Overdue actions are caught and addressed weekly, not months later
- Team accountability increases because commitments are visible to everyone
- A searchable history of decisions that provides context for future planning

## Coach Notes

Simplicity is everything here. The most common failure is building an elaborate system that nobody maintains. Start with the absolute minimum - a shared spreadsheet with five columns is plenty. You can add complexity later if needed, but you probably will not need to. The second common failure is inconsistent capture. If decisions from some meetings get tracked and others do not, trust in the system erodes quickly. Insist on universal adoption from day one. The read-back at the end of meetings is the single highest-value habit in this entire action. It takes 2 minutes and prevents hours of confusion. If a client only adopts one thing from this action, make it the read-back.
