# Weekly Operations Meeting

## What to Do

Establish a structured weekly meeting that reviews key metrics, highlights problems, and ensures nothing critical slips through the cracks. This meeting is focused exclusively on this week and next week. It is not a strategy session, a brainstorming meeting, or a status update where everyone reads out what they did. It is a tight, disciplined check-in that keeps the business on track between longer planning cycles.

## Why It Matters

Verne Harnish's research across thousands of scaling companies shows that a consistent weekly meeting rhythm is the single most impactful operational discipline a business can adopt. Without it, problems hide until they become crises, priorities drift without anyone noticing, and communication gaps create duplicate work or dropped balls. The weekly meeting is your early warning system. It catches issues when they are small, cheap, and easy to fix rather than large, expensive, and disruptive.

## How to Use This

Set up the meeting structure once, then run it consistently every week. Same day, same time, same format. Consistency is more important than perfection.

---

## Meeting Setup

- [ ] **Choose a fixed day and time** - Pick a day early enough in the week to act on what you learn (Monday or Tuesday work well) and protect it ruthlessly. This meeting does not move for anything short of a genuine emergency.
- [ ] **Limit attendance to direct reports or department leads** - Keep it small. 4-8 people maximum. If you need more people, your meeting is too broad. Everyone in the room should have accountability for something.
- [ ] **Set a strict 60-minute time limit** - Start on time. End on time. If you cannot cover everything in 60 minutes, you are covering too much. The discipline of the time limit forces focus.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Block the next 12 weeks in everyone's calendar right now** - Recurring invite, mandatory attendance, no exceptions. Making it non-negotiable from the start prevents the slow erosion of "can we skip this week?"

## Meeting Agenda (Follow This Order)

- [ ] **Good news (5 minutes)** - Each person shares one positive thing from the past week. This is not fluff. It sets a constructive tone, builds awareness of wins across the business, and prevents the meeting from becoming a problem-only session.
- [ ] **Metrics review (10 minutes)** - Review 5-8 key performance indicators. Each metric has an owner. Green means on track, red means off track. No discussion on green metrics. Only discuss red metrics and what is being done about them.
- [ ] **Customer and employee updates (5 minutes)** - Any customer escalations or concerns. Any team issues that need visibility. Quick flags only, not detailed discussions.
- [ ] **Priorities check (15 minutes)** - Each person confirms: Are you on track with this week's priority? If not, what is the obstacle and what help do you need? This is accountability, not interrogation.
- [ ] **Issues and problem-solving (20 minutes)** - Surface the top 1-3 issues that need resolution. Discuss. Decide. Assign an owner and deadline. If an issue needs more than 5 minutes of discussion, schedule a separate meeting for it.
- [ ] **Next week preview (5 minutes)** - Any upcoming deadlines, events, or potential disruptions that the team should know about. What could go wrong next week, and who is responsible for preventing it?

## Making It Stick

- [ ] **Assign a meeting owner** - One person is responsible for running the agenda, keeping time, and ensuring follow-through. This can be you or a trusted team member, but it must be consistent.
- [ ] **Take concise notes** - Decisions made, actions assigned, deadlines set. No minutes of what was discussed. Just what was decided and who is doing what by when.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Send action items within 1 hour of the meeting ending** - A brief email or message listing every action, owner, and deadline from the meeting. If it is not written down and shared, it does not exist.
- [ ] **Review action completion at the start of next week's meeting** - The first thing you check is whether last week's actions were completed. This closes the loop and builds accountability.
- [ ] **Rate the meeting quarterly** - Ask your team: "Is this meeting useful? What would make it better?" If the answer is "it is a waste of time," something in the format needs to change.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A consistent weekly rhythm that keeps the business aligned and accountable
- Problems surfaced early when they are cheap and easy to fix
- Clear accountability for actions with documented owners and deadlines
- Better cross-functional awareness so departments are not working in silos
- Reduced need for ad-hoc meetings because the weekly covers operational essentials

## Coach Notes

The biggest risk is that the weekly meeting becomes a status update where everyone talks and nothing gets decided. Push hard on the issues section. That is where the value is. If the meeting consistently runs over 60 minutes, the owner is trying to cover too much. Help them strip it back to essentials. If attendance starts slipping, it means the meeting is not valuable enough. The fix is to make it more useful, not to mandate attendance harder. Ask the team what would make them want to be there.
