# Successor Development Plan

## What to Do

Identify potential successors for your role and build their capability systematically over time. Succession works when the successor is genuinely ready, not just willing. This means identifying the right candidates, assessing their current gaps, creating a structured development plan, and giving them progressively more responsibility until they can lead independently.

This is not about finding a clone of yourself. It is about finding someone who can lead the business through its next chapter, which may require different skills than the ones that built it.

## Why It Matters

Ram Charan's succession research shows that companies which plan succession 3-5 years ahead achieve significantly better transitions than those who scramble when the moment arrives. The reason is simple: leadership capability cannot be developed in months. It requires years of progressively larger challenges, real decision-making experience, and targeted skill building.

The cost of getting succession wrong is enormous. A failed successor damages client relationships, demoralises the team, and can set the business back years. The cost of not planning at all is worse - a sudden departure with no prepared replacement creates crisis.

## How to Use This

Work through the identification and assessment steps first. Then build a 12-24 month development plan for your top candidate(s). Review progress quarterly and adjust the plan based on how they perform.

---

## Identify Potential Successors

- [ ] **List every internal candidate** - Who on your current team has leadership potential? Consider people across all departments, not just the obvious next-in-line. Sometimes the best successor is not the most senior person.
- [ ] **Assess each candidate against three criteria: capability, motivation, and cultural fit** - Capability can be developed. Motivation cannot be manufactured. Cultural fit determines whether the team will follow them.
- [ ] **Consider external candidates honestly** - If no internal candidate is viable, acknowledge it early. Recruiting and developing an external successor takes even longer.
- [ ] **Have candid conversations with top candidates** - Do they want the role? What concerns do they have? Some people are excellent at their current level and do not want the pressure of leading the whole business. Better to know now.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Write down the top 3 capabilities your successor must have** - Not personality traits. Specific capabilities: strategic thinking, financial acumen, client management, team leadership. This becomes your selection filter.

## Assess Current Gaps

- [ ] **Map each candidate's current capabilities against the requirements** - Where are they strong? Where are they weak? Be specific and evidence-based, not based on gut feeling.
- [ ] **Identify the 3 biggest development gaps for your top candidate** - These become the focus of the development plan. Trying to develop everything at once develops nothing well.
- [ ] **Assess their leadership credibility with the team** - Does the team respect them? Would they follow this person? If not, what needs to change?
- [ ] **Identify what you do that they cannot yet do** - Not tasks, but judgement areas. Where does your experience give you insight they have not yet earned? These are the areas that need the most development time.

## Build the Development Plan

- [ ] **Create progressive responsibility milestones** - Month 1-6: shadow key decisions. Month 6-12: make decisions with your review. Month 12-18: make decisions independently with your oversight. Month 18-24: lead independently.
- [ ] **Assign stretch projects that test and develop weak areas** - If financial acumen is a gap, have them lead the budget process. If client management is weak, have them take over a key account. Real experience beats training courses.
- [ ] **Schedule regular development conversations** - Monthly one-to-ones focused specifically on their growth, not operational issues. What are they learning? What surprised them? Where do they feel out of their depth?
- [ ] **Expose them to external development** - Industry events, peer networks, executive coaching, board exposure. Leadership develops fastest when people see how others lead.
- [ ] **Create a "decision diary" exercise** - For 3 months, have them write down every significant decision they would make, then compare their call with yours. The gaps in reasoning reveal what they still need to learn.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Invite your top candidate to your next strategic planning session as a participant, not an observer** - Let them contribute, challenge, and see how strategy is made.

## Expected Outcome

- Identified successor candidate(s) with honest assessment of readiness
- Specific, time-bound development plan with quarterly milestones
- Progressive transfer of responsibility that builds confidence and capability
- Regular feedback loop that catches problems early
- Successor who is genuinely prepared to lead, not just promoted into the role

## Coach Notes

The biggest risk in succession planning is the owner who cannot let go. They identify a successor, start the development plan, then undermine it by overriding decisions, withholding information, or swooping in during difficult moments. Every time they rescue the successor, they delay their readiness by months. Coach the owner on the discipline of letting the successor struggle and learn.

Watch for the "mini-me" trap. Owners often gravitate toward successors who think and act like them. But the business may need different leadership for its next phase. A founder who built through hustle may need a successor who builds through systems. Help the owner separate "like me" from "right for the business."

If no viable internal successor exists after honest assessment, that is a critical finding. It means either recruiting externally (12-24 month timeline) or reconsidering the exit route entirely. Better to face this reality early than to discover it during deal negotiations.
