# Knowledge Transfer System

## What to Do

Extract and document the institutional knowledge that lives in your head. Focus on three categories: relationships (who matters and why), decision contexts (how and why you make key judgements), and strategic understanding (what you know about the market, competitors, and your business model that nobody else does).

This is not about writing an operations manual. Those cover processes. This covers the wisdom, context, and judgement that took you years to build and that your successor will need to lead effectively.

## Why It Matters

When a founder leaves a business, they take with them decades of accumulated context. Why that client requires special handling. Why that pricing structure exists. Why that market was abandoned five years ago. What the real competitive threat is. This knowledge is invisible until it is gone, and then it is devastating.

Noel Tichy calls this the "teachable point of view" - the leader's accumulated wisdom about how the business works and why. Transferring it is not a one-time document dump. It is a structured, ongoing process that turns implicit knowledge into shared understanding. The businesses that manage transitions well are the ones that start this transfer years before the transition happens.

## How to Use This

Design the system in one session. Then commit to 2-3 hours per week for 3 months to capture the most critical knowledge. After the initial capture, maintain the system with monthly updates.

---

## Map What Needs Transferring

- [ ] **List every key relationship and what the successor needs to know about it** - Client histories, supplier politics, partner dynamics, investor expectations. Not just who they are but what drives them, what they value, and what to watch out for.
- [ ] **Document your pricing and commercial decision logic** - Why things are priced the way they are. What margin floors you maintain and why. Which clients get special terms and the history behind it.
- [ ] **Write down the strategic decisions you made that shaped the business** - Markets entered and exited, products launched and killed, hires made and missed. Include the reasoning, not just the outcome.
- [ ] **Identify the "unwritten rules" of your business** - Every company has them. Things everyone knows but nobody has written down. Cultural norms, client expectations, industry conventions.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Record a 15-minute video explaining the one thing about this business that nobody else fully understands** - Capture it before you forget to do it.

## Choose Transfer Methods

- [ ] **For relationship knowledge: use joint meetings and warm handovers** - Introduce your successor to key relationships in context. Attend meetings together. Debrief afterwards. This cannot be done through documents alone.
- [ ] **For decision logic: use the "decision diary" method** - When a significant decision comes up, explain your thinking out loud to the successor before deciding. Record these conversations if possible.
- [ ] **For strategic context: write narrative documents, not bullet points** - "We exited the retail market in 2019 because..." needs a paragraph, not a line item. Context is lost in brevity.
- [ ] **For cultural knowledge: create "lessons learned" sessions** - Monthly sessions where you share stories about what worked, what failed, and what you would do differently. Stories transfer culture better than policies.
- [ ] **For technical or process knowledge: use screen recordings and walkthroughs** - Show, do not just tell. Record yourself doing the work with commentary on why you make the choices you make.

## Build the System

- [ ] **Create a central knowledge repository** - One place where all transfer documents, recordings, and notes are stored. Not scattered across email, Slack, and shared drives.
- [ ] **Set a weekly transfer session** - 60-90 minutes per week with your successor, focused purely on knowledge transfer. Not operational catch-ups. Dedicated learning time.
- [ ] **Prioritise by risk and urgency** - What knowledge would cause the most damage if lost tomorrow? Transfer that first. Work down the priority list over 3 months.
- [ ] **Include the "why behind the why"** - It is not enough to know what the business does. The successor needs to understand why it does things that way, what alternatives were considered, and what would trigger a change in approach.
- [ ] **Test the transfer by letting the successor handle situations independently** - Then review how they performed. Did they have the context they needed? If not, what was missing?
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Schedule your first weekly transfer session for next week** - Put it in the calendar. If it is not scheduled, it will not happen.

---

## Expected Outcome

- Structured repository of institutional knowledge covering relationships, decisions, and strategy
- Successor has direct relationship exposure to key clients, suppliers, and partners
- Decision-making logic documented so future leaders understand not just what to do but why
- Cultural and strategic context preserved beyond any individual leader
- Ongoing transfer rhythm that continues building shared understanding over time

## Coach Notes

Knowledge transfer is one of those actions owners agree is important but consistently deprioritise because it feels less urgent than day-to-day operations. Hold them accountable to the weekly sessions. If they cancel three in a row, the transfer is not happening.

The best transfer method depends on the type of knowledge. Relationships transfer through introductions. Decision logic transfers through conversation. Strategic context transfers through narrative writing. If the owner defaults to one method for everything (usually a big document), push them to vary the approach.

Watch for the owner who hoards knowledge unconsciously. They agree to transfer but then give partial information, skip the hard parts, or say "You will figure that out." This is often an identity issue - the knowledge is what makes them valuable. Letting go of it feels like letting go of their importance. Name it gently and help them through it.
