# Governance & Authority Framework

## What to Do

Design a decision-making structure that lets the business operate independently of you. Define clear authorities (who can decide what), escalation paths (what happens when someone is unsure), and governance systems (how strategic decisions are made and reviewed). This is the structural foundation that allows the business to function without you in the room.

The goal is not to remove yourself from all decisions. It is to ensure the business has a clear system for making decisions whether you are present or not.

## Why It Matters

Most small and medium businesses have informal governance: the owner decides everything, and when they are unavailable, things either wait or someone guesses. This works when the business is small, but it becomes a bottleneck as it grows and a serious risk when the owner exits.

Verne Harnish emphasises that "rhythm and structure" are what allow businesses to scale beyond the founder. A governance framework is the formal version of the informal habits that got you this far. It preserves the quality of decision-making while removing the requirement for your personal involvement.

## How to Use This

Design the framework in one session. Then implement it progressively over 3-6 months, starting with the areas where your absence creates the most risk. Test it by stepping back and observing what happens.

---

## Decision Authority Matrix

- [ ] **List all recurring decisions in the business** - Spending, hiring, pricing, client issues, product changes, strategy shifts, partnerships. Map every type of decision that gets made.
- [ ] **Assign each decision to a level: Team (anyone can decide), Manager (department head decides), Leadership (senior team decides), Owner (you decide)** - Be aggressive about pushing decisions down. If someone closer to the problem can decide, they should.
- [ ] **Set financial thresholds for each level** - Team: up to [amount]. Manager: up to [amount]. Leadership: up to [amount]. Owner: above [amount]. Make the numbers explicit so there is no ambiguity.
- [ ] **Document the matrix and share it with the entire leadership team** - It only works if everyone knows the rules. Publish it, discuss it, and commit to following it.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Identify 5 decisions that currently come to you but should not** - Delegate them this week with clear authority. See what happens.

## Escalation Paths

- [ ] **Define what counts as an escalation trigger** - Not "when you are unsure" (that is too vague) but specific scenarios: financial impact above a threshold, client at risk, legal exposure, safety issue, reputational risk.
- [ ] **Create a clear escalation chain** - Who do you escalate to first? If they are unavailable, then who? Every person in the chain should know they are in it and what is expected of them.
- [ ] **Set response time expectations for escalations** - Urgent: same-day response. Important: 48-hour response. Non-urgent: next leadership meeting. Define what qualifies for each.
- [ ] **Build a "if in doubt" default** - When the framework does not clearly cover a situation, what should people do? A good default: "Make the decision that best serves the client, and report it to your manager within 24 hours."

## Governance Structure

- [ ] **Establish a leadership meeting rhythm** - Weekly operational meeting (60 minutes, action-focused). Monthly strategic review (90 minutes, performance and direction). Quarterly planning session (half day, priorities and goals). Annual strategy day (full day, vision and major decisions).
- [ ] **Define standing agenda items for each meeting** - Structure prevents drift. Key numbers, open decisions, blocked items, and upcoming risks should be standard agenda items.
- [ ] **Create a board or advisory structure** - Even if informal, having 2-3 external advisers who meet quarterly adds perspective and accountability. This becomes critical post-succession.
- [ ] **Document how strategic decisions are made** - Who proposes? Who evaluates? Who decides? Who is informed? Use a simple RACI or similar framework for major decisions.
- [ ] **Build a conflict resolution process** - When leaders disagree, how is it resolved? Voting? Discussion until consensus? Owner decides? Whatever the method, make it explicit.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Add a standing "decisions made this week" item to your next leadership meeting** - Visibility creates accountability and shows the team that independent decision-making is expected and valued.

---

## Expected Outcome

- Written decision authority matrix covering all major decision types
- Clear escalation paths with defined triggers and response times
- Regular governance rhythm (weekly, monthly, quarterly, annually)
- Leadership team empowered and accountable for decisions at their level
- Business capable of operating through any leadership transition without disruption

## Coach Notes

The hardest part of this action is not designing the framework - it is the owner actually following it. Most owners design a delegation framework then continue to make the decisions themselves, either because the team asks them to or because they cannot resist. The coaching role is to hold the line: "Your framework says this is a Manager decision. Why are you making it?"

Implementation should be gradual. Do not hand over all decisions on day one. Start with the lowest-risk decisions, build confidence, then progressively move up the authority levels. If you push too much too fast, mistakes happen and the owner pulls everything back.

Watch for the leadership team that is afraid to use their authority. This usually means the owner has a history of overriding or second-guessing decisions. If the team does not trust that delegation is real, they will keep escalating everything to the owner. The fix is consistency - the owner must visibly respect the framework even when they disagree with a specific decision.
