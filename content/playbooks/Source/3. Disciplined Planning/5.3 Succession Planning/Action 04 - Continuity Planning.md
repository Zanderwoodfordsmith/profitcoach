# Continuity Planning

## What to Do

Plan the practical transition of ownership, leadership, and stakeholder relationships. This covers the mechanics that turn a succession plan into reality: equity transfer, client communication, team announcement, partner and supplier transitions, and strategic partnership continuity.

This is the execution layer of succession. The other actions in this playbook prepare the successor and the organisation. This one plans the actual handover.

## Why It Matters

Even well-prepared successions fail at the execution stage. The successor is ready, the governance is in place, but the transition itself is handled poorly. Clients feel blindsided. Key employees worry about their future. Partners question whether commitments will be honoured. The business loses momentum during the handover because nobody planned the communication and logistics.

Richard Branson's successions at Virgin work because every transition is planned in detail: who gets told when, what message they receive, and what continuity assurances they need. The detail matters. A single poorly handled client conversation during a transition can cost years of relationship building.

## How to Use This

Work through each section to build a complete transition plan. Not every item will apply to your situation, but most will. The goal is a documented plan that could be executed smoothly when the time comes, even if that is years away.

## Equity & Ownership Transfer

- [ ] **Define the ownership transfer structure** - Full sale, phased buyout, gifting, trust transfer, or ESOP. Each has different tax, legal, and financial implications. Get professional advice early.
- [ ] **Establish a valuation method and timeline** - Agree how the business will be valued and when. Valuation disputes during transition are common and destructive.
- [ ] **Structure earn-out or transition terms** - If the outgoing owner stays involved during a transition period, define exactly what that looks like: role, hours, authority, duration, and compensation.
- [ ] **Address shareholder agreements and minority interests** - If there are other shareholders, how does the transition affect their position? Pre-emption rights, tag-along clauses, and consent requirements all need reviewing.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Check your shareholder agreement for succession-related clauses** - Many owners have not read theirs in years. Know what it says before you plan around it.

## Client & Customer Communication

- [ ] **Create a client communication plan** - Who gets told first? Key accounts need personal conversations, not a mass email. Plan the sequence carefully.
- [ ] **Draft key messages for different client segments** - Top clients get a personal call. Mid-tier clients get a personal email. Smaller clients get a general announcement. Tailor the message to the relationship.
- [ ] **Prepare answers for client concerns** - "Will my service change?" "Who is my new contact?" "Are you still involved?" Anticipate the questions and have clear, reassuring answers.
- [ ] **Schedule joint meetings during the transition period** - Outgoing and incoming leaders meeting key clients together provides continuity and builds confidence.
- [ ] **Monitor client retention during and after transition** - Track whether clients stay, increase, or leave during the transition period. Early warnings allow intervention.

## Team Communication

- [ ] **Plan the internal announcement sequence** - Leadership team first (they need time to process and prepare), then broader team. Never let staff learn about succession through rumours.
- [ ] **Address job security concerns directly** - People will worry about their roles, their managers, and the culture. Be specific about what is changing and what is not.
- [ ] **Identify flight risks** - Which key employees might leave during transition? Have retention conversations early. Consider retention bonuses or role enhancements for critical people.
- [ ] **Define the outgoing owner's role during transition** - Visible, supportive, but progressively stepping back. The team needs to see them endorsing the successor, not competing with them.

## External Stakeholder Management

- [ ] **List all external stakeholders who need managing** - Suppliers, partners, banks, investors, advisers, industry bodies. Each has different concerns and needs different handling.
- [ ] **Notify banking and financial partners** - Loan covenants, personal guarantees, and credit facilities may need restructuring. Start this early as banks move slowly.
- [ ] **Transition key supplier relationships** - Where the owner has personal relationships with suppliers, introduce the successor and ensure commercial terms are documented, not dependent on personal goodwill.
- [ ] **Update all legal and regulatory registrations** - Company directors, signatory authorities, regulatory filings, insurance policies. Create a checklist of every registration that needs updating.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **List every document or account where you are the sole signatory** - Bank accounts, contracts, government filings, insurance. These all need changing during transition. Knowing the list is step one.

## Expected Outcome

- Complete transition plan covering equity, clients, team, and external stakeholders
- Communication plans and draft messages ready for each stakeholder group
- Legal and financial transition requirements identified and professional advice engaged
- Key relationship handovers planned with joint meeting schedules
- Smooth, well-managed transition that preserves business value and relationships

## Coach Notes

Timing is everything in transition communication. Tell people too early and you create months of uncertainty. Tell people too late and you create shock and mistrust. For most businesses, the right sequence is: agree the plan (months ahead), tell the leadership team (6-8 weeks before), tell key clients (4-6 weeks before), tell the broader team (2-4 weeks before), then make it public.

The emotional dimension is significant. The outgoing owner may struggle with letting go even when they planned to. Watch for signs: micromanaging the successor, attending meetings they said they would not, or undermining the transition timeline. Gently hold them to the plan.

Client retention during transition is the highest-risk period for revenue. If a client has a 20-year relationship with the owner, no amount of planning makes the transition seamless. The goal is "as smooth as possible," not "nobody notices." Accept some disruption and plan to manage it rather than prevent it entirely.
