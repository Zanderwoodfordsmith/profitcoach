# Bottleneck Identification

## What to Do

Find the constraint points where work consistently gets stuck in your business and analyse what causes the slowdown. A bottleneck is any point where the flow of work narrows, slows, or stops. Fixing even one bottleneck can dramatically improve throughput, delivery speed, and team morale.

## Why It Matters

Your business can only move as fast as its slowest point. If your delivery team can handle 20 projects a month but your sales process can only close 8, you have a sales bottleneck. If you can close 20 but can only onboard 10, onboarding is the bottleneck. Every business has bottlenecks. The question is whether you know where yours are and are actively working to widen them.

## How to Use This

Use your process maps (Actions 1-3) as inputs. If you have not mapped your processes yet, you can still identify bottlenecks by looking at where work piles up and where people wait.

---

## Find Your Bottlenecks

- [ ] Look at each stage of your main process: where does work pile up or queue? That is a bottleneck.
- [ ] Ask your team: "Where do you spend time waiting for something or someone before you can continue?" Their answers point to bottlenecks.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Think about the last 3 times a project or delivery was late. What was the common cause of the delay? That is likely your primary bottleneck.
- [ ] Check your own calendar: are you the person people are waiting for? (The owner-as-bottleneck is the most common pattern in SMEs)
- [ ] List every bottleneck you can identify. Do not worry about solutions yet - just find them all.

## Analyse Each Bottleneck

For each bottleneck identified:

- [ ] **Type:** Is it a people bottleneck (not enough capacity), a process bottleneck (too many steps or approvals), a technology bottleneck (manual work that should be automated), or a decision bottleneck (waiting for someone to say yes)?
- [ ] **Frequency:** How often does this bottleneck cause delays? (Daily / Weekly / Monthly)
- [ ] **Impact:** When this bottleneck hits, what is the cost? (Lost revenue, delayed delivery, frustrated clients, stressed team)
- [ ] **Root cause:** What is the underlying reason this bottleneck exists? (Understaffing, poor planning, unclear authority, outdated tools, owner control habits)

## Prioritise and Fix

- [ ] Rank bottlenecks by impact x frequency: the one that hits hardest and most often gets fixed first
- [ ] For your #1 bottleneck, identify 3 possible solutions:
  - A quick fix (what could you do this week to reduce the impact?)
  - A medium-term fix (what could you implement over 30 days?)
  - A structural fix (what would permanently eliminate this bottleneck?)
- [ ] Start with the quick fix while planning the structural one
- [ ] Set a measurable target: "After fixing this bottleneck, [process X] should complete in [Y days] instead of [Z days]"
- [ ] After fixing the primary bottleneck, re-assess: the next bottleneck in the chain will now become visible

---

## Expected Outcome

- A complete list of bottlenecks across your main processes
- Each bottleneck typed, assessed for frequency and impact, and root-caused
- Your #1 bottleneck identified with a tiered solution plan
- Quick fixes implemented this week for immediate relief
- A structured approach to progressively removing constraints from your business

## Coach Notes

Eliyahu Goldratt's Theory of Constraints applies perfectly here: improving anything other than the bottleneck does not improve the system. Help clients resist the urge to fix everything at once and focus on the single biggest constraint. Once that is resolved, the next bottleneck reveals itself. The most emotionally charged finding: when the owner is the bottleneck. Handle this carefully. Frame it as a success indicator ("the business has outgrown having you in the middle of everything") rather than a failure. Connect solutions to Delegation (2.0 Action 8), Processes (1.8), and the Scalability Audit (2.2 Action 4).
