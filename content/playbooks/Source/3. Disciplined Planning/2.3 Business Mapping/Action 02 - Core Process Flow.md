# Core Process Flow

## What to Do

Map your most important internal process step by step - usually your main delivery process or your sales process, whichever drives the most revenue. Draw out every step from start to finish, who does each step, how long it takes, and where things regularly go wrong.

## Why It Matters

Your core process is how you make money. If it is running on memory and improvisation rather than a clear, documented flow, you are leaving quality, speed, and profit on the table. Mapping it reveals inefficiencies that have been hiding in plain sight: duplicate steps, unnecessary approvals, bottlenecks where one person holds everything up, and steps that add time without adding value. Once visible, these problems become solvable.

## How to Use This

Choose your single most important process (the one that directly generates revenue or delivers value to clients). Then map it from trigger to completion.

---

## Choose Your Process

- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Pick the one process that, if it ran 20% faster or 20% more reliably, would have the biggest impact on revenue or client satisfaction. That is the one to map.
- [ ] Define the trigger: what event starts this process? (A new enquiry, a signed contract, a support ticket, etc.)
- [ ] Define the end point: what does "complete" look like? (Invoice sent, project delivered, problem resolved, etc.)

## Map the Steps

- [ ] Write down every step between the trigger and the end point in order
- [ ] For each step, note:
  - Who does it (name or role)
  - How long it typically takes
  - What tools or systems are used
  - What information is needed to start this step
- [ ] Be honest: map how it actually works today, not how it should work
- [ ] Include the informal steps: "wait for approval," "check with the boss," "hope someone remembers to..."
- [ ] Note any step where the outcome depends on who does it (inconsistency signal)

## Analyse the Flow

- [ ] Mark any step that regularly causes delays - what causes the delay?
- [ ] Mark any step where the same information is entered or communicated more than once (duplication)
- [ ] Mark any step that requires you personally when it could theoretically be done by someone else
- [ ] Mark any approval step - is the approval genuinely adding value, or is it just a control habit?
- [ ] Count the total number of handoffs (work passing from one person to another). Each handoff is a risk point.
- [ ] Calculate the total time from trigger to completion: what is the best case, worst case, and typical case?

## Improvement Opportunities

- [ ] Identify steps that could be eliminated without affecting the outcome
- [ ] Identify steps that could be combined (two separate steps by the same person that could happen together)
- [ ] Identify steps that could be automated (standard communications, data entry, routine approvals)
- [ ] Identify steps that could be delegated to free up higher-value people
- [ ] Prioritise the top 3 improvements by impact and ease of implementation

---

## Expected Outcome

- A complete visual map of your most important process
- Every step, owner, duration, and tool documented
- Delays, duplications, and bottlenecks identified
- A prioritised list of improvements to make the process faster, more reliable, and less dependent on you
- A foundation for documentation (connects to 1.8 Processes) and automation (connects to 2.8 AI & Automation)

## Coach Notes

Get the client to draw this out rather than just describe it verbally. The physical act of mapping reveals gaps that talking misses. When they say "and then we..." interrupt with "Who specifically? How long? What if they are busy?" The detail matters. Common findings: most processes have 2-3 unnecessary steps that exist because "we have always done it that way," at least one critical handoff that regularly fails, and the owner as an unnecessary bottleneck in the approval chain. This exercise directly feeds into 1.8 Processes (documentation) and 2.8 AI & Automation (which steps to automate).
