# Resource Allocation & Capacity Planning

## What to Do

Map the actual available capacity of your team against the demands of your active projects. This means knowing how many hours each person genuinely has for project work after you subtract their day-to-day responsibilities. Then allocate people to projects based on real numbers, not wishful thinking.

The goal is simple: stop overloading your team and start making honest trade-offs about what can actually get done.

## Why It Matters

The number one reason projects miss deadlines is not poor planning. It is overcommitment. Michael Gerber's E-Myth principle applies here - the owner assumes everyone can absorb more because they themselves work 60-hour weeks. But most team members have 25-30 hours of productive work per week after meetings, admin, and day-to-day operations. Projects that need 15 hours a week from someone who only has 5 available hours will fail. Every time.

## How to Use This

You need a list of your team members, their current roles, and your active project list from the previous two actions. A simple spreadsheet works best for this.

---

## Step 1: Calculate True Capacity

- [ ] **List every person involved in project work** - Include yourself. If contractors or part-time staff contribute, include them too with their actual hours.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Estimate each person's weekly "BAU" hours** - BAU means business-as-usual: the recurring tasks, meetings, admin, and client work they do every week regardless of projects. Be honest. Most people underestimate this.
- [ ] **Calculate available project hours** - Total contracted hours minus BAU hours. For most people, this is 5-15 hours per week. If someone shows zero available hours, they cannot take on project work without dropping something else.
- [ ] **Factor in leave, holidays, and known absences** - Check the next 90 days. Adjust capacity for weeks where key people are away.

## Step 2: Map Demand Against Capacity

- [ ] **Estimate hours per person per project per week** - For each active project, how many hours does it need from each team member? Be specific. "John: 8 hours/week on Project A" is useful. "The team works on it" is not.
- [ ] **Compare total demand to available capacity** - Add up all project demands for each person. If someone is allocated 20 hours of project work but only has 10 hours available, you have a problem to solve now rather than later.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Identify the bottleneck person** - There is almost always one person who is overallocated across multiple projects. Find them. They are your constraint, and everything flows through their capacity.
- [ ] **Flag skills gaps** - Where a project needs a capability that nobody on the team has. This is a hire, outsource, or train decision that needs making before the project starts.

## Step 3: Make Allocation Decisions

- [ ] **Resolve overallocations** - Three options: reduce the scope of one project, delay a project start, or move a task to someone else. Do not "solve" it by assuming people will just work harder.
- [ ] **Protect critical capacity** - Your bottleneck person needs their time defended. Block their calendar. Reduce their meeting load. Give them permission to say no to requests outside their allocated projects.
- [ ] **Set a capacity check rhythm** - Every two weeks, spend 15 minutes reviewing whether allocations still match reality. People get pulled into fires. Capacity shifts. Catch it early.
- [ ] **Document the allocation** - A simple table: Person, Project, Hours/Week, Start Date, End Date. Share it with the team so everyone knows what is expected.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A realistic picture of how much project capacity your team actually has
- Clear allocation of people to projects based on real hours, not assumptions
- Bottleneck people identified and their capacity protected
- Overallocations resolved before they cause missed deadlines
- A rhythm for reviewing and adjusting capacity as things change

## Coach Notes

This is often an uncomfortable exercise because it reveals that the business has committed to more projects than it can deliver. That is the point. Better to face it now and make conscious trade-offs than to discover it in month three when everything is behind schedule.

If the owner is the bottleneck (which is common), challenge them on what they can delegate or defer. The owner's time is the scarcest resource in most small businesses, and it gets allocated last when it should be allocated first.

For clients who resist the detail, start with just the top two projects and their top three people. Even that rough cut usually surfaces a capacity problem worth solving.
