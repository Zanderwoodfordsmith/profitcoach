# Strategic Project Prioritisation

## What to Do

List every project, initiative, and "thing we should probably do" that is currently competing for attention in your business. Then score each one against a simple framework that measures real impact, actual effort, and strategic alignment. The goal is to go from "too many things, not enough time" to a short, ranked list that makes decisions obvious.

Most business owners carry around 15-30 potential projects in their head at any time. Without a structured way to compare them, the loudest voice or the most recent crisis wins. This tool replaces gut feel with a repeatable scoring method.

## Why It Matters

Greg McKeown's Essentialism principle applies directly here: if you do not prioritise, everything feels equally urgent and nothing gets finished. Verne Harnish makes the same point in Scaling Up - the best-run companies focus on a small number of "rocks" each quarter and say no to everything else. The cost of taking on too many projects is not just slow delivery. It is context-switching, half-finished work, exhausted teams, and the opportunity cost of the projects that would have actually moved the needle.

## How to Use This

Gather your leadership team or, if you are a solo operator, block 90 minutes with no distractions. You need a whiteboard, spreadsheet, or simple table. Be honest about effort estimates - most people underestimate by 50%.

---

## Step 1: Build the Project List

- [ ] **Brain dump every active and potential project** - Include everything: product launches, system upgrades, hiring plans, marketing campaigns, process improvements, tech changes. Nothing is too small or too ambitious at this stage.
- [ ] **Remove anything that is already complete or dead** - If it has been "on the list" for six months with zero progress and no real intention to start, cross it out.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Group projects by category** - Operations, marketing, product, finance, team. This often reveals that 70% of your projects sit in one or two areas while others are neglected.

## Step 2: Score Each Project

- [ ] **Score impact (1-5)** - What is the likely revenue, profit, or strategic benefit if this project succeeds? A 5 is transformational. A 1 is a nice-to-have.
- [ ] **Score effort (1-5)** - How much time, money, and attention does this project require? A 5 is massive (months of work, significant budget). A 1 is quick and light.
- [ ] **Score strategic alignment (1-5)** - Does this project directly support your 3-year vision, annual goals, or core business model? A 5 is dead-centre strategic. A 1 is a tangent or distraction.
- [ ] **Calculate priority score** - (Impact x Alignment) divided by Effort. Higher scores go first. This penalises high-effort distractions and rewards quick strategic wins.

## Step 3: Make the Decisions

- [ ] **Select your top 3-5 projects for this quarter** - No more. If you cannot get it down to 5, the scoring was too generous. Go back and be harder.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Identify your "not now" list** - These are good projects that lose to better ones. Put them on a dated parking list so they do not creep back in through side doors.
- [ ] **Name one project to kill entirely** - Every business has at least one zombie project that nobody wants to admit is dead. Find it. End it. Free up the energy.
- [ ] **Assign a single owner to each selected project** - Not a committee. One person who is accountable for progress. If you cannot name an owner, the project is not ready.
- [ ] **Set a review date** - Book 30 minutes in your calendar, 4 weeks from now, to revisit this list and check whether priorities still hold.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A complete inventory of all projects competing for your time and resources
- A scored, ranked list that makes trade-offs visible and defensible
- A clear top 3-5 for this quarter with named owners
- A "not now" parking list that stops good ideas from becoming distractions
- At least one killed project that frees up capacity

## Coach Notes

The biggest resistance you will hit is "but they are all important." They are not. Push the client to force-rank even when it feels uncomfortable. If two projects score the same, ask: "If you could only do one, which one?" That question always breaks ties.

Watch for pet projects that score low but the client defends emotionally. These are fine to keep if they are low-effort, but they should not occupy a top-5 slot. Also watch for projects that score high on impact but have no realistic owner - these will stall within weeks.

Run this exercise quarterly. The list changes as the business evolves, and the discipline of re-scoring prevents project creep.
