# Project Tracking & Reviews

## What to Do

Build a simple, visible tracking system for all active projects and install a regular review rhythm that catches problems before they become crises. This is not about software or dashboards for the sake of it. It is about making project status obvious to everyone who needs to know, in under 60 seconds.

The rule is: if you cannot tell whether a project is on track, behind, or blocked within one minute of looking at it, your tracking system is not working.

## Why It Matters

Harnish's Scaling Up methodology is built around meeting rhythms and visible metrics because the alternative is "management by walking around and hoping." Newport's work on Deep Work reinforces the same principle from the individual level - if you do not track what matters, shallow urgencies will consume every available hour. Projects drift silently. A task that was "nearly done" three weeks ago is still "nearly done" today. Without regular, structured reviews, you only find out a project is in trouble when it misses its deadline.

## How to Use This

Choose your tracking format first (whiteboard, spreadsheet, or project tool), then set up the review cadence. Keep it as simple as possible. The best system is one people actually update.

---

## Step 1: Set Up the Tracker

- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Create a one-page project dashboard** - One row per project. Columns: Project Name, Owner, Status (Green/Amber/Red), Next Milestone, Due Date, Blockers. That is it. Resist adding more columns.
- [ ] **Define what Green, Amber, and Red mean** - Green: on track, no issues. Amber: at risk, needs attention this week. Red: blocked or behind, needs escalation now. Write these definitions down so everyone uses them consistently.
- [ ] **Make it visible** - Put the dashboard where the team sees it regularly. A wall in the office, a pinned message in your team chat, or the first slide in your weekly meeting. Out of sight means out of mind.
- [ ] **Assign update responsibility** - Each project owner updates their row before the review meeting. Not during. Before. If it is not updated, the meeting starts with that conversation.

## Step 2: Install the Review Rhythm

- [ ] **Schedule a weekly 30-minute project review** - Same day, same time, every week. Non-negotiable. This is where you look at the dashboard together and make decisions.
- [ ] **Follow a tight agenda** - Three questions per project: (1) What was the milestone for this week? (2) Did we hit it? (3) What is the plan for next week? No long updates. No problem-solving in the meeting. Just status and decisions.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Start every review with blockers** - Ask "What is stuck?" before anything else. Blockers that are cleared quickly save weeks of delay. Blockers that sit unaddressed kill projects.
- [ ] **Document decisions, not discussions** - Keep a running log of decisions made in each review. "Decided to delay Project B start by two weeks" is useful. Minutes of the conversation are not.

## Step 3: Respond to What the Tracker Shows

- [ ] **Amber projects get a recovery plan within 48 hours** - If something moves from Green to Amber, the project owner writes a short plan: what went wrong, what needs to happen, and when it will be back to Green.
- [ ] **Red projects get an immediate escalation conversation** - Do not wait for the next weekly review. The owner and decision-maker meet within 24 hours to decide: fix it, rescope it, or kill it.
- [ ] **Celebrate Green completions** - When a milestone or project finishes on time, acknowledge it. Teams that only hear about problems stop caring about the tracker.
- [ ] **Review the tracker itself quarterly** - Is it being used? Is it accurate? Are the right projects on it? A tracker that nobody updates is worse than no tracker at all because it creates false confidence.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A single-page project dashboard that shows status of all active projects at a glance
- A weekly review rhythm that catches problems early and drives accountability
- Clear escalation rules for amber and red projects
- A running log of project decisions for reference
- A team habit of updating and using the tracker consistently

## Coach Notes

Most clients will overcomplicate this. They want to buy project management software before they have the discipline to update a spreadsheet. Start analogue or simple. If they cannot keep a whiteboard current, they will not keep Monday.com current either.

The weekly review is the real tool here, not the dashboard. The dashboard just gives the review something to look at. Push the client to protect this meeting and keep it short. The moment it becomes a 90-minute discussion forum, people will start skipping it.

If a project stays Amber for more than two consecutive weeks, it is actually Red. Challenge the owner on this. Perpetual Amber is a sign that someone is avoiding a difficult conversation about scope, resources, or feasibility.
