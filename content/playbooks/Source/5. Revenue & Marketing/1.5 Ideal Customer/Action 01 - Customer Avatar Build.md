# Customer Avatar Build

## What to Do

Create a detailed profile of your ideal customer — not a made-up persona from a marketing textbook, but a real person based on your best existing clients. This becomes the foundation for all your marketing, sales, and product decisions.

## Why It Matters

Hormozi's Grand Slam Offer principle starts here: you cannot create an irresistible offer without knowing exactly who you are creating it for. Allan Dib's 1-Page Marketing Plan makes the same point — your ideal customer is "cell one" because everything else (message, media, lead generation) flows from it. And Michalowicz's Pumpkin Plan is blunt: find your best clients, kill the rest, and pour all your resources into the winners. You cannot do any of this without knowing who the winner looks like.

## How to Use This

Build this from real data — your actual best clients — not from imagination. If you do not have clients yet, use the closest equivalent: people you have spoken to, researched, or deeply understand.

---

## Step 1: Identify Your Best Clients

- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win · **List your 5 best clients from the past 12-24 months** — "Best" means: most profitable, most enjoyable, best results, referred others, and stayed longest. Not just "paid the most."
- [ ] **What do they have in common?** — Industry, company size, revenue range, role, years in business, team size. Find the demographic patterns.
- [ ] **What situation were they in when they came to you?** — What was happening in their business that made them seek help? Was it growth, pain, opportunity, or crisis? This is the trigger.

## Step 2: Map Their World

- [ ] **What is their biggest frustration right now?** — In their words, not yours. What do they complain about to their spouse, their friends, or their accountant? Write it the way they would say it.
- [ ] **What have they already tried that did not work?** — Failed solutions create scepticism. If they have tried a consultant before and been burned, you need to know that. If they have tried DIY and it did not stick, you need to know that too.
- [ ] **What does "success" look like to them?** — Not your definition. Theirs. For some it is revenue. For others it is time freedom. For others it is respect or legacy. The answer shapes how you sell to them.
- [ ] **Where do they spend time and attention?** — Which social platforms, events, publications, communities, podcasts? This tells you where to find more of them.
- [ ] **Who do they trust and listen to?** — Accountant, mentor, peer group, specific authors or thought leaders? These are your partnership and positioning clues.

## Step 3: Write the Avatar

- [ ] **Give them a name** — Not because it is a cute exercise, but because "Dave, 48, runs a £600K plumbing business with 12 staff and works 60-hour weeks" is infinitely more useful than "SME owners in the trades sector."
- [ ] **Write a paragraph describing their day** — What does a typical Monday look like? What stresses them before they get to work? What keeps them up at night? The more vivid this picture, the better your marketing speaks to them.
- [ ] **Summarise in one sentence: "[Name] is a [role] who [situation] and needs [transformation]"** — This becomes your targeting filter. Every marketing decision, every piece of content, every sales conversation gets measured against this person.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A named, specific avatar based on real client data
- A clear picture of their frustrations, failed solutions, and definition of success
- Knowledge of where to find more of them and who influences their decisions
- A one-sentence summary you can use as a filter for all marketing and sales decisions
- The foundation for Pain & Trigger Mapping and your Objection Playbook

## Coach Notes

The most common mistake is going too broad. "Business owners" is not an avatar. Push the client to be uncomfortably specific — they will resist because specificity feels like turning away money. Reframe: you are not turning away other clients, you are making your marketing speak directly to the ones most likely to buy and stay.

If a client cannot name 5 best clients, start with 3. If they cannot name 3, they either have not been in business long enough or they have not been selective enough — both are useful realisations.

Have them do the avatar build in the session rather than as homework. The conversation draws out insights they would not write down on their own.
