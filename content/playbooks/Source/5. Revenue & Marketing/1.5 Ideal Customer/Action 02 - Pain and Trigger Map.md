# Pain & Trigger Map

## What to Do

Map your ideal customer's biggest pain points in their language, then identify the specific triggers — the moments, events, or breaking points — that make them move from "I should probably do something" to "I need to fix this right now."

## Why It Matters

Most marketing describes the solution. Effective marketing describes the problem — in the exact words the customer uses. When a business owner reads your marketing and thinks "this person understands my exact situation," you have won before the sales conversation starts. But knowing the pain is only half the picture. You also need to know what makes them act. Most people live with business problems for months or years. Something specific tips them over the edge — a lost client, a failed hire, a tax bill, a health scare, a competitor's success. That trigger is when your marketing needs to reach them.

## How to Use This

Build this from real conversations, not assumptions. If you have not spoken to your ideal clients about their problems recently, this action starts with doing that.

---

## Map the Pain Points

- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win · **Talk to 3 existing or past ideal clients** — Ask: "What was the biggest frustration in your business before you started working with us?" Write down their exact words. Do not paraphrase or clean up the language — their words are better than yours.
- [ ] **Categorise their pains into 3 levels** — Surface pain (what they say first — "I need more leads"), deeper pain (what is underneath — "I'm doing all the sales myself and I'm exhausted"), and core pain (the real fear — "If this doesn't change, I'll burn out or the business will fail"). Your marketing should address all three, but the deeper ones create the strongest connection.
- [ ] **Identify which pain comes up most often** — If 3 out of 3 clients mention the same frustration, that is your lead message. Not every pain point you discover goes in your marketing — the most common and most emotional ones go first.
- [ ] **Note the language they use** — Specific phrases, metaphors, and descriptions. "I feel like I'm spinning plates" is marketing gold. "Operational inefficiency" is marketing death. Use their words, not yours.

## Identify the Triggers

- [ ] **Ask past clients: "What was happening when you decided to reach out to us?"** — Not why they chose you. What was happening in their life or business at that specific moment. There is always a trigger event.
- [ ] **List the common trigger events** — Examples: lost a key client, missed a tax deadline, partner threatened to leave, competitor launched something new, failed a hire, hit a revenue ceiling, had a health scare. Be specific.
- [ ] **Identify seasonal or cyclical triggers** — Does your ideal client face predictable pressure points? End of financial year? January planning? Pre-summer slump? September "back to business"? These tell you when to intensify your marketing.
- [ ] **Match triggers to marketing timing** — If the most common trigger is "end of financial year panic," your most aggressive marketing should run in February-March (UK) or October-November (US). Time your message to when the pain is sharpest.

## Build the Map

- [ ] **Create a simple document with 3 columns: Pain Point, Trigger, and Marketing Message** — Each row connects a real pain to the event that makes it urgent and the message that speaks to it. This becomes your marketing content calendar.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win · **Write 3 social media posts or email subject lines using the exact language from your client conversations** — Not polished marketing copy. Raw, honest language that makes your ideal client stop scrolling and think "that's me."

---

## Expected Outcome

- A list of real pain points in your customer's exact language — ready to use in marketing
- Identified trigger events that tell you when prospects are most likely to buy
- A marketing message map connecting pains to triggers to messages
- Content ideas based on real conversations, not guesswork
- A foundation for your Objection Playbook (what stops them buying once they are triggered)

## Coach Notes

If a client says "I don't have time to call past clients," push back. Three 10-minute conversations will produce better marketing insight than 10 hours of brainstorming. The call is the shortcut, not the extra work.

The depth matters. Surface pain ("I need more leads") is where everyone competes. Deeper pain ("I'm scared I'm going to lose the business I spent 15 years building") is where connection happens. Help the client go deeper — they often know the real pain but are uncomfortable saying it out loud.

This action feeds directly into Content Marketing, Sales Conversations, and Lead Generation at higher levels. The language and triggers discovered here should be referenced throughout.
