# Customer Grade Analysis

## What to Do

Grade every current customer or client A, B, C, or D based on how closely they match your ideal customer profile. Then decide what to do with each grade — protect the As, develop the Bs, tolerate the Cs, and exit the Ds.

## Why It Matters

Michalowicz's Pumpkin Plan is built on one insight: the biggest pumpkins grow when you remove the small ones and pour all resources into the winners. Most business owners treat all clients equally — same attention, same effort, same energy. But your A clients are worth 5-10x what your D clients are worth, and your D clients often consume more of your time than anyone. The Customer Grade Analysis makes this visible so you can stop subsidising the wrong clients with your best energy.

## How to Use This

You need your Customer Avatar (from the Avatar Build action) as your benchmark. Grade every active client against that ideal.

---

## Define Your Grading Criteria

- [ ] **Choose 4-6 criteria that matter to your business** — Common criteria include: revenue generated, profit margin, ease of working with, referral likelihood, payment reliability, growth potential, and alignment with your purpose. Pick the ones that genuinely drive value in your business.
- [ ] **Weight them if needed** — Not all criteria are equal. For some businesses, profitability matters more than revenue. For others, referral potential is king. Decide which 1-2 criteria matter most.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win · **Use this as a starting framework and adapt it to your business type:**

**Service businesses:**
- A = High revenue + profitable + easy to work with + refers others
- B = Good revenue but missing one element (e.g., profitable but never refers)
- C = Average across the board — pays bills but does not excite
- D = Low value + difficult + unprofitable + drains energy

**Recurring/subscription businesses:**
- A = Long tenure + high spend + low support needs + advocates
- B = Good tenure but could spend more or engage more
- C = Month-to-month, price-sensitive, minimal engagement
- D = High churn risk + high support cost + complains publicly

**Retail/hospitality:**
- A = Frequent visits + high spend per visit + brings others + leaves reviews
- B = Regular but modest spend, or high spend but infrequent
- C = Occasional, discount-driven, no loyalty
- D = Returns frequently, complains, costs more to serve than they spend

## Grade Your Clients

- [ ] **List every active client or customer segment** — For businesses with many customers, group into segments rather than grading individually.
- [ ] **Score each one against your criteria** — Be honest. The client you enjoy chatting with but who pays late and never refers is not an A.
- [ ] **Assign the grade: A, B, C, or D** — Do not overthink it. Your gut reaction plus the criteria will give you the right answer in most cases.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win · **Calculate what percentage of your revenue comes from each grade** — Often the result is startling: 20% of clients (the As) generate 60-80% of revenue. The Ds might represent 5% of revenue but 30% of your headaches.

## Decide What to Do

### A Clients — Protect and Replicate

- [ ] **What do your A clients have in common?** — This feeds directly back into your Customer Avatar. Your As ARE your ideal client profile in real life.
- [ ] **How are you showing them they are valued?** — If your A clients get the same experience as your D clients, you are underinvesting in the relationship that matters most. What could you do for your As that you do not do for everyone?
- [ ] **Where did they come from?** — The source of your A clients tells you where to find more of them. Double down on that channel.

### B Clients — Develop

- [ ] **What is missing?** — For each B, identify the one thing that would make them an A. Is it spend? Referrals? Engagement? Then create a plan to develop it.
- [ ] **Is it worth the effort?** — Some Bs will never become As. That is fine — they are still good clients. Focus development effort on the Bs with the highest potential.

### C Clients — Tolerate Strategically

- [ ] **Are any Cs costing you more than they generate?** — When you factor in time, energy, and opportunity cost, some Cs are actually loss-makers. Identify them.
- [ ] **Can you systematise the C relationship?** — Automate, templatise, or reduce the service level so Cs require less of your personal attention. They can stay, but they should not consume A-level effort.

### D Clients — Exit

- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win · **Pick one D client and plan a graceful exit this month** — Refer them elsewhere, let the contract expire, or have the honest conversation. Every D client you remove frees capacity for a future A.
- [ ] **Set a policy for future Ds** — How will you spot them before they become clients? What red flags will you watch for in sales conversations? Loop this back into your Objection Playbook and Serve & Swerve criteria.

## Expected Outcome

- A clear grade for every active client, making the invisible visible
- A data-backed understanding of where your revenue and profit actually come from
- A protection plan for your A clients — the relationships that matter most
- An exit plan for D clients — freeing up energy and capacity
- Sharpened ideal customer criteria based on what your real As look like
- A quarterly habit: re-grade and re-assess as your client base evolves

## Coach Notes

This action produces strong emotional reactions. Clients often realise their biggest revenue client is actually a C or D when graded honestly (difficult, low margin, never refers). Help them separate emotion from economics — the grading is about business value, not personal feelings.

The Pumpkin Plan metaphor works well: "You cannot grow a giant pumpkin while feeding 20 small ones. Cut the small ones, feed the big one." Most clients intellectually agree but emotionally resist. The grading makes the decision data-driven rather than gut-feel.

Revisit this quarterly. Client grades change — Bs become As, Cs become Ds, new clients enter. The grading should be a living practice, not a one-off exercise.

For businesses with hundreds of customers (retail, e-commerce), grade by segment rather than individually. "Repeat customers who spend over £X per visit" is your A segment. The principle is the same at any scale.
