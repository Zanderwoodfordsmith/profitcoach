# Content Cadence System

## What to Do

Design a consistent, repeatable schedule of valuable touchpoints that keeps your business in front of prospects throughout their buying journey. This means deciding what content goes out, through which channels, how often, and to which audience segments. The result is a system that runs predictably rather than a sporadic burst of content followed by weeks of silence.

Consistency beats brilliance in nurture marketing. A good email every week outperforms a great email once a quarter.

## Why It Matters

Ryan Deiss's Customer Value Optimization framework treats consistent touchpoints as the engine that moves prospects through the journey. Without regular contact, even warm prospects forget about you. Research consistently shows that most B2B purchases require 7-13 touchpoints before a decision is made. If you are making contact twice and then going quiet, you are losing to competitors who stay visible. Allan Dib reinforces this: your nurture system should work like a dripping tap, steadily building trust and familiarity until the prospect is ready to act.

## How to Use This

Start with your Nurture Journey Map. You need to know the stages and the gaps before you can plan the content. Then work through this process channel by channel.

---

## Step 1: Choose Your Channels

- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Pick your primary nurture channel** - For most B2B businesses, this is email. For some, it is LinkedIn or direct mail. Choose the one channel where your prospects are most likely to engage and commit to doing it well before adding others.
- [ ] **Identify your secondary channel** - One backup channel that reinforces the primary. If email is primary, social media is a good secondary. Two channels, done consistently, beats five channels done sporadically.
- [ ] **Audit what you already have** - Existing email sequences, social posts, blog articles, videos, guides. List everything. You probably have more content than you think, just not organised into a system.

## Step 2: Design the Cadence

- [ ] **Set a contact frequency for each stage** - Early stage (Aware): weekly or fortnightly. Mid stage (Interested/Considering): weekly. Late stage (Ready): 2-3 times per week. More frequent contact is appropriate as prospects warm up.
- [ ] **Plan content types for each stage** - Awareness: educational content, thought leadership, industry insights. Interest: case studies, how-to guides, webinars. Consideration: comparison content, objection handling, proof. Decision: offers, deadlines, direct calls to action.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Build a 4-week content calendar** - Map out exactly what goes out, to whom, through which channel, for the next 4 weeks. Do not plan further ahead until you have proven you can sustain a monthly rhythm.
- [ ] **Create content templates** - A standard email format, a standard social post structure, a standard case study format. Templates reduce creation time by 50% and maintain consistency.
- [ ] **Batch your content creation** - Set aside one block per week (or per month for smaller businesses) to create all nurture content in one go. Batching is dramatically more efficient than creating content ad hoc.

## Step 3: Systematise and Sustain

- [ ] **Automate what you can** - Email sequences should run automatically once triggered. Social posts can be scheduled in advance. The less manual work required, the more likely the system survives contact with reality.
- [ ] **Assign ownership** - Someone owns the nurture system. They are responsible for content going out on time, monitoring engagement, and flagging when something is not working. If nobody owns it, it dies within a month.
- [ ] **Set review points** - Monthly: check open rates, click rates, and engagement. Quarterly: review the full cadence against your nurture journey map. Are the right messages reaching the right people at the right time?
- [ ] **Build a content bank** - As you create content, store it in an organised library tagged by stage, topic, and channel. This makes it easy to repurpose, update, and re-use rather than starting from scratch every time.
- [ ] **Plan for seasonal adjustments** - Some periods are busier for your audience (year-end, new financial year, seasonal peaks). Adjust your cadence to increase contact during high-intent periods and pull back during low-engagement windows.

## Expected Outcome

- A documented content cadence with specific frequencies, channels, and content types per stage
- A 4-week content calendar ready to execute
- Templates that speed up content creation and maintain quality
- An automation plan that reduces manual work
- Clear ownership and review rhythms that keep the system running

## Coach Notes

The number one reason nurture systems fail is ambition. The client designs a beautiful plan with daily emails, three social channels, monthly webinars, and a podcast, then collapses under the workload within three weeks. Start small. One channel, one piece of content per week, for four weeks. Once that rhythm is established, add more.

If the client has no existing email list or content, start with a simple welcome sequence: 5 emails over 2 weeks that introduce the business, share a key insight, present a case study, address a common objection, and offer a next step. That alone puts them ahead of most competitors.

Push back on "we do not have time for content." They do not have time to chase every lead individually either. Nurture content is leverage: one email reaches hundreds of prospects simultaneously. The maths always works in favour of the system.
