# Objection Handling System

## What to Do

Prepare and practise responses to the five objections that appear in roughly 80% of all sales conversations. Instead of being caught off guard each time, you will build a documented response for each objection and rehearse until the responses feel natural. The goal is not to "overcome" objections through manipulation but to genuinely address legitimate concerns so the prospect can make an informed decision.

## Why It Matters

Objections are not rejection. They are requests for more information or reassurance. The prospect who objects is still engaged. The one who says nothing and disappears is the one you have lost. Most salespeople (and business owners who sell) either freeze when they hear an objection, get defensive, or start discounting. All three responses kill deals. A prepared, empathetic response keeps the conversation moving and often reveals the real concern behind the stated objection.

## How to Use This

Work through each of the five universal objections below. Write your specific responses in your own words and practise them until they feel natural, not robotic.

---

## Step 1: Map Your Common Objections

- [ ] Review your last 20 sales conversations and list every objection you heard
- [ ] Group similar objections together (most will fall into the five categories below)
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Note the one objection that throws you off most. Write a response for that one first.
- [ ] Identify any objections unique to your industry or offer that do not fit the five universals

## Step 2: Build Responses to the Five Universal Objections

### "It's too expensive" (Price)

- [ ] Write a response that reframes cost in terms of value, ROI, or cost of inaction
- [ ] Prepare a comparison: what is the cost of not solving this problem for another 6-12 months?
- [ ] If relevant, offer payment terms or a smaller starting package rather than discounting

### "I need to think about it" (Stall)

- [ ] Write a response that respects the request while uncovering the real concern underneath
- [ ] Ask: "Of course. What specifically do you want to think about? Is it the investment, the timing, or whether this is the right approach?"
- [ ] Set a specific follow-up date. "Thinking about it" with no deadline means the deal dies quietly.

### "I need to talk to my partner/team" (Authority)

- [ ] Write a response that helps them sell it internally rather than trying to bypass the decision-maker
- [ ] Offer to join a call with the other decision-maker or send a summary they can share
- [ ] Ask what the other person's main concern is likely to be, then address it proactively

### "The timing is not right" (Priority)

- [ ] Write a response that explores whether timing is a real constraint or a polite deflection
- [ ] Ask: "What would need to change for the timing to be right?" If they cannot answer specifically, timing is not the real issue.
- [ ] If timing is genuinely bad, propose a start date that works and get commitment now

### "I've tried something like this before and it did not work" (Scepticism)

- [ ] Write a response that acknowledges their experience without dismissing it
- [ ] Ask what specifically did not work and why. Then show how your approach addresses that gap.
- [ ] Use social proof from a client who had the same concern and succeeded

## Step 3: Practise and Refine

- [ ] Role-play each objection with a colleague, partner, or coach until responses feel conversational
- [ ] Record yourself delivering each response and listen back critically
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Practise the "think about it" response first. This is the most common objection and the one most people handle worst.
- [ ] Create a one-page cheat sheet with your five responses for reference before calls
- [ ] After each real sales call, note which objections came up and how your response worked. Refine based on what actually happens.

## Expected Outcome

- Written responses to the five universal sales objections
- Responses practised until they feel natural and conversational
- A one-page reference sheet for pre-call preparation
- A process for capturing new objections and building responses over time
- Increased confidence in handling pushback without freezing, discounting, or getting defensive

## Coach Notes

Objection handling is a skill, which means it improves with deliberate practice, not just experience. Most clients have heard the same objections dozens of times but have never written down a good response. The exercise of writing it out is transformative. Watch for clients who interpret "handling objections" as "pressuring people into buying." That is not the goal. The goal is to make sure the prospect has all the information they need to make a good decision for themselves. If the answer is genuinely no, let them go gracefully. The follow-up process (Action 9) will catch the ones who need more time. Role-play is the key coaching tool here. Do not just talk about it. Actually play the prospect and throw objections at them.
