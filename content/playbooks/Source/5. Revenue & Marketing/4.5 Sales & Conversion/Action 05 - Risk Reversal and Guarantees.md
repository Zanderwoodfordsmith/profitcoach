# Risk Reversal and Guarantees

## What to Do

Design a guarantee or risk reversal strategy that removes the "what if it does not work" objection from your prospects' minds without creating operational nightmares for your business. The right guarantee makes buying feel safe. The wrong one attracts tyre-kickers and drains your cash. This action helps you find the right balance.

## Why It Matters

Every purchase involves risk. The buyer is trading certain money for an uncertain outcome. The more you can shift that risk from the buyer to yourself, the easier the buying decision becomes. Alex Hormozi calls this "the grand slam offer" principle: make the offer so good people feel stupid saying no. But risk reversal is not just about money-back guarantees. It can be performance guarantees, conditional guarantees, milestone-based commitments, or simply removing barriers to getting started. The goal is to answer the prospect's unspoken question: "What happens if this does not work for me?"

## How to Use This

Think about the specific risks your prospects worry about before buying. Then design a guarantee that directly addresses those fears.

---

## Identify Your Buyer's Risks

- [ ] List the top 3-5 fears or concerns your prospects have before buying
- [ ] Common fears: it will not work for me, I will waste money, I will waste time, it is not what I expected, I will look foolish, I cannot get out if it is wrong
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Look at your last 10 lost deals. What reasons did people give for not buying? Those reasons are the risks your guarantee should address.
- [ ] Rank these fears by frequency and intensity
- [ ] Note which fears are rational (based on real risk) and which are emotional (based on uncertainty)

## Choose Your Guarantee Type

- [ ] **Unconditional money-back guarantee**: Full refund within a time period, no questions asked. Strongest signal but highest risk to you. Best for: lower-priced products, digital offerings, subscription-based services.
- [ ] **Conditional guarantee**: Refund or credit if specific conditions are met (e.g. "If you follow the programme and do not see results in 90 days, we will refund you"). Protects against tyre-kickers while still shifting risk.
- [ ] **Performance guarantee**: You commit to a specific measurable outcome. If you do not deliver, you keep working until you do or refund the difference. Best for: consultants, agencies, service providers.
- [ ] **Milestone guarantee**: Break the engagement into stages with go/no-go decisions. Client can exit after any milestone without further commitment.
- [ ] **Extended trial or pilot**: Let the client experience a small piece of the full offer before committing. Reduces perceived risk without requiring a formal guarantee.

## Design Your Specific Guarantee

- [ ] Choose the guarantee type that best fits your offer and audience
- [ ] Define the exact terms: what is guaranteed, for how long, under what conditions
- [ ] Calculate the financial risk to your business. What percentage of clients would you expect to claim?
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Ask 3 past clients what would have made their buying decision easier. Their answers will tell you exactly what to guarantee.
- [ ] Test whether the guarantee creates perverse incentives (people buying with the intention to claim)
- [ ] Write the guarantee in plain language, not legal jargon

## Implement and Communicate

- [ ] Add the guarantee to your sales script at the objection-handling stage
- [ ] Include it on your website, proposals, and marketing materials
- [ ] Train anyone who sells for you on how to present the guarantee with confidence
- [ ] Track claim rates monthly. If claims are above 5-10%, the guarantee terms need tightening or your delivery needs improving.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A clear understanding of the risks your buyers perceive
- A specific guarantee designed to address those risks
- Financial exposure calculated and deemed acceptable
- The guarantee integrated into your sales process and materials
- A tracking system for claim rates

## Coach Notes

Many clients resist guarantees because they focus on the downside (people claiming refunds). Reframe it: a guarantee that increases conversions by 20% but gets claimed 5% of the time is a massive net win. The other reframe is that if you cannot guarantee your work, what does that say about your confidence in your own product? Conditional guarantees are usually the sweet spot for service businesses. They shift risk while filtering out people who are not serious. Push clients to actually use the guarantee in sales conversations, not just put it on the website. Spoken guarantees convert better than written ones because they feel personal and committed.
