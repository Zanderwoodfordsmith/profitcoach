# Implement Graduated Responsibility

## What to Do

Systematically increase the scope and complexity of what each leader is accountable for, building confidence and capability progressively. Think of it as a structured series of steps - not one big leap from "team member" to "leader of everything." Each step adds a little more responsibility, a little more complexity, and a little more independence. Each step is only taken when the previous one is solid.

## Why It Matters

The most common way leaders fail is by being given too much, too fast. They get promoted from managing 3 people to managing 12 overnight. Or they go from handling one function to being responsible for three. The result: overwhelm, mistakes, and loss of confidence. Graduated responsibility prevents this by matching the increase in scope to the person's growing capability. It's how you build leaders who are genuinely ready for each next level, not just thrown into the deep end.

## How to Use This

Map out the levels of responsibility for each leader, assess where they are now, and create a deliberate plan for what comes next. Move them up only when they've demonstrated readiness.

---

## Step 1: Define the Levels

- [ ] **Map 4-5 levels of responsibility for each leadership role** - Example for an operations leader: Level 1 - manage daily tasks for a small team. Level 2 - own weekly team meetings and reporting. Level 3 - manage department budget and resource allocation. Level 4 - lead cross-functional projects. Level 5 - full P&L responsibility for the area.
- [ ] **Make each level specific and observable** - "More responsibility" is not a level. "Accountable for monthly department reporting and budget variance explanations" is a level.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Identify what level each current leader is genuinely operating at** - Not where their job title suggests they should be. Where they actually are. The gap between title and reality is your starting point.
- [ ] **Define what "ready to move up" looks like at each level** - What evidence would you need to see before adding the next layer? Consistent delivery? Good decision-making? Positive team feedback? Write it down.

## Step 2: Assess Current Position

- [ ] **Place each leader on their level map** - Be honest. Some people are at Level 2 but their title says Level 4. That mismatch causes problems for everyone.
- [ ] **Discuss the assessment with each person** - "Here's where I see you right now. Here's the next level. Here's what I'd need to see to move you there." Transparency builds trust and motivation.
- [ ] **Identify any leaders who've been over-promoted** - Sometimes people are already in roles beyond their current capability. That's not a failure - it's a gap to address. Pull back support where needed, or adjust the role.

## Step 3: Plan the Next Step

- [ ] **Choose one additional responsibility to add for each leader** - Just one. Not three. The next logical step from where they are now.
- [ ] **Set a timeframe** - "Over the next 6-8 weeks, you'll take on weekly team reporting. I'll support you through the first 3 weeks, then you'll run it independently."
- [ ] **Provide support structures** - Templates, coaching time, a peer to consult with. The responsibility is new. The support should be there until it's not needed.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Hand over one thing you currently do to your most ready leader this week** - Something specific, bounded, and within their capability. "From this week, you run the Monday team stand-up." Immediate growth, immediate relief for you.
- [ ] **Monitor without micromanaging** - Check in on how it's going, but resist the urge to hover. If you're reviewing their every decision, you haven't actually delegated.

## Step 4: Review and Progress

- [ ] **Evaluate after the agreed timeframe** - Did they handle it? What went well? What was hard? Are they ready for the next step?
- [ ] **Celebrate each successful step** - Moving up a level is a genuine achievement. Acknowledge it publicly and privately.
- [ ] **Adjust pace to the individual** - Some people move through levels quickly. Others need more time at each stage. That's fine. This isn't a race. Pushing someone up before they're ready creates problems for everyone.
- [ ] **Document the progression** - Keep a simple record of what each leader has taken on and when. This becomes valuable evidence for future promotion decisions, pay reviews, and succession planning.

---

## Expected Outcome

- Leaders grow into bigger roles gradually, with high success rates
- Overwhelm and burnout from sudden responsibility jumps are eliminated
- You have a clear, documented view of each leader's progression
- Promotion decisions are backed by evidence of demonstrated capability
- The business builds leadership capacity systematically, not accidentally

## Coach Notes

The hardest part is patience. Owners who are ready to step back want to hand over everything at once. Coach them to resist that urge. Each step should feel slightly uncomfortable for the emerging leader but not overwhelming. If they're not slightly stretched, the step isn't big enough. If they're drowning, it's too big. Watch for the "snap-back" pattern: the owner delegates something, the leader struggles, and the owner takes it back. That's worse than never delegating at all. Instead, provide coaching and support through the struggle. The struggle is the growth.
