# Build Your Leadership Accountability System

## What to Do

Set up regular review meetings where your leaders report on outcomes, decisions made, and challenges faced. This is the system that holds leaders accountable for results, not just activity or effort. Each leader presents what happened in their area, what decisions they made, what worked, what didn't, and what they're doing about it. You listen, ask questions, and coach.

## Why It Matters

Delegation without accountability is abdication. You can grant authority, build development plans, and assign challenge projects, but without a consistent system for reviewing outcomes, you have no idea whether it's working. Leaders who know they'll be asked to account for their decisions make better decisions. Leaders who know nobody's watching drift. An accountability system keeps everyone sharp and gives you the visibility you need without being in the middle of everything.

## How to Use This

Set up the structure, run the first few meetings yourself to establish the standard, then make it self-sustaining.

---

## Step 1: Design the Review Structure

- [ ] **Choose the review frequency** - Monthly for most leaders. Fortnightly for those in critical or fast-changing areas. Weekly is too frequent for leadership reviews and creates micromanagement. Monthly is the sweet spot.
- [ ] **Set a fixed meeting slot** - Same day, same time, every month. Leaders plan their reporting around it. Consistency is the foundation of accountability.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Start with a simple standing agenda** - Every leader presents: (1) Key results this period. (2) Decisions made and rationale. (3) Challenges and how they were handled. (4) What's planned for next period. Same structure every time.
- [ ] **Decide on individual or group format** - Individual reviews give depth and privacy. Group reviews create peer accountability and shared learning. Best practice: monthly individual reviews with quarterly group leadership reviews.
- [ ] **Set the tone clearly from the start** - This is not a tribunal. It's a professional review focused on outcomes and learning. The atmosphere should be direct but supportive. Hard questions asked with genuine curiosity, not judgement.

## Step 2: Define What Leaders Report On

- [ ] **Outcomes, not activities** - "We delivered 15 client projects on time" is an outcome. "The team was really busy" is not. Push leaders to quantify results wherever possible.
- [ ] **Decisions made** - Every leader should come prepared to discuss 2-3 significant decisions they made. What were the options? Why did they choose this path? What was the result? This builds decision-making rigour over time.
- [ ] **Variances from plan** - What was supposed to happen vs what actually happened? If there's a gap, what caused it and what's being done? This isn't about blame. It's about understanding reality.
- [ ] **People and team health** - How is their team performing? Any issues? Any wins? Leaders who only talk about numbers and never mention people aren't leading.
- [ ] **Help needed** - What's stuck? What decisions need escalation? What resources are missing? This is where you add value without taking back control.

## Step 3: Hold the Right Standard

- [ ] **Praise outcomes publicly, address problems privately** - In group settings, celebrate wins. Take performance concerns offline to individual conversations.
- [ ] **Challenge excuses constructively** - "The market was tough" might be true, but "what could you have done differently given those conditions?" is the leadership question.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **End each review with one specific commitment from the leader for next period** - Written down, specific, with a deadline. This creates natural accountability for the next meeting.
- [ ] **Track commitments from meeting to meeting** - Start each review by checking last meeting's commitments. Were they delivered? This simple practice transforms accountability from theoretical to real.
- [ ] **Distinguish between results and effort** - Good effort with bad results still needs addressing. Good results from lucky breaks shouldn't be over-praised. Look at the quality of thinking and decision-making, not just the outcome.

## Step 4: Make It Self-Sustaining

- [ ] **Have leaders prepare a one-page written report before each meeting** - This forces them to reflect and organise their thinking. A verbal-only review lets people ramble and dodge.
- [ ] **Keep records of each review** - Brief notes: key outcomes reported, decisions discussed, commitments made. Over time, this creates a valuable record of each leader's development and performance.
- [ ] **Evolve the format based on what works** - After 3-4 cycles, ask: "What's adding value and what's just process?" Cut what doesn't work. Add what's missing. The system should serve the leaders, not burden them.

---

## Expected Outcome

- Leaders report on outcomes and decisions consistently, creating a clear performance record
- Decision-making quality improves because leaders know they'll be asked to explain their reasoning
- Problems surface faster because reviews create a natural checkpoint
- You maintain visibility without micromanaging day-to-day operations
- A culture of accountability and transparency develops across the leadership team

## Coach Notes

The first 2-3 reviews will feel uncomfortable. Leaders aren't used to presenting their outcomes formally, and owners aren't used to reviewing without jumping in to solve problems. Coach the owner to ask questions, not give answers. The review meeting is where leaders practise being leaders, not where the owner takes back control. If a leader consistently arrives unprepared, that's the first accountability conversation. If they consistently report poor results with no improvement plan, that's the second. The system only works if the standards are maintained. Inconsistency kills accountability faster than anything.
