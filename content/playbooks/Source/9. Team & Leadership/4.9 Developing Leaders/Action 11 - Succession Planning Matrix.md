# Build Your Succession Planning Matrix

## What to Do

Map who could step into each key role if needed, identify the skill gaps between where they are and where they'd need to be, and create development plans to close those gaps. This isn't about expecting people to leave. It's about making sure the business isn't fragile. If your best salesperson resigned tomorrow, who would pick up their clients? If your operations lead went on maternity leave, who would keep things running? Succession planning answers those questions before you need to answer them in a crisis.

## Why It Matters

Most small businesses have at least 2-3 roles where one person leaving would cause serious disruption. The owner knows this but avoids thinking about it because the answer is uncomfortable ("nobody could replace them"). A succession matrix forces that conversation now, when you have time to develop people, rather than later, when you're scrambling. It also reduces your dependency on any single individual, which makes the business more resilient and more valuable.

## How to Use This

Identify your critical roles, assess backup readiness, and create development plans for the gaps. Update the matrix every 6 months.

---

## Step 1: Identify Critical Roles

- [ ] **List every role where one person leaving would cause significant disruption** - Think about: who holds critical client relationships, who has knowledge nobody else has, who runs a process that would break without them, and who makes decisions that would stall.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Apply the "hit by a bus" test** - For each person, ask: "If they were suddenly unavailable for 3 months, what would break?" Any answer other than "we'd manage fine" means the role needs a succession plan.
- [ ] **Include your own role** - This is the one most owners skip. Who could run the business if you needed to step away? If the answer is "nobody," that's your most urgent succession gap.
- [ ] **Rank roles by criticality** - Not all gaps are equal. A role that would shut down revenue is more critical than a role that would slow down admin. Prioritise accordingly.

## Step 2: Assess Backup Readiness

- [ ] **For each critical role, name potential successors** - Who could step in? They don't need to be perfect. They need to be the best available option. Sometimes the answer is: "nobody internal, we'd need to hire."
- [ ] **Rate each successor's readiness** - Ready now (could step in tomorrow). Ready in 6 months (with focused development). Ready in 12+ months (significant gaps to close). Not viable (wrong skills or temperament).
- [ ] **Identify the specific gaps between current capability and role requirements** - Not vague gaps like "needs more experience." Specific gaps like "doesn't understand client pricing model" or "has never managed more than 2 people."
- [ ] **Check for single points of failure** - Any role where only one person holds the knowledge? Any process that only one person can run? These are your highest-risk areas.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Identify one piece of critical knowledge that lives in only one person's head and document it this week** - A process document, a client relationship brief, a pricing methodology. Getting it out of one head reduces risk immediately.

## Step 3: Build the Matrix

- [ ] **Create a simple grid** - Rows: Critical roles. Columns: Potential successors, readiness rating, key gaps, development actions.
- [ ] **Fill in every cell honestly** - Empty cells (no successor identified) are the most important finding. They show you where the business is most exposed.
- [ ] **Highlight the highest-risk combinations** - Critical role + no ready successor = urgent. Critical role + successor ready in 6 months = manageable. Critical role + successor ready now = strong position.
- [ ] **For each gap, write one development action** - Something specific that would move the successor closer to readiness. Shadow the current role-holder for a month. Take over one responsibility. Complete a specific training.

## Step 4: Act on It

- [ ] **Start development for your top 2-3 succession priorities** - You can't develop everyone at once. Focus on the highest-risk gaps first.
- [ ] **Cross-train where possible** - Two people who can do one job is always better than one. Cross-training doesn't require formal succession planning. It just requires sharing knowledge.
- [ ] **Build "run books" for critical roles** - Document how the role operates: key processes, important contacts, regular commitments, access credentials, and where to find things. If someone had to step in tomorrow, what would they need?
- [ ] **Review the matrix every 6 months** - People leave. People grow. Priorities shift. The matrix is a living document, not a one-off exercise.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Pick one critical role and have the current holder spend 30 minutes teaching their backup one key process this week** - Knowledge transfer starts with a single conversation. Don't wait for a formal programme.

---

## Expected Outcome

- You know exactly where the business is exposed if key people leave
- Development plans are in place for the most critical succession gaps
- Critical knowledge is documented and shared, not locked in individual heads
- The business becomes more resilient and less dependent on any single person
- You can make future hiring and development decisions with a clear view of what the business needs

## Coach Notes

This action often triggers anxiety. Owners don't want to think about key people leaving, and they especially don't want to think about their own succession. Normalise it: "This isn't about expecting the worst. It's about building a business that's strong enough to handle anything." For very small businesses (under 10 people), full succession planning may feel excessive. Scale it down: focus on the 2-3 most critical roles and identify one backup action for each. That's enough to dramatically reduce risk. The most valuable outcome is often the knowledge documentation, not the matrix itself. Getting critical processes out of people's heads and into written form pays dividends far beyond succession planning.
