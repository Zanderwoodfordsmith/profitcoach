# Map Authority for Each Leader

## What to Do

Define clear boundaries around what decisions each leader in your business can make independently, what requires consultation with others, and what needs your approval. The output is a documented authority map for each leader showing their decision rights in black and white. No ambiguity. No guessing.

This builds on the Decision Authority Matrix (4.8) but goes deeper - giving each individual leader a personalised view of their authority that matches their capability and role.

## Why It Matters

Undefined authority creates two equally damaging problems. Some leaders hold back and check everything with you, creating bottlenecks and slowing the business down. Others charge ahead and make decisions they shouldn't, creating risk and cleaning up messes. Authority mapping eliminates both by making the boundaries explicit. Leaders move faster within their boundaries. They stop before crossing them. And you stop getting interrupted for things that don't need you.

## How to Use This

Work through the checklist for each leader individually. Their authority map should reflect their current capability, not where you hope they'll be in a year.

---

## Authority Mapping Checklist

- [ ] **List each leader and their area of responsibility** - Operations lead, sales manager, finance manager, whoever holds a leadership role. Each gets their own authority map.
- [ ] **Define three authority zones for each person** - Zone 1: Full authority (decide and act, no approval needed). Zone 2: Consult first (discuss with specific people, then decide). Zone 3: Escalate (bring to the owner for decision).
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Start by listing the 10 most common decisions that come to you from each leader** - These are your candidates for Zone 1 (push down) or Zone 2 (share). Most of them don't need you.
- [ ] **Map spending authority** - What can each leader spend without approval? Set a specific number. "Jane can approve any operational spend under £500 without checking." Clear. Simple. Effective.
- [ ] **Map people decisions** - Can they approve leave? Assign work? Give informal feedback? Initiate a disciplinary process? Each of these needs a clear zone.
- [ ] **Map client-facing decisions** - Can they resolve a complaint? Offer a discount? Change a delivery date? Reject a request? Client decisions often need speed, so push authority as far down as capability allows.
- [ ] **Map process decisions** - Can they change how their team works? Adopt a new tool? Restructure a workflow? Process authority is where many leaders feel most constrained unnecessarily.
- [ ] **Set financial signing limits** - Purchases, invoices, expenses, and contracts. Each leader should know their exact limit without having to ask.
- [ ] **Document exceptions clearly** - "Full authority for all hiring except senior roles" is better than "authority for hiring" because it removes ambiguity about where the boundary is.
- [ ] **Review each map with the leader** - Walk through it together. Ask: "Does this feel right? Is there anything here that would slow you down unnecessarily? Anything that feels like too much?" Their input matters.
- [ ] **Put it in writing** - A simple one-page document per leader. Post it somewhere accessible. Authority that lives only in conversation gets forgotten or reinterpreted.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Expand one authority zone this week for your most capable leader** - Something they've been checking with you about but could clearly handle alone. Tell them: "This is yours now. You don't need to ask."

---

## Expected Outcome

- Every leader knows exactly what they can decide independently
- Bottlenecks reduce because people aren't waiting for unnecessary approvals
- Risk reduces because boundaries are clear and documented
- You get interrupted less for decisions that don't need your input
- New authority can be granted deliberately as leaders develop, not accidentally

## Coach Notes

Authority mapping often reveals that the owner's biggest frustration ("nobody makes decisions") is actually the owner's own creation ("I've never told them what they can decide"). The conversation itself is therapeutic. Many owners discover they've been holding authority they didn't even realise they were holding. Start conservative and expand. It's much easier to grant more authority over time than to pull it back. Pulling authority back after granting it damages trust and confidence. Review authority maps every 6 months as leaders grow and the business evolves.
