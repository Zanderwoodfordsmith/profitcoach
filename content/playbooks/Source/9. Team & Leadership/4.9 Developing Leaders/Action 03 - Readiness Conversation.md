# Have the Readiness Conversation

## What to Do

Sit down with each potential leader and have a direct, honest discussion about their interest in leadership, their career aspirations, and how ready they feel for broader responsibility. Don't assume people want to lead just because they're good at their job. Some do. Some don't. And some want to but have concerns they haven't voiced. This conversation surfaces all of that.

## Why It Matters

You can build the best development plan in the world, but if the person doesn't actually want to be a leader, it's wasted effort. Worse, promoting someone who didn't really want the role creates resentment, underperformance, and often a resignation. The readiness conversation prevents that by checking alignment before you invest. It also signals to the person that you're thinking about their future, which builds loyalty and engagement.

## How to Use This

Schedule a private, unhurried conversation. Not in a corridor. Not at the end of a busy day. Give it proper time and space. Follow the conversation guide below.

---

## Before the Conversation

- [ ] **Review their leadership potential assessment** - Know where they sit on your matrix. What are their strengths? Where are the gaps? What does their 360 feedback say?
- [ ] **Prepare your honest view** - What role or level could you see this person growing into? In what timeframe? Be realistic, not aspirational.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Think about what you'd want to hear if you were in their position** - People want to know: Am I valued? Is there a future for me here? What would it look like? That's what you're addressing.

## During the Conversation

- [ ] **Start by sharing why you wanted to talk** - "I've been thinking about the future of the business and your role in it. I wanted to have an honest conversation about where you see yourself heading."
- [ ] **Ask about their career aspirations** - "Where do you see yourself in 2-3 years? What kind of work excites you? What would your ideal role look like?" Listen. Don't pitch your plan yet.
- [ ] **Explore their interest in leadership specifically** - "How do you feel about managing people? Leading a team? Taking on broader responsibility beyond your current role?" Some people light up. Others hesitate. Both responses are useful information.
- [ ] **Ask about concerns or barriers** - "Is there anything that worries you about stepping into a leadership role? What would need to be true for you to feel confident doing it?" This is where the real conversation happens.
- [ ] **Share your perspective honestly** - "Here's what I see in you. Here's the opportunity I think exists. Here's what development would look like." Be specific. Vague promises don't land.
- [ ] **Discuss the trade-offs openly** - Leadership means less hands-on work, more meetings, harder conversations, and being responsible for other people's performance. Not everyone wants that. Make sure they understand what they're signing up for.
- [ ] **Ask for their honest answer** - "Given all of that, is this something you genuinely want to pursue? It's completely fine to say no or not yet." Give them permission to be honest. Pressure creates false yeses.
- [ ] **Agree on next steps** - If yes: outline the development plan and timeline. If no: discuss what growth looks like in their current track. If not yet: agree what would need to change for them to feel ready.

## After the Conversation

- [ ] **Document what was agreed** - Brief notes: aspirations discussed, interest level, concerns raised, next steps agreed.
- [ ] **Follow through on whatever you committed to** - If you said you'd create a development plan, do it within a week. Broken promises after a readiness conversation destroy trust faster than anything.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Send a brief follow-up message within 24 hours** - "Thanks for the conversation yesterday. Here's what we agreed on..." This shows you take their development seriously.
- [ ] **Revisit in 3 months** - Things change. Someone who said "not yet" might be ready. Someone who said "yes" might be having second thoughts. Keep the dialogue open.

---

## Expected Outcome

- You know who genuinely wants leadership responsibility, not just who seems capable
- Potential leaders feel valued and invested in, which increases retention
- You avoid promoting people into roles they don't want
- Concerns and barriers are surfaced early so you can address them
- Development plans are built on genuine alignment, not assumptions

## Coach Notes

This conversation requires vulnerability from both sides. The owner needs to be honest about what they see and what they can offer. The potential leader needs to be honest about what they want. Coach the owner to create space for honesty by being open themselves. "Here's what I think your strengths are. Here's where I see a gap. And here's what I can commit to." That kind of directness invites directness in return. Watch for the "yes person" who agrees because they think they should. Probe deeper. "What specifically excites you about this?" If they can't answer, the yes might not be real.
