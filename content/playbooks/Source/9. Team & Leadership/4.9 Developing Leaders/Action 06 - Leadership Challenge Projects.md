# Assign Leadership Challenge Projects

## What to Do

Give emerging leaders specific projects that require them to influence without authority, manage complexity, and deliver results through others. These aren't their normal work. They're stretch assignments designed to build leadership muscles in a controlled way. The project is real (it matters to the business), but the primary purpose is development.

## Why It Matters

You can't coach someone into being a leader through conversation alone. At some point, they need to lead something. Challenge projects create that opportunity with a safety net. The emerging leader gets to practice leading a cross-functional effort, managing ambiguity, and being accountable for an outcome - while you provide support and coaching throughout. It's leadership training that also produces business results.

## How to Use This

Select a project, brief the emerging leader, provide support throughout, and debrief at the end. The debrief is where most of the learning happens.

---

## Project Selection Checklist

- [ ] **Choose a project that matters but isn't mission-critical** - Important enough that effort is taken seriously. Not so critical that failure would cause real damage. Improving a process, launching a small initiative, or solving a persistent problem all work well.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Think about what's been on your "I should really sort this out" list for months** - Those neglected-but-important projects are perfect challenge assignments. You get it handled and they get development.
- [ ] **Make sure the project requires working across teams or functions** - Leading people who don't report to you is the hardest and most valuable leadership skill. Pick projects that force collaboration with people outside their usual area.
- [ ] **Set a clear objective and deadline** - "Improve client onboarding" is too vague. "Reduce client onboarding time from 3 weeks to 1 week by end of Q2" gives them a clear target to lead towards.
- [ ] **Define the boundaries** - What resources can they use? What decisions can they make? Who do they report to? What's the budget? Clear boundaries prevent frustration and scope creep.

## Briefing the Leader

- [ ] **Explain why you've chosen this project for them specifically** - Connect it to their development plan. "This project requires you to influence the ops team without having authority over them. That's exactly the skill we identified as your priority."
- [ ] **Be honest about the development purpose** - "This is a real project and I need it done well. It's also a deliberate stretch assignment for your growth. Both things are true."
- [ ] **Agree on check-in points** - Weekly or fortnightly touchpoints where they update you on progress and you provide coaching. Not micromanaging. Supporting.
- [ ] **Set them up for success, not failure** - Introduce them to key stakeholders. Give them context they'll need. Remove obvious obstacles upfront. The challenge should be in the leadership, not in navigating politics without a map.

## During the Project

- [ ] **Resist the urge to intervene** - Unless something is going seriously wrong, let them lead. Mistakes are learning opportunities. Jumping in tells them (and everyone else) that you don't trust them.
- [ ] **Coach, don't direct** - When they come to you with a problem, ask "what do you think?" before giving your view. If they're heading for a mistake, ask questions that help them see it rather than telling them the answer.
- [ ] **Watch how they handle setbacks** - Do they blame, panic, withdraw, or problem-solve? How they respond to adversity tells you more about their leadership potential than how they handle success.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Ask the people they're working with how it's going** - Quick, informal check. "How's the onboarding project going? How's [name] doing leading it?" This gives you data the emerging leader might not share.

## After the Project

- [ ] **Run a proper debrief (60-90 minutes)** - What was the objective? What happened? What went well? What would they do differently? What did they learn about leadership? This conversation is where development crystallises.
- [ ] **Acknowledge the result and the growth** - Celebrate what they achieved and name the specific leadership skills they demonstrated. "You got the ops team on board despite initial resistance. That's influence without authority. That's leadership."
- [ ] **Agree on the next challenge** - Each project should be slightly harder than the last. More stakeholders, bigger scope, higher stakes. Progressive difficulty builds capability.

---

## Expected Outcome

- Emerging leaders gain real leadership experience in a supported environment
- Business problems get solved by people other than you
- You see how people perform under leadership pressure before promoting them
- The team sees emerging leaders stepping up, which normalises growth
- Each completed project builds confidence for the next, bigger challenge

## Coach Notes

Project selection is everything. Too easy and there's no development. Too hard and they fail, lose confidence, and the business suffers. Aim for about 70% difficulty - hard enough to stretch them, achievable enough that success is likely with effort. The most common mistake owners make is rescuing too early. When the project hits a rough patch (and it will), the owner swoops in and takes over. Coach the owner to sit on their hands. The struggle IS the development. If they rescue, the emerging leader learns: "When it gets hard, someone else will handle it." That's the opposite of leadership.
