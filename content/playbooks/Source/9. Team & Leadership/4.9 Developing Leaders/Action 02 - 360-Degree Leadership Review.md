# Run a 360-Degree Leadership Review

## What to Do

Gather structured feedback from peers, direct reports, and customers about how a potential leader is perceived across the business. Your view of someone is only one perspective. A 360-degree review shows you how they operate when you're not in the room. It reveals blind spots, strengths they don't know they have, and gaps that only surface in day-to-day interactions.

## Why It Matters

Owners often overestimate or underestimate their people because they only see one dimension of their work. The person who's brilliant with you in meetings might be dismissive with their team. The person who seems quiet might be the one everyone turns to when things go wrong. A 360 review gives you the full picture so you make leadership development decisions based on reality, not your limited viewpoint.

## How to Use This

Choose a potential leader to assess, select reviewers, collect feedback, and synthesise it. Keep it simple - this isn't an HR exercise. It's a practical tool.

---

## Step 1: Prepare the Review

- [ ] **Choose who you're reviewing** - This should be someone already identified as having leadership potential. Don't run 360s on everyone. Start with your top 2-3 candidates.
- [ ] **Select 5-8 reviewers per person** - Mix of peers (same level), direct reports (if they have any), people they collaborate with frequently, and 1-2 clients if appropriate.
- [ ] **Explain the purpose clearly** - Tell reviewers: "We're exploring leadership development for [name]. Your honest feedback helps us support them better. Responses are confidential." People give better feedback when they understand why.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Use a simple form, not a complex survey** - 5-7 open-ended questions beat 50 rating scales. You want stories and observations, not averages.

## Step 2: Ask the Right Questions

- [ ] **Question 1: What does this person do well that others should learn from?** - Surfaces strengths they might not recognise in themselves.
- [ ] **Question 2: When things get difficult, how does this person respond?** - Reveals character under pressure. This is the single most important leadership trait.
- [ ] **Question 3: How well does this person communicate - especially when delivering bad news or managing disagreements?** - Communication is where most new leaders struggle first.
- [ ] **Question 4: Would you want to work for this person? Why or why not?** - Direct and revealing. The reasons behind "yes" or "no" are more valuable than the answer itself.
- [ ] **Question 5: What one thing would make this person a stronger leader?** - Forces specific, actionable feedback rather than vague praise or criticism.
- [ ] **Question 6: Rate overall leadership readiness on a scale of 1-10** - Gives you a quick benchmark across reviewers. Big variance in scores is itself a finding worth exploring.

## Step 3: Collect and Synthesise

- [ ] **Give reviewers a deadline** - One week is enough. Any longer and it gets forgotten. Send one reminder at day 5.
- [ ] **Read all responses looking for patterns** - One person saying "they don't listen" is an opinion. Three people saying it is a trend. Look for themes that appear across multiple reviewers.
- [ ] **Separate facts from feelings** - "They interrupted me in the meeting on Tuesday" is a fact. "They don't respect people" is an interpretation. Focus on the facts.
- [ ] **Create a one-page summary** - Three sections: Key strengths (what everyone agrees they do well), Development areas (patterns in what needs work), and Surprises (anything unexpected or contradictory).
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Look for the gap between self-perception and team perception** - If the candidate thinks they're a great communicator but three peers say they don't listen, that gap is your coaching priority.

## Step 4: Deliver the Feedback

- [ ] **Share the summary with the candidate in a one-to-one** - Not via email. In person or video. Allow time for questions and reactions.
- [ ] **Lead with strengths** - Start with what people value about them. Then move to development areas. This isn't a performance review. It's a development conversation.
- [ ] **Keep responses anonymous** - Never reveal who said what. The moment you do, people stop being honest in future reviews.

---

## Expected Outcome

- You understand how potential leaders are perceived across the business, not just by you
- Blind spots surface before they become leadership problems
- The candidate gets specific, actionable feedback to guide their development
- You make better decisions about who to invest in and how to develop them
- A culture of feedback starts forming, making future reviews easier

## Coach Notes

First-time 360s can feel threatening. The candidate may get defensive or upset. That's normal. Coach the owner to deliver feedback with care and frame it as investment in the person's growth, not criticism. If the business has never done anything like this before, start with one person who's confident and receptive. Let the positive experience spread. For businesses under 10 people, a full 360 may feel too formal. In that case, the owner can have informal conversations with 3-4 people and synthesise the themes. The structure matters more than the formality.
