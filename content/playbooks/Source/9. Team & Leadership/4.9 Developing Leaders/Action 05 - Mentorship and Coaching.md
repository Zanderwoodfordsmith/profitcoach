# Set Up Mentorship and Coaching Sessions

## What to Do

Establish regular one-to-one sessions with each emerging leader focused specifically on their leadership development. These are not operational check-ins about projects and deadlines. They're dedicated time to discuss leadership challenges, practice decision-making, and build the skills outlined in their development plan.

## Why It Matters

Leadership isn't learned in a classroom. It's learned through experience, reflection, and guided practice. The mentorship session is where learning actually happens. It's the space where an emerging leader can say "I handled this situation and I'm not sure I got it right" and get honest, constructive feedback. Without this dedicated time, leadership development gets crowded out by day-to-day urgency. Every week.

## How to Use This

Set up the sessions, follow the structure below, and commit to consistency. Missing sessions sends a clear message that development isn't a real priority.

---

## Step 1: Set Up the Sessions

- [ ] **Schedule a fixed time slot** - Every week or fortnight, 30-45 minutes. Same day, same time. Put it in both calendars as recurring and non-negotiable.
- [ ] **Separate from operational one-to-ones** - If you already have a weekly catch-up about their work, keep that. This is different. This is about their growth as a leader, not their task list.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Book the first 8 sessions right now** - Booking one at a time leads to cancellations and drift. Block out 8 weeks and protect them.
- [ ] **Choose the right setting** - Private, unhurried, no interruptions. Not at a desk between emails. Coffee shop, walking meeting, or a closed office door all work well.

## Step 2: Run Effective Sessions

- [ ] **Start with their agenda, not yours** - "What's on your mind this week?" lets them bring real challenges. If they have nothing, they're not reflecting enough between sessions, and that's worth discussing.
- [ ] **Ask more than you tell** - Your job is to help them think, not to give them answers. "What options did you consider?" "What would you do differently?" "What's the risk of each approach?" These questions build their judgement.
- [ ] **Use real situations as teaching moments** - When they describe a challenge, resist the urge to solve it. Instead, guide them through their own thinking. "What do you think the right move is? Why?"
- [ ] **Practice difficult conversations in the session** - If they need to give tough feedback, hold a difficult meeting, or deliver bad news, rehearse it together first. Role-play the conversation. This feels awkward at first but dramatically improves the real conversation.
- [ ] **Review decisions they've made since last session** - Not to judge, but to build a habit of reflection. "What went well? What would you change? What did you learn?"
- [ ] **End with one specific action for the next session** - Something to practice, try, or reflect on. Small, concrete, and connected to their development plan.

## Step 3: Build the Coaching Relationship

- [ ] **Be honest, not just supportive** - Encouragement matters, but sugarcoating doesn't help. If they handled something poorly, say so clearly and help them see what they'd do differently.
- [ ] **Share your own mistakes** - Nothing builds trust faster than "I made a similar mistake three years ago. Here's what happened." Vulnerability models the honesty you want from them.
- [ ] **Celebrate progress genuinely** - When they handle something well, name it specifically. "The way you managed that disagreement between Tom and Sarah was really effective because you listened to both sides before proposing a solution." Specific praise builds confidence.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **After each session, send a one-line message noting something they did well that week** - Takes 30 seconds. Impact is significant. People rarely hear specific positive feedback about their leadership.

## Step 4: Evaluate and Evolve

- [ ] **Review session quality every 8 weeks** - Are the conversations getting deeper? Is the person bringing more complex challenges? That's growth. If every session feels the same, the format needs adjusting.
- [ ] **Increase challenge over time** - Early sessions might be about basic management skills. Over months, push towards strategic decisions, cross-functional thinking, and bigger accountability.
- [ ] **Know when to step back** - The goal is independence, not dependence. As they grow, sessions can become fortnightly, then monthly. The best outcome is a leader who no longer needs you in the room.

---

## Expected Outcome

- Emerging leaders have dedicated, protected time for development
- Leadership challenges are processed and learned from, not just experienced
- Decision-making quality improves visibly within 2-3 months
- Trust between owner and emerging leader deepens significantly
- A coaching culture starts spreading through the business

## Coach Notes

This is where your coaching skills as a profit coach transfer directly. Help the business owner become a coach to their own emerging leaders. Many owners default to directive mode ("do it this way") instead of coaching mode ("what do you think?"). Model the coaching approach in your sessions with the owner so they can replicate it with their team. If the owner genuinely doesn't have time for regular mentorship sessions, that's a signal that their own workload needs addressing first. You can't develop leaders while drowning in operations.
