# Plan Cross-Functional Exposure

## What to Do

Rotate potential leaders through different areas of the business so they understand how everything connects and learn to think strategically about the whole, not just their area. This doesn't mean moving someone full-time into a new department. It means giving them structured time in another function - attending their meetings, working on a project, shadowing a team lead - so they build a broader perspective.

## Why It Matters

Leaders who only understand one area make narrow decisions. A sales leader who doesn't understand operations creates promises the team can't deliver. An ops leader who doesn't understand finance cuts costs in the wrong places. Cross-functional exposure builds the strategic thinking that separates managers (who run one area) from leaders (who see the whole picture). It also builds empathy and relationships across the business, which makes collaboration smoother long after the rotation ends.

## How to Use This

Pick the right people, choose meaningful rotations, and set them up properly. A badly planned rotation wastes everyone's time. A well-planned one creates lasting understanding.

---

## Planning the Rotation

- [ ] **Select who rotates** - Focus on your highest-potential leaders. People already identified through your potential matrix and readiness conversations. Don't rotate everyone - it's disruptive. Pick 1-2 people at a time.
- [ ] **Choose rotation areas that fill their blind spots** - A sales-focused person should spend time in operations. An ops person should spend time with finance. Pick the area that would most broaden their understanding.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Start with meeting attendance, not full rotations** - Have the person attend another department's weekly meeting for 4 weeks. Costs almost nothing. Creates immediate perspective shift.
- [ ] **Set a clear learning objective** - "Understand how the ops team handles capacity planning and how sales commitments affect delivery schedules." Specific objectives prevent the rotation from becoming aimless observation.
- [ ] **Brief the receiving team** - Tell the host department who's joining, why, and what they should include them in. Without a brief, the visitor gets sidelined or treated as an extra pair of hands for grunt work.
- [ ] **Agree on a time commitment** - One day per week for 6 weeks? Two hours per week for 12 weeks? Full immersion for 2 weeks? Match the depth to what you're trying to achieve and what the business can afford.

## During the Rotation

- [ ] **Give them a real task or project in the new area** - Observation is useful but doing is better. Have them contribute something meaningful so they experience the area's challenges firsthand.
- [ ] **Pair them with someone in the host team** - A buddy who can explain context, answer questions, and include them naturally. Without this, they sit in meetings feeling like an outsider.
- [ ] **Schedule a weekly reflection conversation** - 15 minutes. "What surprised you? What did you learn? How does this area connect to yours?" These questions turn experience into insight.
- [ ] **Watch for resistance from the home team** - "We can't spare them" is the most common pushback. Address it upfront by adjusting workload, not just adding the rotation on top.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Ask them to present one insight from the rotation at your next leadership meeting** - This forces reflection and creates value for the wider team. It also signals that cross-functional understanding matters.

## After the Rotation

- [ ] **Run a debrief with the person** - What did they learn? How has their thinking changed? What would they do differently in their own area based on what they saw?
- [ ] **Capture specific insights that benefit the business** - Rotations often surface inefficiencies, miscommunications, and opportunities that neither department saw on its own. Don't let those insights disappear.
- [ ] **Build on the relationships formed** - The connections made during a rotation often become the informal collaboration channels that make the business run better. Encourage them to maintain those relationships.
- [ ] **Plan the next rotation** - Each exposure should build on the last. After ops, try finance. After finance, try customer-facing work. The goal is a leader who understands the full business.

---

## Expected Outcome

- Emerging leaders develop strategic, whole-business thinking
- Decisions in one area start accounting for impact on other areas
- Cross-team collaboration improves because people have personal relationships and shared understanding
- You develop leaders who can genuinely step into general management, not just functional management
- Hidden inefficiencies and opportunities surface as fresh eyes examine each area

## Coach Notes

This works best in businesses with at least 10-15 people and clear functional areas. In very small teams (under 10), everyone already has some cross-functional exposure by necessity. For those businesses, focus the rotation on specific skills or client-facing work rather than departments. The most common failure is treating the rotation as a holiday from real work. Set clear expectations: this is development, not downtime. They should come back with insights, not just stories. Some owners resist rotations because they feel disruptive. Challenge that: "Is the short-term disruption worse than having leaders who can only think about one piece of the business?"
