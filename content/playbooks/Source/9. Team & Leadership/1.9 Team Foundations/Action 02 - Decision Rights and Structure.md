# Decision Rights & Structure

## What to Do

Define who makes which decisions in your business and build a simple organisational structure that makes authority clear. Remove the bottleneck of every decision flowing through you.

## Why It Matters

In most small businesses, the owner is the default decision-maker for everything — from strategic direction to which supplier to use for printer paper. This creates two problems: the owner is constantly interrupted, and the team waits around for approvals that should not need them. Decision rights answer the question: "Who has the authority to make this decision without checking with anyone else?" When that is clear, the business moves faster and the owner gets their time back.

## How to Use This

Work through this even if your team is small. Decision bottlenecks start with 2 people and get worse with every hire.

---

## Map Your Decisions

- [ ] **List the 10 most common decisions in your business** — Not just big strategic ones. Include daily operational decisions: spending under £X, client scheduling, pricing quotes, hiring contractors, changing processes. The decisions that happen every week.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win · **For each decision, write who currently makes it** — If the answer is "me" for 8 out of 10, you have found your bottleneck. That is where the team is waiting for you instead of acting.
- [ ] **Categorise each decision into 3 levels:**
  - **Level 1: Just do it** — Decisions team members can make without asking anyone. Spending under £100, scheduling changes, standard client responses.
  - **Level 2: Inform after** — Decisions they can make but should tell you about. Spending £100-£500, non-standard client requests, process changes.
  - **Level 3: Ask first** — Decisions that need your approval before action. Spending over £500, hiring, pricing changes, new client commitments, anything that sets a precedent.
- [ ] **Write down the levels and communicate them to your team** — Explicitly. "You can make any decision under £100 without asking me. Between £100 and £500, make the call but tell me the same day. Over £500, ask first." Clear, simple, memorable.

## Build Your Structure

- [ ] **Draw a simple org chart** — Even if it is just 3 boxes. Who reports to whom? This is not about hierarchy for hierarchy's sake — it is about clarity. When someone has a question, who do they go to?
- [ ] **Every person should report to one person** — Not two. Not "it depends." One. Dual reporting creates confusion and conflicting priorities.
- [ ] **If you have fewer than 5 people, your org chart is flat and that is fine** — Everyone reports to you. But the chart still clarifies: here is who we are and here is what each person does. As you grow, the structure evolves.

## Unblock Yourself

- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win · **Identify the decision you get asked about most often that someone else could handle** — Delegate it explicitly. "From now on, you make this call. I trust you." The first one is the hardest.
- [ ] **Set a weekly "decision review" instead of real-time approvals** — For Level 2 decisions, instead of being interrupted throughout the day, batch them. Team members make the call and report in a weekly 15-minute check-in. You review, give feedback, adjust authority levels if needed.
- [ ] **Track decisions you get pulled into for 1 week** — Every time someone asks you to decide something, write it down. At the end of the week, categorise: which of these could someone else have handled? The answer is usually 60-80%.

---

## Expected Outcome

- Clear decision authority so the team acts without waiting for you
- Fewer interruptions because people know what they can decide independently
- A simple org chart that clarifies reporting and authority
- A delegation framework you can expand as the team grows
- More of your time spent on strategic decisions, less on operational ones

## Coach Notes

Most owners resist delegating decisions because "what if they get it wrong?" Reframe: what is the cost of them getting it right 90% of the time versus the cost of you being the bottleneck 100% of the time? The 10% of mistakes is cheaper than the owner spending 3 hours a day approving things.

Start small. Delegate one decision category this week. Review it after 2 weeks. If it went well, delegate another. Build trust incrementally rather than dumping everything at once.

The spending thresholds (Level 1/2/3) are powerful because they are concrete and measurable. Adjust the numbers to fit the business — a £5M business might set Level 1 at £500 and Level 3 at £5,000. The principle is the same.
