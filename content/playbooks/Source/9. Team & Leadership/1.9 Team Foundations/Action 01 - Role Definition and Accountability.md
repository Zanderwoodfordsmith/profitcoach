# Role Definition & Accountability Map

## What to Do

Define what every person in your business is responsible for — including yourself — and create a simple accountability map that shows who owns what. No gaps, no overlaps, no "I thought you were handling that."

## Why It Matters

Ryan Deiss's H.O.T. Canvas (High Output Team) starts with Clear Accountability as one of three essentials. Without it, things fall through the cracks, people duplicate effort, and everyone looks to the owner for decisions that should not need them. The problem is rarely that people do not work hard — it is that they do not know what "winning" looks like in their role. When you define roles by outcomes rather than activities, people start owning results instead of just completing tasks.

## How to Use This

Work through this even if you are a team of 2-3 people. Especially if you are a team of 2-3 people — small teams assume everyone knows their role, and that assumption causes most of the friction.

---

## Define Each Role

For every person in the business (including yourself):

- [ ] **Write their role title and primary function in one sentence** — Not a job description. A function: "Ensures every client receives consistent, high-quality delivery on time." This is what the role exists to achieve.
- [ ] **Define what "winning" looks like in 90 days** — 2-3 specific, measurable outcomes. Not activities ("make sales calls") but outcomes ("close 8 new clients this quarter"). If they hit these outcomes, the role is performing.
- [ ] **List their 3-5 key responsibilities** — The core things they own. Not a comprehensive task list — the responsibilities that, if done well, make everything else work. Keep it to 5 maximum. If a role has 15 responsibilities, the person cannot possibly focus.
- [ ] **Identify what they do NOT own** — Boundaries matter as much as responsibilities. "You are responsible for client delivery but NOT for client acquisition" prevents role creep.

## Build the Accountability Map

- [ ] **List every important business function down the left side** — Sales, marketing, delivery, finance, client management, admin, operations, product, hiring. Include everything that matters to the business running.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win · **For each function, write one name** — The person who is ultimately accountable for that function's results. If the answer is "me" for everything, you have just discovered why you are overwhelmed. That is useful information.
- [ ] **Identify the gaps** — Any function with no name is a gap. Something important is happening by accident or not happening at all.
- [ ] **Identify the overlaps** — Any function with multiple names is an overlap. Shared accountability is no accountability — decide who owns it.
- [ ] **Flag the functions that should not be yours** — As the business grows, which functions need to move from your name to someone else's? This is your delegation roadmap.

## Set Performance Standards

- [ ] **For each role, define 2-3 explicit standards** — How fast should they respond to clients? What quality level is expected? What does "on time" mean? Make the invisible expectations visible.
- [ ] **Share the role definition with the person** — Not by email. In conversation. Ask: "Does this accurately describe what you think your role is?" If there is a gap between your definition and theirs, close it now.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win · **Ask each team member: "What is the single most important thing you should be achieving in your role?"** — If their answer does not match yours, you have an alignment problem that no amount of harder work will fix.

---

## Expected Outcome

- Every person knows exactly what winning looks like in their role
- A simple accountability map showing who owns each business function — no gaps, no overlaps
- Clear boundaries so people focus on their responsibilities, not everyone else's
- A delegation roadmap showing what needs to move off your plate as the business grows
- Explicit performance standards that make feedback conversations objective, not personal

## Coach Notes

The accountability map often reveals that the owner is listed against 70-80% of functions. This is the moment they understand why they are overwhelmed — they are doing 7 jobs, not one. Use this as the foundation for a delegation plan, but do not overwhelm them with "you need to hire 5 people." Start with: "Which one function, if taken off your plate, would free up the most of your time?"

If a team member's answer about their role does not match the owner's expectations, that is the coaching opportunity. The gap is usually about communication, not competence — they have been guessing at what matters because no one told them explicitly.

For solo operators with contractors or part-time help, this is still valuable. Define the VA's role, the bookkeeper's role, the subcontractor's role. Even informal roles benefit from clarity.
