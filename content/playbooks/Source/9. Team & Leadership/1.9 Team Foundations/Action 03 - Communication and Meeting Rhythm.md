# Communication & Meeting Rhythm

## What to Do

Set up a simple, consistent communication rhythm that keeps everyone aligned without drowning in meetings. One weekly check-in with a fixed agenda that replaces the ad hoc interruptions, status-chasing, and "quick question" culture that consumes most small teams.

## Why It Matters

Verne Harnish's Scaling Up framework identifies the Meeting Rhythm as one of three pillars of execution (alongside Priorities and Data). Without a consistent rhythm, communication is reactive — people only talk when something is wrong. With a rhythm, problems surface early, alignment stays tight, and the owner stops being the central switchboard for every piece of information.

At Level 1, you do not need daily huddles, monthly off-sites, or quarterly planning retreats. You need one weekly meeting that actually works.

## How to Use This

Set up one weekly meeting. Get the format right. Make it a habit. Everything else comes at higher levels.

---

## Set Up the Weekly Check-In

- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win · **Pick a day and time — same slot every week** — Monday morning or Friday afternoon work well. Monday sets the week. Friday reviews it. Pick one and commit. Moving it around kills the habit.
- [ ] **Set the length at 30 minutes maximum** — If your weekly meeting takes longer than 30 minutes with a small team, the agenda is wrong or the conversation is drifting. 30 minutes, timer on, done.
- [ ] **Invite everyone who needs alignment** — Your core team. Not clients, not contractors unless they are deeply embedded. Keep it tight.

## The Agenda (Same Every Week)

- [ ] **Wins (5 minutes)** — Each person shares one win from the past week. This is not optional fluff — it creates a habit of recognising progress and sets a positive tone. One win each, no discussion.
- [ ] **Numbers (5 minutes)** — Each person reports their key metric for the week. Revenue, leads, deliveries completed, whatever matters for their role. Just the number. If it is red, it goes on the Issues list.
- [ ] **Priorities (5 minutes)** — Each person states their #1 priority for the coming week. One thing. Saying it out loud creates accountability.
- [ ] **Issues (15 minutes)** — The real value. List the problems, blockers, or decisions that need attention. Discuss the most important ones. Make a decision or assign an action. If it cannot be resolved in the meeting, assign it to someone with a deadline.
- [ ] **Actions (2 minutes)** — Recap: who is doing what by when? Write it down. Start next week's meeting by reviewing whether actions were completed.

## Make It Stick

- [ ] **The meeting starts on time, even if someone is missing** — Waiting for people teaches them that being late is acceptable. Starting without them teaches them to be on time.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win · **No phones, no laptops (unless needed for notes)** — 30 minutes of full attention. If people are checking email during the meeting, the meeting is either too long or not valuable enough.
- [ ] **End on time** — If the 30 minutes is up and there are still issues, they go to next week or get handled offline. Respecting the time boundary is what makes people trust the meeting.
- [ ] **Send a 3-line summary after** — What was decided, what actions were assigned, what is on the agenda for next week. Takes 2 minutes. Creates accountability.

## For Solo Operators

- [ ] **If it is just you, do a solo weekly review instead** — Same agenda, 15 minutes, written. What went well, what are my numbers, what is my #1 priority next week, what issues need resolving? The discipline of checking in with yourself matters as much as checking in with a team.

---

## Expected Outcome

- One consistent weekly meeting that replaces dozens of ad hoc conversations
- Problems surfacing early instead of festering until they are crises
- Clear weekly accountability — everyone knows what they committed to
- Fewer "quick question" interruptions because people save non-urgent issues for the meeting
- A foundation you can build on — daily huddles, monthly reviews, and quarterly planning come at higher levels

## Coach Notes

The weekly check-in is the single highest-leverage team habit at Level 1. If a client does nothing else from this playbook, this one action will improve team alignment more than any other.

The most common failure: the meeting starts strong for 3-4 weeks, then starts getting skipped. Hold the client accountable. If they skip the meeting, ask "what happened?" The reason they skipped it reveals what they value more than team alignment — and that is usually the conversation that needs to happen.

EOS calls this the "Level 10 Meeting." Harnish calls it the "Weekly." Deiss calls it the "Team Huddle." The name does not matter. The rhythm does. Pick a format, start it this week, and iterate from there.
