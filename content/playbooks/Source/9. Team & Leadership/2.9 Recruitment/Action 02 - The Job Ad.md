# The Job Ad

## What to Do

Write a job ad that sells the role to the right person. Lead with what they will achieve, why your business is worth joining, and what the first 90 days look like. A great job ad attracts great candidates. A boring one attracts whoever is available.

## Why It Matters

Your job ad is a sales page for the role. The best candidates have options. They are scrolling past dozens of generic ads that list requirements and responsibilities. If your ad reads like every other one, you will get the same generic applicants as everyone else. The businesses that attract top talent write ads that make good people stop scrolling and think "this sounds like somewhere I could do great work."

## How to Use This

Use your Role Scorecard (Action 1) as the foundation and follow the structure below.

---

## Ad Structure

### The Hook (First 2-3 Lines)

- [ ] Lead with what the person will ACHIEVE, not what you need: "You will build a lead generation engine that produces 50 qualified prospects per week" not "We are looking for a marketing person"
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Write your opening line right now using this template: "You will [specific achievement] for [type of business] that [one compelling thing about your company]."
- [ ] Make it specific to the role, not generic

### Why This Role (The Sell)

- [ ] What will they get to do that is exciting, challenging, or meaningful?
- [ ] What will they learn or develop?
- [ ] What kind of impact will they have?
- [ ] What is the team or culture like? (Be honest, not aspirational)

### What You Will Do (Responsibilities)

- [ ] List 5-8 responsibilities from your scorecard, written as active statements
- [ ] Lead with the most exciting/impactful responsibilities, not the admin
- [ ] Use "You will..." not "The candidate should..."
- [ ] Be specific: "Manage a pipeline of 50+ leads and convert 10% to paying clients monthly" not "Handle sales"

### What Success Looks Like in 90 Days

- [ ] Pull directly from your scorecard outcomes
- [ ] This is powerful because it tells candidates exactly what they are signing up for
- [ ] It also self-selects: candidates who read this and feel excited are the right ones. Those who feel overwhelmed are probably not.

### Who You Are

- [ ] List must-have qualities from your scorecard (not a laundry list of requirements)
- [ ] Lead with traits and behaviours, not just qualifications
- [ ] Be honest about experience level: "You have managed at least one marketing channel end-to-end" is more useful than "5+ years of marketing experience"
- [ ] Include deal-breakers if relevant: "This role requires [location/hours/specific skill]. If that does not work for you, this is not the right fit."

### Compensation and Details

- [ ] Include salary range (ads with salary get 30-50% more applicants)
- [ ] Location and flexibility details
- [ ] Employment type
- [ ] How to apply: clear instructions, simple process

## Final Check

- [ ] Read the ad from a top candidate's perspective: would YOU apply?
- [ ] Check: does the ad sound like your business, or like generic corporate HR?
- [ ] Check: is the first sentence compelling enough to stop someone scrolling?
- [ ] Check: is the salary range realistic for the quality of person you want?
- [ ] Have someone outside the business read it and tell you what they think the role is. If they get it wrong, the ad is unclear.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A compelling job ad that attracts the right candidates and repels the wrong ones
- Clear expectations set before anyone applies
- Higher-quality applications because candidates self-select based on specific outcomes
- A foundation for consistent messaging throughout the recruitment process
- Less time wasted on interviews with candidates who are not a fit

## Coach Notes

Most SME job ads are terrible. They list requirements, mention "competitive salary," and say "we are a dynamic team." Challenge clients to write something a real person would actually want to respond to. The 90-day outcomes section is the secret weapon - very few companies include this, and it immediately signals "this is a serious, well-run business." If the client says "I cannot share the salary," push back: hiding salary wastes everyone's time and reduces application volume. The only reason to hide salary is if you plan to lowball candidates, which damages your employer brand.
