# Candidate Scorecard

## What to Do

Create a simple rubric for scoring each candidate across the criteria that matter. Every interviewer completes it independently before any group discussion. The scorecard turns subjective impressions into comparable data so your hiring decision is made on evidence, not gut feel.

## Why It Matters

Without a scorecard, hiring discussions default to "I liked them" or "they seemed good." These impressions are heavily influenced by unconscious bias, interviewer mood, and how recently you spoke to the candidate. A scorecard forces you to evaluate each candidate against the same criteria and produce a number you can compare. It does not remove human judgment, it structures it so the right factors get weighted and the decision is defensible.

## How to Use This

Create the scorecard before interviews begin. Score every candidate immediately after their interview, before discussing with anyone else.

---

## Build the Scorecard

- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Create a simple table with these columns: Criteria, Weight (1-3), Candidate A Score (1-5), Candidate B Score (1-5), etc.
- [ ] Pull 5-7 evaluation criteria from your Role Scorecard (Action 1):
  - 2-3 skill criteria (can they do the core work?)
  - 1-2 values/culture criteria (do they fit how you work?)
  - 1 motivation criterion (are they genuinely interested?)
  - 1 practical task score (how was their work output?)
- [ ] Weight each criterion 1-3 based on importance (3 = critical, 1 = nice to have)

## Example Criteria

- [ ] Core technical skill for the role (Weight: 3)
- [ ] Communication quality (Weight: 2)
- [ ] Problem-solving approach (Weight: 2)
- [ ] Cultural alignment with company values (Weight: 2)
- [ ] Relevant experience (Weight: 2)
- [ ] Motivation and enthusiasm for the role (Weight: 1)
- [ ] Practical task quality (Weight: 3)

## Scoring Rules

- [ ] Score immediately after each interview or task evaluation
- [ ] Score INDEPENDENTLY - do not discuss with other interviewers first
- [ ] Use the full 1-5 range: 1 = poor, 2 = below average, 3 = acceptable, 4 = good, 5 = excellent
- [ ] For each score, write one sentence explaining why (this prevents score inflation and forces honest evaluation)
- [ ] Calculate weighted total: (Score x Weight) for each criterion, then sum

## Making the Decision

- [ ] Compare weighted totals across candidates
- [ ] Review the individual criterion scores: is there a deal-breaker anywhere (a 1 or 2 on a Weight: 3 criterion)?
- [ ] If multiple interviewers scored, compare independently before discussing
- [ ] Where scores differ significantly between interviewers, discuss the specific evidence behind each score
- [ ] Use the scorecard as the primary input for the decision, supplemented by interview notes and practical task output
- [ ] If the top two candidates are very close, look at the highest-weighted criteria to break the tie

---

## Expected Outcome

- A structured, comparable evaluation of every candidate
- Bias reduced through independent scoring and weighted criteria
- A clear, defensible basis for your hiring decision
- Agreement between multiple interviewers grounded in evidence
- A record of why you chose one candidate over another (useful if challenged or for future reference)

## Coach Notes

The scorecard discipline is where most SME owners push back: "This feels too corporate for a small business." Reframe: it takes 10 minutes per candidate and prevents months of pain from a bad hire. The independent scoring rule is the most important part - once interviewers discuss before scoring, the loudest opinion dominates. Also watch for the "halo effect": a candidate who is charming and likeable gets inflated scores on every criterion. The scorecard catches this because you evaluate each criterion separately. If a candidate scores 5 on likeability but 2 on core skills, the numbers tell the truth.
