# Manager Check-Ins

## What to Do

Schedule weekly one-to-one check-ins for the first 90 days to remove blockers, give feedback, and adjust expectations. These are short (15 minutes), consistent, and focused conversations that keep the new hire on track and prevent small issues from becoming big problems.

## Why It Matters

New hires are most vulnerable in their first 90 days. They are learning, adjusting, and forming opinions about whether this was the right decision. A weekly check-in gives them a safe space to ask questions, raise concerns, and get feedback before problems compound. For the manager, it provides early warning if something is going wrong and regular opportunities to reinforce what is going well. The cost is 15 minutes per week. The return is a faster ramp, fewer surprises, and a new hire who feels supported.

## How to Use This

Schedule the check-ins before they start. Run them consistently for 12 weeks.

---

## Setup

- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Open your calendar now and block a recurring 15-minute slot weekly for the new hire's first 90 days. Same day, same time, every week.
- [ ] Choose a format: in-person, video call, or phone - whatever feels most natural for the relationship
- [ ] Make it clear this is not a performance review - it is a support conversation

## Check-In Agenda (15 Minutes)

Use the same simple structure every week:

### How Are Things Going? (5 minutes)
- [ ] Let them lead: "What has gone well this week?"
- [ ] Listen for energy and enthusiasm - are they engaged or deflated?
- [ ] Note any recurring themes (positive or negative) across weeks

### What Is Blocking You? (5 minutes)
- [ ] "Is anything slowing you down or getting in your way?"
- [ ] "Do you have everything you need to do your job this week?"
- [ ] "Is there anything you are unsure about or want clarification on?"
- [ ] Commit to resolving blockers before the next check-in

### Feedback and Next Steps (5 minutes)
- [ ] Give one piece of specific positive feedback: "I noticed you did X well this week. That is exactly what we need."
- [ ] If needed, give one piece of constructive feedback: "I noticed X. Next time, try Y. Here is why."
- [ ] Align on priorities for next week: "The most important thing to focus on next week is..."

## Tracking Progress

- [ ] Keep brief notes from each check-in (2-3 bullet points is enough)
- [ ] Reference the 30-60-90 Day Plan (Action 11): are they on track for the current milestone?
- [ ] At the 30, 60, and 90 day marks, replace the check-in with the formal review from the 30-60-90 Day Plan
- [ ] If the same blocker or concern appears 3+ weeks in a row, escalate it - it is not going to resolve itself

## After 90 Days

- [ ] Transition from weekly to fortnightly check-ins (the relationship and rhythm is established)
- [ ] After 6 months, move to monthly one-to-ones (standard management cadence)
- [ ] The habit of regular check-ins should continue permanently, just at lower frequency
- [ ] If the new hire asks to keep weekly check-ins longer, that is a good sign - they value the support

---

## Expected Outcome

- Consistent weekly support for the new hire throughout their first 90 days
- Blockers identified and resolved before they cause damage
- Regular feedback that accelerates learning and performance
- A documented record of progress that informs the 90-day decision
- A new hire who feels supported, valued, and clear about expectations

## Coach Notes

Manager check-ins are where coaching and management overlap most clearly. The weekly format is deliberately short - 15 minutes is enough if you are consistent. The temptation is to cancel when things seem fine, but that is exactly when check-ins matter most: they maintain the connection and catch the subtle signals that something is shifting. The positive feedback element is the most frequently skipped but most important part. New hires are anxious about whether they are doing well. Specific, genuine praise builds confidence and reinforces the right behaviours. If the client says "I do not have time for weekly check-ins," do the maths: 15 minutes per week for 12 weeks = 3 hours total. A bad hire costs months. It is not even close.
