# Structured Interview

## What to Do

Run a consistent interview using the same behavioural and values-based questions for every candidate. This means you are comparing people against the same bar using the same criteria, rather than having a different conversation each time and making decisions based on who you liked most.

## Why It Matters

Unstructured interviews are barely better than flipping a coin. Research consistently shows that "going with your gut" in interviews leads to hiring people who are similar to you or who are good at interviewing - neither of which predicts job performance. Structured interviews, where every candidate answers the same questions and is scored against the same criteria, are 2x more predictive of actual job performance. They also protect you legally by ensuring fair, consistent treatment.

## How to Use This

Design your interview guide before the first interview and use the same guide for every candidate.

---

## Interview Design

- [ ] Pull 3-5 key outcomes and traits from your Role Scorecard (Action 1)
- [ ] For each, write 1-2 behavioural interview questions (see below)
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Use this formula for every question: "Tell me about a time when you [behaviour related to scorecard trait]. What was the situation, what did you do, and what was the result?"
- [ ] Prepare a scoring rubric: for each question, what does a great answer (5), good answer (3), and poor answer (1) look like?

## Interview Structure (45-60 Minutes)

### Opening (5 minutes)
- [ ] Welcome them, explain the interview structure: "I will ask you some questions about your experience, then tell you more about the role, and we will leave time for your questions."
- [ ] Put them at ease: "There are no trick questions. I am interested in real examples from your experience."

### Behavioural Questions (25-30 minutes)
Ask each candidate the same questions in the same order:

- [ ] **Question 1 (Core skill):** "Tell me about a time when you [relevant skill from scorecard]. Walk me through the situation, your approach, and what happened."
- [ ] **Question 2 (Challenge/problem-solving):** "Describe a situation where [relevant challenge for this role] went wrong. What did you do?"
- [ ] **Question 3 (Values/culture):** "Give me an example of when you [behaviour that demonstrates a key value]. What did you do and why?"
- [ ] **Question 4 (Initiative/ownership):** "Tell me about something you improved or built without being asked to."
- [ ] **Question 5 (Role-specific):** Design one question specific to the most important outcome on your scorecard.

### For each answer, listen for:
- [ ] **Specificity:** Do they give a real example or speak in generalities?
- [ ] **Their role:** What did THEY do (not the team)?
- [ ] **Result:** What was the measurable outcome?
- [ ] **Learning:** What did they take from the experience?

### Role Presentation (10 minutes)
- [ ] Describe the role, the 90-day outcomes, and the culture honestly
- [ ] Explain the challenges as well as the opportunities (this builds trust and helps candidates self-select)
- [ ] Share what success and failure look like

### Candidate Questions (10 minutes)
- [ ] Let them ask anything
- [ ] Pay attention to the quality of their questions - great candidates ask about impact, growth, and expectations. Weak candidates ask about holidays and perks.

## After the Interview

- [ ] Score each question 1-5 using your rubric BEFORE discussing with anyone else (prevent groupthink)
- [ ] Write 3 bullet points: strengths, concerns, and overall recommendation
- [ ] Update the Candidate Tracker (Action 4)
- [ ] If multiple interviewers: each scores independently, then compare and discuss

---

## Expected Outcome

- Every candidate assessed against the same criteria using the same questions
- Objective scoring that reduces bias and gut-feel decisions
- Better prediction of actual job performance
- A fair, professional process that candidates respect
- Clear data for comparing candidates and making confident hiring decisions

## Coach Notes

The hardest part is getting clients to stick to the structure. They want to "have a conversation" and "see if they click." That is fine for 5 minutes, but the core 30 minutes should be structured. The behavioural question format ("Tell me about a time when...") is critical: past behaviour is the best predictor of future behaviour. If a candidate cannot give a specific example, they probably do not have the experience. Probe with: "Can you be more specific?" or "What was your personal contribution?" Generic answers ("We improved sales") should prompt "What did YOU do specifically?" Independent scoring before discussion is essential to prevent the loudest voice in the room driving the decision.
