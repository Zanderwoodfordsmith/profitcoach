# Role Scorecard

## What to Do

Define the outcomes, responsibilities, and non-negotiable traits for a role before you write a single word of the job ad. A scorecard answers three questions: What does winning look like in 90 days? What are the ongoing responsibilities? What kind of person thrives in this role?

## Why It Matters

Most bad hires happen because the role was never clearly defined. The owner had a vague sense of needing "someone to help with marketing" but never specified what success looks like. Without a scorecard, you attract the wrong candidates, ask the wrong interview questions, and have no way to measure whether the hire is working. The scorecard is the foundation that makes everything else in the recruitment process work.

## How to Use This

Complete one scorecard for every role before you begin recruiting. Update it before re-hiring for the same role.

---

## Define the Role

- [ ] **Role title:** What is the role called? (Keep it simple and recognisable - creative titles confuse candidates)
- [ ] **Reports to:** Who does this person report to?
- [ ] **Works with:** Who will they interact with daily?
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Before writing anything else, answer: "If this hire is a total success, what will be different about my business in 90 days?" Write that down. That is the core of your scorecard.

## Outcomes (What Winning Looks Like)

- [ ] Define 3-5 measurable outcomes for the first 90 days. These are results, not activities.
  - Good: "Generate 20 qualified leads per week through LinkedIn and email outreach"
  - Bad: "Do marketing stuff"
- [ ] For each outcome, specify how you will measure it
- [ ] Prioritise: if they achieve only one outcome, which one matters most?

## Ongoing Responsibilities

- [ ] List 5-8 core responsibilities this role will own permanently
- [ ] For each, note the time allocation: how much of their week should go to this? (e.g., "Client delivery: 50%, Admin: 20%, Business development: 30%")
- [ ] Identify which responsibilities are the most important (the ones you would hire for even if they did nothing else)

## Non-Negotiable Traits

- [ ] List 3-5 traits or qualities that are essential for this role:
  - **Skills:** What must they already know how to do? (vs. what can you train)
  - **Values:** Which of your company values are most critical for this role?
  - **Behaviours:** What specific behaviours would you see from the right person? (e.g., "Takes initiative without being asked", "Communicates proactively when problems arise")
- [ ] Separate "must have" from "nice to have" - be honest about which is which
- [ ] Identify any deal-breakers: traits or behaviours that would make someone wrong for this role regardless of other qualities

## Compensation and Logistics

- [ ] Salary range: £___ to £___
- [ ] Employment type: Full-time / Part-time / Contract / Freelance
- [ ] Location: On-site / Remote / Hybrid
- [ ] Start date: When do you need them?

## Expected Outcome

- A one-page scorecard that clearly defines what the role is, what success looks like, and what kind of person fits
- A foundation for writing a compelling job ad (Action 2)
- Interview criteria aligned with actual role requirements (Action 6)
- A 90-day success measurement framework for onboarding (Action 11)
- Shared understanding between you and anyone else involved in the hiring decision

## Coach Notes

The scorecard is the single most important recruitment tool. If the client resists ("I just need someone, I will figure out the details later"), push back hard. Hiring without a scorecard is how you end up with someone who interviews well but cannot do the job. The outcomes section is where most clients struggle - they default to listing activities rather than results. Keep asking: "But what result does that activity produce?" until they get to something measurable. This scorecard also becomes the basis for the 30-60-90 day plan (Action 11) and ongoing performance management.
