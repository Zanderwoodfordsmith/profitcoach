# Practical Task

## What to Do

Design a short, realistic task that mirrors the actual work the person will be doing. Seeing someone do the job tells you more than any interview answer. The task should take 1-2 hours to complete and produce a tangible output you can evaluate.

## Why It Matters

Some people interview brilliantly but cannot do the work. Others interview poorly but are exceptional at the job. A practical task reveals the truth. It shows you their thinking process, their attention to detail, their communication style, and the quality of their output in a way that no amount of conversation can. It also gives the candidate a realistic preview of the work, which helps them decide if this is the right role for them.

## How to Use This

Design the task before you start interviewing so every candidate gets the same brief. Send it after the interview to your top 2-3 candidates.

---

## Task Design

- [ ] Choose one real task from the role that represents the core work
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Ask yourself: "What is the first real thing I would ask them to do in week one?" That is your task.
- [ ] Make it realistic but bounded: it should take 1-2 hours, not a full day
- [ ] Provide clear context: what is the situation, what do you need, and what does good look like?
- [ ] Set a deadline: typically 3-5 days to complete (enough time to fit around their current commitments)

## Task Examples by Role Type

- [ ] **Marketing:** "Write 3 social media posts for our business targeting [audience]. Include headlines, body copy, and suggested images."
- [ ] **Sales:** "Prepare a 10-minute pitch for our core service to [type of prospect]. Present it over a video call."
- [ ] **Operations:** "Review this process document and identify 3 improvements you would make. Explain why and how."
- [ ] **Finance:** "Here is a P&L for a fictional business. Write a one-page summary of what you see, the top 3 concerns, and what you would investigate next."
- [ ] **Admin:** "Organise this messy shared folder into a logical structure. Explain your reasoning in a brief note."
- [ ] **Management:** "Review these team performance summaries and write a brief plan for addressing the underperformer."

## Brief the Candidate

- [ ] Send a clear brief: what to do, what format to deliver, and when it is due
- [ ] Include enough context about your business for them to do a reasonable job
- [ ] Be explicit about time: "We expect this to take approximately 1-2 hours. Please do not spend more than that."
- [ ] Offer to answer questions before they start (how they ask questions is also revealing)

## Evaluate the Output

- [ ] Score each candidate's task independently before discussing with others
- [ ] Evaluate on:
  - Quality of thinking (did they understand the problem?)
  - Attention to detail (typos, formatting, following instructions)
  - Communication (is their writing clear and professional?)
  - Initiative (did they go beyond the brief where appropriate?)
  - Relevance (does their approach suit your business, or is it generic?)
- [ ] Compare outputs side by side if you have multiple candidates
- [ ] Use the task output in the final decision alongside interview scores

---

## Expected Outcome

- A realistic preview of each candidate's actual work quality
- A tangible output to evaluate alongside interview impressions
- Better prediction of on-the-job performance
- Candidates who self-select by engaging seriously with the task (those who do not complete it are telling you something)
- A more confident hiring decision based on evidence, not just conversation

## Coach Notes

The practical task is where hiring decisions often become clear. Interview scores between top candidates are usually close, but task quality reveals the gap. Two important principles: first, the task must be realistic, not theoretical. "How would you approach X?" is an interview question. "Do X" is a practical task. Second, respect candidates' time - keep it to 1-2 hours and consider compensating them (£50-100 is reasonable for a senior role). Candidates who produce great work deserve respect, even if they are not selected. Also note: candidates who ask smart clarifying questions before starting often produce better work than those who just dive in.
