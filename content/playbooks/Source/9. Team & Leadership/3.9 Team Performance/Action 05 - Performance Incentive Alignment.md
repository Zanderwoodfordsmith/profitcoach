# Align Your Performance Incentives

## What to Do

Review and redesign how recognition, rewards, and advancement decisions get made in your business to ensure they reinforce the behaviours and results you actually want. Most incentive systems were not designed. They evolved. And they often reward the wrong things or miss the right ones entirely. This action brings deliberate thinking to how you motivate, recognise, and reward your team.

## Why It Matters

People do what they get rewarded for. If you pay salespeople purely on revenue but want them to find profitable clients, you have a misalignment. If you promote based on tenure but want high performance, you are sending the wrong signal. Incentive misalignment does not just waste money. It actively drives the wrong behaviours. When incentives are aligned with your business goals and values, the team naturally pulls in the direction you need.

## How to Use This

Audit your current incentive structure, identify misalignments, and redesign the elements that need fixing. Not everything will need changing. Focus on the areas where misalignment is costing you the most.

---

## Audit Current Incentives

- [ ] **List every formal incentive in your business** - Base pay, bonuses, commissions, profit share, benefits, perks, equity, promotion criteria. Get the full picture.
- [ ] **List every informal incentive** - Who gets the best projects? Who gets public praise? Who gets the most flexibility? Informal incentives often drive behaviour more than formal ones.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Ask each team member: "What gets rewarded here?"** - Their answers will tell you what your incentive system actually communicates, which may be very different from what you intend.
- [ ] **Map each incentive to the behaviour it encourages** - Commission on revenue encourages selling. But does it encourage selling the right things at the right price to the right customers? Follow the logic through.
- [ ] **Identify misalignments** - Where are incentives encouraging behaviours that conflict with your goals? Common ones: rewarding individual performance when you need teamwork, rewarding activity when you need outcomes, rewarding short-term results when you need long-term relationships.

## Redesign for Alignment

- [ ] **Start with your business goals** - What are the 3-5 results that matter most right now? Your incentive system should push people toward those results.
- [ ] **Ensure every incentivised metric connects to a business outcome** - If you bonus someone on a number, that number should drive revenue, profit, customer satisfaction, or operational efficiency. If it does not, question why it is incentivised.
- [ ] **Balance individual and team incentives** - Pure individual incentives create competition. Pure team incentives create free-riders. A mix (typically 60-70% individual, 30-40% team) drives both personal accountability and collaboration.
- [ ] **Include quality, not just quantity** - If salespeople are bonused on deals closed but not on client retention, they will close anyone who fogs a mirror. Add a quality measure to every quantity incentive.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Fix the single most obvious misalignment this month** - There is usually one incentive that everyone knows rewards the wrong thing. Changing it sends a powerful signal that you are serious about alignment.

## Recognition and Non-Financial Incentives

- [ ] **Design a recognition programme that reinforces your values** - Public acknowledgment, team awards, or spotlight features that celebrate people who demonstrate the behaviours and results you value most.
- [ ] **Make recognition specific, not generic** - "Great job this month" is forgettable. "Your follow-up system brought back three clients who were about to leave, saving us £15K in annual revenue" is memorable and reinforces exactly what you want more of.
- [ ] **Create non-financial rewards that people actually value** - Extra time off, flexible hours, first pick of projects, development budget, or a simple handwritten note. Often these matter more than a cash bonus.
- [ ] **Ensure advancement criteria are transparent** - If people do not know what it takes to get promoted or advance, they will assume it is political. Publish the criteria. Remove the guesswork.

## Protect Against Gaming

- [ ] **For every incentive, ask: "How could someone hit this number without doing the right thing?"** - If you can think of a way to game it, someone will. Build safeguards or add balancing metrics.
- [ ] **Monitor for unintended consequences** - After changing incentives, watch behaviour closely for the first 2-3 months. Are people responding the way you expected? Adjust quickly if not.
- [ ] **Review incentive structure every 6 months** - Business goals change. Incentives should change with them. A bonus structure from two years ago may be rewarding last year's priorities.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A complete picture of every formal and informal incentive in the business
- Misalignments between incentives and business goals identified and addressed
- A redesigned incentive structure that rewards the right behaviours and results
- Recognition and non-financial rewards that reinforce company values
- Safeguards in place to prevent gaming and unintended consequences

## Coach Notes

This is a sensitive topic because changing incentives affects people's income and perception of fairness. Advise the client to communicate changes openly and explain the reasoning. "We are aligning rewards with what matters most for the business and for customers" is much better received than silent changes people notice on their pay slip.

If the client has commission-only salespeople, watch for the common trap where commission structure has not been updated since the business was small. What worked for a startup hustling for any revenue may be wrong for a business that needs profitable, long-term clients.

For very small businesses without formal bonus structures, this action is still relevant. The informal incentives (who gets praise, who gets the interesting work, whose ideas get listened to) are still shaping behaviour every day. Making those informal systems intentional is just as important as designing a bonus plan.
