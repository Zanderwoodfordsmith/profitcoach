# Build Your Performance Review System

## What to Do

Design and implement a structured, consistent process for regular performance conversations. The same framework, the same frequency, the same expectations for everyone. No more ad hoc chats that vary depending on the manager's mood or the employee's confidence. A proper system that makes performance discussions productive, predictable, and fair.

## Why It Matters

Performance reviews have a terrible reputation, mostly because most businesses do them badly. Annual reviews that surprise people with feedback from 11 months ago. Vague conversations that end with "keep up the good work" or "try harder." The problem is not the concept. It is the execution. A well-designed review system gives people regular, honest feedback, catches problems early, celebrates progress, and creates a paper trail when difficult decisions need to be made.

## How to Use This

Work through each step to design your review framework, then prepare for and run your first round.

---

## Step 1: Design the Framework

- [ ] **Set your review frequency** - Quarterly is the sweet spot for most small businesses. Annual is too infrequent. Monthly is too burdensome unless your team is very new. Pick a rhythm and commit to it.
- [ ] **Choose a standard format** - Every review should cover: scorecard results (how did they perform against their outcomes?), strengths demonstrated, areas for improvement, development goals, and actions for next quarter.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Create a one-page review template** - A simple document with sections matching your format. Same template for every role. Consistency makes preparation faster and comparison easier.
- [ ] **Define who reviews whom** - Direct manager reviews their team members. If you are a solo founder managing everyone, that is fine for now. As you grow, this will need to cascade.
- [ ] **Build in self-assessment** - Before each review, the employee fills out the same template about their own performance. This gives you their perspective before the conversation starts and often surfaces things you would have missed.

## Step 2: Prepare for Reviews

- [ ] **Gather data before the conversation** - Pull scorecard results, customer feedback, project outcomes, and any notes you have made during the quarter. Reviews based on data are fair. Reviews based on memory are biased.
- [ ] **Keep a running notes file for each person** - When something notable happens (good or bad), jot it down. Do not rely on remembering everything at review time. A quick note takes 30 seconds and prevents recency bias.
- [ ] **Schedule reviews in advance** - Put them in the calendar 2 weeks ahead. Give the employee time to prepare their self-assessment. No ambush reviews.
- [ ] **Prepare your key messages** - Go into each review knowing the 2-3 most important things you want to communicate. What is the person doing well? What needs to change? What is the most important development focus?

## Step 3: Run the Review

- [ ] **Start with the scorecard** - Results first, feelings second. "Here is how you performed against your defined outcomes." This grounds the conversation in facts.
- [ ] **Discuss strengths before development areas** - This is not about being soft. It is about building trust so the person can hear the constructive feedback without becoming defensive.
- [ ] **Be specific, not general** - "Your client retention rate was 92%, above the 85% target" is useful. "You are great with clients" is not. Apply the same specificity to improvement areas.
- [ ] **Agree on 1-2 development goals for next quarter** - Not ten. One or two. With specific actions and a timeline. These get reviewed at the next session.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **End every review with: "What do you need from me to succeed next quarter?"** - This flips the power dynamic and often reveals blockers you did not know about.

## Step 4: Follow Through

- [ ] **Document the review conversation** - Both parties should have a copy of what was discussed, agreed, and expected. This protects everyone.
- [ ] **Schedule a mid-quarter check-in** - A 15-minute conversation halfway through to check progress on development goals and address any emerging issues. Not a full review, just a pulse check.
- [ ] **Track development goals across quarters** - If someone's improvement area has been the same for three quarters, either the support is not working or the person is not the right fit. Both need addressing.
- [ ] **Act on what you learn** - If multiple reviews surface the same systemic issue (poor tools, unclear processes, lack of training), fix the system. Do not keep coaching individuals around a broken environment.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A consistent, structured review process that runs every quarter without fail
- Every team member receives honest, data-driven feedback on a predictable schedule
- Development goals set and tracked over time
- A documented history of performance conversations for every person
- Problems caught quarterly instead of festering until they become crises

## Coach Notes

The number one excuse for not doing reviews is "we are too busy." Reframe it for the client: "You are too busy NOT to do reviews. Without them, you spend far more time on misalignment, underperformance, and preventable turnover."

Help the client rehearse difficult conversations before their first round. Most managers have never been taught how to give constructive feedback. Role-play the conversation. Practice saying "Here is what needs to change and here is why" without softening it into uselessness.

For businesses with no review history, the first round will feel awkward. That is normal. By the third quarter, it becomes routine. Encourage the client to start even if it feels imperfect. A mediocre review is better than none.
