# Create Development Plans

## What to Do

Build clear, practical development pathways for each team member that map the capabilities, experiences, and results needed to reach the next level. This is not about creating elaborate career frameworks. It is about answering one question for every person: "What do I need to learn, demonstrate, and achieve to progress here?"

## Why It Matters

People do not leave businesses because of money alone. They leave because they cannot see a future. When there is no development pathway, ambitious people start looking elsewhere. When the pathway exists but is vague ("just keep doing a good job and we will see"), it creates frustration. A clear development plan retains your best people, improves performance across the board, and builds the bench strength you need to grow the business without doing everything yourself.

## How to Use This

Work through the process for each team member, starting with your highest-potential people and anyone at risk of leaving.

---

## Step 1: Assess Current Capability

- [ ] **Map the key skills required for each person's current role** - What do they need to be competent at? List 5-8 core skills.
- [ ] **Rate current proficiency for each skill** - Use a simple scale: developing, competent, or advanced. Be honest. Overrating helps nobody.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Have the team member self-assess before you rate them** - Comparing their view to yours reveals blind spots on both sides and makes the development conversation much richer.
- [ ] **Identify strengths to leverage** - What is this person already great at? Development is not just about fixing weaknesses. It is also about pushing strengths further.
- [ ] **Identify gaps to close** - What skills are they missing or underperforming in? Prioritise the gaps that have the biggest impact on their role performance.

## Step 2: Define the Next Level

- [ ] **Describe what the next level looks like** - Whether that is a promotion, expanded responsibilities, or mastery of the current role. Be specific about what changes.
- [ ] **List the capabilities needed at the next level** - What skills, knowledge, and behaviours are required that they do not have yet?
- [ ] **Define the experiences needed** - Some development only happens through doing. Leading a project, handling a difficult client, managing a team member for the first time. What experiences will build the capabilities they need?
- [ ] **Set the results threshold** - What performance results would demonstrate readiness for the next level? "Consistently hits scorecard targets for 2+ quarters" is a clear threshold.

## Step 3: Build the Plan

- [ ] **Create 2-3 development goals per person** - No more. Each goal should be specific, time-bound, and linked to a skill gap or required experience.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Identify one development action each person can start this week** - A book, a course module, a shadowing opportunity, or a stretch assignment. Momentum matters more than perfection.
- [ ] **Mix learning methods** - Not everything is a training course. Include on-the-job projects, mentoring, peer learning, self-study, and real responsibility increases. The 70-20-10 rule applies: 70% of development comes from challenging work, 20% from others, 10% from formal training.
- [ ] **Assign a development sponsor or mentor** - Someone other than their direct manager who can offer guidance, open doors, and provide a different perspective.
- [ ] **Set review checkpoints** - Monthly or quarterly touchpoints to discuss progress, adjust the plan, and address blockers. Development plans that get set and forgotten are worthless.

## Step 4: Support and Track

- [ ] **Allocate time and budget for development** - If you tell people to develop but give them no time or resources, the message is clear: it is not really a priority.
- [ ] **Track development progress alongside performance** - Include development goals in your quarterly reviews. They are not separate from performance. They are part of it.
- [ ] **Celebrate progress and milestones** - When someone completes a development goal or demonstrates a new capability, acknowledge it publicly. This signals that growth matters in your business.
- [ ] **Adjust plans when circumstances change** - Business priorities shift, people's interests evolve, and new opportunities appear. Review and update plans at least every 6 months.

---

## Expected Outcome

- Every team member has a clear, written development plan with 2-3 specific goals
- Each person knows what capabilities, experiences, and results are needed for the next level
- A mix of development activities scheduled and underway
- Regular check-ins tracking progress and maintaining momentum
- Higher retention of your best people because they can see a future in the business

## Coach Notes

The biggest pitfall here is creating plans that are too ambitious. A development plan with 8 goals and 20 actions will overwhelm the person and get abandoned. Two or three focused goals with clear next steps are far more effective.

Push the client to have the development conversation even if they do not have a formal career ladder or promotion pathway. Not every business needs titles and levels. But every person needs to know they are growing, valued, and heading somewhere. "You are going to get better at X, take on more responsibility in Y, and we will review where that leads in 6 months" is enough.

For very small businesses where there is no obvious "next level" role, development can focus on mastery, expanded scope, or preparing for a future role that does not exist yet. Growth does not always mean promotion. It can mean becoming genuinely excellent at what you do.
