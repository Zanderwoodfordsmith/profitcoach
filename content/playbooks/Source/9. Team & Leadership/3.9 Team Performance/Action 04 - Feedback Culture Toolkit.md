# Install Your Feedback Culture Toolkit

## What to Do

Build the systems, skills, and habits that make candid, helpful feedback a normal part of how your team operates. Not annual review feedback. Not "can I give you some feedback?" (which makes everyone tense up). Regular, in-the-moment, two-way feedback that helps people improve continuously. This action gives you the tools and frameworks to make that happen.

## Why It Matters

In most small businesses, feedback only happens when something goes wrong. A mistake gets made, frustration builds, and eventually someone has an awkward conversation that should have happened weeks earlier. Meanwhile, good work goes unrecognised because nobody thinks to mention it. A feedback culture changes the default. When feedback is normal, problems get addressed quickly, people improve faster, and the team trusts each other more because honesty is expected, not feared.

## How to Use This

Pick the elements that fit your team's size and maturity. Not everything here will be relevant for every business. Start with the foundations and add more as the culture develops.

## Feedback Frameworks

- [ ] **Adopt a simple feedback model for your team** - SBI (Situation, Behaviour, Impact) works well. "In the client meeting yesterday [situation], when you interrupted the client twice [behaviour], it made them hesitate to share their real concerns [impact]." Teach everyone the same model.
- [ ] **Separate feedback from judgement** - Feedback describes what happened and its effect. Judgement evaluates the person. "The report had three errors" is feedback. "You are careless" is judgement. Train the difference.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Start your next team meeting with one piece of positive feedback per person** - Go around the room. Each person recognises something a colleague did well this week. Takes 5 minutes, shifts the tone immediately.
- [ ] **Create a "how to receive feedback" guide** - Giving feedback is hard. Receiving it is harder. Teach your team to listen, ask clarifying questions, and say "thank you" before reacting. Defensiveness kills feedback culture faster than anything.

## Manager Skills

- [ ] **Train every manager (including yourself) on delivering constructive feedback** - Practice out loud. Role-play difficult conversations. Knowing a framework and using it under pressure are very different things.
- [ ] **Teach the 24-hour rule** - Give feedback within 24 hours of the event. After that, the details get fuzzy, the emotion fades, and the learning opportunity passes.
- [ ] **Model vulnerability as the leader** - Ask your team for feedback on your own performance regularly. "What is one thing I could do better as your manager?" When the boss asks for feedback and acts on it, everyone else feels safer doing the same.
- [ ] **Address feedback avoidance directly** - If a manager is avoiding difficult conversations, coach them through it. Avoidance is the most common and most damaging feedback failure.

## Systems and Rituals

- [ ] **Build peer feedback into project wrap-ups** - At the end of every project or sprint, include a 10-minute "what worked, what did not, what should we do differently?" conversation. Normalise it as part of the workflow.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Set up a recognition channel in your team chat** - A dedicated Slack or Teams channel where anyone can post a quick "thanks to [name] for [specific thing]." Low effort, high visibility.
- [ ] **Run a quarterly feedback health check** - Ask the team: "How comfortable are you giving feedback? Receiving it? Is feedback happening enough?" Track the trend over time.
- [ ] **Create a "stop, start, continue" ritual** - Once a quarter, each team member writes down one thing the team should stop doing, one thing to start doing, and one thing to continue. Discuss as a group.
- [ ] **Protect psychological safety** - Feedback culture only works if people feel safe. If someone gives honest feedback and gets punished for it (formally or informally), the culture dies instantly. Watch for this and address it immediately.

## Scaling Feedback

- [ ] **Build feedback into your onboarding** - New hires should learn on day one that feedback is expected, welcomed, and normal here. Set the expectation early.
- [ ] **Use 360-degree feedback for senior roles** - For managers and leaders, gather feedback from their team, peers, and their own manager. This gives a rounded picture that self-assessment and top-down review miss.

## Expected Outcome

- A common feedback framework that everyone in the business uses
- Managers confident and practised in giving both positive and constructive feedback
- Feedback happening regularly and informally, not just in formal reviews
- A recognition system that makes good work visible
- A team that trusts each other enough to be honest

## Coach Notes

Feedback culture is one of the hardest things to build because it requires the business owner to go first. If they avoid difficult conversations, their managers will too. If they only give feedback when something goes wrong, the team will associate feedback with criticism. The leader sets the tone.

Start with positive feedback. It is easier, it builds trust, and it creates the safety that makes constructive feedback possible later. A team that has never heard "great job on that" is not ready for "here is what you need to change."

For very small teams (2-3 people), skip the formal systems and focus on the leader modelling the behaviour. Regular one-to-one conversations with honest, specific feedback will do more than any framework or Slack channel.
