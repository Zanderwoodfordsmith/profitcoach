# Define Your Role Success Scorecards

## What to Do

For each role in your business, define 3-5 measurable outcomes that make it clear what winning looks like. Not tasks, not activities, not job descriptions. Outcomes. The question each scorecard answers is simple: "If this person is doing a great job, what results would I see?"

## Why It Matters

Most employees are unclear on what success actually looks like in their role. They know their tasks, they know their responsibilities, but they could not tell you the 3-5 results that define whether they are performing well. This ambiguity creates two problems. For high performers, it means they never feel a sense of achievement because the target keeps moving. For underperformers, it means problems go unaddressed because there is no clear standard to measure against.

## How to Use This

Work through each role in the business, starting with the ones that have the biggest impact on revenue and customer satisfaction.

---

## Build the Scorecard

- [ ] **List every role in the business, including your own** - Use actual role titles, not people's names. You are defining the role, not evaluating the person.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **For each role, write one sentence: "This role exists to..."** - If you cannot finish that sentence clearly, the role itself needs clarifying before you can define success.
- [ ] **Identify 3-5 measurable outcomes for each role** - Not activities ("make sales calls") but outcomes ("close £X in new revenue per month"). What results would you see if this role were being done brilliantly?
- [ ] **Set specific targets for each outcome** - "Improve customer satisfaction" is not measurable. "Maintain NPS above 40" or "Resolve 90% of tickets within 24 hours" is. Every outcome needs a number.
- [ ] **Define how each outcome gets measured** - Where does the data come from? Who reports it? How often? If you cannot measure it reliably, it is not a useful outcome.

## Test and Validate

- [ ] **Check that outcomes are within the role's control** - A salesperson can control their pipeline activity and close rate. They cannot control whether marketing generates enough leads. Only include outcomes the role holder can genuinely influence.
- [ ] **Verify that outcomes connect to business goals** - Every role scorecard should ladder up to the company KPIs. If a role's success metrics do not connect to business results, something is misaligned.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Share the draft scorecard with the person in the role** - Ask: "Does this feel fair and accurate? Is anything missing?" Their input improves the scorecard and builds buy-in at the same time.
- [ ] **Check for conflicting outcomes across roles** - If sales is measured on new deals and delivery is measured on utilisation, they may be pulling in opposite directions. Resolve conflicts before publishing.

## Implement

- [ ] **Format each scorecard consistently** - Role title, purpose statement, 3-5 outcomes with targets and measurement methods. Keep it to one page per role.
- [ ] **Use scorecards in your hiring process** - When you recruit for a role, the scorecard tells candidates exactly what success looks like. This attracts people who want accountability.
- [ ] **Build scorecards into your performance review process** - Every review conversation should start with the scorecard. How did the person perform against their defined outcomes?
- [ ] **Review scorecards every 6 months** - Roles evolve. Priorities shift. Update the scorecards to reflect the current reality, not last year's expectations.
- [ ] **Display scorecards where people can see them** - Not locked in an HR file. On the wall, in a shared document, or in your team wiki. Visibility reinforces clarity.

---

## Expected Outcome

- Every role in the business has a clear, one-page success scorecard
- 3-5 measurable outcomes per role with specific targets
- Team members understand exactly what "winning" looks like in their role
- Scorecards used in hiring, reviews, and daily management
- Alignment between individual role outcomes and business-level KPIs

## Coach Notes

The most common mistake is writing activity-based scorecards instead of outcome-based ones. "Send 50 emails per week" is an activity. "Generate 10 qualified leads per week" is an outcome. Keep pushing the client toward outcomes. Activities are how people choose to achieve the outcome, not the measure of success itself.

For very small teams where people wear multiple hats, create a scorecard for the role, not the person. If Sarah handles both sales and customer service, she has two role scorecards. This makes it clear when the business is ready to split the role into two positions.

Some clients resist putting numbers on everything. Help them see that vague expectations are unfair to the employee. "Do a good job" is not a target anyone can hit with confidence.
