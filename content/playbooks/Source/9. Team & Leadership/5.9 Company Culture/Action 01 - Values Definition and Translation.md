# Values Definition and Translation

## What to Do

Move beyond generic value statements to specific, operational principles that guide real decisions. Take words like "integrity," "teamwork," and "excellence" and translate them into observable behaviours that people can see, measure, and hold each other accountable for. The goal is not to create a list for the wall. It is to create a decision-making tool that everyone in the business actually uses.

## Why It Matters

Most businesses have values. Very few have useful ones. "We value integrity" means nothing because everyone thinks they have integrity. "We tell customers the truth even when it costs us the sale" means something specific. It guides behaviour. It makes the right action obvious in a difficult moment. Patrick Lencioni's work on organisational health makes the distinction clear: core values only create culture when they are specific enough to be controversial. If nobody would disagree with your value, it is not distinctive enough to guide behaviour. Generic values are wallpaper. Translated values are tools.

## How to Use This

Identify your real values, translate each one into specific behaviours, and test them against real decisions.

---

## Identify Your Real Values (Not Your Aspirational Ones)

- [ ] **List the 3-5 principles that actually guide how you run the business today** - Not what you wish was true. What is true. Look at your last 10 decisions: what patterns emerge? What did you prioritise when things got difficult?
- [ ] **Ask yourself: "What behaviour would I never tolerate, even from a top performer?"** - The things you would fire someone for reveal your real non-negotiables.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Think of the best person who ever worked for you. What 3 qualities made them great? Now think of the worst. What 3 qualities made them terrible? Your values are hiding in those answers.
- [ ] **Cross out anything generic** - "Excellence," "innovation," "teamwork," "customer focus" appear on 90% of company value lists. If your value could apply to any business, it is not distinctive enough. Sharpen it or replace it.
- [ ] **Ask your team independently: "What do we really stand for here?"** - Compare their answers to yours. Alignment means the values are real. Divergence means the values are aspirational.

## Translate Each Value into Behaviours

- [ ] **For each value, write 3-5 specific behaviours that demonstrate it** - These should be observable. Someone watching from outside could see whether the behaviour is happening.
  - Example: "Ownership" becomes: "If you see a problem, you fix it or flag it within 24 hours. You never say 'that is not my job.' You follow up on commitments without being chased."
- [ ] **For each value, write 2-3 behaviours that violate it** - Knowing what the value is NOT is as useful as knowing what it is. This creates clarity at the boundaries.
  - Example: "Ownership" is NOT: "Agreeing in a meeting then doing nothing. Waiting to be told. Blaming someone else when things go wrong."
- [ ] **Use plain language** - If a new employee on their first day cannot understand the behaviour, it is too complicated. Write for clarity, not sophistication.
- [ ] **Check for tension between values** - If two of your values conflict in practice (speed vs. thoroughness, for example), define which one wins in specific situations. Unresolved tension between values creates confusion.

## Test Against Real Decisions

- [ ] **Pick 3 difficult decisions you have made in the last year** - Hiring, firing, pricing, strategy, customer disputes. Anything where the right answer was not obvious.
- [ ] **Apply your translated values to each decision** - Would the values have made the right answer clearer? If yes, they are working. If the values pointed in a different direction from what you actually did, either the values or the decision was wrong.
- [ ] **Create a "values decision" scenario for each value** - A short, realistic scenario that a team member might face, where the value tells them what to do. Use these in onboarding and team discussions.
- [ ] **Share the translated values with your team** - Get feedback. Do these feel true? Are the behaviours realistic? Would they change how people work?
- [ ] **Finalise and publish** - One page. Values on the left, behaviours on the right. Put it somewhere everyone can see it, not buried in a handbook nobody reads.

---

## Expected Outcome

- 3-5 core values that are distinctive to your business, not generic
- Each value translated into specific, observable behaviours (what it looks like in practice)
- Anti-behaviours defined for each value (what it does not look like)
- Values tested against real decisions to confirm they work as decision tools
- A one-page values document that the whole team can use

## Coach Notes

The most revealing question is: "Would you keep these values even if they cost you money?" If a client says their value is "always put the customer first" but would not give a refund to an unhappy customer, the value is not real. Real values sometimes come at a cost. That is what makes them values instead of slogans.

Many clients will resist limiting to 3-5 values. They want 8 or 10 because they do not want to leave anything out. Push them to choose. The discipline of selection is part of the exercise. If everything is a value, nothing is.

Watch for values that describe what the owner wants to be, not what the business actually is. Aspirational values create cynicism when the team sees the gap between the poster on the wall and reality on the ground. Start with what is genuinely true, then add one aspirational value as a growth target.
