# Recognition Flywheel

## What to Do

Design a system that spots, celebrates, and amplifies the behaviours you want to see more of. Make cultural exemplars visible so the whole team sees what "great" looks like. Recognition is the positive reinforcement that makes your values sticky - people repeat behaviour that gets noticed and appreciated.

## Why It Matters

Most businesses rely entirely on correction to shape culture: telling people what they did wrong. Recognition is the other half of the equation, and for most people, the more powerful half. Research consistently shows that employees who feel recognised are more engaged, more productive, and more likely to stay. But recognition only works when it is specific, timely, connected to values, and genuine. Generic "employee of the month" programmes feel hollow. A flywheel works differently: specific recognition makes values visible, which inspires more values-aligned behaviour, which creates more recognition opportunities. The cycle builds on itself.

## How to Use This

Build each component of the flywheel: spotting, celebrating, and amplifying.

---

## Spotting: Build Your Observation System

- [ ] **Train yourself and your managers to watch for values-aligned behaviour** - Not just big achievements. Daily actions that demonstrate your values. The team member who stayed late to help a colleague. The person who flagged a problem early instead of hiding it. The one who said "I got this wrong" instead of making excuses.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: This week, notice one team member demonstrating a value and tell them specifically what you saw and why it mattered. Do this before building any system.
- [ ] **Create a "values in action" log** - A simple shared document where anyone can note when they see a colleague demonstrating a value. Include: who, what they did, and which value it demonstrates.
- [ ] **Enable peer-to-peer recognition** - Do not make recognition top-down only. Give the whole team a way to recognise each other. A Slack channel, a physical board, a standing item in team meetings. Peer recognition often means more than manager recognition.
- [ ] **Set a weekly observation goal for leaders** - Each manager spots and records at least 2 values-aligned behaviours per week. This builds the habit of looking for the good, not just the bad.

## Celebrating: Design Your Recognition Methods

- [ ] **Match recognition to the behaviour** - Not everything needs a grand gesture. Small behaviours get a quick thank you. Significant behaviours get public recognition. Exceptional behaviours get a meaningful reward.
- [ ] **Build a recognition toolkit with multiple options:**
  - Immediate verbal recognition (private or in a meeting)
  - Written recognition (email, message, handwritten note)
  - Public shout-out (team meeting, company channel, noticeboard)
  - Tangible reward (gift card, extra time off, experience, team lunch)
  - Career recognition (new responsibilities, development opportunity, involvement in strategic decisions)
- [ ] **Always connect recognition to a specific value and behaviour** - "Great job this week" is nice but forgettable. "The way you handled that customer complaint showed exactly what [value] means in practice - you [specific behaviour] and the result was [outcome]" is memorable and teaches everyone what the value looks like.
- [ ] **Recognise effort and process, not just results** - Someone who demonstrates values-aligned behaviour but does not get the perfect result still deserves recognition for the behaviour. If you only recognise results, people learn to chase outcomes at any cost.

## Amplifying: Make Culture Visible

- [ ] **Share recognition stories in team communications** - Weekly email, team meeting, internal newsletter. When everyone hears about values-aligned behaviour, it raises the bar for the whole team.
- [ ] **Create a "values wall" or digital equivalent** - A visible, updated collection of recognition moments. New hires see it. Visitors see it. The team sees it daily.
- [ ] **Use recognition in onboarding** - Share real examples of team members living the values with every new hire. This shows that values are not just words - they are things people actually do here.
- [ ] **Reference past recognition in performance reviews** - "Over the last quarter, you were recognised three times for [value]. Here is how that connects to your growth..." This links daily recognition to career development.
- [ ] **Celebrate milestones** - When the team collectively hits an improvement target, solves a long-standing problem, or receives exceptional customer feedback, celebrate together. Shared wins reinforce shared culture.

## Keep the Flywheel Spinning

- [ ] **Review recognition frequency monthly** - Is recognition happening regularly across the team? Or only for the same 2-3 people? Spread the net wider.
- [ ] **Ask the team quarterly: "Do you feel recognised for living our values?"** - If the answer is no, the flywheel is stalling. Investigate why and adjust.
- [ ] **Refresh your recognition methods every 6 months** - The same approach gets stale. Introduce new ways to recognise and celebrate. Keep it fresh.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A systematic approach to spotting values-aligned behaviour across the team
- Multiple recognition methods available, matched to the significance of the behaviour
- Recognition consistently connected to specific values and observable behaviours
- Culture made visible through shared stories, public recognition, and onboarding
- A self-reinforcing flywheel where recognition drives more values-aligned behaviour

## Coach Notes

The biggest mistake is making recognition complicated. Clients want to design elaborate reward programmes before they have built the habit of simply saying "I noticed what you did and it mattered." Start with verbal recognition. Build the habit of noticing. Add systems and rewards later once the observation muscle is strong.

Watch for recognition that is too infrequent or too concentrated. If only the owner recognises people, and they do it once a month, the flywheel will not spin. It needs to be frequent (multiple times per week across the team) and distributed (peer-to-peer, not just top-down).

The most common objection is: "My team should not need praise for doing their job." This misunderstands what recognition does. It is not about rewarding the expected. It is about making the desired behaviour visible so everyone knows what good looks like. The alternative - only giving feedback when things go wrong - teaches people to hide rather than to strive.
