# Culture Stack Implementation

## What to Do

Build a systematic framework for reinforcing your defined values through every stage of the employee lifecycle: hiring, onboarding, daily work, performance management, and recognition. Culture becomes a process rather than an accident. Each layer of the stack reinforces the same values, so the message is consistent from job ad to annual review.

## Why It Matters

Defining values is step one. Making them real is where most businesses fail. Values only become culture when they are embedded in systems. If you hire for skills but not values, you get talented people who undermine your culture. If you onboard without values, people learn "how things work around here" from whoever they sit next to, not from you. If you review performance without including values, you signal that only results matter, not how those results are achieved. The culture stack ensures every system reinforces the same message.

## How to Use This

Work through each layer of the stack, checking whether your current systems reinforce your values or ignore them.

---

## Layer 1: Hiring

- [ ] **Add values-based questions to your interview process** - For each value, create at least one behavioural question. "Tell me about a time when..." questions reveal past behaviour, which is the best predictor of future behaviour.
- [ ] **Include values in your job descriptions** - Not a generic "our values are..." section. Specific statements like: "You will thrive here if you [behaviour]. You will struggle if you [anti-behaviour]."
- [ ] **Score candidates on values fit alongside skills fit** - A candidate who scores high on skills but low on values is a culture risk. Define your threshold: would you reject a skilled candidate who does not fit your values? If yes, make that policy explicit.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Before your next hire, write down the top 3 values-based behaviours you want to see. Use these as an explicit filter during interviews.

## Layer 2: Onboarding

- [ ] **Introduce values on day one** - Not as a slide in an induction pack. As a conversation. Explain each value, share real examples of what it looks like in practice, and explain why it matters.
- [ ] **Assign a values buddy** - A team member who models the values well and can answer "how do we do things here?" questions during the first 30 days.
- [ ] **Include values-based scenarios in onboarding** - "Here is a situation you might face. Based on our values, what would you do?" This is more effective than reading a list.
- [ ] **Check values understanding at the 30-day mark** - Not a test. A conversation. "Which of our values resonates most with you? Which one do you find hardest to apply?"

## Layer 3: Daily Operations

- [ ] **Reference values in meetings and decisions** - When discussing options, ask: "Which choice aligns best with our values?" This normalises values as a decision-making tool, not a poster.
- [ ] **Use values language in feedback** - "That was a great example of [value]" or "That did not align with [value] because..." makes feedback specific and connected to shared standards.
- [ ] **Make values visible** - Not a laminated poster nobody reads. Integrate values into the tools people use daily: project templates, Slack channels, meeting agendas, one-to-one formats.
- [ ] **Model from the top** - The owner and leaders must visibly demonstrate the values. If leadership behaves differently from the values, the values are dead. This is the most important layer.

## Layer 4: Performance Management

- [ ] **Include values in performance reviews** - Alongside results and skills, assess whether the person consistently demonstrates the defined behaviours.
- [ ] **Make values a promotion criterion** - People who get results through values-aligned behaviour should be promoted ahead of those who get results despite violating values.
- [ ] **Address values violations directly** - When someone behaves in a way that contradicts a value, address it immediately and specifically. "In that situation, our value of [X] means we would do [Y]. Here is why that matters."
- [ ] **Create consequences for persistent values violations** - Values without consequences are suggestions. Define what happens when someone repeatedly violates a value, even if their results are strong.

## Layer 5: Recognition

- [ ] **Recognise values-aligned behaviour publicly** - When someone demonstrates a value exceptionally, call it out. In a meeting, in a message, in a review. Specific recognition reinforces the behaviour.
- [ ] **Connect recognition to the specific value** - Not just "great job." Instead: "That was a perfect example of [value] because you [specific behaviour]." This teaches everyone what the value looks like in practice.

---

## Expected Outcome

- Values embedded in every stage of the employee lifecycle
- Hiring process updated with values-based questions and scoring
- Onboarding redesigned to include values introduction and scenarios
- Daily operations language and tools updated to reference values
- Performance management includes values assessment
- A complete culture stack where every layer reinforces the same message

## Coach Notes

The most common failure is building the stack but not maintaining it. Values in hiring is easy to implement. Values in daily operations requires ongoing discipline from the owner and managers. Check in regularly: are leaders actually referencing values in decisions, or did it fade after the first month?

Watch for the "brilliant jerk" dilemma. Clients will have someone who delivers great results but consistently violates values. This is the ultimate test of the culture stack. If the brilliant jerk is tolerated, the whole stack collapses because the team sees that values are optional for high performers. Help clients see that keeping a values violator is more expensive than losing their output.

Start with the layer the client can implement this week. For most businesses, that is adding values questions to the next interview or starting to use values language in team meetings. Build momentum before trying to overhaul everything.
