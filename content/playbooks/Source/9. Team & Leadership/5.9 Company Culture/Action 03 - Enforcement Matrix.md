# Enforcement Matrix

## What to Do

Create clear boundaries around what behaviour gets which response. Map the spectrum from minor drift to serious violation, and define proportionate consequences for each level. Consistency in enforcement turns values from suggestions into standards. Without clear enforcement, your values depend entirely on the mood and personality of whoever happens to be managing that day.

## Why It Matters

Inconsistent enforcement is the number one killer of company culture. If one manager lets value violations slide while another addresses them firmly, the team learns that values are optional. If a top performer gets a pass on behaviour that would earn a junior team member a warning, the message is clear: results matter more than values. Ray Dalio's principle is relevant here: "If you are not willing to enforce a standard, it is not really a standard." The enforcement matrix removes ambiguity and personal discretion. Everyone knows what to expect, regardless of who they report to.

## How to Use This

Define your enforcement levels, map specific behaviours to each level, and create response protocols that anyone in a leadership role can follow.

---

## Define Your Enforcement Levels

- [ ] **Level 1: Drift** - Minor, possibly unintentional deviation from values. First occurrence, no pattern. Example: missing a deadline without communicating, being slightly dismissive in a meeting.
- [ ] **Level 2: Pattern** - The same type of drift recurring 2-3 times after being addressed. No longer accidental but not yet deliberate. Example: repeatedly missing deadlines after being coached on it.
- [ ] **Level 3: Violation** - Clear, significant breach of a defined value. Deliberate or reckless. Example: lying to a customer, taking credit for someone else's work, refusing to take responsibility.
- [ ] **Level 4: Dealbreaker** - Behaviour so far outside your values that it cannot be resolved with coaching or warnings. Example: theft, harassment, intentional sabotage, sustained dishonesty.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Think of the last time someone's behaviour bothered you at work. What level would it fall under? This gives you an immediate sense of whether your instincts match the framework.

## Map Behaviours to Levels

- [ ] **For each of your defined values, list 2-3 example behaviours at each enforcement level** - What does Level 1 drift look like for "ownership"? What does a Level 3 violation of "honesty" look like? Be specific enough that two different managers would categorise the same behaviour at the same level.
- [ ] **Check for grey areas** - Where does behaviour sit between two levels? These boundary cases need discussion and agreement among leaders before they happen, not during.
- [ ] **Involve your team leads or managers** - If you have them. They need to agree with the categorisation because they are the ones who will enforce it. If they disagree, the matrix will be applied inconsistently.
- [ ] **Write it down in plain language** - No legal jargon. No HR-speak. Write it so anyone could read it and understand what behaviour sits at which level.

## Define Response Protocols

- [ ] **Level 1 response: Private conversation** - "I noticed [specific behaviour]. Our value of [X] means we do [Y] instead. Can you help me understand what happened?" Supportive tone. Assumption of good intent. Document the conversation.
- [ ] **Level 2 response: Formal feedback with documented plan** - "This is the second/third time we have discussed [behaviour]. Here is what needs to change, by when, and here is the support available. If it continues, here is what happens next." Clear expectations. Written record.
- [ ] **Level 3 response: Written warning with performance improvement plan** - Formal documentation. Specific changes required. Timeline. Consequences of failure to improve. Follow your legal requirements here.
- [ ] **Level 4 response: Immediate action** - Depending on severity: suspension pending investigation, termination, or other immediate measures. Follow employment law and take legal advice for serious matters.
- [ ] **Document everything** - Every conversation, at every level, gets a brief written record. Date, what was discussed, what was agreed. This protects you legally and ensures continuity if a manager changes.

## Implement the Matrix

- [ ] **Share the matrix with all people managers** - Walk through it together. Discuss the examples. Practise categorising real scenarios.
- [ ] **Share the principles (not the full matrix) with the whole team** - Everyone should know that values are enforced and what the general approach is. They do not need to see every detail of the escalation process.
- [ ] **Review the matrix every 6 months** - Are the levels right? Have new grey areas emerged? Does the matrix need updating based on real situations?
- [ ] **Track enforcement consistency** - Are similar behaviours receiving similar responses across different managers? If not, recalibrate.

---

## Expected Outcome

- Four clear enforcement levels defined with proportionate responses
- Specific behaviours mapped to each level for every core value
- Response protocols documented so any manager can follow them
- Grey areas identified and resolved before they become real situations
- A consistent enforcement approach that builds trust and reinforces culture

## Coach Notes

This is one of the most difficult actions for clients because it forces them to confront something uncomfortable: what will you actually do when someone violates a value? Many clients prefer to keep things vague because clarity feels harsh. Help them see that vagueness is actually harsher. Without clear boundaries, people do not know where they stand, and enforcement becomes arbitrary and unfair.

The Level 1 conversation is the most important one. If drift gets addressed consistently at Level 1, you rarely need Levels 2-4. Most culture problems grow because early drift was ignored. Coach clients to get comfortable with early, supportive conversations rather than waiting until a pattern develops.

Watch for clients who build the matrix but exempt themselves. If the owner is not held to the same standards (ideally by a trusted peer, mentor, or advisor), the matrix loses credibility. Culture starts at the top.
