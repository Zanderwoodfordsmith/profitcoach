# Clean Chart of Accounts

## What to Do

Organise your income and expense categories so your financial reports actually make sense. A chart of accounts is the list of categories your accounting software uses to classify every transaction. When it is clean, your P&L tells a clear story. When it is messy, your P&L raises more questions than it answers.

## Why It Matters

Most small businesses end up with a chart of accounts that grew organically - categories added on the fly, duplicate categories, a massive "miscellaneous" or "general expenses" bucket, and income categories that do not match how the business actually earns money. The result: financial reports that are technically accurate but practically useless. You cannot see which revenue streams are growing, which costs are creeping, or where profit is being made and lost. A clean chart of accounts turns your P&L from a confusing document into a decision-making tool.

## How to Use This

Review your current chart of accounts, simplify and reorganise, then set rules to keep it clean.

---

## Review Current State

- [ ] Open your accounting software and look at your chart of accounts (the full list of income, expense, and other categories)
- [ ] Run a P&L for last month and review: can you understand what each line means? Are there categories with no transactions, or catch-all categories with too many?
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Find your "miscellaneous" or "general expenses" category. If it has more than 5% of total expenses in it, those transactions need proper homes.
- [ ] List any categories that are duplicates or so similar they should be merged
- [ ] List any categories that are too broad (e.g., "Marketing" when you need to see the difference between ads, content, and events)

## Design Your Clean Structure

### Income Categories

- [ ] Create one category for each distinct revenue stream (match the streams you identified in 2.2 Revenue Stream Analysis)
- [ ] Keep it to 3-7 income categories. Too few and you lose visibility; too many and reports become noisy.
- [ ] Example: "Consulting Revenue", "Training Revenue", "Product Sales", "Recurring Subscriptions"

### Cost of Sales (Direct Costs)

- [ ] Create categories for costs directly tied to delivering your service/product
- [ ] These costs should increase when you sell more: subcontractors, materials, direct labour, commissions
- [ ] Separating cost of sales from overhead expenses lets you calculate gross margin

### Operating Expenses

- [ ] Group expenses into logical categories. A good starting structure for most SMEs:
  - Premises (rent, utilities, rates, maintenance)
  - Team (salaries, NI, pension, training, recruitment)
  - Marketing (advertising, content, events, subscriptions)
  - Technology (software, hosting, hardware, IT support)
  - Professional services (accounting, legal, consulting)
  - Travel and entertainment
  - Insurance
  - Finance costs (bank charges, interest, merchant fees)
  - Office and admin (stationery, postage, cleaning)
- [ ] Aim for 10-15 expense categories total. Enough detail to spot trends, not so much that every report is overwhelming.

## Clean Up and Maintain

- [ ] Merge duplicate categories
- [ ] Recategorise transactions currently sitting in miscellaneous or catch-all categories
- [ ] Delete any categories with zero transactions in the last 12 months
- [ ] Set a rule: before creating a new category, ask "does this fit in an existing one?" New categories should be rare.
- [ ] Brief anyone who does your bookkeeping on the new structure so they categorise consistently
- [ ] Review the chart of accounts every 6 months to ensure it still matches how the business operates

---

## Expected Outcome

- A clean, logical chart of accounts with 3-7 income categories and 10-15 expense categories
- Financial reports that tell a clear story about where money comes from and where it goes
- The ability to track revenue by stream and expenses by function
- Gross margin visibility (by separating cost of sales from overhead)
- A structure that stays clean because the rules prevent drift

## Coach Notes

This is unsexy but essential work. Clients often resist it because it feels like accounting rather than business building. Reframe: "Your P&L is only as useful as the categories it uses. Right now it is like a map with the wrong labels." The biggest value: being able to see gross margin by revenue stream. Many clients discover that their most popular service has the worst margin once direct costs are properly separated. Work with the client's accountant or bookkeeper on this if possible - they will appreciate the effort and it ensures the structure works for tax reporting too.
