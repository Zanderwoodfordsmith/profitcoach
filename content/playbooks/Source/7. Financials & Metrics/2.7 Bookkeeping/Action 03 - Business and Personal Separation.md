# Business and Personal Separation

## What to Do

Draw clear lines between business and personal finances. Separate accounts, separate cards, separate everything. Mixed finances create tax complications, legal risks, and decision-making confusion. This action gets them properly separated so your numbers are clean and your business is protected.

## Why It Matters

When personal and business money mix, three things go wrong. First, your business numbers become unreliable - you cannot tell what the business actually costs to run when your personal Amazon orders are mixed in. Second, you create tax headaches - your accountant has to untangle every mixed transaction, which costs you money and time. Third, you create legal risk - if your business is a limited company, mixing finances can pierce the corporate veil and put your personal assets at risk. Clean separation solves all three.

## How to Use This

Audit your current separation, fix the gaps, and establish rules going forward.

---

## Separation Audit

- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Open your business bank statement from last month. Highlight any personal purchases. If there are more than 2-3, separation is needed.
- [ ] Check: do you have a dedicated business bank account? (If not, this is step one)
- [ ] Check: do you have a separate business credit/debit card?
- [ ] Check: are all business subscriptions paid from the business account?
- [ ] Check: are all personal subscriptions paid from your personal account?
- [ ] Check: does your personal spending ever go through the business account?
- [ ] Check: does business spending ever go through your personal account?

## Set Up Proper Separation

- [ ] **Business bank account:** If you do not have one, open a dedicated business account this week. Most banks offer free business current accounts. Move all business transactions to this account.
- [ ] **Business card:** Get a separate debit or credit card for all business purchases. This alone eliminates most mixing.
- [ ] **Salary/drawings:** Set up a regular transfer from business to personal account (salary if limited company, drawings if sole trader). This is your "pay." Do not dip into the business account for personal spending.
- [ ] **Subscriptions audit:** List every recurring payment. Move business subscriptions to the business account and personal subscriptions to your personal account.
- [ ] **Expense process:** If you ever pay for a business expense personally (unavoidable sometimes), create a simple expense claim process: receipt, amount, purpose, reimbursement from business account within the week.

## Ongoing Rules

- [ ] **Rule 1:** All business income goes into the business account. No exceptions.
- [ ] **Rule 2:** All business expenses come from the business account. No exceptions.
- [ ] **Rule 3:** Personal spending never goes through the business account.
- [ ] **Rule 4:** If you pay business expenses personally, reimburse yourself promptly with a clear paper trail.
- [ ] **Rule 5:** Review separation monthly during your reconciliation (Action 2) - catch any slip-ups immediately.

---

## Expected Outcome

- Complete separation between business and personal finances
- A dedicated business bank account and card for all business transactions
- A regular salary/drawings process for paying yourself
- Cleaner books, simpler tax returns, and reduced accountant fees
- Legal protection maintained through proper financial separation

## Coach Notes

This is non-negotiable for any client with a limited company - mixed finances create genuine legal risk. For sole traders, it is less legally critical but just as important for clarity. The most common resistance: "It is easier to just use one account." It is easier in the moment but creates 10x more work at year-end. For clients who are deeply mixed, do not try to fix the history - just draw a line from today. "From this date forward, everything is separate." The bookkeeper or accountant can untangle the past. Focus coaching on the going-forward discipline.
