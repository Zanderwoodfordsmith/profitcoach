# Install Your Review Rhythm

## What to Do

Set up a weekly rhythm for looking at your numbers, discussing trends, and making adjustments. Having a scorecard is pointless if nobody reviews it. This action installs the habit and structure that turns measurement into action. The same meeting, same time, same format, every single week.

## Why It Matters

The gap between businesses that measure and businesses that improve is not better data. It is rhythm. The companies that grow consistently are the ones that look at the same numbers every week, spot trends early, and adjust fast. A weekly review rhythm means problems get caught in days, not months. It means the team stays aligned on what matters. And it means nobody can hide from underperformance because the numbers are discussed regularly.

## How to Use This

Design your weekly review meeting, set the ground rules, and then commit to running it without exception.

---

## Step 1: Design the Meeting

- [ ] **Pick a fixed day and time** - Monday morning or Friday afternoon. Monday sets the week. Friday closes it. Choose whichever fits your team's energy and schedule.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Block 30 minutes in everyone's calendar, recurring weekly, for the next 12 weeks** - Make it immovable. This meeting does not get cancelled or rescheduled for client calls.
- [ ] **Set the agenda** - Keep it tight. Five minutes on scorecard review, ten minutes on trends and exceptions, ten minutes on actions, five minutes on priorities for the coming week.
- [ ] **Assign a meeting owner** - Someone runs the meeting, keeps it on time, and ensures actions are captured. This does not have to be the business owner.
- [ ] **Define who attends** - Everyone who owns a metric should be in the room. If you have a large team, break it into departmental reviews that feed into a leadership review.

## Step 2: Run the Review

- [ ] **Start with the scorecard, not with opinions** - Open the scorecard. Read the numbers. Let the data set the agenda. Do not let the loudest voice in the room decide what gets discussed.
- [ ] **Focus on red and amber metrics** - Green metrics get a nod. Red and amber get discussion. What caused the miss? What is the plan? Who owns the fix?
- [ ] **Look at trends, not just this week's snapshot** - A metric that has been declining for 4 weeks needs a different response than one that dipped once. Train the team to think in trends.
- [ ] **End with clear actions** - Every discussion should result in a specific action, owned by a specific person, with a specific deadline. Write it down. Review it next week.

## Step 3: Build the Habit

- [ ] **Run the first 4 meetings yourself** - Model the behaviour. Show the team what good looks like. After 4 weeks, hand the facilitation to someone else.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Send a pre-meeting update the day before** - A quick message with the current scorecard so people arrive prepared. This cuts meeting time in half because nobody is seeing the numbers for the first time.
- [ ] **Keep a running log of actions and outcomes** - Over time, this becomes a powerful record of decisions made, problems solved, and patterns that repeat.
- [ ] **Protect the meeting fiercely** - The moment you cancel one review "because we're too busy," you have started the slide. The meeting happens every week. No exceptions.

## Step 4: Evolve the Rhythm

- [ ] **Add a monthly deep dive** - Once a month, extend the review to 60 minutes and go deeper on one area. This is where you question targets, revisit strategy, and make bigger adjustments.
- [ ] **Add a quarterly reset** - Every quarter, review whether the KPIs themselves are still the right ones. Update targets based on what you have learned.
- [ ] **Gather feedback after 8 weeks** - Ask the team: "Is this meeting useful? What would make it better?" Iterate based on what you hear.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A weekly meeting that happens every single week without exception
- Scorecard reviewed, trends discussed, and actions assigned in 30 minutes
- Problems caught within a week instead of a quarter
- Team aligned and accountable for their numbers
- A decision log that tracks what was discussed and what was done

## Coach Notes

This is the action that makes all the other KPI actions work. Without the review rhythm, the scorecard becomes decoration. With it, the scorecard becomes a management tool.

The biggest risk is meeting bloat. Clients love to add agenda items, invite more people, and extend the time. Fight this aggressively. The weekly review is 30 minutes, focused on numbers and actions. Everything else gets its own meeting. The moment this meeting becomes a general catch-up, it loses its power.

For solo businesses or very small teams, the "meeting" can be 15 minutes with yourself reviewing the scorecard and journaling your observations. The rhythm matters more than the format.
