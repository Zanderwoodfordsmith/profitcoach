# Audit Your Critical Metrics

## What to Do

Review everything you currently measure in your business and identify the 3-5 numbers that actually predict success. Then cut everything else. Most businesses either measure nothing meaningful or measure too many things, which amounts to the same problem. This exercise strips your metrics back to the handful that genuinely drive decisions.

## Why It Matters

Peter Drucker was right that what gets measured gets managed. But the reverse is also true: when everything gets measured, nothing gets managed. Businesses drowning in dashboards and reports often make worse decisions than businesses tracking 4 numbers on a whiteboard. The goal is not more data. It is the right data, looked at consistently, driving clear action.

## How to Use This

Start by listing everything you currently track. Then use the filtering questions to separate the metrics that matter from the ones that just make you feel busy.

---

## Current State Audit

- [ ] **List every number you currently track or report on** - Spreadsheets, dashboards, accounting software, CRM reports, marketing analytics. Get everything in one list.
- [ ] **For each metric, answer: "If this number changed by 20%, what would I do differently?"** - If the answer is "nothing," the metric is not driving decisions. Mark it for removal.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Identify any metrics you track but have not looked at in the last 30 days** - If nobody is looking at it, it is not a KPI. It is decoration. Stop tracking it immediately.
- [ ] **Flag any metrics where you are not confident the data is accurate** - An inaccurate metric is worse than no metric. It gives you false confidence.

## Identify Your Critical Few

- [ ] **Revenue metrics** - Pick one: total revenue, monthly recurring revenue, or revenue per customer. Not all three. Which one, if you could only see one number, would tell you most about business health?
- [ ] **Profitability metric** - Gross margin, net profit margin, or cash profit. Pick the one your business model makes most useful.
- [ ] **Pipeline or demand metric** - Leads generated, proposals sent, discovery calls booked, or pipeline value. The number that predicts future revenue.
- [ ] **Customer health metric** - Retention rate, NPS, repeat purchase rate, or active customer count. The number that tells you whether customers are staying and happy.
- [ ] **Capacity or efficiency metric** - Utilisation rate, delivery time, output per person, or cost per acquisition. The number that tells you how well your engine runs.

## Clean Up

- [ ] **Write your final 3-5 KPIs on a single page** - If they do not fit on one page with definitions, targets, and current performance, you have too many.
- [ ] **Define each KPI clearly** - Exact calculation, data source, who owns it, and how often it gets updated. No ambiguity.
- [ ] **Set a target for each KPI** - A good target is specific, time-bound, and achievable but stretching. "Increase revenue" is not a target. "Hit £85K MRR by end of Q3" is.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Delete or archive at least 3 reports or dashboards you no longer need** - Fewer reports means less noise and more focus on what matters.
- [ ] **Communicate the change to your team** - Explain why you are simplifying and what the new critical metrics are. Everyone should know the 3-5 numbers that matter.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A complete inventory of everything currently being measured
- Clear distinction between metrics that drive decisions and metrics that are just noise
- 3-5 critical KPIs defined with calculations, owners, and targets
- Redundant reports and dashboards archived or deleted
- The entire team aligned on which numbers matter most

## Coach Notes

The hardest part of this exercise is getting the business owner to let go of metrics they have been tracking for years. They feel like removing a report means losing control. Reframe it: "You are not losing visibility. You are gaining focus."

Watch for two common traps. First, the client who insists on 10+ KPIs because "they are all important." Push back hard. If everything is critical, nothing is. Second, the client who picks vanity metrics (social followers, website visits) over substance metrics (conversion rate, profit margin). Always ask: "Can you make a business decision based on this number?"
