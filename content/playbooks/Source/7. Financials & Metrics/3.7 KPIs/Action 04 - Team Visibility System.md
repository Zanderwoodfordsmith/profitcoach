# Build Your Team Visibility System

## What to Do

Make sure every person in your business knows which metrics they influence and can see their numbers in real time. Right now, your KPI scorecard probably lives with you or your management team. This action pushes the right numbers down to the people who actually move them, so everyone is playing with the scoreboard visible.

## Why It Matters

Imagine playing football without a scoreboard. You would run around for 90 minutes and find out at the end whether you won. That is how most employees experience work. They do their tasks, go home, and find out at their annual review whether they performed well. When people can see their numbers in real time, they self-correct. They push harder when they are behind and maintain momentum when they are ahead. Visibility drives performance without management having to chase anyone.

## How to Use This

Start by mapping which metrics connect to which roles, then build simple visibility mechanisms for each team or individual.

---

## Step 1: Connect Metrics to People

- [ ] **List every role in the business** - Include yourself. Everyone should have at least one number they own.
- [ ] **For each role, identify 1-3 metrics they directly influence** - A salesperson influences pipeline and close rate. A delivery person influences customer satisfaction and completion time. Be specific about the connection.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Ask each team member: "Do you know which numbers you are measured on?"** - If the answer is no or vague, that tells you everything about your current visibility problem.
- [ ] **Check that individual metrics roll up to business KPIs** - Every person's numbers should connect logically to the company scorecard. If someone's metrics do not connect, either the metrics or the role needs rethinking.

## Step 2: Make Numbers Visible

- [ ] **Create individual or team dashboards** - These do not need to be sophisticated. A shared spreadsheet, a whiteboard, or a simple screen display. The format matters less than the visibility.
- [ ] **Choose the right update frequency for each metric** - Sales metrics might update daily. Delivery metrics weekly. Financial metrics monthly. Match the rhythm to how fast the number changes.
- [ ] **Put numbers where people can see them without logging in** - A TV screen on the wall, a printed weekly sheet, or a pinned message in your team chat. The best dashboard is the one nobody has to go looking for.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Set up a daily or weekly "numbers check" in your team chat** - A simple message: "Here are today's numbers: [pipeline, calls, deliveries]." Takes 2 minutes. Creates instant visibility.

## Step 3: Build the Conversation Around Numbers

- [ ] **Start every team meeting with a 2-minute numbers review** - What are we at? Are we on track? What is off? This trains the team to think in numbers, not feelings.
- [ ] **Teach your team to read their metrics** - Do not assume people know what a conversion rate means or why a trend matters. Spend 15 minutes explaining each metric and what good looks like.
- [ ] **Celebrate improvements publicly** - When someone's numbers improve, acknowledge it in front of the team. This reinforces that metrics matter and effort gets noticed.
- [ ] **Address declines without blame** - When numbers dip, the question is "What happened and what do we need to change?" not "Why did you fail?" The goal is problem-solving, not punishment.

## Step 4: Maintain the System

- [ ] **Review individual metrics quarterly** - Are they still the right numbers? As roles evolve, the metrics should evolve too.
- [ ] **Check for gaming** - Any metric can be gamed if people are measured on it. Watch for behaviours that hit the number but miss the intent. A salesperson who hits call targets by making short, useless calls is gaming the system.
- [ ] **Get feedback from the team** - Ask: "Are the numbers you see helping you do your job better?" If not, adjust what is visible.

---

## Expected Outcome

- Every team member knows exactly which 1-3 metrics they own
- Numbers are visible without effort - displayed, shared, or posted regularly
- Team meetings start with data, not opinions
- Individuals self-correct based on real-time performance data
- A culture where numbers are a tool for improvement, not a weapon for blame

## Coach Notes

This action often reveals that the business owner has been hoarding information without realising it. They know the numbers. Nobody else does. The instinct is to protect people from "bad numbers," but that just creates a team that cannot self-manage.

Start small. Pick one team or one role and build visibility there first. Once people see the impact, they will ask for it themselves. The biggest risk is overcomplicating the system. A whiteboard with three numbers updated daily is more powerful than a real-time dashboard nobody checks.

For very small teams (under 5 people), individual dashboards are overkill. A shared weekly scorecard reviewed together is enough.
