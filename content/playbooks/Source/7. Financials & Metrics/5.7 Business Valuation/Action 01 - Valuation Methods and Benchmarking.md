# Valuation Methods and Benchmarking

## What to Do

Learn the three main methods for valuing a small business, apply each one to your own business, and compare your metrics against industry standards and recently sold businesses. This gives you a realistic picture of what your business is worth today, and a baseline to improve from.

## Why It Matters

Most business owners have no idea what their business is worth. Some massively overestimate based on revenue. Others massively underestimate because they have never thought about it. Knowing your valuation matters whether or not you plan to sell. It tells you whether you are building an asset or just running a job. It reveals which financial levers matter most. And if you ever do want to sell, retire, or bring in a partner, you need a number that is grounded in reality, not wishful thinking.

## How to Use This

Work through each valuation method, calculate a range, and then benchmark against comparable businesses in your industry.

---

## Method 1: Earnings Multiple (Most Common for Small Businesses)

- [ ] **Calculate your Seller's Discretionary Earnings (SDE)** - Start with net profit, then add back: owner's salary, owner's benefits, one-off expenses, depreciation, and interest. SDE represents the true economic benefit to an owner-operator.
- [ ] **Identify the typical earnings multiple for your industry** - Most small service businesses sell for 2-4x SDE. Product businesses may be higher. Look up benchmarks on BizBuySell, the BVR (Business Valuation Resources), or ask a business broker for recent comparables.
- [ ] **Multiply your SDE by the multiple range** - This gives you a valuation range. Example: SDE of £150,000 at a 2.5-3.5x multiple = £375,000 to £525,000.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: If you do not know your SDE, start with last year's net profit plus your total salary and benefits. That rough number is close enough for a first estimate.

## Method 2: Revenue Multiple

- [ ] **Take your annual revenue** - Use the last 12 months or an average of the last 3 years if revenue has been volatile.
- [ ] **Identify the typical revenue multiple for your industry** - Most small businesses sell for 0.5-1.5x revenue. High-growth, high-margin, or recurring-revenue businesses command higher multiples.
- [ ] **Multiply revenue by the multiple range** - This gives a second valuation estimate. Revenue multiples are less precise than earnings multiples but useful as a cross-check.

## Method 3: Asset-Based Valuation

- [ ] **List all tangible business assets** - Equipment, inventory, property, vehicles, cash reserves, accounts receivable.
- [ ] **List intangible assets** - Customer list, brand reputation, intellectual property, proprietary systems, contracts, trained team.
- [ ] **Subtract all liabilities** - Loans, debts, accounts payable, lease obligations, pending legal issues.
- [ ] **Calculate net asset value** - Total assets minus total liabilities. This is the floor value of your business. If your earnings-based valuation is lower than your asset value, something is wrong with profitability.

## Benchmark Against Comparables

- [ ] **Research 3-5 recently sold businesses in your industry and size range** - BizBuySell, business broker reports, industry association data, or direct conversations with brokers. Note their revenue, profit, and sale price.
- [ ] **Compare your key metrics** - Revenue growth rate, profit margin, customer concentration, recurring revenue percentage. Where do you sit relative to businesses that sold well?
- [ ] **Identify the gaps** - Which metrics are pulling your valuation down? These become your improvement priorities in the other actions in this playbook.
- [ ] **Document your valuation range** - Write down all three method results and your best estimate of current market value. This is your baseline.
- [ ] **Set a date to reassess in 12 months** - Track whether your actions are increasing business value over time.

## Expected Outcome

- Three independent valuation estimates using different methods
- A realistic valuation range based on real data, not guesswork
- Benchmark comparisons against similar businesses that have sold
- Clear gaps identified between current valuation and potential
- A documented baseline to measure improvement against

## Coach Notes

The first valuation is often a shock, in both directions. Some clients discover their business is worth less than they hoped because profits are low despite strong revenue. Others discover hidden value they did not appreciate, like a loyal customer base or strong recurring revenue.

The most important number is SDE, not revenue. Many business owners focus on top-line revenue, but buyers care about earnings. A £1M revenue business with £50K profit is worth less than a £500K revenue business with £200K profit. Help clients shift their focus.

Do not let clients get discouraged by a low first valuation. The whole point is to create a baseline and then use the remaining actions in this playbook to increase it systematically. The number today is just the starting point.
