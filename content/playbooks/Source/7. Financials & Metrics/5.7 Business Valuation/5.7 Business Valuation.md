## **What This Is**

Business valuation is not about preparing to sell tomorrow. It is about understanding what drives the value of your business today so you can engineer that value over time. When you know what buyers look for, every strategic decision becomes a valuation decision.

Most owners discover their business value at the point of sale — and that is too late. By then, the deal structure, timing, and price are largely fixed by choices you made years ago. This playbook changes that. You start thinking like a buyer while you still have time to build what they want to pay for.

This covers the fundamentals of how businesses are valued, what drives those valuations higher, and the specific areas you can work on to increase enterprise value. Not exit planning — that sits in the 5.2 Exit Strategy Playbook. This is about value creation.

---

## **What It Looks Like**

### **🔴 When this is broken**

You have no idea what your business is worth or what drives that value. Revenue feels like the only thing that matters. You make strategic decisions based on short-term cash needs instead of long-term value creation. The thought of being valued makes you nervous because you suspect it would be disappointing. You run the business for lifestyle, not enterprise value.

### **🟠 When this is OK**

You have a rough sense that your business might be worth a few times revenue, but you are not sure how that multiple gets decided. You know some things probably matter more than others for valuation but you could not list them. You make good business decisions but do not explicitly consider their impact on enterprise value. You suspect you could be doing more to build a valuable asset.

### **🟢 When this is working**

You know what your business is worth using multiple valuation methods and track how that value changes over time. Every strategic decision includes a consideration of its impact on enterprise value. Your revenue is predictable, your client base is diversified, and your operations run without you. You have built something that buyers would compete for — whether you plan to sell it or not.

## **Things to Think About**

How predictable is your revenue really? Buyers pay more for businesses they can forecast with confidence. One-off projects and lumpy sales create risk. Recurring contracts, retainers, and subscription elements create certainty. The more predictable your income, the higher the multiple buyers will apply to it.

Could the business operate without you for six months? Owner dependence kills valuations faster than anything else. If you are the main salesperson, key relationship holder, or decision maker for everything, you are not selling a business — you are selling a job. Transferability drives value.

Is your client base a strength or a risk? Twenty percent of revenue from your biggest client might feel great for cash flow, but it is a red flag for buyers. Concentration risk reduces valuations. Geographic, industry, and client diversification protects and increases value.

What do you actually own? Intellectual property, proprietary systems, brand recognition, exclusive partnerships — these are the assets that differentiate your business from competitors. If everything you do could be replicated easily, that limits what someone would pay to acquire it rather than build it themselves.

How does your business compare to others that have sold? Industry multiples exist for a reason. Knowing where your sector typically trades gives you a baseline, but the best businesses in any sector command premium multiples. What would put you in that premium category?

## **The Plays Inside This Playbook**

When you open this playbook in the Profit System, you will find:

Play 1: Valuation Methods & Benchmarking — Learn the three main ways businesses are valued and apply them to get a baseline for your business. Compare your metrics against industry standards and recently sold businesses to understand where you sit in the market.

Play 2: Revenue Predictability Audit — Analyse how much of your revenue is recurring, contracted, or otherwise predictable versus one-off or project-based. Build a plan to increase the predictable portion and reduce revenue volatility.

Play 3: Value Driver Assessment — Score your business across the key factors that drive valuation: financial performance, growth potential, market position, management team, systems, and scalability. Identify which drivers need strengthening.

Play 4: Transferability Review — Audit how dependent your business is on you personally and create a roadmap to reduce that dependency. Cover sales, operations, strategy, and key relationships so the business can thrive without you.

## **Quick Wins**

This week: Calculate your business value using the asset, income, and market approaches. The exact number matters less than understanding how each method works and what drives the differences.

This week: List your top 10 clients and calculate what percentage of revenue each represents. If any single client is above 15% of total revenue, that is your first risk to address.

This week: Write down all the intellectual property, systems, or unique advantages your business has that competitors could not easily replicate. If the list is short, that tells you where to focus.
