# Design Your Executive Dashboard

## What to Do

Build a single-screen view of your business that answers "are we winning?" in under 30 seconds. This is your CEO cockpit - the one page you check every morning before doing anything else. It covers revenue, profit, cash, customers, and team vital signs. Nothing more.

The goal is not to track everything. The goal is to track the right things so you can spot problems early and make faster decisions.

## Why It Matters

Most business owners either look at nothing (flying blind) or look at too much (drowning in data). Neither helps. An executive dashboard gives you the critical numbers in one place so you stop guessing and start knowing. When something goes wrong, you see it in days, not months. When something goes right, you can double down immediately.

## How to Use This

Work through the checklist below to decide what belongs on your dashboard, then build it using whatever tool you already have. A spreadsheet works fine. Don't buy software until the habit is established.

---

## Dashboard Design Checklist

- [ ] **Pick your dashboard tool** - Google Sheets, Excel, Notion, or your accounting software's built-in dashboards. Use what you already know. Fancy tools don't help if nobody opens them.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Write down the 3 questions you ask yourself every Monday** - These reveal what actually matters to you. "Did we hit target?" "How much cash do we have?" "Are clients happy?" Your dashboard should answer these instantly.
- [ ] **Choose your revenue metrics (pick 2-3)** - Revenue this month vs target, revenue vs same month last year, recurring revenue vs one-off. Don't track all of them. Pick the ones that tell you most.
- [ ] **Choose your profit metric** - Gross profit margin or net profit margin. One number. If it's dropping, you investigate. If it's stable or rising, you move on.
- [ ] **Add your cash position** - Cash in bank right now, plus projected cash in 4 weeks. Link this to your 13-week forecast if you have one.
- [ ] **Choose your customer metrics (pick 2-3)** - Active clients, new clients this month, client churn rate, average revenue per client, or NPS. Pick the ones that would keep you up at night if they moved in the wrong direction.
- [ ] **Add one team vital sign** - Team capacity, open roles, or a simple "team health" rating. Just one. You're not building an HR dashboard here.
- [ ] **Choose one leading indicator** - Something that predicts next month's results. Pipeline value, proposals sent, discovery calls booked, or website enquiries. Leading indicators give you time to act.
- [ ] **Set a target or benchmark for every metric** - A number without context is useless. Every metric needs a target, a previous period comparison, or an industry benchmark next to it.
- [ ] **Use traffic light colours** - Green (on track), amber (watch it), red (act now). Visual signals mean you can scan the whole dashboard in seconds.
- [ ] **Remove anything that doesn't trigger a decision** - If a metric being high or low wouldn't change what you do this week, it doesn't belong on your executive dashboard. Be ruthless.
- [ ] **Build the first version** - Get all your chosen metrics onto one screen. It will be rough. That's fine. A working ugly dashboard beats a beautiful one that never gets built.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Set it as your browser homepage or phone wallpaper** - Make it impossible to avoid. The best dashboard is the one you actually look at.

---

## Expected Outcome

- You can answer "how is the business doing?" in 30 seconds flat
- Problems show up in days, not months
- You stop relying on gut feel for major decisions
- Team meetings start with facts, not opinions
- You spend less time buried in spreadsheets because the important stuff is already visible

## Coach Notes

Start with fewer metrics, not more. Most owners want to track 20 things. Push them to pick 8-10 maximum. If it doesn't fit on one screen, it's too much. The first version will be wrong - that's expected. After 2-3 weeks of use, they'll know exactly which metrics they actually look at and which ones they skip. Refine then. The biggest risk is overdesign. Get something live in the first session, then improve it over time.
