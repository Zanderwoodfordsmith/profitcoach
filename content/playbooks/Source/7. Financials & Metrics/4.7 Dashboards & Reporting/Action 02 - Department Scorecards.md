# Create Department Scorecards

## What to Do

Build a focused dashboard for each key area of your business - sales, operations, marketing, and finance - so the people running those areas can see their own performance without waiting for you to tell them. Each scorecard should answer: "Is my area performing? Where do I need to focus?"

This is about pushing visibility down. When team members can see their own numbers, they take ownership. When they can't, everything flows back to you.

## Why It Matters

Your executive dashboard tells you how the whole business is doing. Department scorecards tell each team how their area is doing. Without them, the only person who knows whether marketing is working is you. The only person who knows whether operations is on track is you. That's not leadership, that's bottleneck creation. Scorecards create ownership, accountability, and faster decisions at every level.

## How to Use This

Pick the department that needs visibility most urgently (usually sales or operations). Build that scorecard first. Then repeat for other areas. Don't try to build all four at once.

---

## Step 1: Choose Your Departments

- [ ] **List the 3-5 key areas of your business** - Common ones: Sales, Marketing, Operations/Fulfilment, Finance. Yours might be different. Use whatever matches how you actually run things.
- [ ] **Assign an owner for each area** - Who is responsible for this area's results? If the answer is "me" for everything, that's a problem this action will help surface.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Start with the area that causes you the most stress** - That's where visibility will have the biggest immediate impact.

## Step 2: Design Each Scorecard

For each department, work through these items:

- [ ] **Pick 4-6 metrics that define success for this area** - Sales might be: pipeline value, proposals sent, close rate, revenue booked, average deal size. Operations might be: on-time delivery, client satisfaction, capacity utilisation, rework rate.
- [ ] **Include at least one leading indicator** - A metric that predicts future performance, not just reports past performance. Sales: discovery calls booked. Marketing: qualified leads. Operations: project backlog.
- [ ] **Set targets for every metric** - The department owner should have input here. Targets imposed from above get ignored. Targets agreed together get chased.
- [ ] **Choose an update frequency** - Weekly for fast-moving areas (sales, marketing). Monthly for slower areas (finance, HR). Match the rhythm to the reality.
- [ ] **Decide who updates it** - The department owner, not you. If they won't update their own scorecard, that tells you something important about ownership.

## Step 3: Connect to Your Executive Dashboard

- [ ] **Link the top-line number from each scorecard to your executive dashboard** - Your executive view shows the summary. The scorecard shows the detail. Two layers, not one giant mess.
- [ ] **Set up a simple review rhythm** - Each department reviews their scorecard weekly. You review the executive dashboard daily and do a deeper dive with department owners monthly.
- [ ] **Document what "red" means for each metric** - At what point should someone escalate to you? Define this upfront so people know when to flag and when to handle it themselves.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Send the first scorecard to the department owner today** - Don't wait until they're all perfect. One working scorecard this week beats four perfect ones next month.

---

## Expected Outcome

- Each key area has its own visible performance tracker
- Team members can see how they're doing without asking you
- Problems surface faster because the people closest to the work spot them first
- Your executive dashboard gets cleaner because detail lives in department scorecards
- Accountability conversations shift from "I think" to "the numbers show"

## Coach Notes

The most common pushback is "we're too small for this" or "everyone already knows how things are going." Challenge that. Ask: "If your sales person left tomorrow, how would you know within a week whether their replacement was performing?" If the answer involves gut feel, they need a scorecard. Start with one department. Get it working. Let the owner experience the benefit. Then the other departments will want their own.
