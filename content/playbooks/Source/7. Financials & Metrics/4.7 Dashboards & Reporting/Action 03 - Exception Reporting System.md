# Set Up Your Exception Reporting System

## What to Do

Create a set of automatic alerts that flag when key metrics move outside normal ranges. Instead of checking every number every day, you get notified only when something needs attention. Think of it as a smoke alarm for your business - it stays quiet until there's a problem.

This builds on your executive dashboard and department scorecards. Those show you the current picture. Exception reporting taps you on the shoulder when something changes unexpectedly.

## Why It Matters

You can't watch every number every day. And you shouldn't have to. The businesses that catch problems early aren't the ones with the most dashboards. They're the ones with the best alerts. A cash balance dropping below a threshold, a client churn spike, a sudden drop in sales pipeline - these are the signals that need immediate attention. Everything else can wait for the weekly review.

## How to Use This

Work through the steps in order. You're defining what "normal" looks like, setting boundaries around it, and building alerts for when things go outside those boundaries.

---

## Step 1: Define Your Critical Metrics

- [ ] **List the 6-8 metrics that would cause real damage if they moved sharply in the wrong direction** - Cash balance, monthly revenue, client count, pipeline value, gross margin, debtor days. Focus on the ones where a week's delay in spotting a problem costs real money.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Start with cash** - Set an alert for when your bank balance drops below your minimum comfort level (typically 1-2 months of fixed costs). This alone prevents most cash crises.
- [ ] **Exclude vanity metrics** - Website traffic, social media followers, email open rates. These rarely need exception alerts. Focus on metrics tied directly to revenue, profit, cash, and customers.

## Step 2: Set Your Thresholds

- [ ] **For each metric, define "normal range"** - Use the last 6-12 months of data. What's the typical range? Revenue might normally be between £40K and £55K per month. Client churn might be 2-4% monthly.
- [ ] **Set upper and lower alert boundaries** - When should the alarm go off? Too low is obvious (cash below £20K). But too high can also signal problems (expenses suddenly 30% above normal could mean an error or unauthorised spending).
- [ ] **Define three alert levels** - Watch (approaching the boundary), Warning (at the boundary), Critical (well past the boundary). Not everything is equally urgent.
- [ ] **Write a one-line "so what" for each alert** - "Cash below £15K" is the alert. "Defer all non-essential spending and chase overdue invoices immediately" is the response. Link the alert to the action.

## Step 3: Build the Alerts

- [ ] **Choose your alert method** - Email notifications from your accounting software, conditional formatting in a spreadsheet that turns cells red, Slack alerts, or even a simple weekly checklist where someone checks each threshold manually. Match the tool to your team's habits.
- [ ] **Set up automated alerts where possible** - Xero, QuickBooks, and most CRM tools can send email alerts based on thresholds. Use them. Manual checking works but gets dropped when things get busy.
- [ ] **Assign an owner for each alert** - Who receives it? Who acts on it? If all alerts go to you, you've just built a smarter bottleneck. Push appropriate alerts to department owners.
- [ ] **Test each alert** - Deliberately trigger one (or simulate it) to make sure the notification actually arrives and the right person receives it.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Set up your top 3 alerts this week** - Cash balance, monthly revenue vs target, and client count. These three cover most of what matters. Add more later.

---

## Expected Outcome

- You stop checking every number every day because the system tells you when something needs attention
- Problems get caught in days, not weeks or months
- Team members can respond to alerts in their area without waiting for you
- Your mental load drops because you trust the system to flag what matters
- False alarms decrease over time as you refine your thresholds

## Coach Notes

The biggest mistake is setting too many alerts with thresholds that are too tight. If the system cries wolf every week, everyone ignores it. Start with 3-5 alerts on genuinely critical metrics. Widen the thresholds slightly so alerts only fire when something genuinely unusual happens. You can always tighten them later. For clients without automated tools, a simple weekly checklist where someone manually checks each threshold works perfectly well. The system matters more than the technology.
