# Install Your Weekly Review Rhythm

## What to Do

Set up a structured weekly meeting where you and your team review dashboard data, discuss what's changed, and decide what to do about it. This is the bridge between having data and actually using it. Without a review rhythm, dashboards become wallpaper - nice to look at, but nobody acts on them.

Pick a fixed day and time. Follow a consistent agenda. Keep it short. Make decisions, not just observations.

## Why It Matters

Dashboards don't make decisions. People do. But only if they look at the data regularly and talk about what it means. A weekly review rhythm turns numbers into action. It catches small problems before they become big ones. It keeps everyone aligned on what matters this week. And it creates a culture where decisions are based on evidence, not gut feel or whoever shouts loudest.

## How to Use This

Work through the setup steps, then use the meeting agenda template every week. The first few meetings will feel clunky. By week 4 or 5, the team will wonder how they ever ran without it.

---

## Step 1: Set Up the Meeting

- [ ] **Pick a fixed day and time** - Monday morning (sets priorities for the week) or Friday afternoon (reviews the week just gone) both work. Pick one and protect it. This meeting doesn't get moved for client calls.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Block it in everyone's calendar right now** - Recurring, 30-45 minutes, every week. Making it recurring removes the "should we meet this week?" question.
- [ ] **Decide who attends** - You plus anyone who owns a department scorecard or key metric. Keep it tight. More than 6 people and it becomes a broadcast, not a discussion.
- [ ] **Assign a dashboard updater** - Someone updates all dashboards and scorecards 24 hours before the meeting. Data should be current when the meeting starts.
- [ ] **Create a shared agenda document** - Same structure every week. Consistency is what makes this work. Use the template below.

## Step 2: Build Your Meeting Agenda

- [ ] **Section 1: Wins (5 minutes)** - What went well this week? One highlight per person. This sets the tone and ensures good news doesn't get buried.
- [ ] **Section 2: Scorecard review (10 minutes)** - Walk through the executive dashboard. Only discuss metrics that are amber or red. Green means it's working - move on.
- [ ] **Section 3: Exceptions and alerts (5 minutes)** - Any alerts triggered this week? What happened, what was done, is it resolved?
- [ ] **Section 4: Stuck items (10 minutes)** - What's blocked? What needs a decision? This is where the real value is. Problems that would fester for weeks get resolved in minutes.
- [ ] **Section 5: Priorities for next week (5 minutes)** - Each person states their top 1-3 priorities. Written down, visible to all. Next week's meeting starts by checking whether these got done.
- [ ] **Section 6: Action items (2 minutes)** - Who is doing what by when? Capture every action item with a name and a deadline. No orphan actions.

## Step 3: Make It Stick

- [ ] **Run the first meeting yourself** - Set the standard. Keep it tight. Cut off tangents with "let's take that offline." Show the team what a good review meeting looks like.
- [ ] **Rotate the meeting facilitator after 4 weeks** - Once the format is established, let others run it. This builds leadership and stops the meeting dying if you're away.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **End every meeting with "what's the one thing we need to get right this week?"** - Forces prioritisation and gives everyone a shared focus.
- [ ] **Review and refine the format quarterly** - After 12 meetings, you'll know what works and what's dead time. Cut what doesn't add value. Add what's missing.

---

## Expected Outcome

- Data turns into decisions every single week
- Problems get caught and addressed within 7 days, not 7 weeks
- Team alignment improves because everyone sees the same numbers
- Accountability becomes natural - people deliver because they know they'll be asked
- You spend less time in ad-hoc check-ins because the weekly review covers the important stuff

## Coach Notes

The first 3-4 meetings are the danger zone. They'll feel slow, awkward, and like a waste of time. Push through. By meeting 5 or 6, the team starts bringing issues to the meeting instead of interrupting you throughout the week. That's the turning point. The most common failure mode is letting the meeting run too long. Be strict on time. If a topic needs more than 5 minutes of discussion, take it offline with the relevant people. The weekly review is for flagging and deciding, not for deep problem-solving.
