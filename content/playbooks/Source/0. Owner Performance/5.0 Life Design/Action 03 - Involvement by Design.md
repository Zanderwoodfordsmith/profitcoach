# Involvement by Design

## What to Do

Define your ideal relationship with the business: how much time you spend in it, in what capacity, and on what timeline you want to get there. Turn "I should be involved in everything" into "I choose to be involved in these specific areas because they align with my strengths and my life vision."

Map every area of the business where you are currently involved, then decide deliberately which to keep, which to hand over, and which to phase out. Set a 12-month target for your ideal involvement level and work backward to a quarterly plan.

## Why It Matters

Most owners' involvement in their business is accidental. It is the accumulation of years of being the only person who could do things, combined with habits that never got questioned. At Level 5, you have the team and systems to choose. But choice requires a deliberate design - otherwise you drift back into doing everything.

Michael Gerber's insight still applies: the owner should work on the business, not in it. But at Level 5, the question is sharper. It is not just "on versus in" but "which parts of 'on' do I want to keep?" Some owners love strategy. Some love product. Some love client relationships. The answer is personal, and it should be intentional.

## How to Use This

Work through the involvement mapping first to see reality clearly. Then design your ideal involvement model and build a transition plan to get there.

---

## Current Involvement Map

- [ ] **List every recurring commitment you have in the business** - Meetings, reviews, approvals, calls, decisions. Everything that shows up in your calendar or inbox regularly.
- [ ] **For each commitment, note: hours per week, why you are involved, and whether anyone else could do it** - Be brutally honest about the "why." If the answer is "because I always have," that is not a reason.
- [ ] **Calculate your total weekly hours in the business** - Include the invisible hours: thinking about problems at dinner, checking emails on weekends, calls during holidays.
- [ ] **Categorise each activity: Strategic (only I can do this), Transitional (I am training someone), or Legacy (I am still doing this out of habit)** - Most owners find 40-60% of their time falls in the Legacy category.

## Design Your Ideal Involvement

- [ ] **Define your ideal weekly hours** - Be specific. Not "less" but a number. 20 hours? 10? 5? Match this to your Life Vision Canvas.
- [ ] **Choose your ideal areas of involvement** - Where do you add the most value and get the most energy? Strategy, product, key relationships, culture? Pick no more than 3.
- [ ] **Define what "done" looks like for areas you are leaving** - Who takes over? What authority do they need? What does the handover process look like?
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Identify one meeting you attend out of habit and stop attending this week** - Tell the team you trust them to handle it. See what happens.

## Transition Plan

- [ ] **Set a 12-month target for your involvement level** - Where do you want to be in one year? Write it down as a specific weekly schedule.
- [ ] **Break it into quarterly milestones** - Q1: hand over X. Q2: step back from Y. Q3: delegate Z. Q4: operating at target level.
- [ ] **Identify capability gaps** - Where does the team need development before you can step back? Start building that capability now, not when you are ready to leave.
- [ ] **Build check-in rhythms that replace your presence** - Weekly dashboards, monthly reviews, quarterly strategy sessions. You stay informed without being involved in execution.
- [ ] **Communicate the plan to your leadership team** - They need to know this is intentional, not abandonment. Frame it as trust and growth for them, not just freedom for you.

---

## Expected Outcome

- Written map of current involvement with honest assessment of what is necessary versus habitual
- Clear definition of ideal involvement across no more than 3 focus areas
- 12-month transition plan with quarterly milestones
- Team prepared and capable of operating with less owner involvement
- Owner spending time on what they choose, not what they feel they should

## Coach Notes

This is one of the most identity-challenging actions in the entire system. Many owners intellectually want to step back but emotionally struggle because the business is who they are. If a client resists, explore the identity question: "If you are not the person running this business day-to-day, who are you?"

Watch for the owner who designs an ideal involvement model that is still 50 hours a week but just in "different" areas. That is not redesign, that is rearranging. Challenge them on whether they are genuinely reducing or just reshuffling.

The transition plan must include capability building for the team. Owners who step back without preparing their people create a vacuum that pulls them right back in within weeks.
