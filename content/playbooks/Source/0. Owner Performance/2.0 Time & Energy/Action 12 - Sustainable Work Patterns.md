# Sustainable Work Patterns

## What to Do

Design work rhythms you can maintain for years, not just weeks. This covers optimal work-to-break ratios, managing energy cycles across the day and week, building in genuine recovery time, and creating a sustainable pace that does not sacrifice your health, relationships, or long-term performance for short-term output.

## Why It Matters

Working 60-hour weeks might feel heroic, but it is not sustainable and the research is clear: beyond 50 hours per week, productivity per hour drops sharply. After 55 hours, it falls off a cliff. You are not getting more done - you are just getting more tired. Sustainable work patterns let you maintain high output over years rather than sprinting for months and crashing. The owners who build the best businesses are the ones who are still sharp and energised at year five, not the ones who burned brightest in year one.

## How to Use This

Review your current work patterns honestly, then design a sustainable rhythm using the steps below. This works best after you have completed the earlier actions in this playbook (especially the Power Plan and Energy-Based Calendar).

---

## Assess Your Current Patterns

- [ ] Calculate your average working hours per week over the last month (include evening emails, weekend work, and "just checking" phone time)
- [ ] Rate your current pace on sustainability: could you maintain this for 5 more years without health or relationship consequences? Be honest.
- [ ] Identify the patterns that are unsustainable: late nights, weekend work, no breaks, skipped meals, cancelled personal commitments
- [ ] Note what triggers unsustainable stretches: deadlines, staff issues, busy seasons, or just habit?

## Design Your Sustainable Rhythm

### Daily Rhythm

- [ ] Set a maximum working day length (recommendation: 8-9 hours including breaks)
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Set a hard stop time for today and honour it, no matter what. One day of proof that the business survives.
- [ ] Build in breaks using the 90/20 pattern: 90 minutes of focused work, followed by a genuine 15-20 minute break
- [ ] Protect a proper lunch break (30+ minutes away from your desk)
- [ ] Create a "shutdown ritual" at end of day: review what you accomplished, note what is next for tomorrow, and close everything. Work is done.

### Weekly Rhythm

- [ ] Set your core working days (typically Monday-Friday) and protect at least one full weekend day with zero work
- [ ] Designate one day per week as your "light day" - shorter hours, less intensity, more thinking and planning
- [ ] Build in a weekly review session (Friday afternoon works well): what went right, what needs adjusting, what is the priority for next week
- [ ] Schedule your personal priorities (exercise, family, hobbies) as non-negotiable blocks, not afterthoughts

### Monthly and Quarterly Rhythm

- [ ] Plan one genuine recovery day per month: no work, no email, no "just checking" - proper rest
- [ ] Build in a quarterly review day: step back, assess progress, adjust your 90-day plan, reconnect with your goals
- [ ] Plan holidays in advance and protect them from work creep - out-of-office on, laptop left at home
- [ ] After any intense period (product launch, busy season, difficult project), schedule a deliberate recovery period

## Build the Boundaries

- [ ] Define your "rules of engagement" and share them with your team: when you are available, when you are not, and what constitutes a genuine emergency
- [ ] Remove work email and Slack from your phone, or use scheduled focus modes that block them outside working hours
- [ ] Set expectations with clients about response times: "We respond within 24 hours on business days" is professional, not slow
- [ ] Have the conversation with your family about your new boundaries - their support makes it stick

## Track Sustainability

- [ ] Rate your energy and satisfaction weekly (1-5 scale)
- [ ] Track your actual vs. planned working hours each week
- [ ] Note when you break your own rules and what caused it
- [ ] After 30 days, assess: are the patterns holding? Where do you slip? What needs adjusting?

---

## Expected Outcome

- A designed work rhythm that protects your energy, health, and relationships
- Clear daily, weekly, and quarterly patterns that support long-term performance
- Boundaries communicated to team, clients, and family
- Measurable reduction in working hours without reduction in output
- A sustainable pace you can maintain for years, not just weeks

## Coach Notes

This is often the hardest action in the playbook because it requires owners to confront the story they tell themselves: "I have to work this hard or things will fall apart." Challenge this directly but compassionately. Ask: "If you had a health scare and were forced to work 30 hours a week, what would you stop doing?" That answer reveals what is actually optional. The biggest risk is clients agreeing in the session and then reverting by Wednesday. Weekly accountability on the hard stop time and the weekend protection is essential. Celebrate small wins: "You left at 5:30 three days this week. What happened? The business survived."
