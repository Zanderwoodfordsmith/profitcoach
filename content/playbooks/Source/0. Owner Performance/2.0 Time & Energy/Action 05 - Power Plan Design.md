# Power Plan Design

## What to Do

Design your ideal week broken into 15-minute segments. This is not just time-blocking - it is building the architecture of your perfect work rhythm, based on your energy patterns, business priorities, and the data from your time tracking exercises. The Power Plan becomes your weekly template that you hold yourself accountable to.

## Why It Matters

Without a designed week, your calendar gets filled by other people's priorities. Meetings expand. Admin creeps in. Strategic work gets pushed to "when I have time" which means never. A Power Plan gives you a default schedule that protects your most important work and puts everything else in its proper place. You will not follow it perfectly every week, but having the template means you always know what you are drifting from.

## How to Use This

Use your data from the Time Scan Tracker (Action 2) and the 15-Minute Time Tracker (Action 3) to understand your current patterns. Then design your ideal week using the steps below. Print it out or set it as your default calendar view.

## Design Your Power Plan

### Step 1: Map Your Non-Negotiables

- [ ] Block your personal non-negotiables first: family commitments, exercise, meals, sleep schedule
- [ ] Block any fixed business commitments: regular team meetings, client calls with set times
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Protect your mornings - do not schedule meetings before 10am for the first two weeks and see what happens to your output

### Step 2: Place Your Strategic Blocks

- [ ] Identify your peak energy hours from your time tracking data (usually morning for most people)
- [ ] Block 2-3 sessions of 90+ minutes per week for deep strategic work during peak hours
- [ ] Label these blocks specifically: "Product development", "Business planning", "Growth strategy" - not just "deep work"
- [ ] Make these blocks recurring and treat them as unmovable as a client meeting

### Step 3: Structure Your Operational Time

- [ ] Batch similar tasks together: all calls on one or two days, all admin in one block, all email in defined windows
- [ ] Set specific email/communication windows (e.g., 11am and 4pm, 30 minutes each) rather than checking constantly
- [ ] Place operational work in your medium-energy hours, not your peak hours
- [ ] Build in 15-minute transition buffers between different types of work

### Step 4: Build in Recovery

- [ ] Schedule a 10-15 minute break between every 90-minute block
- [ ] Block a proper lunch break (not at your desk, not checking email)
- [ ] Leave one afternoon per week unscheduled as buffer for overflow and unexpected demands
- [ ] Build in a Friday review block (30 minutes) to assess how the week went vs. the plan

### Step 5: Set Your Boundaries

- [ ] Define your start time and hard stop time for each day
- [ ] Decide which days are meeting-free or meeting-light
- [ ] Set rules for what can override a strategic block (real emergencies only, not "urgent" emails)
- [ ] Communicate your Power Plan to your team so they know when you are available and when you are not

## Weekly Review

- [ ] Every Friday: rate the week 1-5 on how closely you followed the Power Plan
- [ ] Identify what pulled you off plan and whether it was justified
- [ ] Adjust next week's plan based on what you learned
- [ ] Celebrate one thing you protected that would have been lost without the plan

---

## Expected Outcome

- A complete weekly template that reflects your priorities, not other people's demands
- Strategic work protected during your peak energy hours
- Operational work batched and contained rather than spread across the entire week
- Clear boundaries that your team understands and respects
- A weekly rhythm you can hold yourself accountable to

## Coach Notes

This is one of the most transformative exercises for Level 2 owners. The biggest obstacle is not designing the plan - it is following it. Expect clients to struggle in week 1-2 as old habits pull them back. Hold them accountable to the Friday review. Common failure modes: scheduling strategic blocks but then accepting meetings over them, checking email outside the defined windows, and not communicating boundaries to the team. A good coaching question: "What did you protect this week that you would have lost without your Power Plan?"
