# Behaviour Standards Matrix

## What to Do

Create a matrix that maps specific, observable behaviours you will model as a leader and expect from your team. This takes your leadership philosophy and your company values from vague words on a wall to measurable actions that everyone can see, recognise, and hold each other accountable for. Each value or standard gets translated into "what this looks like" and "what this does not look like" in daily practice.

## Why It Matters

Patrick Lencioni's work shows that most companies have values, but few have behaviours attached to those values. "Integrity" means nothing until you define what integrity looks like on a Tuesday afternoon when a client asks you to cut corners. Vague values create inconsistency because everyone interprets them differently. A behaviour standards matrix removes ambiguity. Your team knows exactly what good looks like, and you have a fair, consistent basis for recognition and accountability.

## How to Use This

Start with your top 3-5 values or leadership principles from your Leadership Philosophy. For each one, define specific behaviours. Then share the matrix with your team and use it in hiring, reviews, and daily management.

---

## Define Observable Behaviours

- [ ] **List your 3-5 core values or standards** - Pull these from your Leadership Philosophy action or define them fresh. Keep the list short. Five values with clear behaviours beat ten values nobody remembers.
- [ ] **For each value, write 3-4 "looks like" behaviours** - These must be observable. Not "be respectful" but "respond to internal messages within 4 hours" or "raise concerns directly with the person, not behind their back." If you cannot see it or measure it, it is too vague.
- [ ] **For each value, write 2-3 "does not look like" behaviours** - Defining what you do not want is just as important. "Ownership does not look like blaming other departments when a deadline is missed." This removes grey areas.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Pick one value and complete the full behaviour mapping now** - Do not wait until you have time for all five. One complete entry is more useful than five empty ones.

## Build the Matrix

- [ ] **Create a simple table format** - Value in column one. "Looks like" in column two. "Does not look like" in column three. Keep it to one page. If it does not fit on one page, you have too many items.
- [ ] **Rate yourself honestly against each behaviour** - Before asking anything of your team, check whether you are modelling these behaviours consistently. Your team will mirror what you do, not what you say.
- [ ] **Identify your biggest personal gap** - Where is the largest distance between what you expect and what you actually do? This is your priority.
- [ ] **Add frequency or context where helpful** - Some behaviours are daily (e.g., "acknowledge good work when you see it"). Others are situational (e.g., "admit mistakes openly when they happen"). Adding context makes the standard more practical.

## Team Implementation

- [ ] **Share the matrix with your team** - Walk them through it. Explain why each behaviour matters. Give them space to ask questions and push back. If they helped create it, they will own it more.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **In your next team meeting, recognise someone who demonstrated one of these behaviours** - Public recognition linked to specific behaviours reinforces the standards immediately.
- [ ] **Use the matrix in hiring** - Add behavioural interview questions based on each standard. "Tell me about a time you..." questions reveal patterns better than hypotheticals.
- [ ] **Use the matrix in performance conversations** - Reference specific behaviours, not vague judgements. "You consistently raise concerns directly - that is exactly what we mean by transparency" is far more useful than "you are doing great."
- [ ] **Review and update quarterly** - As your business evolves, some behaviours become more or less important. Keep the matrix current.

---

## Expected Outcome

- Every team member knows exactly what good behaviour looks like in your business
- Performance conversations become easier because you have a shared reference point
- Hiring improves because you are screening for specific behaviours, not just skills
- Culture becomes self-reinforcing as the team holds each other to clear standards
- You model the behaviours you expect, which builds trust and credibility

## Coach Notes

The biggest trap is creating a matrix that is aspirational but not honest. If the owner is not currently modelling the behaviours they want to mandate, the team will see it instantly and the whole exercise loses credibility. Start by helping the client audit their own behaviour against the matrix. The second trap is overcomplication. Push for simplicity. Three values with clear behaviours will change a culture faster than eight values with vague descriptions. If a client struggles to define observable behaviours, ask them to describe a specific moment when someone on their team did something that made them proud, or something that frustrated them. Real stories produce better behaviour definitions than abstract thinking.
