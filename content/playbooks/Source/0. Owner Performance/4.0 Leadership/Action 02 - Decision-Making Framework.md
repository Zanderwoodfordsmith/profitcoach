# Decision-Making Framework

## What to Do

Build a structured process for evaluating strategic decisions so you stop relying on gut feel alone. The goal is a repeatable framework that you (and eventually your team) can use to make better decisions faster, with less second-guessing. This is not about removing judgement. It is about adding discipline to your judgement so the quality of your decisions improves consistently.

## Why It Matters

Poor decisions rarely come from stupidity. They come from speed, emotion, incomplete information, or lack of process. Keith Cunningham calls this "the cost of being wrong" - most business owners underestimate how much a single bad decision costs when you factor in time, money, opportunity cost, and team morale. A decision-making framework does not guarantee perfect decisions, but it dramatically reduces avoidable mistakes. It also allows you to teach your team how to think, not just what to do.

## How to Use This

Work through the framework design once, then test it on your next three decisions before rolling it out to your team. A framework you do not use is worthless. Build it, test it, refine it.

---

## Framework Design

- [ ] **Define your decision categories** - Not all decisions need the same process. Sort them into three tiers: (1) Reversible, low-cost decisions - decide fast and move on. (2) Significant but recoverable decisions - take a day, consult one person. (3) High-stakes, hard-to-reverse decisions - use the full framework below.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Write down the last three decisions you agonised over** - Were they actually Tier 3, or were you overthinking Tier 1 decisions? Most owners spend too long on small calls and too little on big ones.
- [ ] **Create your decision criteria checklist** - For Tier 3 decisions, define 5-7 evaluation criteria. Examples: alignment with strategy, financial impact, reversibility, team capacity, customer impact, opportunity cost.
- [ ] **Assign weightings to each criterion** - Not all criteria matter equally. Financial impact might be weighted 3x higher than team preference. Define this before you need it so emotion does not distort priorities in the moment.

## Decision Process

- [ ] **Write the decision as a clear question** - "Should we..." forces clarity. Vague framing leads to vague thinking. If you cannot state the decision in one sentence, you have not defined it yet.
- [ ] **List what you know and what you do not know** - Separate facts from assumptions. Most bad decisions happen because assumptions are treated as facts. Identify the biggest unknown and decide whether you can resolve it before committing.
- [ ] **Identify who is affected and consult them** - Not for consensus. For perspective. The person closest to the problem often sees things you miss.
- [ ] **Define the "kill criteria" before you start** - What would make you reverse or abandon this decision? Setting this in advance prevents the sunk cost trap where you keep going because you have already invested, not because it is working.
- [ ] **Set a decision deadline** - Every decision needs a "decide by" date. Without one, analysis paralysis wins. For most business decisions, 80% of the information available now is enough.

## Team Rollout

- [ ] **Teach your team the three tiers** - Help them understand which decisions they can make alone (Tier 1), which need a quick check-in (Tier 2), and which come to you (Tier 3).
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Identify three decisions your team currently escalates that they could handle themselves** - Push Tier 1 decisions down immediately. This frees your time and builds their confidence.
- [ ] **Create a simple one-page decision template** - For Tier 3 decisions, give your team a template: What is the decision? What are the options? What do we recommend and why? What are the risks? This trains them to think before they ask.
- [ ] **Review decisions monthly** - Look back at significant decisions from the past month. What went well? What would you change? This builds organisational learning.

---

## Expected Outcome

- Faster decisions on small things because you stop overthinking them
- Better decisions on big things because you apply a consistent process
- Your team starts making more decisions independently because they understand your criteria
- Less decision fatigue because the framework does the heavy lifting
- A track record you can review and learn from

## Coach Notes

The most common issue is owners who want to apply the full framework to every decision. This creates bottlenecks and frustration. Push hard on the three-tier model - the biggest productivity gain comes from deciding faster on Tier 1 items, not from perfecting Tier 3 analysis. Watch for clients who use "I need more information" as a delay tactic. Help them distinguish between genuine information gaps and fear of commitment. If a client consistently makes good gut decisions, do not overengineer the process. Some owners have strong intuition. The framework is a safety net, not a replacement for judgement.
