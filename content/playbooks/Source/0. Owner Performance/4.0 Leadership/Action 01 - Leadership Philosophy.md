# Leadership Philosophy

## What to Do

Write a one-page leadership philosophy that captures your core beliefs about how you lead, what you stand for, and what you will not tolerate. This is not a mission statement for the wall. It is an internal compass that guides every decision you make, every hire, every difficult conversation, and every trade-off. Think of it as your leadership operating manual - the thing you come back to when the pressure is on and clarity is hard to find.

## Why It Matters

Most business owners lead reactively. They respond to whatever is loudest, adjust their style depending on who is in the room, and make inconsistent decisions because they have never defined what consistency looks like. A written leadership philosophy forces you to confront what you actually believe, not what sounds good. It also gives your team something predictable to work with. People perform better when they know what their leader values, rewards, and will not accept.

## How to Use This

Block 60-90 minutes of uninterrupted time. Answer each question honestly. Do not write what you think you should believe. Write what you actually believe. Then refine it into a clear, usable document.

---

## Core Beliefs

- [ ] **Write down 3-5 things you believe about leadership** - What have you learned from the best (and worst) leaders you have worked with? What principles do you come back to again and again?
- [ ] **Define what "good" looks like in your business** - What does a well-run team look like to you? What does a good day feel like? What would you see, hear, and measure?
- [ ] **Identify your leadership strengths** - Where do you naturally excel as a leader? Be specific. "Good communicator" is too vague. "I give clear, direct feedback in the moment" is useful.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Write one sentence that captures your leadership identity** - "I am the kind of leader who..." Finish that sentence. This becomes your anchor.

## Non-Negotiable Standards

- [ ] **List 3-5 behaviours you will always model** - These are the things your team should expect from you every single day. If you would not want your team to copy a behaviour, it should not be on this list.
- [ ] **List 3-5 things you will never tolerate** - Be specific. "Dishonesty" is a start. "Hiding bad news until it becomes a crisis" is better. These become your hard boundaries.
- [ ] **Define how you will handle it when standards are broken** - What happens the first time? The second time? Clarity here prevents inconsistency later.

## Communication Commitments

- [ ] **Write down how you will communicate expectations** - Will you tell people directly? Put it in writing? Both? How will you make sure people know what you expect before you judge them for missing it?
- [ ] **Define your feedback approach** - How quickly will you give feedback? Privately or publicly? What is your style - direct, coaching, questioning?
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Identify one communication habit you will start this week** - Pick the easiest one and build from there.

## Review and Accountability

- [ ] **Share your philosophy with at least one person** - A business partner, coach, or trusted team member. Saying it out loud makes it real.
- [ ] **Set a quarterly review date** - Your philosophy should evolve as you grow. Review it every quarter and ask: "Am I living this?"
- [ ] **Ask your team for honest feedback** - Do they experience you the way you intend? The gap between intent and impact is where the real growth happens.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A written document that captures your leadership beliefs in your own words
- Clear non-negotiable standards that you and your team can point to
- Greater consistency in how you lead, especially under pressure
- A reference point for hiring, firing, promoting, and having difficult conversations
- Reduced decision fatigue because your principles do the filtering

## Coach Notes

This action often surfaces uncomfortable truths. Many business owners discover a gap between how they think they lead and how they actually lead. That gap is the coaching opportunity. Do not let them write an aspirational document that sounds impressive but does not reflect reality. Push for honesty first, then help them close the gap between who they are and who they want to be. If a client struggles to articulate their beliefs, ask about specific moments - "Tell me about a time you were proud of how you handled something" or "When did you let yourself down as a leader?" Stories reveal philosophy faster than abstract thinking.
