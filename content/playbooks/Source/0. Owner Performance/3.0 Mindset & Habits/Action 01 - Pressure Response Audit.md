# Pressure Response Audit

## What to Do

Map how you currently react when things go wrong or stakes are high. Identify the early warning signs that you are entering a pressure state, document your habitual patterns (good and bad), and pre-decide how you will respond in common high-pressure scenarios. This is about replacing autopilot with intentional behaviour.

Most owners never examine their pressure responses. They just react, then deal with the fallout. This action turns unconscious patterns into conscious choices.

## Why It Matters

Your worst decisions happen under pressure. Snapping at a team member, agreeing to a bad deal, avoiding a hard conversation, micromanaging when you should delegate. These reactions feel automatic, but they are habits. And habits can be changed once you can see them clearly.

The cost of poor pressure responses compounds. One reactive email damages trust. One avoided conversation becomes a six-month problem. One panic decision costs tens of thousands. Building awareness of your patterns is the first step to upgrading them.

## How to Use This

Work through the sections below over one focused session. Be brutally honest. The value comes from accuracy, not from painting a flattering picture.

---

## Identify Your Pressure Triggers

- [ ] **List your top 5 recurring pressure situations** - Cash flow drops, team conflict, client complaints, missed deadlines, difficult sales conversations. What reliably puts you on edge?
- [ ] **Rate each trigger 1-10 for intensity** - Not all pressure is equal. Some situations mildly irritate you. Others send you into full fight-or-flight mode.
- [ ] **Note which triggers are increasing in frequency** - As the business grows, which pressure points are getting worse, not better? These need attention first.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Identify your single biggest trigger right now** - The one situation that most reliably causes you to react poorly. Name it specifically.

## Map Your Current Response Patterns

- [ ] **For each trigger, write down what you actually do** - Not what you wish you did. What you really do. Withdraw? Micromanage? Raise your voice? Avoid? Work 16-hour days? Pour a drink?
- [ ] **Identify your early warning signs** - What happens in your body and behaviour before the reaction? Tight chest, short replies, checking phone constantly, poor sleep, skipping meals. These are your alerts.
- [ ] **Document who gets the worst of your pressure behaviour** - Partner? Team? Clients? Yourself? Be specific. This creates accountability.
- [ ] **Note which responses occasionally work well** - Not all your pressure reactions are bad. Some people get sharper under pressure in certain areas. Keep those.

## Design Better Alternatives

- [ ] **For your top 3 triggers, write a pre-decided response** - "When X happens, I will do Y instead of Z." Make it specific enough that you can follow it in the moment without thinking.
- [ ] **Build in a pause mechanism** - The gap between stimulus and response is where change lives. Choose a specific technique: leave the room, take three breaths, write before speaking, sleep on it.
- [ ] **Create an accountability check** - Tell someone (coach, partner, trusted team member) about the patterns you want to change. Ask them to flag when they see the old behaviour.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Set a phone reminder for your highest-pressure time of day** - A simple "Pause before reacting" reminder at the time you are most likely to be triggered.
- [ ] **Schedule a 30-day review** - Behaviour change takes repetition. Put a date in the calendar to review which patterns have improved and which still need work.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A clear, honest map of your top pressure triggers and habitual responses
- Early warning signs documented so you can catch yourself before reacting
- Pre-decided alternative responses for your most common pressure situations
- A pause mechanism you can use in the moment
- Accountability structure to support the change over time

## Coach Notes

This is one of the most personal actions in the Profit System. Some clients will resist it because it feels too "soft" or too close to therapy. Frame it practically: poor pressure responses cost real money and damage real relationships. This is performance optimisation, not navel-gazing.

Watch for clients who list triggers but sanitise their responses. Push for honesty. The client who says "I get a bit frustrated" might actually mean "I shout at my team and then feel awful about it." The real pattern is where the real coaching happens. If a client has severe stress responses that go beyond normal business pressure, recommend professional support alongside coaching.
