# The Distraction Audit

## What to Do

Systematically identify and remove the digital and physical distractions that steal your attention every day. This is not about willpower — it is about designing an environment where focus is the default, not the exception.

## Why It Matters

Your phone, your inbox, your browser, and your workspace are all engineered to interrupt you. Every notification, every open tab, every bit of clutter competes for your attention. Cal Newport's research shows that after a single interruption, it takes an average of 23 minutes to fully refocus. If you are interrupted 6 times a day, you have lost over 2 hours before you have done anything wrong. The solution is not to resist distractions harder — it is to remove them before they reach you.

## How to Use This

Work through each section once, thoroughly. Then review monthly to catch anything that has crept back in. Distractions are persistent — they will return if you let them.

## Digital Distractions

### Your Phone

- [ ] **Turn off all non-essential notifications** — Keep calls and texts from key contacts. Remove everything else: social media, news, email badges, app updates. If you are not sure whether a notification is essential, it is not.
- [ ] **Remove social media apps from your home screen** — You can still access them through the app library if you need to. The goal is to stop the mindless open-scroll-close loop.
- [ ] **Set your phone to Do Not Disturb during work hours** — Allow calls from your team and key contacts. Everything else waits.
- [ ] **Charge your phone in a different room during deep work** — Out of sight, out of mind. If it is within arm's reach, you will check it.

### Your Computer

- [ ] **Close your email client unless you are actively processing email** — Check email 2-3 times per day at set times, not continuously. Batch it.
- [ ] **Close browser tabs you are not using right now** — Every open tab is a micro-decision your brain has to manage. 3 tabs, not 30.
- [ ] **Turn off desktop notifications for chat, email, and apps** — Slack, Teams, email pop-ups — all off. Check them on your schedule, not theirs.
- [ ] **Install a website blocker for your deep work hours** — Tools like Freedom, Cold Turkey, or Focus block distracting sites during your focus time. Use technology to fight technology.
- [ ] **Unsubscribe from newsletters you do not read** — If you have not opened it in the last 3 issues, unsubscribe. Every email that arrives is a decision you have to make.

### Meetings

- [ ] **Audit your recurring meetings** — List every recurring meeting in your calendar. For each one, ask: "What would happen if this meeting stopped?" If the answer is "nothing much" — cancel it.
- [ ] **Set a default meeting length of 25 minutes** — Not 30, not 60. Shorter meetings force focus and create buffers between calls.
- [ ] **Block "no meeting" zones in your calendar** — Protect at least one half-day per week where no meetings can be booked. This is your recovery and thinking time.

## Physical Environment

- [ ] **Clear your desk to only what you need for the current task** — Clutter creates visual noise that drains cognitive energy. A clear desk signals "time to focus."
- [ ] **Remove or relocate anything that triggers task-switching** — Post-it reminders of other projects, printed to-do lists for different workstreams, open notebooks from yesterday's meeting — put them away until they are relevant.
- [ ] **Address noise** — If your workspace is noisy, use noise-cancelling headphones or find a quieter spot. Background noise reduces cognitive performance even when you think you have tuned it out.
- [ ] **Make your workspace signal "work"** — If you work from home, a dedicated space matters. Your brain associates environments with activities. A workspace that is also where you eat, watch TV, or relax will fight your focus.

---

## Expected Outcome

- Your environment supports focus instead of fighting it
- You spend less mental energy resisting distractions and more on actual work
- Your Deep Work Block becomes significantly more productive
- You feel calmer and more in control during work hours
- The benefits compound — less distraction today means better habits tomorrow

## Coach Notes

Most clients underestimate how much their environment is sabotaging them. Have them do the phone notification audit live in a session — the look on their face when they count 40+ notification sources is usually the wake-up call.

This action pairs directly with The Deep Work Block. The Distraction Audit makes the environment ready; the Deep Work Block is what you do in that environment.

For clients who resist (usually "but I need to be available"), ask: "Available for what, specifically?" Most of what they think is urgent is not. Help them define what genuinely requires immediate attention versus what can wait 90 minutes.
