# The Deep Work Block

## What to Do

Schedule a daily 90-minute block of uninterrupted time for your most important work. Treat it like a meeting with your most important client — because it is. This is where your One Thing gets done.

## Why It Matters

Cal Newport's research shows that most knowledge workers get less than 1 hour of genuinely focused work per day. The rest is meetings, messages, and shallow tasks. A business owner who protects 90 minutes of deep work daily gets more strategic work done than one who works 12 fragmented hours. The block is not something you "find time for" — it is the most productive time in your day, and everything else fits around it.

Keith Cunningham calls this "Thinking Time" — the non-negotiable practice of sitting with a single important question without distraction. The most valuable thing a business owner can do is think clearly. You cannot do that in 5-minute gaps between emails.

## How to Use This

Set it up once. Protect it daily. The discipline of showing up is more important than what you work on — though you should always work on your One Thing.

---

## Setup Checklist

- [ ] **Choose your time** — Morning is best for most people (before the world starts demanding your attention). Pick a consistent slot — same time every day so it becomes automatic.
- [ ] **Block it in your calendar** — Label it something that signals "do not touch." Book it as recurring. It is not optional and it is not the first thing that moves when something comes up.
- [ ] **Tell your team** — "I am unavailable from [time] to [time] every day. Unless the building is on fire, it waits." Be specific about what counts as an emergency.
- [ ] **Prepare the night before** — Before you leave work, write down exactly what you will work on during tomorrow's deep work block. When you sit down, you start immediately — no deciding, no browsing, no warming up.

## Daily Execution

- [ ] **Close everything except what you need** — Email closed. Phone in another room or on airplane mode. Browser tabs closed. Slack/Teams off. Chat off. If it can ping, buzz, or notify you — shut it down.
- [ ] **Set a timer for 90 minutes** — Knowing there is an end makes it easier to resist distractions. When the timer goes, you are done. No guilt about stopping.
- [ ] **Work on your One Thing only** — Not three things. Not whatever feels urgent. The one task you identified as most important. If you finish early, sit with it longer — depth beats breadth.
- [ ] **When your mind wanders, write the thought down and return to work** — Keep a notepad for "I must remember to..." thoughts. Capture them and let them go. They will be there after the block.

## Troubleshooting

- [ ] **If 90 minutes feels impossible, start with 45** — Build the habit first. Extend it when 45 minutes feels easy.
- [ ] **If you keep getting interrupted, change location** — Coffee shop, home office, empty meeting room. Sometimes the best solution is being physically unreachable.
- [ ] **If you struggle to start, use the "just 10 minutes" rule** — Commit to 10 minutes. Almost always, once you start, you keep going.

---

## Expected Outcome

- Your most important work gets your best energy, not your leftovers
- You complete meaningful work every day instead of only reacting to other people's priorities
- Over time, the compound effect of daily deep work transforms your business — problems get solved faster, strategy gets clearer, execution improves
- You feel in control of your time instead of controlled by it

## Coach Notes

This is often the hardest action for overwhelmed business owners because they believe they cannot afford 90 minutes away from the noise. Reframe it: they cannot afford NOT to. The noise will fill every minute they give it. The deep work block is how they take that time back.

Start by asking what time they currently waste each day — most will admit to 1-2 hours of low-value activity. The deep work block replaces that, not adds to their day.

If a client consistently cannot protect their block, that is a signal about boundaries, delegation, or team foundations — not about time management.
