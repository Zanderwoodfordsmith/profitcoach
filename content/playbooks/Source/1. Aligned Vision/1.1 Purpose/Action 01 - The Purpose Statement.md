# The Purpose Statement

## What to Do

Define the specific change you exist to create, in clear, practical language. Not a fluffy mission statement for your website — a decision-making filter you will use every day to choose what to pursue and what to ignore.

## Why It Matters

Without a clear purpose, every opportunity looks the same. You say yes to work that drains you, chase trends that distract you, and build a business that looks successful from the outside but feels hollow from the inside. Mike Michalowicz calls this "Lifestyle Congruence" — your business should serve the life you actually want, not the other way around. Simon Sinek's insight is simpler: people do not buy what you do, they buy why you do it. If you cannot articulate your "why," neither can your customers — and your marketing, sales, and hiring all suffer.

A good purpose statement is not inspirational wallpaper. It is a filter that makes hard decisions easy.

## How to Use This

Work through the steps in order. The first draft will not be perfect — that is fine. A rough purpose statement you actually use beats a polished one that sits in a drawer.

---

## Step 1: Find the Raw Material

- [ ] **Complete this sentence: "The problem I want to solve is..."** — Not what you sell. The underlying problem that drew you to this work in the first place. Be specific — "help businesses grow" is too vague. "Help overwhelmed trade business owners stop doing everything themselves" is a purpose.
- [ ] **Complete this sentence: "The people I care most about helping are..."** — Not your broadest possible market. The specific group whose problem you understand deeply because you have lived it, studied it, or seen it up close for years.
- [ ] **Complete this sentence: "If money were not a factor, I would still care about this because..."** — This separates real purpose from convenient positioning. If you would walk away the moment you got rich, your purpose needs more honesty.
- [ ] **Complete this sentence: "I am uniquely positioned to do this because..."** — What combination of experience, skill, and perspective do you bring that most others do not? This is not about being the best — it is about being distinctive.

## Step 2: Draft Your Purpose Statement

- [ ] **Combine your answers into one statement: "I exist to [change/transformation] for [specific people] because [why it matters to you]"** — Keep it under 30 words. If you cannot say it in one breath, it is too complicated.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win · **Read it out loud to someone who does not know your business** — If they can immediately understand what you do and why, it is working. If they look confused, simplify.
- [ ] **Test it against your last 5 business decisions** — Would this statement have helped you decide faster? If it does not change how you make decisions, it is not specific enough.

## Step 3: Pressure Test

- [ ] **Does it exclude people?** — A purpose that applies to everyone is a purpose that guides nothing. You should be able to name specific types of clients or work that fall outside your purpose.
- [ ] **Does it energise you?** — Read it again tomorrow morning. Does it make you want to get to work, or does it feel like corporate filler? If the latter, go back to Step 1 and be more honest.
- [ ] **Could a competitor copy it word for word?** — If yes, it is too generic. Your purpose should reflect something specific about your experience, perspective, or approach.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A clear, specific purpose statement under 30 words
- A decision-making filter you can apply to every opportunity, client, and project
- Clarity on who you serve and who you do not — which makes marketing, hiring, and strategy dramatically easier
- A foundation for your Core Offer, Ideal Customer, and Brand work

## Coach Notes

Most clients will resist being specific because specificity feels like limiting their options. Reframe it: specificity is not about doing less — it is about doing the right things with full commitment instead of everything at half effort.

If a client says "I just want to help people and make money," that is honest but not useful. Dig deeper: which people? Help them with what? Why you and not someone else? The coaching value is in the questions that draw out what they already know but have not articulated.

The purpose statement will evolve. Do not let perfection stall progress — a first draft they can test is more valuable than a perfect statement they never write.
