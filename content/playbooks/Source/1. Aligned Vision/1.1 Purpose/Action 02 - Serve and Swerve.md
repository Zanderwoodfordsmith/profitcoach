# Serve & Swerve Analysis

## What to Do

Create two clear lists: the clients and work you actively want to pursue (Serve), and the clients and work you will decline even when they offer money (Swerve). This turns your purpose statement from an idea into a boundary you can enforce.

## Why It Matters

Purpose without boundaries is just a nice thought. Michalowicz's Pumpkin Plan makes this brutally clear: the businesses that grow fastest are the ones that identify their best clients, cut the rest, and pour all their energy into the winners. Most business owners do the opposite — they take on anyone who can pay, dilute their focus, and end up resenting the work. The Serve & Swerve analysis forces the decision: who deserves your best effort, and who is costing you more than they pay?

## How to Use This

Work through both sides honestly. The Swerve list is usually harder — it means saying no to money. That is the point.

---

## The Serve List — Who You Want

- [ ] **List your 5 best clients or customers from the past 2 years** — Best means: profitable, enjoyable, referred others, valued your work, and got great results. Not just "paid on time."
- [ ] **What do they have in common?** — Look for patterns: industry, business size, personality type, problem they came to you with, stage of business. The pattern is your ideal client profile.
- [ ] **What made working with them great?** — Was it their attitude? Their willingness to implement? The type of problem? Their respect for your process? Name the specific qualities.
- [ ] **Write your Serve criteria** — "I actively want to work with people who are [description], facing [problem], and who [quality/behaviour]." This becomes your intake filter.

## The Swerve List — Who You Avoid

- [ ] **List your 5 worst client experiences** — The ones that drained your energy, caused problems, or were unprofitable. Be specific about what made them bad.
- [ ] **What do they have in common?** — Again, look for patterns. Industry? Attitude? Budget? Expectations? The pattern tells you who to avoid.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win · **Write your red flags** — 3-5 warning signs you can spot in a sales conversation that tell you this person will be a bad fit. These save you months of frustration per bad client avoided.
- [ ] **Write your Swerve criteria** — "I will decline work from people who [description]." Make it specific enough to actually use.

## The Hard Decisions

- [ ] **Review your current client list against both criteria** — Who on your current roster would not pass the Serve filter today? You do not need to fire them all tomorrow, but you need to see the reality.
- [ ] **Identify clients you are keeping out of guilt, fear, or habit** — These are the ones draining your energy and displacing better clients. Plan a graceful exit for at least one.
- [ ] **Calculate the cost of your worst client** — Not just revenue, but time, energy, stress, and opportunity cost. What could you do with that time if they were gone?

---

## Expected Outcome

- A clear definition of who you actively pursue and who you decline
- Red flag criteria you can use in sales conversations to filter fast
- The courage to say no to bad-fit clients — backed by data, not just gut feel
- A current client roster review that shows where you are aligned and where you are drifting
- Over time, a client base that energises you instead of draining you

## Coach Notes

The Swerve list is where the coaching value lives. Most clients know who their best customers are. Very few have explicitly decided who they will not work with. This action often produces an emotional response — "but I need the revenue." Help them calculate the true cost of bad-fit clients (including stress, time drain, and opportunity cost of not having space for great clients).

If a client cannot list 5 bad experiences, they either have not been in business long enough or they are not being honest. Push gently.

This pairs directly with the Purpose Statement — purpose defines the direction, Serve & Swerve defines the boundaries.
