# Strategic Arena Definition

## What to Do

Define the specific domain where you will compete to win. Not your market in general, but the precise customer problem, competitive space, and geographic or demographic boundary you are claiming as yours. This is about drawing a clear line around what you are fighting for so every decision, investment, and hire serves that territory.

## Why It Matters

Most small businesses try to serve everyone and end up being average for all of them. Roger Martin's work on strategy makes this point sharply: strategy is not about what you will do, it is about what you will not do. A clearly defined arena focuses your resources, sharpens your messaging, and makes it possible to genuinely dominate a space. Without it, you spread thin, compete on price, and lose to specialists. Verne Harnish puts it simply - the goal is to own a niche so completely that customers have no reason to look elsewhere.

## How to Use This

Work through each section to draw the boundaries of your competitive arena. Be as specific as possible. Vague answers mean you have not made the hard choices yet.

---

## Customer Problem Definition

- [ ] **Name the specific problem you solve** - Not your service category, but the actual pain your customer experiences. "We do accounting" is a category. "We help owner-managed businesses stop losing money to poor financial visibility" is a problem.
- [ ] **Define who has this problem** - Be precise about company size, stage, industry, geography, and decision-maker profile. The tighter this is, the more powerful your positioning becomes.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Write one sentence that captures your arena** - "[We help] [specific customer] [solve specific problem] [in specific context]." If you cannot finish this sentence clearly, your arena is not defined.
- [ ] **Identify what makes this problem urgent** - Why would someone act now rather than later? What is the trigger that moves them from "I should deal with this" to "I need to deal with this today"?

## Competitive Boundaries

- [ ] **Name your direct competitors** - Not every business in your category. The 3-5 businesses that your ideal customer would consider instead of you. If you cannot name them, you do not understand your arena well enough.
- [ ] **Define how you win against each one** - For each competitor, what is the specific reason a customer would choose you? Price, expertise, speed, relationship, methodology, guarantee? Be honest about where you are genuinely stronger and where you are not.
- [ ] **Draw geographic or demographic boundaries** - Where does your arena start and stop? Local, regional, national, or specific verticals? Trying to compete everywhere is the same as competing nowhere.
- [ ] **Identify adjacent arenas you are choosing NOT to enter** - This is the hard part. What opportunities will you say no to because they fall outside your arena? Write them down so when they come up (and they will), you have already decided.

## Arena Validation

- [ ] **Test with existing customers** - Ask your best 3-5 customers: "Why did you choose us? What problem were we solving?" If their answers do not match your arena definition, listen to them - they know something you do not.
- [ ] **Check for sufficient market size** - Is your defined arena big enough to support your revenue goals? A perfectly defined arena with 50 potential customers might not be viable. Estimate the total addressable market.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Review your last 10 new customers** - How many fit your defined arena perfectly? How many were outside it? This tells you whether you are already competing in your arena or drifting.
- [ ] **Assess your right to win** - Do you have (or can you build) genuine advantages in this arena? Credentials, experience, proprietary methodology, relationships, reputation? Wanting to compete in an arena is not enough. You need a credible path to winning.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A clearly defined competitive arena in one paragraph or less
- Specific boundaries around customer, problem, geography, and competitive set
- A list of adjacent opportunities you are choosing to decline
- Confidence that the arena is large enough and that you have a credible right to win
- A strategic filter for every future decision: "Does this serve our arena?"

## Coach Notes

The hardest part of this action is getting the client to be specific enough. Most will resist narrowing because it feels like leaving money on the table. Reframe it: you are not excluding customers, you are concentrating your advantage. A business that dominates a defined arena attracts customers from adjacent spaces naturally. Watch for arenas defined too broadly ("we help businesses grow") or too narrowly ("we help left-handed dentists in Sheffield"). The right size arena feels slightly uncomfortable - specific enough to be distinctive but large enough to build a real business.
