## **What This Is**

Strategic intent is where vision becomes decision-making power. It is not enough to have a nice picture of the future, you need a measurable, time-bound target that forces choices every single day. Strategic intent defines where you will win and, just as importantly, where you will not compete.

Without this clarity, growth becomes opportunistic. You chase whatever walks through the door, dilute your focus, and spread resources too thin. Strategic intent concentrates your efforts like a laser beam, it tells you exactly what to say yes to and gives you a reason to say no to everything else.

This playbook turns your vision into a strategic filter that drives every resource allocation decision, every project choice, and every hiring decision in your business.

---

## **What It Looks Like**

### **🔴 When this is broken**

You have a vision but it feels abstract and difficult to act on. Growth comes from whatever opportunities appear rather than deliberate choices about where to compete. You find yourself pursuing multiple directions at once, wondering which projects actually matter. Resource allocation happens by accident or urgency rather than strategic priority.

### **🟠 When this is OK**

You have some sense of direction but your strategic choices are not sharp enough to drive daily decisions. You know roughly where you want to go but you still say yes to projects and opportunities that do not clearly align. Your team understands the vision but struggles to prioritise when trade-offs need to be made.

### **🟢 When this is working**

You have a clear, measurable strategic target that everyone understands and can act on. Every resource allocation decision gets filtered through your strategic intent. You confidently say no to good opportunities that do not fit. Your team makes decisions independently because they understand exactly where the business is trying to win. Growth is concentrated and deliberate.

## **Things to Think About**

Where specifically are you choosing to dominate? This is about focus, not ambition. Most businesses try to be good at everything and end up being great at nothing. Your strategic intent should define a specific arena, market, customer type, problem, where you will be the obvious choice.

What competitive advantage can you actually sustain? Not what you hope to build, but what you can realistically defend over three to five years. Strategic intent only works if it is built on advantages that competitors cannot easily copy or undercut.

What opportunities are you deliberately walking away from? The most important strategic decisions are about what you will not do. If your strategic intent does not force you to say no to profitable opportunities, it is not sharp enough.

Does your resource allocation reflect your stated priorities? Look at where your time, money, and best people actually go. If there is a gap between your strategic intent and your investment patterns, one of them is wrong.

What could completely undermine your position in the next five years? Strategic intent is about winning, but it also needs to account for how you could lose. What technology, competitor, or market shift would make your strategy irrelevant?

## **The Plays Inside This Playbook**

When you open this playbook in the Profit System, you will find:

**Play 1: Strategic Arena Definition**

Define the specific domain where you will compete to win. Not your market in general, but the precise customer problem and competitive space you are claiming as your own.

**Play 2: Competitive Advantage Assessment**

Identify and test the sustainable advantages you can build and defend. What makes you genuinely different, and why will that difference matter in three years' time?

**Play 3: Strategic Trade-Off Framework**

Build a clear decision-making filter for what you will and will not pursue. Turn your strategic intent into a practical tool for saying no to distractions and yes to the right opportunities.

**Play 4: Resource Alignment Audit**

Review how your current allocation of time, money, and talent matches your strategic priorities. Identify gaps and redirect resources to support your chosen path to winning.

## **Quick Wins**

This week: Write a one-sentence description of exactly where your business will be the obvious choice in three years. Make it specific enough that someone else could use it to make decisions.

This week: List the three best opportunities you have said yes to in the past six months. Do they all clearly support the same strategic direction? If not, you need sharper intent.

This week: Identify one profitable project or opportunity you should stop pursuing because it does not align with where you want to win. Make the decision to stop it.
