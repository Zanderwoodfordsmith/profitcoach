# The 3-Year Picture

## What to Do

Create a specific, detailed description of what your business and life look like in three years. Not goals, not metrics, not a strategy document. A vivid picture. Something you can see, feel, and describe to someone else as if you were already living it. This becomes the north star for every major decision you make.

Write it in present tense as if it is already happening. "I wake up at..." not "I want to wake up at..."

## Why It Matters

Most business owners either have no long-term picture (they just react to what is in front of them) or they have vague aspirations ("I want to grow the business"). Neither is useful for making decisions. When you have a clear, detailed 3-year picture, decisions become simple: "Does this move me toward the picture or away from it?"

Verne Harnish calls this the BHAG. Dan Sullivan calls it the 25-year framework. We are using three years because it is far enough to be ambitious and close enough to feel real. You can actually imagine yourself there. Five or ten years is too abstract for most owners to use as a practical decision-making tool.

## How to Use This

Find 90 minutes of uninterrupted time. Not at your desk. Not between meetings. Go somewhere that helps you think clearly. Work through each section below, writing at least a full paragraph for each.

---

## Describe Your Business in Three Years

- [ ] **Revenue and profit** - What is the annual revenue? What is the profit margin? Write specific numbers, not ranges. "The business turns over £1.2M with a 25% net margin" is better than "somewhere between £1M and £2M."
- [ ] **Team** - How many people work in the business? What roles exist? Which are employees and which are contractors? What is the culture like? Describe a typical team meeting.
- [ ] **Clients** - Who are you serving? How many clients do you have? What do they pay? What do they say about working with you?
- [ ] **Products and services** - What do you sell? Has the offer evolved? What have you stopped doing? What have you added?
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Describe a typical Monday morning in your business three years from now** - Where are you? What is the first thing you do? Who do you talk to? This single exercise often reveals more about what you want than any amount of goal-setting.

## Describe Your Life in Three Years

- [ ] **Daily routine** - What does a normal day look like from waking up to going to bed? Where do you live? How do you commute (or not)?
- [ ] **Family and relationships** - How much time do you spend with family? What has changed from today? What experiences are you having together?
- [ ] **Health and energy** - How fit are you? What are you doing to maintain it? How much do you sleep?
- [ ] **Financial position** - Beyond business profit, what does your personal financial picture look like? Savings, investments, debts cleared?

## Stress-Test the Picture

- [ ] **Does the business picture support the life picture?** - Many owners describe a life of freedom and a business that requires 60-hour weeks. These are incompatible. Adjust one or both until they align.
- [ ] **Is this YOUR picture or someone else's?** - Are you describing what you genuinely want, or what you think you should want? Family expectations, peer pressure, and social media can distort vision. Be honest.
- [ ] **What are you willing to sacrifice to get there?** - Every vision has a cost. More revenue might mean more travel. A bigger team means more management. Name the trade-offs and decide if you accept them.
- [ ] **Read it aloud to someone you trust** - Does it resonate when you say it out loud? Does it excite you or does it sound like a business plan you wrote for a bank? If it does not light you up, keep refining.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A written, present-tense description of your business and life in three years
- Specific enough to use as a decision-making filter ("Does this serve the picture?")
- Stress-tested for internal consistency between business ambition and lifestyle desire
- Shared with at least one trusted person for accountability
- A document you revisit quarterly to check alignment

## Coach Notes

This is one of the most powerful exercises in the Profit System, but only if the client goes deep enough. Surface-level answers ("I want more revenue and more free time") are useless. Push for specifics. "How much revenue? How much free time? What will you do with it? Where will you be?"

Watch for two failure modes. First, the client who writes what they think you want to hear rather than what they genuinely want. Some owners actually want a small, profitable lifestyle business but feel ashamed to admit it. Validate whatever they truly want. Second, the client whose business picture and life picture are contradictory. This is a coaching goldmine. Helping them resolve that tension often unlocks clarity they have never had before.
