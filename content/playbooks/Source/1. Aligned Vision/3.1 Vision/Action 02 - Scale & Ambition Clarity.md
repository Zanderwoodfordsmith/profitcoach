# Scale & Ambition Clarity

## What to Do

Define exactly how big you want your business to become and what trade-offs you are willing to make to get there. This means putting specific numbers against revenue, team size, market reach, and complexity. Then honestly assessing whether you are prepared for what those numbers actually require.

Many owners chase growth without ever deciding how much is enough. This action forces that decision.

## Why It Matters

Growth for its own sake is a trap. A business doing £500K with two people and 30% margins might be a better life than a business doing £3M with fifteen people and 8% margins. But you cannot make that comparison unless you have thought clearly about what each scenario actually looks like day to day.

Every growth tier brings new complexity. £200K-£500K is mostly about the owner doing the work. £500K-£1M requires building a small team. £1M-£3M demands management systems, middle layers, and real leadership. £3M-£5M is a different game entirely. The skills, stress, and trade-offs are completely different at each level. Choose deliberately.

## How to Use This

Work through the sections below with total honesty. There are no wrong answers. A deliberately small business is just as valid as an ambitious growth plan. The only wrong answer is one you have not thought through.

---

## Define Your Target Scale

- [ ] **What is your target annual revenue in 3 years?** - Write a specific number. Then write what that number actually requires in terms of clients, products, pricing, and sales volume.
- [ ] **What is your target team size?** - How many people, in what roles? Full-time employees, part-time, contractors? Be specific.
- [ ] **What is your target profit margin?** - Revenue means nothing without margin. A £2M business at 5% profit pays the owner £100K. A £500K business at 30% pays £150K.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Calculate your personal income at your target revenue and margin** - Does the number excite you or disappoint you? Adjust until it works.
- [ ] **What market reach do you want?** - Local, regional, national, international? Niche specialist or broad service provider?

## Assess the Trade-offs

- [ ] **Hours per week** - At your target scale, how many hours per week will you realistically work? Not the fantasy number. The realistic one based on what that size of business demands.
- [ ] **Management burden** - At your target team size, how much of your time goes to managing people versus doing the work you enjoy? A team of 15 means you are a manager first, practitioner second.
- [ ] **Complexity tolerance** - More revenue usually means more products, more systems, more things that can break. Rate your tolerance for complexity 1-10. Be honest.
- [ ] **Financial risk** - Bigger businesses carry bigger overheads. What fixed costs will you carry at your target scale? Can you sleep at night with that number going out every month?
- [ ] **Lifestyle impact** - What changes at home? More travel? Less flexibility? More financial security but less time? Name the specific impacts.

## Choose Your Path

- [ ] **Lifestyle business (optimise for freedom)** - Keep it small, keep it simple, maximise profit per hour. Nothing wrong with this. Many of the happiest business owners chose this path deliberately.
- [ ] **Growth business (optimise for scale)** - Build something bigger than yourself. Accept the management overhead. Invest in systems and people. This path requires a fundamentally different mindset.
- [ ] **Write a one-paragraph "ambition statement"** - "I am building a [size] business that [does what] because [why]. I am willing to [trade-offs] to achieve this."
- [ ] **Check alignment with your 3-Year Picture** - Does your ambition statement match what you described in [[3.1 Vision]]? If not, one of them needs to change.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A specific, numbers-based definition of what "success" means for your business
- Clear understanding of the trade-offs required at your chosen scale
- An honest assessment of whether you are prepared for those trade-offs
- An ambition statement that guides strategic decisions
- Alignment between scale ambitions and lifestyle desires

## Coach Notes

This action often produces uncomfortable realisations. Some clients discover they have been chasing a target that, once examined, they do not actually want. Others realise they have been playing too small because they never gave themselves permission to think bigger. Both realisations are valuable.

The most common coaching moment is the client who wants £2M revenue and a 4-day week with no direct reports. Those numbers rarely coexist. Help them see the mismatch without being judgmental. The goal is clarity, not a "correct" answer. Some clients will need to revisit this action multiple times as their ambitions evolve. That is normal and healthy.
