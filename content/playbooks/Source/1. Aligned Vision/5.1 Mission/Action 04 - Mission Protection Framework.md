# Mission Protection Framework

## What to Do

Create clear criteria for decisions that protect your mission from compromise. Build a framework you can use whenever growth, profit, or opportunity conflicts with purpose. When those moments arrive - and they will - you need a pre-made decision tool, not a debate.

Define your non-negotiables, create a decision matrix for grey-area situations, and establish review triggers that flag when the business is drifting from its mission.

## Why It Matters

Every successful mission-driven company faces the same test: a lucrative opportunity arrives that requires compromising the purpose. Without a framework, these decisions get made emotionally, inconsistently, or by whoever argues loudest. Over time, small compromises accumulate. The company still talks about mission, but the behaviour tells a different story.

Jim Collins found that companies which endure over decades protect their core purpose while adapting everything else. The key word is "protect." Purpose does not survive passively. It requires active defence against the constant pressure to prioritise short-term gains. Yvon Chouinard built Patagonia's decision framework around environmental impact. When a profitable product line conflicted with environmental values, the framework made the decision clear - even when it was expensive.

## How to Use This

Build the framework once, then use it every time a significant decision creates tension between mission and other priorities. Review and update it annually as the business evolves.

---

## Define Non-Negotiables

- [ ] **List 3-5 things you will never compromise, regardless of financial impact** - These are your absolute boundaries. Examples: "We will never sell a product we do not believe in," "We will never treat our team as disposable," "We will never mislead customers about results."
- [ ] **For each non-negotiable, write a specific scenario that would test it** - Make it real. "A client offers double our normal fee but wants us to deliver something that conflicts with our values." Thinking through scenarios now prevents hesitation later.
- [ ] **Share non-negotiables with your leadership team** - They need to know the boundaries so they can protect them when you are not in the room.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Write down one decision from the past where you compromised and regretted it** - What would your framework need to include to prevent that from happening again?

## Build the Decision Matrix

- [ ] **Create a scoring system for grey-area decisions** - Not everything is black and white. For situations where mission alignment is unclear, score the opportunity on: mission alignment (1-5), financial impact (1-5), long-term strategic fit (1-5), and reversibility (1-5). Set a minimum total score for proceeding.
- [ ] **Define "mission drift" indicators** - What early warning signs show the business is moving away from purpose? Revenue from non-mission activities exceeding 20%? Team members unable to explain why the company exists? Decisions consistently favouring profit over purpose?
- [ ] **Create a "regret test" question** - "In 10 years, will I be proud of this decision or will I wish I had chosen differently?" Require this question in every major decision review.
- [ ] **Document recent decisions that felt uncomfortable** - Were they mission-aligned or not? These real examples calibrate the framework better than hypothetical scenarios.

## Establish Review Triggers

- [ ] **Set quarterly mission alignment reviews** - Look at all major decisions from the past quarter. How many passed the mission test? How many were compromises? Track the trend.
- [ ] **Create financial trigger points** - "If revenue from non-mission activities exceeds X%, we review and course-correct." Specific thresholds prevent gradual drift.
- [ ] **Assign a mission guardian** - One person (could be you, a board member, or a senior leader) whose explicit role includes flagging mission drift. Give them authority to raise concerns without being seen as obstructive.
- [ ] **Review the framework annually** - As the business grows, new types of decisions emerge. Update the framework to cover them.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Apply the framework to one current decision you are wrestling with** - Test it immediately. If it does not help, the framework needs refining.

---

## Expected Outcome

- Written list of 3-5 non-negotiable principles with real scenario tests
- Decision matrix for evaluating grey-area opportunities
- Quarterly review rhythm that catches mission drift early
- Leadership team aligned on what the company will and will not do
- Confidence to say no to lucrative opportunities that compromise purpose

## Coach Notes

This framework is only valuable if it gets used. Many owners create it, feel good about it, and then ignore it when a real test arrives. The coaching role is to hold them accountable: "What does your framework say about this decision?"

The hardest moment is when the framework says no to something the owner wants to say yes to. A big contract, a high-profile partnership, a fast-growth opportunity. That is exactly when the framework earns its value. If they override it, ask why. If the reason is good, update the framework. If the reason is just "but the money," the mission is not real yet.

Be careful not to let the framework become an excuse for avoiding hard decisions. Sometimes mission-aligned decisions are uncomfortable, expensive, or unpopular. The framework should guide, not paralyse.
