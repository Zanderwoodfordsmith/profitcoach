# Mission Definition

## What to Do

Define the lasting change your company exists to create. Not a marketing slogan or a wall poster, but a clear statement of intention that guides decisions and inspires action. Your mission answers one question: "What change will exist in the world because this company existed?"

Work through a structured process to move from vague purpose to a specific, memorable mission statement. Then test it against real decisions you have made and will make.

## Why It Matters

Simon Sinek's "Start With Why" popularised the idea, but most companies got it wrong. They wrote a purpose statement, put it on the website, and carried on as before. A real mission is not a statement - it is a filter. Every strategic decision, every hire, every product choice either serves the mission or distracts from it. Without that clarity, the business drifts toward whatever is most profitable or most urgent, and those are not always the right direction.

At Level 5, mission becomes especially important because the owner is preparing for legacy. The business will eventually operate without them. A strong mission gives the organisation a north star that outlasts any individual leader.

## How to Use This

Work through each step in order. The early steps generate raw material. The later steps refine it into a usable mission statement. Do not rush to the statement - the thinking matters more than the words.

---

## Discovery

- [ ] **Answer: "What problem in the world makes you angry?"** - Not mildly annoyed. Genuinely angry. The problem your business was built to fight. Write at least a paragraph.
- [ ] **Answer: "What would be different if your company succeeded completely?"** - Describe the world after your mission is accomplished. Be specific and visual.
- [ ] **Answer: "Why did you start this business, really?"** - Not the polished story. The real reason. Financial need, frustration, opportunity, ambition, desire to prove something. Honesty here matters.
- [ ] **List 5 decisions you have made that felt deeply right** - Decisions where you chose purpose over profit, or long-term impact over short-term gain. What pattern emerges?

## Drafting

- [ ] **Write a one-sentence mission statement** - Format: "We exist to [change] for [who] by [how]." Keep it under 20 words. If you cannot explain it simply, you have not thought about it enough.
- [ ] **Test it: Does this statement help you say no?** - A good mission excludes as much as it includes. If your statement could apply to any company in your industry, it is too vague.
- [ ] **Test it: Would you still pursue this if the business could never grow beyond its current size?** - If the answer is no, your mission might actually be "growth" wearing a purpose costume.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Say your mission statement out loud to someone outside the business** - If they do not understand it immediately, rewrite it.

## Validation

- [ ] **Review your last 10 major decisions against the mission** - How many align? How many contradict? This tells you whether the mission reflects reality or aspiration.
- [ ] **Ask your leadership team: "What do you think our mission is?"** - If their answers are wildly different from yours, the mission has not been communicated, no matter how clear it is in your head.
- [ ] **Identify one current initiative that does not serve the mission** - There will be at least one. Deciding what to do about it is the first test of whether the mission has teeth.
- [ ] **Write down what you will say no to because of this mission** - Opportunities, markets, partnerships, products. A mission that does not cause you to decline things is not doing its job.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A clear, memorable mission statement under 20 words
- Alignment between the stated mission and actual business decisions
- Leadership team able to articulate the mission consistently
- A decision-making filter that helps say no to distractions
- Foundation for brand narrative, hiring, and strategic planning

## Coach Notes

Mission work often stalls because owners confuse mission with marketing. They try to write something that sounds good on a website instead of something that is genuinely true. Push for honesty over polish. A rough, authentic mission is infinitely more useful than a slick, empty one.

Watch for missions that are actually financial goals in disguise: "To be the leading provider of X" is a market position, not a purpose. Keep asking "Why does that matter?" until you hit something that resonates emotionally, not just commercially.

Some owners discover their personal purpose and their company's mission are not aligned. That is a critical finding. It does not mean the business is wrong, but it does mean something needs to change - either the mission or the owner's role within it.
