# Positioning Statements

## What to Do

Craft clear, compelling language that explains your differentiation in terms prospects immediately understand and care about. You will create a set of positioning statements at different lengths - from a single sentence to a full paragraph - that can be used across your website, sales conversations, social media, and marketing materials.

Good positioning statements do not describe what you do. They describe why you are different and why that difference matters to the person listening.

## Why It Matters

You can have the best product in the world, but if you cannot articulate why it is different in language that resonates with prospects, you are invisible. Most businesses describe themselves in generic terms that could apply to any competitor. "We help businesses grow." "We provide quality service." "We are passionate about what we do." These say nothing. They occupy no position in the prospect's mind.

April Dunford's work on positioning shows that how you frame what you do determines who considers buying it and what they are willing to pay. The same product positioned differently attracts different buyers at different price points. Positioning is not wordsmithing. It is strategic decision-making expressed in language.

## How to Use This

Complete the Competitive Intelligence, Unique Advantage Audit, and Category Strategy actions first. Those provide the raw material. This action shapes that material into usable language.

## Gather Your Positioning Ingredients

- [ ] **Review your unique advantage statement** - From the Unique Advantage Audit. This is the core of what makes you different.
- [ ] **Review your category strategy** - From Category Strategy. This defines where you compete and how you frame it.
- [ ] **Collect the language your best clients use** - How do they describe you to others? What words do they use in testimonials and reviews? Client language almost always resonates better than your own.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Write down the single most common compliment clients give you** - "You really understand our industry" / "You made it so simple" / "We finally felt like someone was in our corner." This is positioning gold.

## Craft Your Core Positioning Statement

- [ ] **Complete the positioning framework** - "We help [specific audience] achieve [specific outcome] through [unique method/approach], unlike [alternative] which [limitation of alternative]." Write 3-5 versions and pick the strongest.
- [ ] **Test each version against "Would I say this in a conversation?"** - If it sounds like marketing copy, it will not work in sales conversations. Good positioning is conversational.
- [ ] **Remove every word that a competitor could also claim** - "Expert," "leading," "innovative," "passionate." If a competitor can say it too, it is not positioning. It is filler.
- [ ] **Check that it passes the "interested eyebrow" test** - Read it to someone unfamiliar with your business. Do they lean in and ask "How do you do that?" If they nod politely, it is too generic.

## Create Statement Variations

- [ ] **The one-liner (under 15 words)** - Your answer to "What do you do?" at a networking event. Short enough to remember, specific enough to intrigue.
- [ ] **The elevator pitch (3-4 sentences)** - Expands the one-liner with audience, method, and proof. Usable in sales conversations, LinkedIn summary, website hero section.
- [ ] **The full positioning paragraph (5-7 sentences)** - Complete positioning including outcome, method, differentiation, and a proof point. For your About page, proposals, and detailed marketing.
- [ ] **The "why us" list (5 bullet points)** - Five clear reasons to choose you over alternatives. Each one specific, provable, and meaningful to your target audience.

## Test and Refine

- [ ] **Use the one-liner in your next 5 networking conversations** - Note reactions. Which version gets the best response? Which falls flat?
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Update your email signature with your one-liner** - Every email you send becomes a positioning touchpoint. Takes 2 minutes.
- [ ] **Test the elevator pitch with 3 existing clients** - Ask: "Does this accurately describe why you work with us?" Their feedback will sharpen your language.
- [ ] **A/B test the full paragraph on your website** - If you have enough traffic, test your new positioning against your current copy. Measure enquiry quality, not just volume.
- [ ] **Review and refine quarterly** - As your business evolves, your positioning should too. What was true six months ago may need updating.

## Deploy Across All Touchpoints

- [ ] **Update your website hero section** - The first thing visitors see should be your strongest positioning statement.
- [ ] **Update your LinkedIn profile and company page** - Consistent positioning across all platforms.
- [ ] **Brief your team on the new language** - Everyone who speaks to prospects or clients should use the same positioning language. Create a simple one-page reference.
- [ ] **Update your proposal template** - Your positioning should appear in the first paragraph of every proposal.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A core positioning statement that clearly differentiates you from alternatives
- Four variations (one-liner, elevator pitch, full paragraph, "why us" list) for different contexts
- Language tested with real prospects and clients, not just internally approved
- Consistent positioning deployed across website, social media, proposals, and conversations
- A quarterly review habit to keep positioning current

## Coach Notes

The most common mistake is writing positioning statements that describe what the business does rather than why it is different. "We provide business coaching" describes a service. "We help trade business owners add £200K in profit within 12 months using a proven diagnostic system" is positioning. The difference is specificity and differentiation.

Many clients will want to keep their positioning broad because they are afraid of turning away potential clients. Remind them: clear positioning repels the wrong clients and attracts the right ones. That is the point. A statement that appeals to everyone positions you with no one. Help clients find the language that makes their best clients say "That is exactly what I need" and their worst-fit prospects say "That is not for me." Both reactions mean the positioning is working.
