# Category Strategy

## What to Do

Decide whether to compete within an existing category or create a new one. This is a fundamental strategic choice that shapes everything from your messaging to your pricing to how prospects find you. Sometimes being the best in an existing category wins. Sometimes creating a new category wins bigger. The wrong choice wastes years of effort.

This action walks you through the analysis needed to make that decision clearly.

## Why It Matters

If you compete in an existing category, prospects already understand what you do. "Accountant," "marketing agency," "business coach." The advantage is that you do not need to educate the market. The disadvantage is that you are compared directly to everyone else in that category, and the category leader usually wins.

If you create a new category, you get to set the rules. You define what "good" looks like. You have no direct competitors. The advantage is that you can dominate a space. The disadvantage is that you must educate the market about why the category exists. Al Ries and Jack Trout showed that it is almost always better to be first in a new category than to be second in an existing one. But creating a category requires more upfront investment in education and patience.

## How to Use This

Work through the assessment sections before making your decision. The answer might be obvious. Or it might surprise you.

---

## Assess Your Current Category Position

- [ ] **Name the category prospects currently put you in** - Not what you call yourself. What do prospects call you when describing you to others? "Our accountant," "that marketing company," "a coach." This is your current perceived category.
- [ ] **Identify the category leader** - Who is the default choice in your category? The name that comes to mind first when someone needs what you sell? If it is you, your strategy is to defend that position. If it is not, you need a different approach.
- [ ] **Assess your rank in the category** - Are you top 3 in your market? Top 10? Unknown? If you are not in the top 3, fighting for category leadership is an uphill battle.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Google the phrase a prospect would use to find you** - What comes up? Where do you appear? This shows you your real category position, not the one you imagine.

## Evaluate the "Compete Within" Option

- [ ] **Can you realistically become number 1 or 2 in your category within your target market?** - If yes, competing within the existing category makes sense. You have a clear path to leadership.
- [ ] **What would it take to overtake the current leader?** - More marketing spend? Better product? Lower prices? Stronger reputation? Estimate the investment realistically.
- [ ] **Is the category growing or shrinking?** - Winning in a declining category is a hollow victory. Check whether demand is increasing, stable, or falling.
- [ ] **Can you sub-segment the category?** - You might not be the best "accountant" but you could be the best "accountant for e-commerce businesses doing £1M-£5M." Niche leadership within a broader category is a powerful middle path.

## Evaluate the "Create New" Option

- [ ] **Is there a genuine unmet need that no existing category addresses?** - Not a slight improvement. A fundamentally different problem or approach that deserves its own name. If you have to force the distinction, it is probably not a new category.
- [ ] **Can you name the new category in 3-5 words that a prospect immediately understands?** - "Revenue operations consultant" is clear. "Holistic business transformation facilitator" is not. If you cannot name it simply, the market will not adopt it.
- [ ] **Do you have the resources to educate the market?** - Creating a category requires content, speaking, media, and patience. It takes 2-3 years minimum before the market starts using your category language. Can you sustain that investment?
- [ ] **Are there at least 2-3 other businesses that could exist in your new category?** - A real category has room for multiple players. If only your business fits, it is a positioning statement, not a category.

## Make Your Decision

- [ ] **Choose your path: compete within, sub-segment, or create new** - Based on the analysis above, which strategy gives you the strongest long-term position?
- [ ] **Write a one-sentence category statement** - "We are the [category position] in [category name] for [target market]." This becomes the foundation for all your positioning work.
- [ ] **Identify the first 3 actions to execute your category strategy** - What needs to happen in the next 90 days to start building your category position?
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Update your LinkedIn headline and website tagline to reflect your chosen category position** - Small change, big signal. Start showing up as what you want to be known as.

## Expected Outcome

- Clear understanding of your current category position
- Honest assessment of whether you can win in the existing category
- Informed decision about whether to compete within, sub-segment, or create new
- A one-sentence category statement that guides your positioning
- First actions identified to start building category leadership

## Coach Notes

Most small business owners have never thought about category strategy at all. They just describe what they do and hope people buy it. Introducing this concept often creates an immediate shift in how they think about marketing and sales.

The most common trap is the client who wants to create a new category because it sounds exciting, but does not have the resources to educate the market. For most businesses under £2M, sub-segmenting an existing category is the highest-leverage move. "The business coach for tradespeople" is more powerful than inventing a new category from scratch. Push clients toward specificity. The narrower the category definition, the easier it is to dominate. "Business coach" is impossible to win. "Business coach for UK trades businesses doing £500K-£2M" is very winnable.
