# Competitive Intelligence

## What to Do

Map your real competitive landscape. Not just the obvious direct competitors, but every alternative solution a prospect might choose instead of working with you. This includes doing nothing, hiring internally, using software, reading a book, or choosing a completely different type of provider.

True competitive intelligence goes beyond listing company names. It means understanding why prospects choose each alternative, what those alternatives do well, and where they fall short.

## Why It Matters

Most business owners either ignore competitors entirely ("I just focus on what I do") or obsess over the wrong ones (watching a direct competitor's social media while missing the real threat). Both approaches leave you blind.

Your prospect is always choosing between options. If you do not understand what those options are, you cannot explain why yours is better. You end up competing on price because you have not given them a reason to compare on anything else. Al Ries and Jack Trout built the entire discipline of positioning on this insight: your position exists relative to alternatives, not in isolation.

## How to Use This

Work through each section systematically. You will not have all the information in one sitting. Start with what you know and identify gaps to research over the following weeks.

---

## Map Direct Competitors

- [ ] **List every business that offers a similar service to your primary audience** - Same type of service, similar price range, targeting similar clients. These are the names prospects mention when they say "we are also looking at..."
- [ ] **For each, document their positioning** - What do they claim to do differently? What is their headline promise? How do they describe their ideal client?
- [ ] **Identify their pricing model and approximate price point** - You do not need exact figures. Ballpark is fine. Are they premium, mid-range, or budget?
- [ ] **Note their strengths honestly** - What do they genuinely do well? Where might they be a better fit than you for certain clients? Honest assessment is more useful than dismissal.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Visit your top 3 competitors' websites and read their home page as if you were a prospect** - What impression do you get? What do they promise? How does it compare to what you promise?

## Map Indirect Competitors

- [ ] **List alternative solutions that are not direct competitors** - Software platforms, freelancers, in-house hires, DIY resources, courses, books, free content. Anything a prospect might choose instead of paying you.
- [ ] **Identify the "do nothing" competitor** - For many businesses, the biggest competitor is inaction. The prospect decides the problem is not urgent enough to solve. Understand why they stay put.
- [ ] **Assess which alternatives are growing** - Is AI, automation, or a new platform making an alternative more attractive? Spotting trends early gives you time to respond.
- [ ] **Note which alternatives your lost prospects chose** - If you track why deals fall through, look at where those prospects went. This is the most valuable competitive intelligence you have.

## Analyse Competitive Gaps

- [ ] **Identify what no competitor (including you) currently does well** - These are market gaps. Unmet needs. Underserved segments. Potential opportunities for differentiation.
- [ ] **Map the common complaints about each alternative** - What do clients complain about with your competitors? With software solutions? With doing it themselves? Reviews, forums, and conversations with prospects are gold mines for this.
- [ ] **Find the "table stakes" vs. "differentiators"** - Table stakes are what everyone must offer just to be considered. Differentiators are what make someone choose you over the alternatives. If you are competing on table stakes, you are competing on price.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Ask your last 3 new clients: "What other options did you consider before choosing us?"** - Their answers will tell you more about your real competitive landscape than any amount of desk research.

## Document and Maintain

- [ ] **Create a simple competitive landscape document** - One page that maps direct competitors, indirect alternatives, and the "do nothing" option. Include their positioning, strengths, weaknesses, and pricing.
- [ ] **Set a quarterly review** - The landscape changes. New competitors appear. Existing ones pivot. Alternatives evolve. Keep your intelligence current.
- [ ] **Share relevant insights with your team** - Sales team needs to know what prospects are comparing you to. Marketing needs to know what messaging competitors use. Product needs to know where alternatives fall short.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A complete map of direct competitors, indirect alternatives, and the "do nothing" option
- Clear understanding of what each alternative does well and where it falls short
- Identified gaps in the market that no one is serving well
- A practical document your sales and marketing teams can use
- A quarterly habit of updating competitive intelligence

## Coach Notes

Many clients resist this exercise because they find it uncomfortable to look closely at competitors. Some are afraid of what they will find. Others feel it is beneath them. Reframe it: understanding the landscape is not about copying anyone. It is about knowing what your prospects are comparing you to so you can position yourself clearly.

The "do nothing" competitor is almost always underestimated. For most service businesses, the biggest threat is not another provider. It is the prospect deciding the problem can wait. Help clients understand that their marketing and sales must address inertia as much as competing offers. The "ask your last 3 clients" quick win often produces revelations. Clients are frequently surprised by who their real competitors turn out to be.
