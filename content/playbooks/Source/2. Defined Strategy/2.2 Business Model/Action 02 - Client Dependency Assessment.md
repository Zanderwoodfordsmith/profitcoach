# Client Dependency Assessment

## What to Do

Calculate how much of your revenue comes from your top clients and assess the risk that concentration creates. Then build a plan to reduce dangerous dependencies before they become a crisis. This is about stress-testing your business: what happens if your biggest relationship ends tomorrow?

## Why It Matters

Client concentration is one of the most common hidden risks in small businesses. When 30-50% of your revenue comes from one or two clients, you do not really have a business - you have a high-risk employment arrangement where you can be "fired" without notice. Every decision gets filtered through "will this upset the big client?" which limits your independence and growth. Diversification is not about dropping big clients. It is about making sure no single loss can threaten your survival.

## How to Use This

Pull your client revenue data for the last 12 months. Sort by revenue generated. Then work through the framework below.

---

## Concentration Analysis

- [ ] List your top 10 clients by revenue (or all clients if you have fewer than 10)
- [ ] Calculate the percentage of total revenue each represents
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: If any single client represents more than 25% of your revenue, write down what would happen to your business if they left next month. Be specific about the financial impact.
- [ ] Calculate the "top 3 concentration": what percentage of revenue do your top 3 clients represent combined?
- [ ] Calculate the "danger zone": how many clients would need to leave before your business could not cover its fixed costs?

## Risk Assessment (For Each Top 5 Client)

- [ ] **Contract status:** Is there a contract in place? When does it expire? How much notice is required?
- [ ] **Relationship depth:** How many people in their organisation do you have relationships with? (1 contact = high risk, 3+ contacts = lower risk)
- [ ] **Switching cost:** How easy would it be for them to replace you? (Easy = high risk, difficult = lower risk)
- [ ] **Payment reliability:** Do they pay on time, or do you chase? Are there ever disputes?
- [ ] **Growth potential:** Is this client likely to spend more, the same, or less with you over the next 12 months?

## Dependency Score

- [ ] Score each top client: Green (healthy relationship, <15% of revenue), Amber (growing dependency, 15-25%), Red (dangerous dependency, >25%)
- [ ] For any Red clients: what specific steps would reduce the dependency? (Win new clients, diversify service offerings, build recurring revenue from other sources)
- [ ] Set a 12-month target: no single client should represent more than [X]% of revenue (15-20% is a healthy target for most SMEs)

## Diversification Plan

- [ ] Identify 3 potential new clients or segments you could pursue to spread your revenue
- [ ] Calculate how much new revenue you would need to bring your top client below 20% of total
- [ ] Review your marketing and sales activity: is your lead generation currently focused on winning clients similar to your biggest one, or genuinely different?
- [ ] Set a quarterly review to re-run the concentration analysis and track progress

---

## Expected Outcome

- An honest picture of how concentrated your revenue is
- Risk scores for your most important client relationships
- A specific plan to reduce dangerous dependencies over the next 12 months
- A target concentration level to work toward
- Confidence that no single loss can threaten your business survival

## Coach Notes

This exercise often creates discomfort - owners know they are too dependent on a few clients but have been avoiding the conversation. Do not let them minimise the risk ("they would never leave"). Push back with: "What if they get acquired? What if their budget gets cut? What if their key contact leaves?" None of these require the client to be unhappy with your work. The best time to reduce dependency is when everything is going well, not when the call comes.
