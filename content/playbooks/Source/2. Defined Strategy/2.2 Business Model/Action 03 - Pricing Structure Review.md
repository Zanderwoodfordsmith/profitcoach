# Pricing Structure Review

## What to Do

Examine whether your pricing model supports growth or limits it. Assess your current pricing against market positioning, value delivered, and scalability, then identify opportunities to build recurring revenue, improve margins, or create pricing that rewards scale rather than punishing it.

## Why It Matters

Pricing is the most powerful lever in your business. A 10% price increase on the same volume drops straight to profit. But most SME owners set prices once, based on what competitors charge or what "feels right," and never revisit them. Your pricing structure determines whether growth is energising (higher volume = better margins) or exhausting (more clients = more hours for the same money). Getting pricing right can transform your business model without changing anything else.

## How to Use This

Review your current pricing across all services and products. Be honest about how prices were set and whether they still make sense.

---

## Current Pricing Audit

- [ ] List every product or service you sell with its current price
- [ ] For each, note how the price was set: market rate, competitor matching, cost-plus, gut feel, or value-based?
- [ ] Calculate the profit margin on each (revenue minus direct costs, before overheads)
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Identify one service where you have not raised prices in 12+ months. Research what competitors charge for the same thing today.
- [ ] Note which pricing models you use: hourly/daily rate, fixed project fee, retainer, subscription, value-based, performance-based

## Pricing Health Check

- [ ] **Do you trade time for money?** If your primary pricing is hourly or daily rates, your revenue is capped by your available hours. Consider whether fixed-price, retainer, or productised pricing could break that ceiling.
- [ ] **When was your last price increase?** If it has been more than 12 months, you have effectively given yourself a pay cut (inflation, rising costs). What would a 10-15% increase look like?
- [ ] **Do your prices reflect the value you deliver?** Calculate the ROI your clients get from working with you. If you charge £5,000 for a service that saves them £50,000, you are dramatically underpriced.
- [ ] **Are clients pushing back on price, or accepting without negotiation?** If no one ever questions your price, you are probably too cheap. Some price resistance is healthy.
- [ ] **Do you have a pricing ladder?** Can clients start small and grow with you? (Entry offer → core offer → premium offer). A ladder maximises lifetime value.

## Recurring Revenue Opportunity

- [ ] Review your current client base: which clients need ongoing support, maintenance, or advice?
- [ ] For each, could you convert project work into a retainer, subscription, or ongoing contract?
- [ ] Calculate what monthly recurring revenue would look like if you converted just 3-5 project clients to retainers
- [ ] Design a simple retainer or subscription offer: what would you include, what would it cost, and what is the minimum commitment?
- [ ] Identify services that could be productised: standard scope, standard price, repeatable delivery

## Action Plan

- [ ] Decide: which one pricing change would have the biggest impact on revenue, margin, or stability?
- [ ] Set a timeline for implementing the change (new pricing for new clients is usually easiest to start)
- [ ] Draft the communication for existing clients if prices will change for them too
- [ ] Set a review date to assess the impact of the change after 90 days

---

## Expected Outcome

- A clear picture of how your pricing was set and whether it still makes sense
- Specific gaps identified: underpricing, time-for-money traps, missing recurring revenue
- At least one concrete pricing change to implement this quarter
- A recurring revenue opportunity identified and designed
- Confidence that your pricing supports growth rather than limiting it

## Coach Notes

Pricing conversations often trigger fear: "If I raise prices, I will lose clients." Challenge this with data. Most SMEs can raise prices 10-20% with minimal client loss, and the clients who leave over a 10% increase are often the least profitable anyway. The recurring revenue conversation is especially powerful for project-based businesses running feast-or-famine cycles. Even a small retainer base (5-10 clients at £500-1,000/month) creates stability that transforms how the owner sleeps at night. Connect this to the Revenue Stream Analysis (Action 1) for a complete picture.
