# Dependency Audit

## What to Do

Systematically identify and eliminate every personal dependency in your business. Map every decision, relationship, process, and piece of knowledge that requires you specifically. Then build a plan to transfer, document, or restructure each one so the business can operate independently.

This is the most practical exit preparation action. A business that depends on its owner is not a saleable asset - it is a job with a fancy title.

## Why It Matters

Bo Burlingham found in "Finish Big" that the number one reason business sales collapse after due diligence is owner dependency. The buyer discovers that key clients have personal relationships with the owner, that critical decisions cannot be made without the owner, and that institutional knowledge lives nowhere but the owner's head. The deal either dies or the valuation drops significantly.

Even if you are not planning to sell soon, reducing dependency is valuable. It gives you freedom now - the ability to take holidays, focus on strategy, or simply step back when you need to. Owner dependency is not just an exit problem. It is a quality-of-life problem.

## How to Use This

Work through each dependency category methodically. For each item you identify, classify it as Critical (must fix before any exit), Important (should fix within 12 months), or Minor (nice to fix). Start with the Critical items.

---

## Relationship Dependencies

- [ ] **List every key client who would leave if you left** - Be honest. If clients buy from you personally rather than from the company, that is a dependency. Name each one.
- [ ] **List every supplier or partner relationship that depends on your personal connection** - Who would stop returning calls if you were not the one calling?
- [ ] **Identify key referral sources that are tied to you personally** - Referral networks built on personal trust are valuable but fragile. They need to be transferred or institutionalised.
- [ ] **Start introducing your team to key relationships** - Do not wait until exit. Begin transitioning relationships now by having team members join calls, attend meetings, and build their own connections.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Introduce one key client to a senior team member this week** - Frame it as "I want you to have more than one point of contact." Start the transition casually.

## Decision Dependencies

- [ ] **Track every decision that comes to you in a normal week** - Keep a log for 5 working days. Every question, approval, sign-off, or consultation. Count them.
- [ ] **Categorise each decision: Must be me / Could be someone else / Should not be me at all** - Most owners find 70-80% of decisions reaching them could be made by someone else.
- [ ] **Create decision authority levels** - Define who can decide what, up to what value, without your approval. Write it down. Share it. Enforce it.
- [ ] **Identify the 3 decisions your team is most afraid to make without you** - That fear is the dependency. Address it through training, clear criteria, or explicit permission.

## Knowledge Dependencies

- [ ] **List everything you know that nobody else does** - Pricing logic, client history, product rationale, supplier arrangements, market insights, financial context. All of it.
- [ ] **Prioritise by risk: what would hurt most if lost?** - If you were hit by a bus tomorrow, which missing knowledge would cause the biggest problems? Start there.
- [ ] **Document the top 10 items** - Not in perfect detail. A rough document that captures 80% of the knowledge is infinitely better than nothing. Use video recordings if writing takes too long.
- [ ] **Create "run books" for critical processes** - Step-by-step guides for the processes only you currently manage. Invoicing, pricing, client renewals, board reporting, whatever only you do.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Record a 10-minute video explaining the one thing only you understand** - Loom, phone video, anything. Store it somewhere the team can access.

## Process Dependencies

- [ ] **Identify processes that stop when you are away** - What grinds to a halt during your holidays? Those are your process dependencies.
- [ ] **Map your involvement in each core business process** - Sales, delivery, finance, hiring, strategy. Where are you a bottleneck?
- [ ] **Assign a backup for every process you are involved in** - Not just a name on a chart. A person who has been trained and has actually done the work at least once.
- [ ] **Test independence: take a full week off with no contact** - The problems that surface reveal your remaining dependencies. Do this at least once a year.

---

## Expected Outcome

- Complete inventory of personal dependencies across relationships, decisions, knowledge, and processes
- Each dependency classified by severity (Critical, Important, Minor)
- Transfer or elimination plan for all Critical dependencies
- Decision authority framework that empowers the team
- Measurable reduction in owner involvement over 12 months

## Coach Notes

This is often uncomfortable work. The owner's identity is wrapped up in being needed. Being the person who knows everything, who makes the final call, who has the key relationships - that feels like importance. Letting go of it can feel like becoming irrelevant. Help them reframe: reducing dependency is not reducing importance. It is upgrading from operator to architect.

The "bus test" is a useful but harsh framing. A gentler version: "Could you take 3 months off to travel and come back to a healthy business?" If the answer is no, the dependencies are real and need addressing.

Relationship dependencies are the hardest to fix and the most damaging to valuation. They take 12-24 months to transition. Start early. The owner needs to gradually introduce team members to key relationships without alarming clients or partners.
