# Resource Allocation Matrix

## What to Do

Prioritise your growth initiatives by scoring each one against four dimensions: strategic impact, investment required, time to results, and alignment with core strengths. The output is a ranked list that tells you where to invest first, what to defer, and what to drop entirely. This is not a wishlist. It is a forced prioritisation that matches ambition to reality.

## Why It Matters

Growing businesses have more ideas than resources. Without a disciplined allocation process, resources flow to whoever argues loudest or whatever feels most urgent, rather than what is most strategically important. Keith Cunningham calls this "the tyranny of the urgent over the important." A resource allocation matrix forces objectivity into a process that is usually driven by emotion, politics, or habit. It ensures your best resources go to your best opportunities.

## How to Use This

List all growth initiatives on the table, score them honestly, then use the output to make allocation decisions. Update quarterly as initiatives progress and new opportunities emerge.

---

## Initiative Inventory

- [ ] **List every active growth initiative** - Include everything that is consuming time, money, or attention. Marketing campaigns, new product development, market expansion, partnership projects, technology investments. Nothing is too small to list.
- [ ] **Add proposed initiatives not yet started** - Include ideas that are being discussed or planned. They need to compete for resources alongside existing commitments.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Remove anything that has been "in progress" for more than 6 months without clear results** - These are resource drains disguised as initiatives. Either commit properly or stop.
- [ ] **Confirm the owner of each initiative** - Every initiative needs one person accountable. If it does not have a clear owner, it will not get done regardless of how well it scores.

## Scoring Framework

- [ ] **Score strategic impact (1-10)** - How much will this initiative move the needle on your most important strategic goals? A 10 is transformative. A 3 is incremental.
- [ ] **Score investment required (1-10, inverted)** - How much will it cost in money, time, and people? Score 10 for low cost, 1 for high cost. This inverts the scale so higher is always better.
- [ ] **Score time to results (1-10, inverted)** - How quickly will you see meaningful returns? Score 10 for quick payback, 1 for long-horizon bets.
- [ ] **Score alignment with core strengths (1-10)** - Does this play to your existing capabilities, or does it require building new ones? Initiatives that leverage what you already do well have a higher probability of success.
- [ ] **Calculate a composite score** - Add the four scores for each initiative. Maximum possible is 40. Rank from highest to lowest.

## Allocation Decisions

- [ ] **Divide initiatives into three tiers** - Tier 1 (top 3): Full investment, best resources, active leadership attention. Tier 2 (next 3): Moderate investment, clear milestones, review monthly. Tier 3 (everything else): Defer, reduce, or stop.
- [ ] **Assign specific resources to Tier 1** - Name the people, define the budget, block the time. If you cannot assign specific resources, the initiative is not really Tier 1.
- [ ] **Set milestone checkpoints for Tier 2** - Define what needs to happen before Tier 2 initiatives get promoted to Tier 1 or demoted to Tier 3.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Identify one Tier 3 initiative to stop this week** - Free up the resources and redirect them to Tier 1. Stopping is the fastest way to create capacity.

## Review Rhythm

- [ ] **Schedule quarterly matrix reviews** - Scores change as initiatives progress, market conditions shift, and new opportunities appear. The matrix should be a living tool, not a one-time exercise.
- [ ] **Track actual resource spend against allocation** - Are Tier 1 initiatives actually getting the resources you allocated? If not, something is pulling resources away. Find it and fix it.
- [ ] **Celebrate Tier 1 progress publicly** - The team needs to see that strategic priorities get attention, results, and recognition. This reinforces the allocation discipline.

---

## Expected Outcome

- Every growth initiative scored objectively on four dimensions
- A clear ranking that separates high-value initiatives from resource drains
- Specific resource commitments for top-tier initiatives
- At least one low-performing initiative stopped to free up capacity
- A quarterly review rhythm that keeps allocation aligned with strategy

## Coach Notes

The most powerful moment in this exercise is when clients see that their current resource allocation does not match their stated priorities. They will have Tier 3 initiatives consuming Tier 1 resources, often because of emotional attachment or historical momentum. Help them confront this without judgement. The hardest part is stopping things. Owners hate stopping because it feels like failure. Reframe it: stopping a low-priority initiative to fund a high-priority one is the definition of good strategy. If a client cannot rank their initiatives (everything is a "10 for strategic impact"), push back firmly. Forced ranking is the whole point. Without it, the exercise is just a list.
