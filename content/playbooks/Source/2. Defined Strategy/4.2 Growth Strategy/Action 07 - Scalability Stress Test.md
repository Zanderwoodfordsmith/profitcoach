# Scalability Stress Test

## What to Do

Evaluate whether your proposed growth initiatives can scale without proportional increases in complexity, cost, or management overhead. For each initiative, stress test it at 2x, 5x, and 10x current volume to identify where it breaks. The goal is to find the bottlenecks and structural limitations before you hit them, not after.

## Why It Matters

Many businesses grow into a wall they did not see coming. What works at £500K revenue breaks at £1M. What works with 5 employees creates chaos with 20. The problem is rarely the growth itself - it is that the business was not designed to handle it. Michael Gerber's E-Myth work shows this clearly: businesses built around the owner's personal capacity hit a ceiling that no amount of harder work can break through. Scalability has to be designed, not hoped for.

## How to Use This

Take each significant growth initiative and run it through the stress test. Be honest about where the cracks appear. The point is not to avoid growth but to prepare for it.

---

## Volume Stress Test

- [ ] **Define your current baseline** - For the initiative being tested, what is the current volume? Customers served, transactions processed, projects delivered, or whatever your relevant unit is.
- [ ] **Project 2x volume** - What changes at double the current volume? Can your team handle it with current headcount? Do your systems and processes still work? Where does strain first appear?
- [ ] **Project 5x volume** - This is where most businesses break. What completely stops working? What needs to be rebuilt from scratch rather than patched? Be specific about the failure points.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Identify the single biggest bottleneck at 2x** - This is your most urgent scalability issue. If you can solve it before you reach 2x, you buy yourself significant runway.

## Cost Scaling Analysis

- [ ] **Map your cost structure at current volume** - What are the fixed costs (do not change with volume) and variable costs (increase with each unit)?
- [ ] **Identify costs that scale linearly** - These increase at the same rate as volume. If you need one more person for every 10 new customers, that scales linearly. It works but caps your margins.
- [ ] **Identify costs that scale exponentially** - These increase faster than volume. Coordination costs in large teams. Quality control overhead. Management layers. These are the dangerous ones.
- [ ] **Find opportunities for economies of scale** - Where do costs decrease per unit as volume grows? Technology, purchasing power, shared infrastructure. These are your scalability advantages.

## People and Process Test

- [ ] **Assess management span** - How many people does each manager oversee? At 2x and 5x, does the management structure still work, or do you need additional layers? Each layer adds cost and slows communication.
- [ ] **Test your processes at scale** - Walk through your core delivery process assuming 5x volume. Where do manual steps become unsustainable? Where does quality slip because there are too many tasks for too few people?
- [ ] **Evaluate key person dependencies** - Which parts of the initiative depend on a specific individual? If that person is sick, on holiday, or leaves, what happens? Single points of failure become critical risks at scale.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Document the one process that most depends on tribal knowledge** - If it only works because one person knows how to do it, it will not scale. Write it down this week.

## Technology and Systems

- [ ] **Audit current systems capacity** - Can your CRM, project management tools, communication platforms, and financial systems handle 5x volume? Many tools have limits that only become visible under load.
- [ ] **Identify manual workarounds** - Where are people doing things manually that a system should handle? These workarounds multiply with volume and eventually collapse.
- [ ] **Estimate technology investment needed** - What systems upgrades or new tools will you need at each growth milestone? Factor these into your growth budget, not as an afterthought.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A clear picture of where each growth initiative breaks under increased volume
- Identified bottlenecks ranked by urgency and impact
- Understanding of how costs scale with growth (linear, exponential, or leveraged)
- Key person dependencies and process risks documented
- A prioritised list of scalability investments to make before growth outpaces capacity

## Coach Notes

Clients often resist this exercise because it feels like looking for problems instead of celebrating opportunities. Reframe it: you are stress-testing to protect the growth, not to prevent it. The most common finding is key person dependency. Almost every business in the £200K-£2M range has at least one person (often the owner) who is a bottleneck for multiple processes. Addressing this is usually the highest-leverage scalability improvement they can make. If a client's growth initiative fails the stress test at 2x, they should not be scaling it yet. They should be redesigning it first. Scaling a broken process just creates a bigger mess faster.
