# Partnership Structure Design

## What to Do

Create partnership models that protect your brand, maintain quality, and ensure sustainable economics for both parties. This is the practical architecture of how a partnership actually works day to day - who does what, how money flows, how quality is maintained, and what happens when things go wrong. Without this structure, even the best-matched partners will eventually run into problems that could have been prevented.

## Why It Matters

Most partnerships fail not because the partners are wrong for each other, but because the structure is unclear. Ambiguity about roles, money, and expectations creates friction that slowly erodes the relationship. Well-designed structure does the opposite - it reduces friction, builds trust, and allows both parties to focus on creating value instead of negotiating terms every time something comes up. The time you invest in structure upfront saves ten times that in conflict resolution later.

## How to Use This

Work through each section for every partnership you are formalising. Adapt the depth of structure to the partnership type - a simple referral arrangement needs less than a full joint venture. But every partnership needs clarity on the fundamentals.

---

## Roles and Responsibilities

- [ ] **Define who owns the customer relationship** - At every stage of the customer journey, one partner must be the primary contact. Shared ownership of customer relationships leads to confusion and dropped balls.
- [ ] **Map the handoff points** - Where does one partner's responsibility end and the other's begin? Be specific. "We handle the sales conversation up to proposal stage, then hand over to [partner] for implementation" is clear. "We collaborate on delivery" is not.
- [ ] **Assign quality control responsibilities** - Who monitors and maintains service quality for the shared customer? What happens if quality drops below agreed standards?
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Write a one-paragraph description of how the partnership works from the customer's perspective** - If you cannot explain it simply to a customer, the structure is too complicated.

## Financial Model

- [ ] **Choose your commercial structure** - Options: referral fee (fixed or percentage per introduction), revenue share (ongoing split), joint pricing (bundled offering), or value exchange (no money changes hands, you trade access to each other's audiences).
- [ ] **Define payment terms and triggers** - When does a referral fee get paid? On introduction, on closed deal, or on delivery? Each option creates different incentives. Be deliberate about which incentive you want.
- [ ] **Set minimum viability thresholds** - What volume of referrals or revenue makes this partnership worth the management overhead? If the answer is "one or two a year," it is probably not worth formalising.
- [ ] **Agree how to handle disputes about attribution** - When a customer comes from multiple sources, how do you decide who gets credit? Agreeing this in advance prevents the most common commercial argument in partnerships.

## Brand and Quality Protection

- [ ] **Define how your brand is represented** - Can the partner use your name, logo, or testimonials? Under what conditions? Who approves materials before they go out?
- [ ] **Set quality standards for the partner's delivery** - If a partner delivers a service under your brand or alongside your offering, what minimum standards must they meet? Put it in writing.
- [ ] **Create an escalation process** - When a shared customer has a problem, who do they contact? How quickly must it be resolved? What happens if one partner drops the ball?
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Draft a simple one-page partnership summary** - Cover: what each party does, how money works, how quality is maintained, and how either party can exit. This is not a legal contract - it is a clear agreement you both sign before lawyers get involved.

## Exit and Evolution

- [ ] **Define exit terms from day one** - How much notice is required? What happens to existing shared customers? Can either party exit for any reason, or only for cause?
- [ ] **Build in review and renewal points** - Every partnership should have a formal review date. Good partnerships get renewed and strengthened. Underperforming ones get restructured or ended.
- [ ] **Plan for evolution** - How will the partnership grow if it works well? More services? Deeper integration? Geographic expansion? Having a growth path keeps both parties invested.
- [ ] **Document everything** - Even informal partnerships need written agreements. Memories differ. Circumstances change. People leave. Written records protect both parties.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A clear structure for each partnership covering roles, money, quality, and exit
- Reduced friction because expectations are documented, not assumed
- Brand protection through defined quality standards and approval processes
- Financial arrangements that incentivise the right behaviour from both parties
- Exit terms that allow graceful separation if the partnership stops working

## Coach Notes

The financial model conversation is where most clients get uncomfortable. They either want to be overly generous (giving away too much to avoid awkwardness) or overly aggressive (trying to minimise what they pay). Help them find the zone where both parties feel the arrangement is fair and motivating. If one side feels short-changed, the partnership will not last regardless of how good the structure looks on paper. The exit clause is equally important and equally avoided. Nobody wants to talk about ending a partnership before it starts. But defining exit terms upfront is a sign of professionalism, not pessimism. It actually makes both parties more comfortable committing because they know there is a way out.
