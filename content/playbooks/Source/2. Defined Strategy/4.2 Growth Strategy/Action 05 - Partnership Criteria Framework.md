# Partnership Criteria Framework

## What to Do

Define clear, written standards for evaluating partnership opportunities so you invest in relationships that create genuine mutual value. This framework becomes your decision filter - every potential partnership gets measured against the same criteria before you commit time, energy, or reputation. No more gut-feel decisions about who to work with.

## Why It Matters

Bad partnerships cost more than they deliver, and the damage is often invisible until it is too late. A partner who sends low-quality referrals erodes your brand. A joint venture with someone whose values do not match yours creates customer confusion. A channel partner who underdelivers on your service destroys trust you spent years building. Having clear criteria before you need them prevents emotional or opportunistic decisions that look good in the moment but fail over time.

## How to Use This

Build your criteria framework once, then apply it to every partnership opportunity. Review and update the framework annually or whenever a partnership fails - failures teach you what criteria were missing.

---

## Non-Negotiable Requirements

- [ ] **Define your quality threshold** - What minimum standard must a partner meet in how they deliver to their own customers? If their customers are unhappy, your shared customers will be too.
- [ ] **Define your values alignment test** - List 3-4 values that a partner must share. Not identical values, but compatible ones. If you prioritise transparency and they prioritise aggressive selling, it will not work.
- [ ] **Set your reputation test** - Would you be comfortable with your best customer knowing about this partnership? If not, it is not the right partner.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Write one sentence describing your ideal partner** - "We partner with businesses that..." This becomes your quick screening filter before any detailed evaluation.

## Evaluation Criteria

- [ ] **Audience quality and overlap** - Does their customer base match your ideal customer profile? A large audience of the wrong people is worse than a small audience of the right ones.
- [ ] **Mutual value balance** - Can you clearly define what each party gives and gets? If the value flows primarily in one direction, it is not sustainable.
- [ ] **Operational compatibility** - How do they communicate? How quickly do they move? How do they handle problems? If your working styles clash, the partnership will create friction, not growth.
- [ ] **Financial transparency** - Are they financially stable? Will revenue-sharing or referral fees work for both parties? Can you have an honest conversation about money?
- [ ] **Track record check** - Have they partnered successfully before? What happened with previous partnerships? A pattern of failed partnerships is a red flag regardless of how good the opportunity looks.

## Scoring System

- [ ] **Create a simple scorecard** - List your 8-10 criteria. Score each potential partner 1-5 on each criterion. Set a minimum total score for partnership approval.
- [ ] **Weight the criteria** - Not all criteria matter equally. Reputation and values alignment might be weighted 2x because the downside of getting them wrong is severe.
- [ ] **Define your "automatic no" triggers** - Any single criterion that, if scored 1, means you walk away regardless of total score. Reputation and values alignment should probably be automatic nos.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Score your current partnerships against your criteria** - How do they hold up? This often reveals partnerships that should be restructured or gracefully ended.

## Application Process

- [ ] **Build a partnership proposal template** - When someone approaches you (or you approach them), capture: What they offer, what they want, how it works, what success looks like, and what happens if it does not work out.
- [ ] **Define a trial period** - Every new partnership starts with a 90-day trial. Defined scope, clear metrics, and a review date. This protects both parties from over-committing before the relationship is proven.
- [ ] **Set review dates** - Every partnership gets a formal review at 90 days, 6 months, and annually. No partnership should run on autopilot indefinitely.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A written criteria framework you can apply to any partnership opportunity
- A scoring system that brings objectivity to relationship decisions
- Clear non-negotiable standards that prevent costly mismatches
- An audit of existing partnerships against your criteria
- A trial period structure that reduces risk on new partnerships

## Coach Notes

The trial period is the most important practical element. Many clients resist it because they do not want to offend a potential partner by suggesting one. Reframe it: professionals expect due diligence. A partner who is offended by a trial period probably would not survive a real evaluation either. Also watch for clients who have partnerships running purely on goodwill with no structure, no metrics, and no review dates. These relationships drift and often become net negatives without anyone noticing. Help them formalise the good partnerships and gracefully exit the underperforming ones.
