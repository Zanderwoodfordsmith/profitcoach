# Growth Lever Analysis

## What to Do

Map your expansion options across two dimensions: depth (getting more from existing customers) and breadth (reaching new markets or segments). Then score each option for impact, feasibility, and risk so you can focus on the growth levers that deliver the highest return with the lowest complexity cost.

## Why It Matters

Most business owners default to one growth approach - usually chasing new customers - because it feels like the most obvious path. But Jay Abraham's three ways to grow a business still hold true: get more customers, increase transaction value, or increase purchase frequency. The cheapest, fastest growth almost always comes from depth - selling more to people who already trust you. Breadth is necessary for long-term growth, but it costs more and takes longer. Without a structured analysis, you miss the easy wins and overspend on the hard ones.

## How to Use This

Map all your growth options first, then score and prioritise. Resist the urge to jump straight to the most exciting idea. The most exciting idea is rarely the most impactful.

---

## Depth Levers (Existing Customers)

- [ ] **Calculate your customer retention rate** - What percentage of customers stay year to year? A 5% improvement in retention often delivers more profit than a 20% increase in new customers.
- [ ] **Identify upsell and cross-sell opportunities** - What else could your existing customers buy from you? What problems do they have that you could solve but currently do not?
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **List your top 10 customers and ask: "Are they buying everything they should be?"** - Most businesses have immediate revenue sitting in existing relationships. One conversation could unlock it.
- [ ] **Assess your pricing power** - When did you last raise prices? Could you increase by 5-10% without losing customers? For most service businesses, the answer is yes, but fear prevents the conversation.
- [ ] **Map referral potential** - How many of your existing customers actively refer new business? What would it take to increase that? A structured referral programme costs almost nothing to run.

## Breadth Levers (New Markets)

- [ ] **Identify adjacent customer segments** - Who is similar to your ideal customer but not currently being served? What would it take to reach them?
- [ ] **Assess geographic expansion options** - Could you serve a wider geographic area? What infrastructure would that require? Is the opportunity worth the complexity?
- [ ] **Evaluate new product or service opportunities** - What could you add that serves a different need for a different audience? Be cautious here - new products for new markets is the highest risk combination.
- [ ] **Consider channel expansion** - Could you reach new customers through different channels? Partnerships, online delivery, licensing, white-labelling?

## Scoring and Prioritisation

- [ ] **Score each lever on four criteria** - (1) Revenue potential (1-10), (2) Cost to implement (1-10, where 10 is cheapest), (3) Time to results (1-10, where 10 is fastest), (4) Alignment with core strengths (1-10).
- [ ] **Plot your top options on a 2x2 matrix** - Impact (high/low) vs Effort (high/low). Focus on high impact, low effort first. Park high impact, high effort for later. Drop low impact entirely.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Pick the single growth lever you could activate this month** - The one that scores highest on the matrix with the lowest barrier to starting. Do not wait for the perfect plan.
- [ ] **Create a sequenced growth roadmap** - Order your chosen levers by quarter. Depth levers typically come first (faster, cheaper), followed by breadth levers as capacity and confidence build.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A complete map of growth options across depth and breadth
- Honest scoring that separates exciting ideas from genuinely impactful ones
- A prioritised shortlist of 3-5 growth levers to pursue
- A sequenced roadmap showing which levers to activate and when
- Immediate action on at least one quick-win growth lever

## Coach Notes

The most common mistake is chasing breadth when depth opportunities are untapped. Challenge clients who want to expand into new markets before they have maximised their existing customer base. Ask: "If you could only grow from existing customers for the next 12 months, what would you do?" This question often reveals surprising opportunities. The second mistake is trying to pull too many levers at once. Each growth initiative needs resources and management attention. Two well-executed initiatives beat five half-hearted ones.
