# Market Entry Framework

## What to Do

Build a systematic process for evaluating new markets before you commit resources. For every potential market, assess strategic fit, resource requirements, competitive position, and scalability. This prevents the common pattern of chasing a shiny opportunity, investing heavily, then discovering it does not work after the money is spent.

## Why It Matters

Entering the wrong market is one of the most expensive mistakes a growing business can make. It is not just the direct cost. It is the distraction, the team fatigue, the brand dilution, and the opportunity cost of what you could have done instead. Clayton Christensen's research shows that most market entry failures are not caused by bad products - they are caused by misunderstanding the market. A structured evaluation catches the problems before you commit, not after.

## How to Use This

Run every potential new market through this framework. If it does not pass all four tests, do not enter. Discipline here saves enormous pain later.

---

## Strategic Fit Assessment

- [ ] **Define why you are considering this market** - Is it pull (customers are asking for it) or push (you want to grow into it)? Pull is safer. Push requires more validation.
- [ ] **Check alignment with your strategic arena** - Does this market sit within or adjacent to your defined competitive space? The further you drift from your arena, the higher the risk.
- [ ] **Assess brand fit** - Will your brand credibility transfer to this market? If your reputation was built serving manufacturers, entering professional services requires a different kind of trust.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Ask yourself: "Would I enter this market if I were starting from scratch today?"** - If the answer is no, you are probably being pulled by momentum rather than strategy.

## Resource Requirements

- [ ] **Estimate total investment needed** - Not just money, but time, attention, and team capacity. Be honest about hidden costs: learning curve, relationship building, marketing in a new space, and inevitable mistakes.
- [ ] **Identify capability gaps** - What skills, knowledge, or infrastructure do you need that you do not currently have? How long will it take to build or buy?
- [ ] **Assess impact on existing operations** - Will entering this market take resources away from your core business? Can your current team absorb the additional workload, or do you need to hire?
- [ ] **Define the minimum viable commitment** - What is the smallest investment you can make to test whether this market works before going all in?

## Competitive Position

- [ ] **Map existing competitors in this market** - Who already serves these customers? How entrenched are they? What would it take to win business from them?
- [ ] **Identify your entry advantage** - Why would customers in this market choose a newcomer over established providers? You need a compelling answer. "We are good at what we do" is not enough.
- [ ] **Assess barriers to entry** - Regulatory requirements, established relationships, technical standards, long sales cycles. The higher the barriers, the longer and more expensive your entry will be.
- [ ] **Evaluate competitive response** - How will incumbents react to your entry? Will they compete on price, service, or relationships? Can you withstand that response?

## Scalability Check

- [ ] **Estimate market size and growth trajectory** - Is this market growing, stable, or declining? Is it large enough to justify the entry investment?
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Talk to 3-5 potential customers in this market before committing anything** - Ask what they need, what frustrates them about current providers, and what would make them switch. Real conversations beat market research reports.
- [ ] **Define success metrics and timeline** - What does a successful entry look like at 6 months, 12 months, and 24 months? Be specific about revenue targets, customer numbers, and profitability.
- [ ] **Set a kill date** - If you have not hit defined milestones by a specific date, you will exit. Decide this before you start so sunk cost bias does not keep you going too long.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A structured evaluation of each potential market across four dimensions
- Clear understanding of resource requirements before committing
- Honest assessment of competitive position and right to win
- Defined success metrics and a kill date to prevent indefinite investment in underperforming markets
- Confidence to say no to markets that do not pass the test

## Coach Notes

The biggest risk in market entry is overconfidence. Owners who have succeeded in one market assume their skills transfer directly. Sometimes they do, sometimes they do not. Push for evidence over assumption. The "talk to 3-5 potential customers" step is the single most valuable thing they can do, and the step most often skipped. Insist on it. Also watch for the "strategic creep" pattern where a client enters one new market, then another, then another, until they are spread across five markets and dominant in none. Each entry should be fully validated and resourced before the next one is considered.
