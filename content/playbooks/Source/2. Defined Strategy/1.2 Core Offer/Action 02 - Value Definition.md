# Value Definition

## What to Do

Define the specific transformation your core offer delivers — in your client's language, not yours. Then stress-test it using Hormozi's Value Equation to make sure it is genuinely compelling, not just well-described.

## Why It Matters

Most business owners describe their offer in terms of what they do ("we provide marketing consultancy") rather than what the client gets ("you will have a marketing system that generates 20 qualified leads per month without you doing any of the work"). The first is a service description. The second is a value proposition. Clients pay for the second.

Alex Hormozi's Value Equation makes this concrete:

**Value = (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood) ÷ (Time Delay × Effort Required)**

To increase value: make the dream outcome bigger, increase their confidence it will work, reduce how long it takes, and reduce how much effort they need to put in. Your value definition should hit all four.

## How to Use This

Work through each step using your core offer (identified in the Offer Audit). If you have not done the Offer Audit yet, do that first.

---

## Step 1: Define the Transformation

- [ ] **Describe your client's "Before" state in their words** — What does their life or business look like before working with you? What are they frustrated by, worried about, or stuck on? Use the exact language they would use with a friend, not marketing speak.
- [ ] **Describe the "After" state in their words** — What does their life or business look like after working with you? What has changed? What do they have now that they did not have before? Be specific — "they feel better" is not a transformation. "They have a reliable pipeline of 15+ leads per month and have stopped doing cold outreach" is.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win · **Ask 3 past clients: "What changed for you after working with us?"** — Their answers will be better than anything you write yourself. Use their exact words.

## Step 2: Apply the Value Equation

- [ ] **Dream Outcome — Is the end result something they desperately want?** — Rate it honestly. "Slightly improved marketing" is weak. "A marketing system that fills your calendar without you lifting a finger" is a dream outcome. If your outcome is not exciting enough, you may be solving the wrong problem or describing it too timidly.
- [ ] **Perceived Likelihood — Do they believe it will actually work for them?** — This is about credibility and proof. Do you have case studies, testimonials, data, or a guarantee? What evidence reduces their risk? List your 3 strongest proof points.
- [ ] **Time Delay — How quickly do they see results?** — The faster the result, the more valuable the offer. Can you deliver a quick win in the first week? The first month? If results take 6 months with nothing visible in between, you have a time delay problem. Identify your earliest visible result.
- [ ] **Effort Required — How much work do they have to do?** — Less effort from the client = more valuable offer. What can you do for them instead of teaching them to do it? Where can you reduce their workload? The best offers feel easy to the buyer.

## Step 3: Write Your Value Statement

- [ ] **Combine everything into one statement: "I help [specific person] go from [Before] to [After] in [timeframe] by [how you do it differently]"** — Keep it under 30 words. This is what you say when someone asks "what do you do?"
- [ ] **Test it with someone who does not know your business** — Do they immediately understand who it is for and what they get? If they say "oh, I know someone who needs that" — you have nailed it.
- [ ] **Compare it to your top 3 competitors' messaging** — Is yours more specific? More compelling? More credible? If your statement could be swapped onto a competitor's website without anyone noticing, it is not distinctive enough.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A clear Before/After transformation described in your client's own language
- A value proposition stress-tested against all four elements of the Value Equation
- A value statement under 30 words that you can use in every conversation, on every page, and in every pitch
- Clarity on where your offer is strong (dream outcome, speed, proof) and where it needs improvement

## Coach Notes

The most common mistake is describing features instead of transformation. Push the client past "we offer X" to "after working with us, you will have Y." The transformation is always about the client's life, not your deliverable.

If the Value Equation reveals a weakness (e.g., time delay is long, effort required is high), that is valuable product design insight — not just a marketing problem. Help them think about how to restructure their delivery to address the weakness, not just describe it differently.

Pair this with the Offer Audit (do that first) and the Pricing Reality Check (do that after).
