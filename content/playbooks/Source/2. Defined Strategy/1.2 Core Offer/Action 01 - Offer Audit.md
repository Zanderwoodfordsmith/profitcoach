# Offer Audit

## What to Do

List everything you currently sell, then ruthlessly evaluate what stays, what goes, and what becomes your primary focus. Most businesses need to subtract before they can multiply.

## Why It Matters

Alex Hormozi's core insight is that a "Grand Slam Offer" — one offer, one market, one channel — beats a scattered portfolio every time. Most business owners doing £200K–£5M have accumulated services over the years like barnacles on a hull. Each one made sense at the time. Together, they create confusion for clients, complexity in delivery, and exhaustion for the owner. The audit reveals what is actually driving the business and what is just consuming energy.

## How to Use This

Be honest. This is not about what you could sell or what you used to sell — it is about what you should sell going forward.

---

## List Everything

- [ ] **Write down every product, service, or offering you currently sell** — Include everything: main services, add-ons, one-off projects, consulting, retainers, products. If someone has paid you for it in the last 12 months, it goes on the list.
- [ ] **For each one, note: annual revenue, rough profit margin, and hours per delivery** — Exact numbers are not needed. Rough is fine. You are looking for patterns, not precision.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win · **Circle the one that generates the most profit per hour of effort** — Not the highest revenue. The best return on your time and energy. This is your starting point for a core offer.

## Evaluate Each Offering

For every item on your list, answer these questions:

- [ ] **Does it serve your ideal client?** — If this offering attracts clients you do not want, it is pulling your business off course regardless of the revenue it generates.
- [ ] **Could you explain it in one sentence?** — If you need a paragraph to explain what it is, your clients are confused too. Confusion kills sales.
- [ ] **Does it lead to repeat business or referrals?** — Offerings that generate one-time transactions are worth less than offerings that create ongoing relationships. The best core offer naturally creates a "what's next?"
- [ ] **Would you be excited to sell 10x more of this?** — If the thought of scaling this offering fills you with dread, it should not be your core offer — no matter how profitable it is today.
- [ ] **Is it difficult for competitors to replicate?** — April Dunford's positioning principle: if a competitor can offer the exact same thing, you have no competitive advantage. Your core offer should leverage something distinctive about you.

## Make the Cut

- [ ] **Categorise each offering: Core, Support, or Cut** — Core: this is (or could become) your primary offer. Support: this adds value alongside the core offer. Cut: this needs to go.
- [ ] **For "Cut" items, decide: stop immediately, wind down over 90 days, or refer to someone else** — Not everything needs to die today. But everything in the Cut category needs an exit plan.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win · **Identify one offering you can stop this month** — The one that causes the most hassle relative to the revenue it generates. Dropping it frees up energy for what matters.
- [ ] **For "Support" items, check they genuinely support the core** — Support offerings should make the core offer more valuable, not distract from it. If a support offering could exist independently, it is probably a separate business — not a support.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A clear picture of everything you sell and what each one actually contributes
- One identified core offer (or strong candidate) to build around
- A list of offerings to cut or wind down — with a plan for each
- Reduced complexity in your business that will make marketing, sales, and delivery simpler
- The foundation for your Value Definition and Pricing work

## Coach Notes

Most clients resist cutting offerings because each one represents a revenue stream they are afraid to lose. Help them calculate the true cost — not just the revenue an offering generates, but the time, complexity, and opportunity cost it creates. Often the maths reveals that cutting a £30K/year service frees up enough capacity to win £100K of core offer work.

The client who says "but I do loads of different things" is exactly the client who needs this most. Breadth is not a strategy — it is a symptom of not having chosen.

Do this action before Value Definition and Pricing. You need to know what you are keeping before you refine how you position and price it.
