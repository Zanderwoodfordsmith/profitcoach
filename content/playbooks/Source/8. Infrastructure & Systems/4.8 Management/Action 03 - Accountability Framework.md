# Build Your Accountability Framework

## What to Do

Create a visible, consistent system for tracking performance and addressing gaps. This covers what gets measured, how often it's reviewed, and what happens when targets are missed. Accountability isn't about punishment. It's about making expectations clear, tracking against them, and having honest conversations when things aren't on track.

## Why It Matters

Without a framework, accountability is inconsistent. Some people get away with underperformance because nobody notices. Others get called out unfairly because their work is more visible. Most owners avoid accountability conversations entirely because there's no structured way to have them. This framework gives you the structure. It makes the conversation about the numbers and the expectations, not about personalities. That makes it easier for everyone.

## How to Use This

Work through the steps in order. Set up the system first, then start using it with one person before rolling it out to the whole team.

---

## Step 1: Define What You're Measuring

- [ ] **List the key outputs for each role** - Not activities (how busy they are) but outputs (what they produce). Sales: revenue booked, deals closed. Operations: projects delivered on time. Marketing: qualified leads generated.
- [ ] **Set 2-3 measurable targets per person** - Specific numbers with deadlines. "Improve customer satisfaction" is not a target. "Achieve NPS of 8+ by end of Q2" is a target.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Ask each team member what they think their key metrics should be** - People who set their own targets are more likely to hit them. Use their input, then adjust if needed.
- [ ] **Make sure targets are visible** - Everyone should be able to see their own targets and progress at any time. Spreadsheet, dashboard, whiteboard - the format doesn't matter. Visibility does.

## Step 2: Set Up the Review Rhythm

- [ ] **Schedule regular one-to-ones** - Weekly or fortnightly, 15-30 minutes, same time each week. Not optional. Not "when we get a chance." Fixed in the calendar.
- [ ] **Use a consistent one-to-one format** - Three sections: (1) What were your targets? (2) What did you deliver? (3) What's getting in the way? Same structure every time. No surprises.
- [ ] **Track performance over time** - A simple spreadsheet showing targets vs actuals week by week. Patterns matter more than individual weeks. One bad week isn't a problem. Five bad weeks is a trend.
- [ ] **Separate performance reviews from catch-ups** - The weekly one-to-one is about this week's work. Deeper performance conversations (development, career progression, pay) happen quarterly or twice yearly. Don't mix them.

## Step 3: Address Gaps

- [ ] **Define your escalation process** - What happens when someone consistently misses targets? Step 1: coaching conversation (what's going on, how can I help?). Step 2: performance improvement plan (clear expectations, fixed timeline). Step 3: consequence (role change or exit).
- [ ] **Have the conversation early** - Most owners wait too long. The first miss should trigger a supportive conversation, not silence followed by an explosion at miss number five.
- [ ] **Document everything** - Brief notes after each one-to-one. What was discussed, what was agreed, what the next actions are. This protects both parties and creates a clear record.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Have one honest performance conversation this week** - Pick the person whose performance you've been avoiding discussing. Use the one-to-one format above. You'll feel better, and so will they (uncertainty is worse than a direct conversation).

## Step 4: Recognise and Reward

- [ ] **Acknowledge strong performance publicly** - In team meetings, in Slack, in writing. People who consistently hit their targets should know it's noticed.
- [ ] **Link performance to progression** - People who consistently deliver should get more responsibility, development, and eventually more pay. Make this connection explicit.
- [ ] **Address the "quiet performer" problem** - Some of your best people are invisible because they just get on with it. The framework should surface them, not just the noisy ones.

---

## Expected Outcome

- Every team member has clear, measurable targets they understand
- Performance is reviewed consistently, not sporadically
- Underperformance is caught and addressed within 2-3 weeks, not 2-3 months
- Strong performers feel recognised and stay longer
- You have a clear, documented process for managing people - no more winging it

## Coach Notes

Most owners hate accountability conversations. They feel like confrontation. Reframe it: "You're not criticising someone. You're helping them see where they stand so they can improve." The framework does the heavy lifting - it moves the conversation from personal judgement to objective measurement. Start with yourself. Set your own targets, track them, review them. When the team sees you holding yourself accountable, they'll take the framework seriously. If a client resists one-to-ones, ask: "How would you feel if your coach only checked in with you once a quarter instead of every week?" They'll get the point.
