# Build Your Decision Authority Matrix

## What to Do

Document who can make what decisions, at what level, and with what limits. The output is a simple matrix that tells every person in the business: "Here's what you can decide on your own, here's what you need to check with someone about, and here's what needs to go to the owner." No more guessing. No more bottlenecks caused by people waiting for permission they didn't need.

## Why It Matters

In most small businesses, decision-making works like this: everyone asks the owner. For everything. From hiring decisions to whether they can buy a new keyboard. The owner becomes the bottleneck, the team becomes passive, and growth stalls because nothing moves without one person's approval. A decision authority matrix fixes this by making the rules explicit. People move faster because they know their boundaries. You move faster because you only see the decisions that genuinely need you.

## How to Use This

Build a simple table or spreadsheet. Rows are decision categories. Columns are authority levels. Work through the checklist to populate it.

---

## Decision Authority Matrix Checklist

- [ ] **List your common decision categories** - Spending, hiring, client issues, pricing, scheduling, process changes, vendor selection, refunds, and anything else that regularly needs a decision. Aim for 10-15 categories.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Think about the last 10 decisions people asked you to make** - How many of those could someone else have made? That list tells you exactly where to start delegating authority.
- [ ] **Define three authority levels** - Level A: Decide and act (no approval needed). Level B: Decide, then inform (tell me what you did). Level C: Recommend, then wait for approval (bring it to me before acting).
- [ ] **Set spending thresholds** - Example: Under £100, anyone can approve. £100-£500, team leads approve. £500-£2,000, you approve. Over £2,000, discuss with partner/advisor first. Adjust the numbers to fit your business.
- [ ] **Assign authority for hiring decisions** - Who can approve a new hire? Who can approve a contractor? Who handles recruitment process decisions? Hiring is one of the biggest bottleneck areas.
- [ ] **Assign authority for client-facing decisions** - Who can issue a refund? Who can offer a discount? Who can change scope or delivery timelines? These come up constantly and slow teams down when they need approval.
- [ ] **Assign authority for operational decisions** - Who can change a process? Who can approve time off? Who can reassign work? These feel small but accumulate into massive bottlenecks.
- [ ] **Assign authority for pricing decisions** - Who can adjust pricing? Who can create a custom package? Who can offer payment terms? Be specific.
- [ ] **Check for "ghost authority"** - Where do people already make decisions without formal permission? Make those official. If someone's been handling refunds for a year, give them the authority properly.
- [ ] **Create the matrix document** - Simple table: Decision type in rows, roles across columns, authority level (A/B/C) in each cell. One page maximum.
- [ ] **Share it with the team** - Walk through it together. Answer questions. Adjust based on their feedback. This only works if people know it exists and trust it.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Identify three decisions you currently make that could be Level A for a team member** - Hand those over today. Instant time savings for you, instant empowerment for them.

## Expected Outcome

- Team members know exactly what they can decide without asking
- Decisions happen faster because people aren't waiting in a queue for your approval
- You spend less time on low-stakes decisions and more time on high-impact ones
- Fewer interruptions to your day for things that don't need you
- New team members ramp up faster because the rules are written down, not tribal knowledge

## Coach Notes

The hardest part is letting go. Most owners know intellectually that they should delegate decisions, but emotionally they struggle to trust others with them. Start small. Hand over low-risk decisions first (supplies purchasing, scheduling). As trust builds, expand. If an owner resists, ask: "What's the worst that could happen if [person] made this decision wrong? And how does that compare to the cost of you making every decision yourself?" The maths usually makes the case. Review the matrix every 6 months. As people grow and the business changes, authority levels should shift too.
