# Map Your Management Layers

## What to Do

Define the management structure your business needs right now - not the org chart you'll need in five years. This is about getting clear on who reports to whom, who makes which decisions, and where the gaps are. The output is a simple map showing your actual management layers, who sits in them, and what each layer is responsible for.

Most businesses between £200K and £5M need 2-3 management layers at most. More than that creates bureaucracy. Fewer than that means everything comes back to you.

## Why It Matters

Without a clear management structure, every decision, every problem, and every question flows to one person: you. That works at £200K. It breaks at £500K. It's completely unsustainable at £1M+. A management layer map shows you what structure you actually need so you can stop being the answer to every question and start building a business that runs without you in the middle of everything.

## How to Use This

Grab a blank sheet of paper or whiteboard. Work through the checklist below. Be honest about how things actually work, not how you wish they worked.

---

## Management Layer Map Checklist

- [ ] **List every person in the business** - Include yourself, full-time, part-time, contractors, and freelancers. Everyone who does work for the business.
- [ ] **Draw who actually reports to whom right now** - Not the official structure. The real one. Who do people actually go to when they need a decision? Who do they ask for help? That's your current structure.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Circle yourself and count the lines coming into you** - If more than 5-6 people report directly to you, you've found your bottleneck. This is the core problem a management layer solves.
- [ ] **Identify the natural team clusters** - People who work together on similar things. Sales and marketing. Operations and fulfilment. Finance and admin. These clusters hint at where management layers need to exist.
- [ ] **Decide how many layers you need** - For most businesses at this stage: Layer 1 is you (owner/MD). Layer 2 is team leads or managers (2-4 people). Layer 3 is everyone else. That's it. Three layers. No more.
- [ ] **Name the Layer 2 roles** - These are your managers or team leads. What areas do they cover? Sales? Operations? Finance? Don't create roles for the sake of it. Only add a manager where there's enough work and enough people to justify one.
- [ ] **Check if the right people are in Layer 2** - Do your current team leads have the skills and temperament to manage others? Being good at the work doesn't mean they can manage people doing the work. Be honest here.
- [ ] **Define what each layer is responsible for** - Layer 1: strategy, direction, and key relationships. Layer 2: execution, team management, and day-to-day decisions. Layer 3: delivery of specific work. Write this down clearly.
- [ ] **Identify gaps** - Where do you need a manager but don't have one? Where are you personally filling a management role that someone else should own? Where is nobody managing at all?
- [ ] **Draw the target structure** - What should your management layers look like in 6-12 months? This is your roadmap. You don't need to fill every gap now, but you need to know where you're heading.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Pick one direct report you could shift to report to a Layer 2 manager instead** - Move one person this week. Reduce your direct reports by one. That's immediate relief.

---

## Expected Outcome

- You have a clear, honest picture of how your business is actually managed
- You know exactly how many management layers you need (probably 2-3)
- Direct reports to you are reduced to a manageable number (5-6 maximum)
- Gaps in management are identified with a plan to fill them
- The team understands who they report to and who makes decisions in their area

## Coach Notes

Most owners at this stage resist adding management layers because "we're too small" or "I like being close to everything." Acknowledge that. Then ask: "How many hours a week do you spend answering questions that someone else could handle?" The number is usually shocking. This isn't about adding bureaucracy. It's about creating enough structure so the owner can focus on the work only they can do. Watch for the temptation to promote the best performer into management. Best salesperson doesn't equal best sales manager. Discuss what management actually requires before making that move.
