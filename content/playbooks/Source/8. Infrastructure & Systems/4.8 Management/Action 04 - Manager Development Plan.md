# Create Your Manager Development Plan

## What to Do

Identify the people in your business who need to become genuine managers and build a specific development plan for each one. This isn't about sending them on a leadership course. It's about equipping them with the practical skills, tools, and mindsets that separate someone who does great work from someone who can get great work out of others.

## Why It Matters

The number one constraint on growth in businesses between £500K and £5M is management capability. You hire great people but then expect them to magically become managers. They don't. They default to doing the work themselves (because they're good at it) instead of managing the people doing the work. The result: the best people burn out, the team stays dependent, and you remain stuck in the middle. Developing your managers is the single highest-leverage investment you can make.

## How to Use This

Identify who needs development, assess where they are now, and build a practical plan for each person. Review progress monthly.

---

## Step 1: Identify Who Needs to Develop

- [ ] **List everyone in a management or team lead role** - Anyone who has people reporting to them, formally or informally.
- [ ] **Identify anyone you want to move into management in the next 12 months** - Include them now so development starts before they need the skills.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Pick the one person whose management development would have the biggest impact on you** - Whose growth would free up the most of your time or unblock the biggest team? Start with them.

## Step 2: Assess Current Capability

- [ ] **Rate each person against core management skills** - Use a simple 1-5 scale for: delegation, giving feedback, running meetings, prioritisation, difficult conversations, hiring, developing their team, and managing their own time.
- [ ] **Ask them to self-assess against the same skills** - The gap between your rating and theirs is revealing. If they rate themselves 5 on delegation but you rate them 2, that's your starting point.
- [ ] **Identify their biggest gap** - Not all 8 skills at once. What's the one thing that would make the biggest difference if they improved it? Focus there first.
- [ ] **Check for mindset issues, not just skill gaps** - Some people struggle with management because they still see themselves as a doer, not a leader. They feel guilty delegating. They jump in to fix things instead of coaching. Mindset work often matters more than skill training.

## Step 3: Build the Development Plan

- [ ] **Set 1-2 specific development goals per quarter** - "Get better at management" is not a goal. "Conduct weekly one-to-ones with all 4 direct reports for 12 consecutive weeks" is a goal.
- [ ] **Assign practical learning activities** - Shadow you in a difficult conversation. Run the team meeting for 4 weeks. Deliver feedback to a team member with you observing. Real practice beats theory.
- [ ] **Schedule regular development check-ins** - Monthly, 30 minutes. Review what they've practiced, what's working, what's hard. This is separate from their operational one-to-one.
- [ ] **Give them management tools** - One-to-one templates, delegation frameworks, meeting agendas. Don't expect them to invent management from scratch. Give them the structure and let them adapt it.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Have each developing manager run their next team meeting solo** - Step back, let them lead, give them feedback afterwards. One meeting is worth a month of reading about leadership.
- [ ] **Connect them with peers** - Other managers in similar roles (inside or outside the business) who they can learn from. Management can be lonely, especially for first-time managers.

## Step 4: Track and Adjust

- [ ] **Review the plan quarterly** - Is the person growing? Are the activities working? Adjust based on what you're seeing.
- [ ] **Look for evidence, not just effort** - Is their team performing better? Are they handling more decisions without you? Are their people developing? These are the real measures of management development.
- [ ] **Be honest about fit** - Not everyone is cut out for management, and that's fine. If someone has been developing for 6-12 months with limited progress, consider whether a senior individual contributor role might suit them better. Being a great specialist is valuable too.

---

## Expected Outcome

- Your managers have clear development plans with specific, practical goals
- Management capability across the business improves visibly within 3-6 months
- You spend less time managing the people who should be managing others
- Team members under developing managers report better leadership and clearer direction
- You have a bench of capable managers ready for the business's next stage of growth

## Coach Notes

This is a long game. Management development takes 6-18 months to show real results. Set that expectation upfront. The owner will want fast results, and some quick wins will come (better meetings, clearer delegation), but deep capability takes time. Watch for the owner who says "I'll just do it myself because it's faster." That's the exact behaviour that created the management gap in the first place. Every time they jump in, they're robbing their manager of a learning opportunity. Help them see that short-term slowdown creates long-term speed.
