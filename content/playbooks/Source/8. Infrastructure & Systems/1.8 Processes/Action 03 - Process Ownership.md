# Process Ownership

## What to Do

Assign a named owner to every documented process and set up a simple review schedule so your documentation stays current as the business evolves. Unowned processes decay quickly — within months they become outdated guides that nobody trusts or follows.

## Why It Matters

Documentation without ownership is temporary. Jenyns makes this point clearly: the value of a process document is not in writing it — it is in maintaining it. Businesses change constantly: software updates, team members change, client expectations evolve. A process document from 6 months ago that has not been reviewed might actively cause problems because the process has changed but the document has not.

## How to Use This

This is quick to set up but only works if you follow through on the review schedule. Keep it simple.

---

## Assign Ownership

- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win · **For each documented process, assign one person as the owner** — The owner does not have to be the person who does the work. They are the person responsible for keeping the document accurate and up to date. In a small team, this might be you for everything — that is fine at Level 1.
- [ ] **The owner's responsibilities are:**
  - Update the document when the process changes
  - Review the document on schedule (see below)
  - Flag when the process needs redesigning, not just updating
  - Answer questions from anyone following the process
- [ ] **Write the owner's name on the document** — Literally at the top of the process document. No ambiguity about who maintains it.

## Set the Review Schedule

- [ ] **Choose a review frequency for each process** — Customer-facing processes: review monthly. Internal operations: review quarterly. Support/admin: review every 6 months.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win · **Add process review dates to your calendar** — Not as a "I should probably look at this" reminder. As a 15-minute calendar block that actually happens. Treat it like a recurring meeting.
- [ ] **Each review asks 3 questions:**
  - Has anything changed in how we actually do this? (If yes, update the document)
  - Has anyone had trouble following this process? (If yes, clarify the documentation)
  - Is this process still necessary? (If not, archive it)

## Track It

- [ ] **Create a simple process register** — A single document or spreadsheet that lists: Process Name, Owner, Last Reviewed, Next Review Date. This is your control panel for all your documented processes.
- [ ] **Review the register monthly** — Takes 5 minutes. Are any reviews overdue? Are any processes missing from the register? This keeps the whole system honest.

---

## Expected Outcome

- Every documented process has a named owner
- A review schedule that keeps documentation current without being burdensome
- A process register that gives you oversight of all documented processes in one place
- Documentation that stays trustworthy because it is maintained, not just created
- The foundation for scaling — as you add team members, they inherit maintained processes

## Coach Notes

This action is the one most likely to be skipped because it feels like admin rather than progress. Reframe it: the 5-minute monthly register check is what prevents the 5-hour crisis when someone follows an outdated process and delivers wrong.

For solo operators, they own everything — and that is fine. The value of this action for solos is the review schedule. Without it, they write processes once and never update them, which means in 6 months the documentation is useless and they are back to doing everything from memory.

As the team grows, ownership transfers from the founder to the person closest to the work. This is one of the first real delegation moments — and it is a safe one because it is low risk and highly structured.
