# Document Your Critical Processes

## What to Do

Take the top 3 processes identified in your Process Audit and document them well enough that someone else could follow them and get a consistent result. Not perfect documentation — minimum viable documentation that reduces your risk and frees up your time.

## Why It Matters

Jenyns' SYSTEMology method is built on a simple insight: the best person to run a process is rarely the best person to document it. The expert does things on autopilot and skips the "obvious" steps. But those obvious steps are exactly what a new person needs. The documentation method below captures the real process — including the bits the expert does not think to mention — by recording first and writing second.

Gerber's Franchise Prototype principle applies here too: document every process as if you were going to franchise the business. Not because you will, but because the discipline of making it teachable makes it reliable.

## How to Use This

Work through the 3 processes you identified in the Process Audit. Do them one at a time — complete one before starting the next.

---

## For Each Process

### Capture

- [ ] **Record yourself doing the process** — Screen recording for digital work, video for physical work, or audio narration as you go through it. Talk through every step including the "obvious" ones. Jenyns' method: the expert records, someone else documents.
- [ ] **If recording is not practical, walk someone through it while they take notes** — Have a team member or VA watch you do it and write down every step. They will catch things you skip because "everyone knows that."
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win · **Alternatively, write it as a simple numbered checklist** — Step 1, Step 2, Step 3. No paragraphs, no explanations, just "do this, then do this, then do this." You can add detail later.

### Document

- [ ] **Turn the recording or notes into a step-by-step checklist** — Numbered steps in order. Each step should be a single action, not a paragraph. "Open the client folder in Google Drive" not "Access the relevant documentation."
- [ ] **Add decision points** — Where in the process do you make a choice? "If the client has paid, proceed to Step 5. If not, send payment reminder email." These branching points are where mistakes happen if not documented.
- [ ] **Include the quality check** — How do you know the process was done correctly? What does "done well" look like? Add this as the final step.
- [ ] **Note the tools used** — Which software, templates, or resources are needed? Link to them directly in the document so the person following the process does not have to hunt for them.

### Test

- [ ] **Have someone else follow your documentation without your help** — Give them the checklist and walk away. Where they get stuck reveals gaps in your documentation.
- [ ] **Note every question they ask** — Each question is a missing step or unclear instruction. Add the answer to the document.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win · **Update the document based on the test** — One round of testing and updating typically catches 80% of the gaps. The document does not need to be perfect — it needs to be good enough that someone can follow it.

## Expected Outcome

- 3 critical processes documented as step-by-step checklists
- Documentation tested by someone other than the expert and refined
- Reduced dependency on you or any single person for these processes
- A template and method you can use to document additional processes over time
- The beginning of a process library that grows with your business

## Coach Notes

The biggest barrier is perfectionism. Clients want the documentation to be comprehensive and beautiful. Push for "good enough to follow" — a rough checklist that works is infinitely more valuable than a polished document that never gets written.

Jenyns recommends documenting one process per week as an ongoing habit. After 12 weeks, you have 12 documented processes and your business is fundamentally more resilient. Frame it as a weekly habit, not a project.

If the client does not have anyone to test the documentation with, they can test it themselves — wait a week, then follow their own checklist as if they were new. The gaps will be obvious because they will have forgotten the "obvious" steps.
