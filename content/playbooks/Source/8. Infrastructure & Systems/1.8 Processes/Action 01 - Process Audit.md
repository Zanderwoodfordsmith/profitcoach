# Process Audit

## What to Do

Identify every key workflow in your business and assess which ones live only in people's heads, which are partially documented, and which are properly systematised. This reveals your biggest risks and shows you where to focus your documentation effort.

## Why It Matters

Michael Gerber's E-Myth insight is foundational: most small businesses are not businesses — they are jobs the owner has created for themselves. The reason is that every critical process depends on one person's memory and expertise. When that person is unavailable, sick, or leaves, the process breaks. David Jenyns builds on this with SYSTEMology: you do not need to document everything. You need to document the things that touch customers, generate revenue, or could break the business if done wrong. The audit identifies which processes those are.

## How to Use This

List your processes, assess each one, and prioritise which to document first. This is a diagnostic — you are not documenting anything yet, just mapping what needs attention.

---

## List Your Processes

- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win · **Write down every process you can think of in 10 minutes** — Sales, marketing, delivery, invoicing, hiring, onboarding, client communication, purchasing, reporting, quality control. Brain-dump everything. You will organise later.
- [ ] **Group them into 4 categories:**
  - **Customer-facing** — Anything the client sees, touches, or experiences
  - **Revenue-generating** — Anything that directly brings in money
  - **Operations** — Internal workflows that keep things running
  - **Support** — Admin, finance, HR, IT

## Assess Each Process

For every process on your list, answer these 3 questions:

- [ ] **Is it documented?** — Yes (written down and up to date), Partial (something exists but it is outdated or incomplete), or No (lives entirely in someone's head).
- [ ] **Who knows how to do it?** — If the answer is one person (especially you), that process is fragile. If it is "everyone sort of knows," that means no one does it consistently.
- [ ] **What happens if it fails?** — High impact (client loses trust, revenue stops, or something breaks badly), Medium (causes delays or extra work), or Low (inconvenient but not damaging).

## Prioritise

- [ ] **Flag every process that is: undocumented + one person + high impact** — These are your critical risks. They are the processes that can break your business and no one else can step in. Document these first.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win · **Pick the top 3 most critical processes** — You will document these in the next action. Do not try to do all of them at once — Jenyns recommends starting with your "Critical Client Flow" (the core delivery process) and expanding from there.
- [ ] **Note any processes that are documented but outdated** — These need updating, not writing from scratch. Often easier and faster than creating new documentation.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A complete map of all key processes in your business
- A clear picture of which processes are documented, partially documented, or undocumented
- Identified critical risks — the processes that would break the business if the key person disappeared
- A prioritised list of the top 3 processes to document first
- The foundation for your documentation work in the next action

## Coach Notes

This audit often produces an "oh no" moment when clients realise how much of their business lives only in their head. That reaction is useful — it creates urgency to start documenting. But channel it into action on 3 processes, not panic about all of them.

Jenyns' key principle: you do not need to document everything. Start with the processes that touch customers and generate revenue. Internal processes can wait until the external ones are solid.

If a client says "I don't have time to document processes," point out that they are currently spending hours explaining, fixing, and re-doing work because processes are not documented. Documentation saves time — it does not cost it.
