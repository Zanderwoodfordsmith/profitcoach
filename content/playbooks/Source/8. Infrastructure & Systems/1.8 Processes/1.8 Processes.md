## **What This Is**

If it lives in your head, it is fragile. Processes are not bureaucracy — they are leverage. When your business knowledge sits only in memory, every mistake is a crisis, every holiday is a risk, and every new person needs months of hand-holding.

This playbook is about getting your essential workflows out of your head and onto paper. Not to create red tape, but to create reliability. When key tasks are documented, mistakes drop, quality improves, and your business becomes less dependent on any single person — including you.

This is not about documenting everything you do. It is about documenting the things that matter most: the workflows that touch customers, generate revenue, or could break the business if done wrong.

---

## **What It Looks Like**

### **🔴 When this is broken**

Everything depends on who is in the office that day. You constantly explain the same tasks over and over. Mistakes happen regularly because people "forgot that step" or "didn't know we do it that way." Quality varies wildly depending on who does the work. When key people are away, urgent work sits waiting because no one else knows the process.

### **🟠 When this is OK**

Some things are documented but most exist only in people's heads. You have written down the obvious steps but missed the nuances that make the difference. New team members take ages to get up to speed because they have to learn by watching and asking questions. You know processes need documenting but only get around to it when something goes wrong.

### **🟢 When this is working**

Your essential workflows are documented, up to date, and actually used by your team. New people can follow a checklist and get consistent results. Quality is predictable across different people doing the same work. When someone is away or leaves, the work continues without drama. You spend time improving processes, not explaining them repeatedly.

## **Things to Think About**

What knowledge exists only in your memory? Walk through your key workflows and ask: if I was not here tomorrow, who would know how to do this properly? The gaps you find are your biggest risks. Start there.

Where do mistakes keep happening? Recurring errors are usually process problems, not people problems. If the same mistake happens more than twice, you need a checklist, not better staff.

Is quality consistent across different people? When the same task produces different results depending on who does it, that is a process gap. Good processes create consistent outcomes regardless of who runs them.

Who owns keeping processes current? Documents that nobody maintains become outdated fast. Every process needs an owner — someone responsible for updating it when things change.

What happens when someone new starts? If onboarding means sitting with someone for weeks while they learn by watching, your processes are not doing their job. Good processes turn learning from art into science.

## **The Plays Inside This Playbook**

When you open this playbook in the Profit System, you will find:

Play 1: Process Audit — Identify and list all the key workflows that currently exist only in people's heads. Focus on customer-facing processes, revenue-generating activities, and anything that could break the business if done wrong.

Play 2: Priority Process Documentation — Document your most critical 3-5 processes first using simple checklists and workflow diagrams. Cover the what, when, who, and quality standards that make the difference between good and poor execution.

Play 3: Process Testing & Refinement — Test your documented processes with team members to find gaps, unclear steps, and missing details. Refine them until someone new can follow them and get consistent results.

Play 4: Process Ownership System — Assign ownership for maintaining and updating each process. Create a simple review schedule so processes stay current as your business evolves.

## **Quick Wins**

This week: Pick your most important customer-facing process. Write down every step from start to finish, even the "obvious" ones. Ask someone else to follow your checklist and see where they get stuck.

This week: Look at the last three mistakes that happened in your business. Ask: would a simple checklist have prevented this? If yes, write that checklist today.

This week: Identify one process that only you know how to do properly. Spend 30 minutes documenting it well enough that someone else could handle it in an emergency.
