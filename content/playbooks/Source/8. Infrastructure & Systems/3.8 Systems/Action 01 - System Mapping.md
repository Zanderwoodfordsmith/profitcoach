# Map Your Systems

## What to Do

Map how work actually moves through your business, from the moment a lead comes in to the moment a customer is fully served and paid for. You are drawing a picture of reality, not how you think it works or how you wish it worked. Most businesses are surprised by how disconnected their systems are once they see it all on paper.

## Why It Matters

You cannot improve a system you cannot see. Most business owners carry a rough mental model of how their business works, but that model is usually incomplete, outdated, or wrong. System mapping exposes the gaps, bottlenecks, and manual workarounds that slow everything down. It also reveals the single points of failure where one person leaving or one tool breaking would cause chaos.

## How to Use This

Get a large whiteboard, a wall with sticky notes, or a digital tool like Miro. You need space. Work through each workflow below and draw the actual path work takes.

---

## Core Workflow Mapping

- [ ] **Map your sales workflow** - From first contact to closed deal. Every step, every handoff, every tool used. Include: where leads come from, how they get qualified, who contacts them, how proposals get created, and how deals get closed.
- [ ] **Map your delivery workflow** - From closed deal to completed work. How does a new customer get onboarded? Who does the work? How is progress tracked? When and how does the customer get what they paid for?
- [ ] **Map your financial workflow** - From invoice to cash in the bank. How do invoices get created? When are they sent? How do payments get reconciled? Who chases overdue accounts?
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Map your most repeated daily task** - The thing you or your team do most often. Draw every step. You will almost certainly spot at least one unnecessary step you can eliminate today.

## Identify Gaps and Problems

- [ ] **Mark every manual handoff** - Any point where information moves from one person to another by email, verbal instruction, or someone "just knowing." These are your highest-risk failure points.
- [ ] **Mark every point where information is re-entered** - If the same data gets typed into two different systems, that is a gap in your system design. It wastes time and introduces errors.
- [ ] **Identify your bottlenecks** - Where does work queue up waiting for one person, one approval, or one tool? These are the points where speed dies.
- [ ] **Find your single points of failure** - If one specific person were sick for a week, which workflows would stop? Those are your fragile points.
- [ ] **Note where the real process differs from the official process** - People often create workarounds when the system does not work. Those workarounds are useful data. They tell you where the system is broken.

## Document What You Find

- [ ] **Create a simple visual diagram for each core workflow** - Does not need to be fancy. Boxes and arrows showing steps, people, and tools. Keep it on one page per workflow.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Share the maps with your team and ask: "Is this right?"** - They will spot things you missed. The people doing the work every day know the real process better than anyone.
- [ ] **Prioritise your problem areas** - Rank the gaps and bottlenecks by impact. Which ones cost you the most time, money, or customer satisfaction? Those get fixed first.
- [ ] **Save a dated copy of your current-state maps** - This is your baseline. When you improve systems later, you will want to compare back to where you started.

---

## Expected Outcome

- Visual maps of your 3-4 core business workflows as they actually operate today
- Every manual handoff, data re-entry point, and bottleneck identified
- Single points of failure flagged
- A prioritised list of system problems to address
- A shared understanding across the team of how work actually flows

## Coach Notes

This is a revealing exercise and some clients find it uncomfortable. They built these systems (or let them evolve) and seeing the mess on paper can feel like a personal failure. Reframe it: every business has system gaps. The ones that map them can fix them. The ones that do not just keep tripping over the same problems.

Encourage the client to involve their team in the mapping session. The owner's view of how work flows is often different from the team's experience. The truth usually sits somewhere in between. A 90-minute session with 2-3 key people in the room produces a much more accurate map than the owner working alone.

Do not let this turn into a redesign session. Map first, fix later. The goal today is to see reality clearly.
