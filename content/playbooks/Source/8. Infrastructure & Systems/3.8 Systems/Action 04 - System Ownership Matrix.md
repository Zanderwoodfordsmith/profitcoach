# Build Your System Ownership Matrix

## What to Do

Assign clear ownership for every system, tool, and process in your business so that someone specific is accountable for keeping it running, keeping it current, and improving it over time. Right now, most of your systems are "everyone's responsibility," which really means they are nobody's responsibility. This matrix changes that.

## Why It Matters

Unowned systems decay. The CRM that nobody maintains fills up with duplicate contacts. The process document that nobody updates becomes misleading. The software subscription that nobody reviews keeps charging for features you stopped using. When every system has an owner, problems get spotted early, improvements happen naturally, and nothing falls through the cracks because "I thought someone else was handling it."

## How to Use This

Pull together your system map and tool inventory from previous actions. Then assign ownership for each one using the framework below.

---

## Build the Matrix

- [ ] **Create a spreadsheet or table with these columns** - System/tool name, description (what it does), owner (one person), backup owner (who covers if the owner is away), review frequency, and last review date.
- [ ] **List every system, tool, and documented process** - Your CRM, accounting software, project management tool, email platform, every SOP, and every recurring workflow. Include the infrastructure too - hosting, domains, backups.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Identify any systems that currently have no clear owner** - These are your highest-risk items. Assign an owner to each one today, even if it is temporary.
- [ ] **Assign one owner per system** - Not a team. Not "shared." One person whose name is next to it. They do not have to do all the work, but they are accountable for the system's health.

## Define Ownership Responsibilities

- [ ] **Clarify what "owning" a system means** - The owner is responsible for: keeping it running, keeping documentation current, managing access and permissions, flagging problems, and recommending improvements.
- [ ] **Set review frequency for each system** - Critical systems (CRM, finance) get monthly reviews. Supporting systems (file storage, internal tools) get quarterly reviews. Documentation gets reviewed every 6 months.
- [ ] **Define what a system review includes** - Is it working as expected? Is the documentation accurate? Are there unused features or licences? Are there problems that need fixing? Is there a better option available?
- [ ] **Assign backup owners for critical systems** - If your CRM owner is on holiday when something breaks, who steps in? Critical systems need a named backup.

## Clean Up and Communicate

- [ ] **Identify overlapping systems** - Two project management tools, three places for file storage, duplicate CRMs. Ownership clarity often reveals redundancy. Flag these for consolidation.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Share the matrix with the whole team** - Everyone should know who owns what. This eliminates "I did not know who to ask" and "I thought you were handling that."
- [ ] **Add system ownership to your onboarding process** - When someone new joins, they should see the matrix and know who to ask about each system.
- [ ] **Schedule the first round of reviews** - Put the review dates in calendars now. If it is not scheduled, it will not happen.
- [ ] **Review the matrix itself quarterly** - As people change roles, leave, or join, the matrix needs updating. Build this into your quarterly operations review.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A complete matrix showing every system, its owner, backup owner, and review schedule
- No unowned systems, tools, or processes in the business
- Clear definition of what system ownership means and what is expected
- Overlapping and redundant systems identified for consolidation
- A living document that stays current through regular reviews

## Coach Notes

This exercise often creates awkward conversations because it reveals how much has been running on autopilot. That is normal and healthy. The point is not to blame anyone for the gaps. It is to close them going forward.

Watch for the business owner trying to own everything. In small businesses, the founder often defaults to "I will just own it all." Push back. The goal is to distribute ownership so the business does not depend entirely on one person. Even in a 3-person team, each person should own something.

The matrix does not need to be complicated. A simple Google Sheet with 6 columns is enough. The value is in the assignment and accountability, not in the format. If the client spends more than an hour building the document, they are overengineering it.
