# Create Your Standardisation Framework

## What to Do

Build consistent, documented approaches for the work that gets repeated most often in your business. These are standard operating procedures (SOPs) that people will actually follow, not 40-page manuals that gather dust. The goal is simple: anyone in the business should be able to pick up a documented process and deliver a consistent result, even if the usual person is unavailable.

## Why It Matters

Without standardisation, quality depends on who does the work. Your best person delivers a great result. Your newest person delivers something different. The customer experience becomes unpredictable. Standardisation does not mean removing creativity or judgement. It means building a reliable floor that nobody drops below. The best businesses standardise the routine so their people can focus their energy on the exceptional.

## How to Use This

Work through the steps in order. You are building a framework for creating SOPs, not trying to document everything at once. Start with the highest-impact processes and expand from there.

---

## Step 1: Identify What to Standardise First

- [ ] **List every process that happens more than once a week** - Customer onboarding, invoicing, lead follow-up, content publishing, reporting, quality checks. Get them all down.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Pick the process that causes the most inconsistency or complaints** - This is your first SOP. Document it this week.
- [ ] **Identify processes where mistakes are most costly** - Financial processes, customer-facing communications, and compliance tasks should be standardised before internal admin.
- [ ] **Flag processes that currently depend on one person's knowledge** - If only Sarah knows how to run payroll or only James knows how to set up a new client, those are fragile processes that need documenting urgently.

## Step 2: Build Your SOP Template

- [ ] **Create a standard template for all SOPs** - Every SOP should include: process name, purpose (why it exists), trigger (what starts it), steps (in order), tools needed, output (what "done" looks like), and owner (who is responsible).
- [ ] **Keep steps to 7-12 per process** - If a process has 30 steps, it is either too complex or needs breaking into smaller sub-processes. Simplicity drives adoption.
- [ ] **Include screenshots or visuals for tool-based steps** - A screenshot of where to click beats a paragraph of description every time.
- [ ] **Add a "common mistakes" section** - What goes wrong most often? Warn people upfront so they avoid the traps.

## Step 3: Document Your First 3 SOPs

- [ ] **Watch someone do the process in real time** - Do not write the SOP from memory. Watch it happen, note the actual steps (including the shortcuts and workarounds), and document reality.
- [ ] **Have someone else follow your draft SOP without help** - If they can complete the process using only the document, it works. If they get stuck, the document needs more detail at that step.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Record a screen capture of the process** - A 5-minute video paired with a written checklist is often more useful than a detailed written SOP alone.
- [ ] **Get feedback from the team** - Ask: "Is anything missing? Is anything confusing? Would you actually use this?" Revise based on what they say.

## Step 4: Organise and Maintain

- [ ] **Choose a single location for all SOPs** - A shared drive folder, a wiki, or a project management tool. One place, consistently organised, accessible to everyone who needs it.
- [ ] **Create a naming convention** - Department, process name, version. Example: "Sales - Lead Follow-Up - v2." Consistent naming makes finding things easy.
- [ ] **Assign an owner for each SOP** - One person is responsible for keeping it current. Not for doing all the work, but for ensuring the document stays accurate.
- [ ] **Set a review schedule** - Every SOP gets reviewed at least once every 6 months. Processes change. Documents that do not keep up become misleading.
- [ ] **Track which SOPs exist and which are still needed** - A simple tracker showing: process name, SOP status (documented / needs update / not yet written), owner, and last review date.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A clear framework and template for creating SOPs across the business
- Your top 3 highest-impact processes documented and tested
- A single, organised location where all SOPs live
- Clear ownership and a review schedule so documents stay current
- Reduced dependency on individual knowledge and more consistent delivery

## Coach Notes

The resistance to SOPs usually comes in two flavours. "We are too small for that" (you are not, you are exactly the right size to start). And "it will stifle creativity" (it will not, it will free creativity by handling the routine).

Push the client to document processes as they are, not as they wish they were. If the real process includes workarounds and shortcuts, document those. You can improve the process later. For now, you need an accurate record of how things actually work.

The biggest trap is trying to document everything at once. Three good SOPs that get used are worth more than thirty that sit in a folder. Start with the three processes that would cause the most damage if the person who does them disappeared tomorrow.
