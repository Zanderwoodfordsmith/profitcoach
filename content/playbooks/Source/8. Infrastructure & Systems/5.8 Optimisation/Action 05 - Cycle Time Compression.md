# Cycle Time Compression

## What to Do

Systematically reduce the time between decision and outcome across all key processes. Measure how long things actually take from start to finish, identify where delays happen, and compress those delays. Every unnecessary day in a cycle is a margin leak, a customer experience problem, and a competitive disadvantage.

## Why It Matters

Speed is one of the most underrated competitive advantages in small business. The company that delivers a proposal in 24 hours beats the company that takes a week, even if the week-long proposal is slightly better. The company that onboards new clients in 2 days wins over the one that takes 2 weeks. Fast cycle times improve cash flow (you invoice sooner), improve customer satisfaction (they get results sooner), and improve team morale (people feel momentum instead of stagnation). Most delays are not caused by the work itself. They are caused by waiting, queuing, approvals, and handoffs.

## How to Use This

Measure your current cycle times, identify the biggest delays, and systematically compress them.

---

## Measure Your Current Cycle Times

- [ ] **Identify your 5 most important business cycles:**
  - Lead to proposal (how long from first contact to sending a quote?)
  - Proposal to signed deal (how long from quote to contract?)
  - Signed deal to first delivery (how long until the customer gets something?)
  - Start to completion of core service/product
  - Invoice to payment (how long until you get paid?)
- [ ] **For each cycle, measure the actual elapsed time** - Not the working time (how long someone actively spends on it) but the total calendar time from start to finish. Include weekends, holidays, and waiting periods.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Measure your lead-to-proposal time. If it is more than 48 hours, you are losing deals to competitors who move faster.
- [ ] **Break each cycle into active time vs. wait time** - Active time is when someone is actually working on it. Wait time is everything else. In most businesses, wait time is 70-80% of total cycle time.

## Identify Delay Causes

- [ ] **For each cycle, map where the delays happen** - Common delay causes:
  - Waiting for information from the customer
  - Waiting for internal approvals
  - Queuing (work sits in someone's inbox)
  - Handoffs between team members
  - Batch processing (work accumulates until someone gets to it)
  - Unclear ownership (nobody is sure whose job it is)
- [ ] **Quantify each delay** - How many hours or days does each delay add? Which delays are the biggest contributors to total cycle time?
- [ ] **Identify delays within your control vs. outside your control** - Customer response times are hard to influence. Internal approval queues are entirely within your control. Focus on what you can fix.
- [ ] **Check for sequential steps that could run in parallel** - If Step B does not depend on Step A's output, why are they running one after the other?

## Compress Your Cycles

- [ ] **Eliminate unnecessary approval steps** - Every approval adds delay. If someone approves 95% of requests without changes, the approval is not adding value.
- [ ] **Pre-build templates and standard components** - Proposals, contracts, onboarding materials, and common deliverables should be 80% ready before the customer even signs. Customise the remaining 20%.
- [ ] **Set response time commitments** - "We respond to all enquiries within 4 business hours" is a compression mechanism. It forces speed.
- [ ] **Reduce handoffs** - Every time work passes from one person to another, there is a delay. Can one person handle more of the cycle end-to-end?
- [ ] **Move from batch to flow** - Instead of processing invoices once a week, process each one as it is ready. Instead of reviewing proposals every Friday, review each one within 24 hours.
- [ ] **Set target cycle times** - For each of your 5 cycles, define a target that is 30-50% shorter than current. This is ambitious but achievable for most businesses.
- [ ] **Track weekly** - Measure cycle times weekly for the first 3 months to build the habit and spot regressions.

---

## Expected Outcome

- Current cycle times measured and documented for your 5 most important processes
- Wait time identified and quantified as a proportion of total time
- Specific delay causes mapped and prioritised
- Target cycle times set for each process (30-50% reduction)
- Concrete compression actions implemented
- A weekly tracking rhythm to maintain speed

## Coach Notes

The data is usually eye-opening. Clients think their proposal process takes "a day or two" but when measured, it takes 8 days. The work itself takes 2 hours. The other 7 days and 22 hours are waiting. This gap between perception and reality is where the coaching value lives.

Start with the lead-to-proposal cycle. It is the highest-leverage cycle because every day of delay costs potential revenue. A prospect who waits a week for a proposal is shopping competitors. A prospect who gets a proposal same-day feels prioritised.

Watch for clients who try to compress cycle time by making people work faster. That is the wrong lever. The goal is to reduce waiting, not to increase pace. People are usually working at a reasonable speed already. The system around them is what is slow.
