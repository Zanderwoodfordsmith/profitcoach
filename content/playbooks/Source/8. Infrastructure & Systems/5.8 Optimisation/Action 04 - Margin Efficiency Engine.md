# Margin Efficiency Engine

## What to Do

Identify specific levers that decrease your cost-to-serve without reducing quality. This is about making your delivery machine more efficient, not cheaper. The distinction matters: cheaper means cutting corners. Efficient means achieving the same (or better) outcome with less waste, less rework, and less unnecessary effort.

## Why It Matters

Revenue gets the attention. Margin pays the bills. Many businesses grow revenue year after year but margins stay flat or shrink because the cost of delivery grows alongside it. Every percentage point of margin improvement drops straight to the bottom line. A business doing £500K in revenue that improves margin by 5 points adds £25K in annual profit without selling a single extra unit. Efficiency is the fastest path to profitability because it does not require more customers, more marketing, or more sales. It requires working smarter with what you already have.

## How to Use This

Analyse your cost-to-serve, identify the biggest efficiency levers, and implement improvements.

---

## Calculate Your Cost-to-Serve

- [ ] **For your core product or service, list every direct cost of delivery:**
  - Labour (hours x rate for everyone involved in delivery)
  - Materials or inputs
  - Software and tools used in delivery
  - Subcontractor or outsourced costs
  - Travel or logistics
  - Customer support and aftercare
- [ ] **Calculate total cost-to-serve per customer or per unit** - Total delivery costs divided by number of customers served (or units delivered) in the period.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Compare your cost-to-serve today versus 12 months ago. If it has gone up without a corresponding improvement in quality, you have efficiency leak.
- [ ] **Break cost-to-serve into fixed and variable components** - Fixed costs stay the same regardless of volume. Variable costs change with each customer. Your efficiency opportunities are different for each type.

## Identify Efficiency Levers

- [ ] **Labour efficiency** - Can delivery time per customer be reduced without cutting quality? Look for rework (doing things twice), over-servicing (doing more than the customer needs or values), and skill mismatches (expensive people doing tasks that cheaper people could do).
- [ ] **Input costs** - Are you paying the best price for materials and inputs? Could you buy in bulk, switch suppliers, or negotiate volume discounts?
- [ ] **Template and standardise** - What parts of delivery are reinvented every time? Templates, checklists, and standard operating procedures reduce time without reducing quality. The creative and customised parts should be custom. Everything else should be standardised.
- [ ] **Batch similar work** - Are you switching between different types of work throughout the day? Context switching is expensive. Batching similar tasks (all proposals on Monday, all client calls on Tuesday) can save 20-30% of time.
- [ ] **Reduce rework and errors** - What gets done wrong and then redone? Every instance of rework is double the cost for the same output. Track error rates and fix the root causes.
- [ ] **Review scope creep** - Are you delivering more than customers are paying for? Scope creep is a margin thief. Clear scope boundaries protect your time and your margin.

## Implement and Measure

- [ ] **Pick the 3 biggest efficiency levers** - The ones with the most potential savings and the most feasible implementation.
- [ ] **For each lever, define a specific change** - "Reduce proposal creation time from 3 hours to 1 hour by using templates." Be concrete.
- [ ] **Set a target cost-to-serve reduction** - What should your cost-to-serve be after implementation? Even a 10% reduction is significant at scale.
- [ ] **Implement one change at a time** - Multiple simultaneous changes make it impossible to know what worked. Sequential implementation lets you measure each one.
- [ ] **Remeasure cost-to-serve after 30 days** - Compare to your baseline. Did the change deliver the expected savings?

---

## Expected Outcome

- A detailed understanding of your cost-to-serve, broken down by component
- Specific efficiency levers identified with estimated savings
- At least 3 concrete changes planned and prioritised
- A target cost-to-serve that represents meaningful margin improvement
- A measurement approach to confirm that changes are working

## Coach Notes

Clients often confuse efficiency with cost-cutting. Keep the distinction sharp: efficiency means the same output for less input. Cost-cutting means less output for less input. If a change reduces quality, it is not efficiency. It is degradation.

The biggest lever for service businesses is almost always labour efficiency. Not working harder or longer, but eliminating the time wasted on rework, over-servicing, and low-value admin tasks. Start there.

Watch for clients who resist standardisation because they believe their work is "too bespoke." In reality, even highly customised services have repeatable components. The customised 20% gets better when the standard 80% is handled efficiently.
