# Overhead Dissection

## What to Do

Systematically review all overhead costs and eliminate those that do not directly support revenue generation or client delivery. Pull every line of overhead apart, challenge each one, and decide whether it earns its place. This is not about being cheap. It is about being deliberate.

## Why It Matters

Overhead costs are the silent killer of small business profitability. They creep up gradually - a software subscription here, a bigger office there, an extra hire that seemed necessary at the time. Individually, each cost seems small. Collectively, they can consume 30-50% of revenue before the owner notices. Mike Michalowicz's Profit First principle is relevant here: most businesses spend whatever comes in. Overhead grows to fill available revenue. The fix is to regularly challenge every overhead line and ask "does this earn more than it costs?"

## How to Use This

Pull your overhead data, categorise it, challenge each line, and make cut/keep/reduce decisions.

---

## Pull Your Overhead Data

- [ ] **Export your last 12 months of expenses** - From your accounting software, bank statements, or P&L. You need every recurring cost that is not a direct cost of delivering your product or service.
- [ ] **List every overhead category:**
  - Rent and facilities
  - Software and subscriptions
  - Insurance
  - Professional services (accountant, solicitor, advisor)
  - Marketing and advertising
  - Travel and entertainment
  - Equipment and maintenance
  - Staff costs not tied to delivery (admin, management, support)
  - Training and development
  - Miscellaneous and one-off costs
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Log into your bank account and search for recurring subscriptions. Most businesses have 2-5 subscriptions they have forgotten about and no longer use. Cancel them today.

## Challenge Each Cost

- [ ] **For each overhead line, answer three questions:**
  - Does this directly support revenue or client delivery? (If no, why do we have it?)
  - What would happen if we stopped paying for this tomorrow? (If the answer is "nothing," cut it)
  - Is there a cheaper way to get the same result? (Not inferior - the same result for less money)
- [ ] **Flag "nice to have" costs** - Things that feel good but do not measurably contribute to revenue or delivery. Premium office space when clients never visit. Enterprise software when the basic plan would work. Conference attendance with no clear ROI.
- [ ] **Calculate overhead as a percentage of revenue** - Compare to industry benchmarks. If your overhead percentage is significantly above average, you have a problem.
- [ ] **Look for costs that scaled with revenue but should not have** - Sometimes costs grow as revenue grows even when there is no logical reason. More revenue should not automatically mean more office space or more software licences.

## Make Your Decisions

- [ ] **For each overhead line, decide: Keep (justified), Reduce (renegotiate or downgrade), or Cut (eliminate completely)**
- [ ] **Renegotiate before cutting** - For any cost you want to keep but find expensive, call the supplier. Ask for a better rate, an annual discount, or a reduced plan. The worst they can say is no.
- [ ] **Set cancellation dates** - For everything you are cutting, set specific dates and assign someone to action each cancellation. Good intentions without deadlines do not happen.
- [ ] **Calculate total savings** - Add up the annual savings from all reductions and eliminations. This number goes straight to your bottom line.
- [ ] **Set a 6-month overhead review** - Costs creep back if you are not vigilant. Build a regular review into your calendar.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A complete inventory of all overhead costs with 12-month totals
- Every overhead line challenged and justified (or not)
- Unused subscriptions and forgotten costs identified and cancelled
- Renegotiation opportunities identified for costs you are keeping
- Total annual savings calculated
- A recurring review cycle to prevent overhead creep

## Coach Notes

This action triggers guilt in some clients. They feel like cutting costs means being "tight" or not investing in the business. Reframe it: cutting waste is not the same as cutting investment. Waste produces no return. Investment produces a return. The goal is to eliminate the former and protect the latter.

The software subscription pile is almost always the easiest win. Most businesses pay for 10-15 tools and actively use 5-7 of them. The unused ones accumulate because nobody remembers to cancel.

Be careful with staff cost discussions. Overhead dissection should not become an excuse to understaff or underpay. The question is not "can we have fewer people?" but "are we spending in the right places?" Sometimes the answer is to cut a tool and hire a person, not the other way around.
