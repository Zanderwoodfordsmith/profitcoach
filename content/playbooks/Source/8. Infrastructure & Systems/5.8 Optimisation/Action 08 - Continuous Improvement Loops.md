# Continuous Improvement Loops

## What to Do

Install systematic feedback mechanisms that identify bottlenecks, inefficiencies, and improvement opportunities on an ongoing basis. Instead of optimising in big, infrequent efforts, build a system where small improvements happen continuously and the business gets a little better every week.

## Why It Matters

One-off optimisation projects produce temporary results. Without a system to sustain improvement, processes drift back to their old state within months. The Japanese concept of kaizen (continuous improvement) is built on a simple truth: small, consistent improvements compound over time into massive gains. Toyota built the world's most efficient car manufacturing system not through one breakthrough, but through decades of small daily improvements. Your business can do the same at its own scale. The goal is to build the habit of improvement into how the business operates, not to rely on occasional bursts of effort.

## How to Use This

Set up three feedback loops - one for your team, one for your customers, and one for your metrics - then build a rhythm for acting on what you learn.

---

## Team Feedback Loop

- [ ] **Install a weekly "friction log"** - A shared document or channel where anyone on the team can note things that slowed them down, confused them, or felt unnecessarily difficult during the week. No judgement, just observations.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: At your next team meeting, ask: "What is one thing that annoyed you this week about how we work?" Write down every answer. You will have a list of improvement candidates in 5 minutes.
- [ ] **Run a 15-minute "improvement slot" in your weekly team meeting** - Review the friction log, pick one item, and decide what to change. One improvement per week. 52 improvements per year.
- [ ] **Create a "fixed it" list** - Track every improvement you make, no matter how small. Seeing the list grow builds momentum and reinforces the habit.
- [ ] **Assign an improvement owner** - One person responsible for making sure the weekly improvement actually happens. Not doing the improvement necessarily, but making sure it gets done.

## Customer Feedback Loop

- [ ] **Set up a simple post-delivery feedback question** - After every project, sale, or service delivery, ask: "What is one thing we could do better next time?" One question. Not a 20-question survey.
- [ ] **Review customer feedback monthly** - Look for patterns. If three customers mention the same issue in a month, it is a systemic problem, not a one-off.
- [ ] **Close the loop** - When you fix something a customer flagged, tell them. "You mentioned X, we have changed Y." This builds loyalty and encourages more feedback.
- [ ] **Track your Net Promoter Score (or a simpler alternative) quarterly** - "How likely are you to recommend us?" on a 0-10 scale. Track the trend over time.

## Metrics Feedback Loop

- [ ] **Review your core dashboard weekly** - Not just looking at the numbers, but asking: "What do these numbers tell us to change?"
- [ ] **Set thresholds that trigger investigation** - If a metric drops below X or rises above Y, someone investigates why. Do not wait for a monthly review to notice a problem.
- [ ] **Run a monthly root cause analysis on your biggest problem area** - Pick the metric that is furthest from target. Ask "why?" five times (Toyota's Five Whys technique) until you find the root cause, not just the symptom.
- [ ] **Track improvement velocity** - How many improvements have you implemented this month? Is the pace increasing, steady, or declining? If declining, the system needs attention.

## Build the Rhythm

- [ ] **Weekly: 15 minutes to review friction log and pick one improvement** - Small, consistent action.
- [ ] **Monthly: 30 minutes to review customer feedback and metrics, identify patterns, run one root cause analysis** - Deeper investigation.
- [ ] **Quarterly: 60 minutes to review improvement velocity, celebrate wins, and reset priorities** - Strategic perspective.
- [ ] **Make improvement part of performance conversations** - Ask team members: "What have you improved this quarter?" Reward the behaviour you want to see.

---

## Expected Outcome

- Three active feedback loops capturing team, customer, and metrics data
- A weekly improvement rhythm producing at least one change per week
- A growing "fixed it" list that demonstrates compounding progress
- Root cause analysis applied to persistent problems
- A culture where improvement is expected and normal, not exceptional

## Coach Notes

The hardest part is sustaining the rhythm. The first month is easy because the backlog of obvious improvements is long. Month three is harder because the easy wins are done and improvements require more thought. Coach clients through this dip by focusing on the compound effect: 52 small improvements per year transforms a business.

Watch for clients who use the improvement loop to tackle only minor irritations while ignoring major systemic issues. The friction log is great for catching small things. The monthly root cause analysis is where the bigger problems get addressed. Both are necessary.

If a client says "we do not have time for this," calculate how much time they spent this month fixing problems that a continuous improvement loop would have caught earlier. Prevention is almost always cheaper than repair.
