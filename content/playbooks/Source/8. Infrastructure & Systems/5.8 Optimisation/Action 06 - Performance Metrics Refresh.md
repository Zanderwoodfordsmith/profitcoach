# Performance Metrics Refresh

## What to Do

Audit your current KPIs and dashboards to make sure they still reflect what actually drives outcomes in your business. Remove metrics that no longer matter, add ones that do, and make sure the numbers you track lead to action, not just observation. Old metrics optimise for old priorities.

## Why It Matters

Businesses change but their dashboards often do not. The KPIs you set up two years ago may have been perfect for that stage of growth. But if your strategy has shifted, your market has changed, or your business model has evolved, those old metrics are now optimising for the wrong things. Peter Drucker's principle - "what gets measured gets managed" - cuts both ways. If you measure the wrong things, you manage the wrong things. A metrics refresh ensures you are steering with current instruments, not last year's map.

## How to Use This

Review every metric you currently track, test whether it still drives decisions, and rebuild your dashboard around what matters now.

---

## Inventory Your Current Metrics

- [ ] **List every metric, KPI, and report your business currently tracks** - Include everything: financial dashboards, sales metrics, marketing analytics, delivery metrics, team metrics, customer metrics.
- [ ] **For each metric, note: who looks at it, how often, and what decisions it informs** - Be specific. "We look at it in the monthly review" is not enough. What action does the number trigger?
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Identify any metric you have not looked at in 3+ months. If nobody has missed it, you probably do not need it. Remove it from your dashboard.
- [ ] **Categorise each metric as: Leading (predicts future results) or Lagging (reports past results)** - Most businesses track too many lagging indicators and not enough leading ones. Lagging tells you what happened. Leading tells you what is about to happen.

## Test Each Metric

- [ ] **Apply the "so what?" test** - If this metric goes up by 20%, what would you do differently? If it goes down by 20%, what would you do differently? If the answer to both is "nothing," the metric is decorative, not functional.
- [ ] **Check for vanity metrics** - Numbers that look impressive but do not connect to revenue or profit. Website traffic, social media followers, and email list size are common vanity metrics unless you can draw a clear line to revenue.
- [ ] **Check for conflicting metrics** - Are any of your metrics pulling in opposite directions? For example, tracking both "speed of delivery" and "thoroughness of delivery" can create confusion unless there is a clear priority.
- [ ] **Check alignment with current strategy** - Does each metric connect to one of your top 3 strategic priorities? If not, why are you tracking it?

## Rebuild Your Dashboard

- [ ] **Define your 5-7 core metrics** - These are the numbers you look at every week. They should include: at least one financial metric, one customer metric, one delivery metric, and one leading indicator.
- [ ] **For each core metric, define: target, current performance, owner, and review frequency** - Every metric needs a target. Without one, you are just watching numbers move.
- [ ] **Add 2-3 leading indicators** - Pipeline value, proposal conversion rate, customer satisfaction trends, team capacity. These predict future performance and give you time to react.
- [ ] **Remove everything else from your primary dashboard** - You can still track detailed data in sub-reports. But the dashboard everyone looks at should have 5-7 numbers, not 25.
- [ ] **Set a review cadence** - Weekly for operational metrics, monthly for strategic metrics, quarterly for this full refresh.
- [ ] **Communicate the changes to your team** - If you change what you measure, tell people. Otherwise they keep optimising for the old metrics.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A complete inventory of all currently tracked metrics with usage data
- Unused, vanity, and misaligned metrics identified and removed
- A refreshed dashboard with 5-7 core metrics that drive decisions
- Leading indicators added to give early warning of future performance
- A quarterly refresh cycle to keep metrics aligned with evolving strategy

## Coach Notes

The most common problem is too many metrics, not too few. Clients think more data means better decisions. The opposite is usually true. Decision quality degrades when there are 20 numbers competing for attention. Help clients be ruthless about cutting their dashboard to the essential few.

Watch for emotional attachment to metrics. "We have always tracked that" is not a reason to keep tracking it. The question is always "does this number change what we do?" If no, it goes.

A good test for whether a dashboard is working: can a new team member look at it for 30 seconds and understand whether the business is having a good week or a bad week? If they cannot, the dashboard is too complicated.
