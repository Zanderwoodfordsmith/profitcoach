# Process Archaeology

## What to Do

Map every core process in your business and identify redundant, duplicated, or unnecessary operations. Start with your Critical Client Flow - the path from first lead to delivered result - because that is where waste has the biggest impact on revenue and customer experience. This is archaeology because you are digging into layers of accumulated practice to find what is actually needed versus what has just been there a long time.

## Why It Matters

Every business builds up process debt over time. A workaround gets introduced to fix a problem. The problem gets fixed properly later, but the workaround stays. A new tool gets adopted but the old manual process continues alongside it. Steps get added because someone made a mistake once, and nobody ever reviews whether the safeguard is still needed. David Jenyns calls this "process bloat" - the gradual accumulation of steps that made sense individually but collectively make your business slower, more expensive, and more confusing than it needs to be.

## How to Use This

Map your Critical Client Flow first, then extend to other core processes. For each, identify and remove redundant operations.

---

## Map Your Critical Client Flow

- [ ] **Define the start and end points** - Start: the moment a lead first contacts you. End: the moment the work is fully delivered and paid for. Everything in between is your Critical Client Flow.
- [ ] **List every step between start and end** - Be granular. Include every email sent, every form filled in, every approval needed, every handoff between team members. The detail matters because waste hides in the small steps.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Walk through one recent client engagement from start to finish. Track every touchpoint. You will spot at least 3 unnecessary steps.
- [ ] **Time each step** - How long does each step take? Include both active time (someone working on it) and wait time (nothing happening, waiting for a response or approval).
- [ ] **Identify the wait times** - In most businesses, work spends more time waiting than being worked on. A proposal that takes 30 minutes to write but 5 days to get approved has a 5-day wait built in.

## Identify Redundant Operations

- [ ] **Flag duplicated effort** - Is the same information entered into multiple systems? Is the same check done at multiple points? Does more than one person review the same work?
- [ ] **Flag legacy steps** - Steps that were created to solve a problem that no longer exists. Ask about each step: "What was the original reason for this?" If nobody knows, it is probably legacy.
- [ ] **Flag workarounds** - Temporary fixes that became permanent. The team routes around a broken system instead of fixing it, creating extra steps that nobody questions.
- [ ] **Flag over-processing** - Steps where you do more than the customer needs or values. Elaborate proposals for simple projects. Detailed reports nobody reads. Multiple revisions when one would suffice.
- [ ] **Calculate the total waste** - Add up the time spent on flagged steps per week or per month. Convert to cost (time x hourly rate). This number usually motivates immediate action.

## Extend to Other Core Processes

- [ ] **Repeat the mapping exercise for your next 2-3 most important processes** - Examples: hiring and onboarding, financial reporting, marketing campaign execution, complaint resolution.
- [ ] **Look for cross-process redundancy** - Different teams doing the same thing independently. Data being collected in one process but not shared with another that needs it.
- [ ] **Create a "redundancy register"** - A simple list of every redundant operation you have found, with estimated time wasted and a proposed action (eliminate, simplify, or combine).
- [ ] **Prioritise by impact** - Fix the most wasteful redundancies first. A step that wastes 2 hours per week is more urgent than one that wastes 10 minutes per month.

---

## Expected Outcome

- Your Critical Client Flow mapped in detail with timings
- Wait times identified and quantified (often the biggest source of waste)
- Redundant, legacy, and over-processing steps flagged across core processes
- A redundancy register with estimated time waste and proposed fixes
- A prioritised list of operations to eliminate or simplify

## Coach Notes

This exercise frequently reveals that the actual process is nothing like what the owner thinks it is. Owners describe the intended process. Team members describe what actually happens. The gap between the two is where the waste lives. If possible, involve the team in the mapping rather than relying on the owner's version alone.

The biggest wins are usually in wait times, not processing times. Most clients focus on making active steps faster when the real improvement is eliminating the 3-day waits between steps. Ask: "What is this waiting for? Can we remove the wait entirely?"

Some clients resist removing steps because "they have always been there." Use the archaeology metaphor: just because something is old does not mean it is important. Sometimes it is just old. The value of a step is measured by its current contribution, not its history.
