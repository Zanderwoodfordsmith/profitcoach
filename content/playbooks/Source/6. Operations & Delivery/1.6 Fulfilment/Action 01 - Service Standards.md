# Service Standards

## What to Do

Define the minimum standards that must be met on every single delivery, regardless of client, timeline, or who does the work. These become your non-negotiable quality filter — nothing goes out the door unless it passes.

## Why It Matters

David Jenyns' SYSTEMology starts with a simple observation: when you do not define what "good" looks like, quality becomes random. It depends on who is available, how much pressure they are under, and whether they remembered the important steps. Service standards remove the guesswork. They are not about perfection — they are about consistency. A client should get the same quality whether you deliver personally or your newest team member does.

## How to Use This

Work through each section for your most common type of delivery first. If you offer multiple services, do this for the one that generates the most revenue.

---

## Define Your Standards

- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win · **List the 5 things that must happen on every delivery, no exceptions** — The absolute non-negotiables. If any of these are missed, the client experience is damaged. Examples: "Client receives a confirmation within 24 hours," "Every deliverable is reviewed before sending," "Client is updated at least weekly."
- [ ] **For each standard, define what "good" looks like specifically** — "Quick response" is not a standard. "All client messages receive a response within 4 business hours" is. Make each one measurable.
- [ ] **Identify the standards that are currently being missed** — Be honest. Which of your non-negotiables are you already failing on? These are your first fixes.
- [ ] **Prioritise: which missed standard is most damaging to client trust?** — Fix that one first. Client trust is built on reliability, and reliability is built on consistently meeting standards.

## Communication Standards

- [ ] **Set response time expectations** — How quickly should clients hear back from you? Set the expectation explicitly rather than leaving it undefined. Then communicate it to clients.
- [ ] **Define update frequency** — Weekly? After each milestone? Clients do not complain about over-communication — they complain about silence. Set a minimum and stick to it.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win · **Create a standard "welcome" message or onboarding email** — The first interaction sets the tone. Every new client should receive the same professional start, explaining what happens next and when.

## Delivery Standards

- [ ] **Define your quality check** — What review happens before any work reaches the client? Who checks it? What are they checking for? Even a simple "read it through once before sending" is better than nothing.
- [ ] **Set your delivery timeline standards** — How long should each type of delivery take? What is the maximum acceptable delay before the client is notified? Define it and track it.
- [ ] **Create a simple handoff checklist** — When work passes between team members (or between you and a subcontractor), what information must be included? Missing handoff details cause most quality failures.

## Track Compliance

- [ ] **Pick one standard and track it for 4 weeks** — Did you meet it every time? If not, why? The tracking itself improves compliance.
- [ ] **Ask clients for feedback on the specific standard** — "How was our response time this month?" is more useful than "Are you happy?" Targeted questions get honest, actionable answers.

---

## Expected Outcome

- Clear, written standards that everyone on your team can follow
- Consistent client experience regardless of who delivers the work
- Fewer surprises, complaints, and quality failures
- A foundation you can build on — once Level 1 standards are reliable, Level 2 takes them higher
- Client confidence in your process, which builds trust and referrals

## Coach Notes

Most clients will try to set too many standards at once. Push them to start with 5 non-negotiables. If they cannot maintain 5, adding more is pointless. Master the basics, then expand.

The clients who need this most are the ones who say "we don't need standards — we just deliver great work." That usually means quality depends on who is having a good day. Standards make "great work" the default, not the exception.

This pairs with Client Journey Map (which documents the full process) and Scope & Boundaries (which protects against overdelivery).
