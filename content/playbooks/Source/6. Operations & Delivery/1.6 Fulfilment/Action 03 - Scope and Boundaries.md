# Scope & Boundaries

## What to Do

Define clear boundaries around what is included in your delivery and what is not. Build the scripts and processes for handling scope creep before it happens, so you protect your margins without damaging client relationships.

## Why It Matters

Scope creep is the silent profit killer in service businesses. It starts with "can you just..." and ends with you doing 40% more work than you quoted for, resenting the client, and making less money than if you had never taken the job. The problem is not difficult clients — it is unclear boundaries. When your scope is vague, every request feels reasonable because there is no line to cross. When your scope is explicit, you can say "yes, and here's the cost for that addition" without awkwardness.

## How to Use This

Define your boundaries for your core offer first. If you have multiple offerings, start with the one that suffers most from scope creep.

---

## Define What Is In

- [ ] **List exactly what your core offer includes** — Be specific. Not "marketing support" but "3 blog posts per month, 1 strategy call, and a monthly analytics report." Specificity is the boundary.
- [ ] **Define the number of revisions or iterations included** — If applicable. "2 rounds of revisions included" is clear. "We'll keep going until you're happy" is a blank cheque.
- [ ] **State the timeline** — When does the engagement start and end? What milestones happen when? Open-ended timelines invite scope creep because there is no natural endpoint.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win · **Add your scope to your proposal or contract** — If it is not written down before the client signs, it does not exist. One paragraph in your proposal prevents months of argument.

## Define What Is Out

- [ ] **List the most common "extras" clients ask for** — The requests that come up regularly but are not included. These become your explicit exclusions.
- [ ] **Price the extras** — Every excluded item should have a price tag ready. "That's not included in the current package, but I can add it for £X." This is not hostile — it is professional.
- [ ] **Define where your responsibility ends and theirs begins** — What do you need from the client to do your job? What happens if they do not provide it? What delays are on them versus on you? Clarity here prevents blame games.

## Handle Scope Creep When It Happens

- [ ] **Write your "scope creep" response script** — Something you are comfortable saying every time: "Great idea — that falls outside the current scope. I can add it for £X, or we can include it in the next phase. Which works better for you?"
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win · **Practice saying it out loud** — Seriously. Most business owners know what to say but freeze in the moment because they do not want to seem difficult. Say it 10 times until it feels natural.
- [ ] **Track scope additions** — When you do say yes to extras, log them. At the end of the engagement, review: how much free work did you do? What should have been charged? This data informs your next proposal.
- [ ] **Review your scope quarterly** — Are clients consistently asking for the same extras? That is a signal to either include it (and raise your price) or to make the exclusion more prominent.

---

## Expected Outcome

- Clear, documented scope for your core offer that clients agree to before work starts
- Prepared responses for scope creep that feel professional, not confrontational
- Priced extras ready to go — turning "can you just..." into additional revenue
- Protected margins because you are delivering what you quoted, not 40% more
- Better client relationships because expectations are clear from day one

## Coach Notes

Scope creep is an emotional issue for most clients. They feel guilty saying no, afraid of losing the client, or unsure whether the request is reasonable. Help them see that clear boundaries actually improve client relationships — clients respect you more when you are clear about what they get.

If a client says "I can't say no to clients," dig into why. Often it is a pricing confidence issue (they feel they are not delivering enough value to justify their price) or a scarcity issue (they are afraid to lose any client). Both loop back to other playbooks — Value Definition and Ideal Customer.

The "practice saying it out loud" step sounds silly but works. Role-play a scope creep conversation in the session. The client who can handle it calmly in role-play will handle it calmly in real life.
