# Client Journey Map

## What to Do

Document every step from the moment a client says "yes" to the moment the work is complete and they are delighted. Turn the invisible process in your head into a visible system anyone can follow.

## Why It Matters

Jenyns calls this your "Critical Client Flow" — it is the single most important process to document first because it directly touches revenue and reputation. Ryan Deiss frames it as the "Fulfillment Engine" — the system that delivers what your Growth Engine sold. When this process lives only in your head, every delivery requires you. When it is documented, others can follow it, quality stays consistent, and you stop being the bottleneck.

## How to Use This

Map your most common delivery type first. Use a whiteboard, a document, or sticky notes — whatever gets the steps out of your head and into view.

---

## Step 1: Map the Current Reality

- [ ] **Start from "client says yes" and write every step that happens next** — Do not skip the obvious ones. Include: payment collection, welcome communication, kickoff, information gathering, actual delivery, review, completion, and follow-up.
- [ ] **For each step, note: who does it, when it happens, and how long it takes** — This reveals where your process depends on you personally, where handoffs happen, and where delays creep in.
- [ ] **Mark the client touchpoints** — Every moment the client hears from you, sees something, or needs to do something. These are the moments that shape their experience.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win · **Identify the biggest gap between steps** — Where does the client wait longest without hearing anything? That silence is where trust erodes. Even a quick "we're working on it, here's where we are" email fixes this.

## Step 2: Find the Friction

- [ ] **Where do clients get confused?** — Where do they ask "what happens next?" or "when will I hear from you?" These are missing communication points.
- [ ] **Where do things regularly go wrong?** — Missed steps, delays, quality issues, miscommunication. These are your process failures — and they are usually predictable.
- [ ] **Where do you personally have to intervene?** — Every point where only you can move things forward is a bottleneck. At Level 1, that is expected — but documenting it shows you what needs to change as you grow.
- [ ] **What information do you repeatedly chase from clients?** — If you always need the same 5 things from clients and you always have to ask for them, build a standard request that goes out automatically at the right stage.

## Step 3: Build the Standard Journey

- [ ] **Rewrite the journey as a clean sequence of steps** — Numbered, in order, with who is responsible and when it happens. This becomes your delivery playbook.
- [ ] **Add standard communications at each client touchpoint** — Welcome email, progress update, delivery notification, completion message, feedback request. Write templates once, use them every time.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win · **Create a simple client onboarding checklist** — The first 48 hours after "yes" set the tone for the entire relationship. A consistent onboarding process builds immediate confidence.
- [ ] **Test it** — Have someone else (team member, VA, or friend) read your journey map and tell you where they get confused. If they cannot follow it, a new hire will not be able to either.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A documented, step-by-step delivery process anyone can follow
- Clear client touchpoints with template communications ready to use
- Identified friction points and bottlenecks you can fix immediately
- The ability to delegate parts of delivery because the process is documented
- A foundation for scaling — you cannot grow what you cannot replicate

## Coach Notes

This is often a breakthrough session. When clients see their delivery process mapped out in front of them, they immediately spot 3-5 things that are broken or missing. The map makes problems visible that they have been working around unconsciously.

Jenyns recommends recording yourself walking through the process, then having someone else document it. This is faster than writing it from scratch and captures the nuances that would otherwise be missed.

If the client says "my delivery is different every time," that IS the problem. Even customised work has common steps — find those steps and standardise them. Customisation should happen within a framework, not in place of one.
