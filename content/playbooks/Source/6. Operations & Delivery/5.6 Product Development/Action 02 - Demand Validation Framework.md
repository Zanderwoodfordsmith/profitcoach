# Demand Validation Framework

## What to Do

Test customer demand before building anything. Use pre-orders, landing pages, customer interviews, pilot programmes, and other low-cost methods to validate that people actually want what you plan to create and are willing to pay for it. This prevents the most expensive mistake in business: building something nobody buys.

## Why It Matters

The graveyard of small business is full of products that the owner loved but customers did not want. Building first and selling later is backwards. Every week you spend developing an unvalidated product is a week of revenue you will never get back. Eric Ries' Lean Startup principle applies to every business, not just tech startups: build the minimum version, test it with real customers, and iterate based on real data. The goal is to fail cheaply and learn fast, not to perfect something in isolation and hope the market agrees.

## How to Use This

Pick the validation method that fits your product idea, run the test, and use the results to decide whether to build, adjust, or abandon.

---

## Choose Your Validation Method

- [ ] **Customer conversations (lowest effort, highest insight)** - Talk to 5-10 ideal customers about the problem your product solves. Do not pitch your solution. Ask about their pain, what they have tried, and what they would pay for a fix. If the problem is not urgent for them, the product will not sell.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Send a one-question survey to your existing customer list: "If we offered [product concept in one sentence], would you be interested? Reply yes or no." Simple data, zero cost.
- [ ] **Landing page test** - Build a simple page describing the offer with a "register interest" or "join waitlist" button. Drive traffic to it and measure sign-up rate. No product needed.
- [ ] **Pre-order or pre-sale** - Offer the product at a discount before it exists. Real money committed is the strongest validation signal. If people will not pay in advance, they probably will not pay later either.
- [ ] **Pilot programme** - Deliver a stripped-down version to 3-5 customers at a reduced price in exchange for detailed feedback. You learn what works, what does not, and what customers actually value before building the full version.
- [ ] **Competitor analysis** - If a competitor already sells something similar and is growing, demand is partially validated. Your job becomes differentiation, not demand creation.

## Design Your Test

- [ ] **Define your hypothesis in one sentence** - "I believe [target customer] will pay [price] for [product] because [reason]." Write it down. You need to know what you are testing.
- [ ] **Set your success threshold before you start** - What number or result would make you confident enough to build? 10 pre-orders? 50 waitlist sign-ups? 8 out of 10 interviewees saying they would buy? Define "pass" and "fail" in advance so you cannot move the goalposts.
- [ ] **Set a time limit** - Give the test 2-4 weeks. Long enough to get meaningful data, short enough that you do not lose momentum.
- [ ] **Identify your test audience** - Who specifically will you test with? Existing customers, email list, social media followers, cold prospects? The audience affects the results.
- [ ] **Budget your test** - Most validation tests cost very little. Landing pages, email surveys, and conversations are nearly free. Pre-orders require some marketing spend. Set a budget and stick to it.

## Evaluate Results and Decide

- [ ] **Compare results to your pre-set threshold** - Did you pass or fail? Be honest. Wishful thinking at this stage is expensive later.
- [ ] **Look for patterns in the feedback** - What did customers love? What concerned them? What would they change? This shapes the final product.
- [ ] **Make one of three decisions:**
  - **Build** - Demand is validated, move to development
  - **Adjust** - Interest exists but the offer needs changes before building
  - **Abandon** - Demand is not there, move on to the next idea without guilt
- [ ] **Document what you learned** - Even failed tests produce valuable insight. Write down what you discovered about customer preferences, pricing sensitivity, or market gaps. This feeds your next innovation.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A clear validation method chosen and executed for your product idea
- Real data from real customers, not assumptions
- A confident build/adjust/abandon decision backed by evidence
- Documented learnings that improve your next product decision
- Reduced risk of investing time and money in products that will not sell

## Coach Notes

The biggest barrier is emotional attachment. Clients fall in love with their product idea and treat validation as a formality rather than a genuine test. Push them to define success criteria before running the test, and hold them to those criteria when results come in.

Watch for "false validation" - asking friends and family who say "great idea!" but would never actually buy. Real validation involves people in the target market making a commitment, ideally financial. Compliments are not validation. Purchases are.

If a client is reluctant to test because "it will take too long," reframe it: building an unvalidated product takes 3-6 months. A validation test takes 2-4 weeks. Which is the bigger waste of time if the idea is wrong?
