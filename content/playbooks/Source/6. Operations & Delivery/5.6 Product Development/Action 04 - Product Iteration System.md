# Product Iteration System

## What to Do

Build a repeatable process for gathering customer feedback, prioritising improvements, and rolling out updates to your products and services. Instead of improving things when you feel like it or when something breaks, create a rhythm that ensures your offers get better every quarter.

## Why It Matters

Products that do not improve die slowly. Your customers' expectations rise every year, competitors copy your best ideas, and the market moves on. But most small businesses only improve their products reactively - when a customer complains, when a competitor launches something new, or when the owner has a random burst of inspiration. A systematic iteration process means improvements happen regularly, are based on real feedback, and get implemented without chaos.

## How to Use This

Set up three things: a feedback collection system, a prioritisation method, and a release rhythm. Then run the cycle continuously.

---

## Build Your Feedback Collection

- [ ] **Choose 3 feedback channels and set them up:**
  - Post-purchase or post-delivery survey (keep it to 3-5 questions maximum)
  - Quarterly check-in conversation with top clients (phone or video, not email)
  - Internal team debrief after each project or delivery cycle
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Add one question to your next client interaction: "What is one thing we could do better?" This costs nothing and the answers are gold.
- [ ] **Create a central feedback log** - A simple spreadsheet or document where all feedback goes, regardless of source. Columns: Date, Source, Feedback, Product/Service, Frequency (how often you hear this), Priority.
- [ ] **Assign someone to review the log monthly** - Feedback that sits unread is worse than no feedback at all. Someone needs to own this.
- [ ] **Track feature requests separately from complaints** - Complaints tell you what is broken. Requests tell you what is missing. Both matter but they need different responses.

## Prioritise Improvements

- [ ] **Review your feedback log at the start of each quarter** - Look for patterns. What feedback appears most frequently? What comes from your best customers?
- [ ] **Score each potential improvement on three criteria:**
  - Customer impact (how many customers benefit and how much?)
  - Revenue impact (will this help retain customers, increase prices, or attract new ones?)
  - Implementation effort (how much time and money to build?)
- [ ] **Apply the "quick wins first" principle** - High impact, low effort improvements go first. Always. Saving the easy wins for later while tackling hard ones first is a common trap.
- [ ] **Select 2-3 improvements per quarter** - No more. Fewer improvements done well beats a long list done poorly.
- [ ] **Write a one-sentence brief for each selected improvement** - What changes, why, and what does success look like? Keep it simple enough that anyone on the team can understand.

## Implement and Release

- [ ] **Assign an owner for each improvement** - One person responsible for making it happen, even if multiple people contribute.
- [ ] **Set a deadline within the quarter** - If an improvement cannot be done in a quarter, break it into smaller pieces. Shipping partial progress beats planning indefinitely.
- [ ] **Communicate changes to customers** - When you improve something, tell people. An email, a note in a meeting, a social post. Customers notice when things get better, but they appreciate you pointing it out.
- [ ] **Measure the impact** - After implementation, check: did customer satisfaction improve? Did complaints on that topic decrease? Did retention or revenue change? If you cannot measure it, at least gather anecdotal feedback.
- [ ] **Start the cycle again** - Review the feedback log, pick the next improvements, implement, measure. This never stops.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A working feedback collection system with multiple channels
- A central log where all customer feedback is captured and reviewed
- A quarterly rhythm for selecting and implementing product improvements
- Clear ownership and deadlines for each improvement
- A continuous cycle that ensures your products get better every quarter

## Coach Notes

The biggest risk with this action is over-engineering. Clients hear "system" and think they need software, dashboards, and complex workflows. Push back. A spreadsheet and a quarterly calendar is enough for most small businesses. The system should take minutes to maintain, not hours.

Watch for clients who collect feedback but never act on it. That is worse than not collecting it, because customers feel ignored. If you ask for feedback, you must close the loop. Even if the answer is "we heard you and we are not changing this right now because X."

Help clients distinguish between feedback that represents real patterns and feedback from one loud customer. A single request is an anecdote. The same request from five different customers is a signal. Teach them to look for frequency before acting.
