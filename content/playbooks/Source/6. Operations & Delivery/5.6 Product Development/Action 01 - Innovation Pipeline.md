# Innovation Pipeline

## What to Do

Map your 12-24 month product roadmap with clear timelines, resource requirements, and strategic rationale for each item. Separate core product improvements from new experiments so you can invest in both without confusing the two. This gives you a structured plan for what to build, when to build it, and why it matters.

## Why It Matters

Most small businesses either never innovate (they sell the same thing for years until it stops working) or they innovate chaotically (chasing every new idea without finishing anything). Both paths lead to stagnation. A structured pipeline solves this by giving innovation a container. You know what you are working on now, what is coming next, and what is just an idea being tested. Without this structure, the loudest idea wins - usually whatever the owner got excited about last Tuesday.

## How to Use This

Build your pipeline in three stages: gather all ideas, categorise them, then sequence them into a timeline.

---

## Gather Your Product Ideas

- [ ] **Brain-dump every product idea, improvement, and enhancement you have been thinking about** - New offers, add-ons, upgrades, bundled packages, digital products, events, service improvements. Get everything out of your head and onto paper.
- [ ] **Review customer feedback from the last 6 months** - What have customers asked for? What have they complained about? What do they wish you offered?
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Check your last 10 support tickets or complaint emails. The product improvements hiding in complaints are usually the most valuable ones.
- [ ] **Ask your team: "If you could fix or add one thing to what we offer, what would it be?"** - Front-line staff see problems the owner misses.
- [ ] **Review competitor offerings** - What are competitors offering that you do not? Not to copy, but to spot gaps and opportunities.

## Categorise Each Idea

- [ ] **Label each idea as one of three types:**
  - **Core improvement** - Makes your existing offer better (faster delivery, higher quality, better experience)
  - **Extension** - Adds something new to your existing offer (upsell, add-on, new tier)
  - **Experiment** - Completely new offer or market (untested, higher risk, higher potential reward)
- [ ] **Score each idea on three dimensions (1-5 each):**
  - Revenue potential - How much could this generate?
  - Customer impact - How much would customers value this?
  - Effort required - How much time, money, and resource to build?
- [ ] **Calculate priority: (Revenue + Customer Impact) minus Effort** - Higher scores go higher on the list.
- [ ] **Apply the 70/20/10 rule as a guideline** - 70% of resource on core improvements, 20% on extensions, 10% on experiments. Adjust to fit your business, but the principle holds: protect your core while testing the future.

## Build Your Timeline

- [ ] **Place your top-priority core improvements in the next 0-6 months** - These protect your existing revenue and keep current customers happy.
- [ ] **Place extensions in the 6-12 month window** - These grow revenue from your existing base once the core is solid.
- [ ] **Place experiments in the 12-24 month window** - These explore new territory. Some will fail. That is the point.
- [ ] **For each item in the next 6 months, define: owner, deadline, resources needed, and success metric** - Vague items on a roadmap are wishes, not plans.
- [ ] **Set quarterly review points** - The pipeline is a living document. Review it every quarter and reprioritise based on what you have learned.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A complete inventory of all product ideas, improvements, and innovations
- Each idea categorised as core improvement, extension, or experiment
- A prioritised 12-24 month roadmap with clear sequencing
- The next 6 months planned in detail with owners and deadlines
- A quarterly review rhythm to keep the pipeline current

## Coach Notes

The most common mistake is filling the pipeline with experiments while ignoring core improvements. Exciting new ideas are more fun than fixing what already exists. But core improvements protect revenue and often deliver the biggest return for the least effort. Push clients to strengthen the core first.

Watch for pipelines that are really just wish lists. Every item needs a "why now" and a "what does success look like." If a client cannot answer those questions, the idea is not ready for the pipeline.

If a client has never mapped a product roadmap before, start with just the next 6 months. A 24-month roadmap can feel overwhelming for someone who has been making product decisions on instinct. Build the muscle first, extend the timeframe later.
