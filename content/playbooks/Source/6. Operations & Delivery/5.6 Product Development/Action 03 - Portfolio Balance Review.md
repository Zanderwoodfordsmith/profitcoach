# Portfolio Balance Review

## What to Do

Analyse how your time, money, and team resource is allocated between your core offers and any experimental or new products. Set clear criteria for what percentage of resource goes where, and make deliberate decisions about your product portfolio rather than letting it drift.

## Why It Matters

Left unmanaged, product portfolios become bloated. Every new idea gets a "yes," nothing gets retired, and the team ends up supporting six offers when they only have capacity for three. The result is mediocre delivery across the board. Your best products suffer because resource is being drained by marginal ones. A portfolio review forces the hard conversations: what should we stop offering? What deserves more investment? What looked promising but is not pulling its weight?

## How to Use This

List everything you sell, measure its performance, and rebalance your portfolio around what is actually working.

---

## Inventory Your Current Portfolio

- [ ] **List every product or service you currently offer** - Include every tier, package, add-on, and variant. If a customer can buy it, it goes on the list.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Count the items on your list. If you have more than 5-7 distinct offers, you are almost certainly spread too thin. Most small businesses perform best with 2-4 core offers.
- [ ] **For each offer, record:**
  - Revenue generated in the last 12 months
  - Gross margin (revenue minus direct delivery costs)
  - Number of active customers
  - Time spent delivering and supporting it (hours per month)
  - Age of the offer (how long have you been selling it?)

## Analyse Performance

- [ ] **Calculate revenue per hour for each offer** - Total revenue divided by total hours spent. This reveals which offers are worth your time and which are deceptively busy.
- [ ] **Rank offers by gross margin percentage** - Which offers keep the most money after delivery costs?
- [ ] **Identify your "hero" products** - The 1-2 offers that generate the majority of your revenue and profit. These deserve the most protection and investment.
- [ ] **Identify your "zombie" products** - Offers that still exist but barely generate revenue, consume disproportionate effort, or have not grown in over a year. These are candidates for retirement.
- [ ] **Identify your "rising stars"** - Newer offers showing growth in revenue or customer interest, even if still small. These may deserve more investment.

## Rebalance Your Portfolio

- [ ] **Set your target allocation** - What percentage of your team's time and your budget should go to: hero products (protect and optimise), rising stars (invest and grow), and experiments (test and learn)?
- [ ] **Compare target vs. reality** - Where is the gap? Most businesses over-invest in zombies and under-invest in heroes.
- [ ] **Decide the fate of each zombie product** - For each underperformer, choose: sunset it (stop selling), simplify it (reduce the resource it consumes), or set a 90-day deadline for improvement.
- [ ] **Reallocate freed-up resource** - Every hour or pound saved from retiring a zombie should go to a hero or rising star. Be specific about where it goes.
- [ ] **Set a 6-month review date** - Portfolio balance is not a one-time exercise. Markets change, offers mature, new opportunities emerge. Review every 6 months.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A complete inventory of every product and service with performance data
- Hero, zombie, and rising star products clearly identified
- Underperforming offers retired or given a clear improvement deadline
- Resource reallocated from low-performers to high-performers
- A target allocation set for ongoing portfolio management

## Coach Notes

Retiring a product is emotionally difficult for owners. They built it, they remember the first customer who loved it, and stopping feels like failure. Reframe it: retiring a product that no longer serves you is not failure. It is good management. The resource you free up goes to something better.

Watch for the "but what if someone asks for it" trap. Clients keep zombie products alive because one customer per quarter might want it. Calculate what that one sale actually costs in terms of ongoing support, complexity, and distraction. It is almost never worth it.

If a client has never done a portfolio review, the first one will be revealing. Most owners have no idea how much time each offer actually consumes. The data often triggers immediate decisions to simplify.
