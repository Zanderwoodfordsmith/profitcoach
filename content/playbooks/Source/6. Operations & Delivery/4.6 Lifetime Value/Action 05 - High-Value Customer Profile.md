# High-Value Customer Profile

## What to Do

Identify the characteristics of your most valuable customers so you can find more people like them and tailor your retention efforts to keep them. You will analyse your top customers across revenue, profitability, ease of service, and referral behaviour to build a profile of what your best customers have in common. Then you will use that profile to sharpen your marketing, improve your sales qualification, and focus retention resources where they matter most.

## Why It Matters

Not all customers are equal. In most businesses, the top 20% of customers generate 60-80% of revenue and an even higher share of profit. Yet most businesses treat all customers the same: same marketing, same service level, same retention effort. A high-value customer profile lets you be deliberately unequal. You invest more in attracting and retaining the customers who drive your business, and you stop over-investing in customers who drain it. This is not about ignoring smaller customers. It is about allocating resources proportionally to value.

## How to Use This

You need your Lifetime Value Formula (Action 2) and your customer data. Sort your customers by total value and work through the analysis below.

---

## Identify Your Top Customers

- [ ] Rank all customers by total lifetime revenue (or total revenue in the last 24 months if you do not have full lifetime data)
- [ ] Identify your top 20% by revenue
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Write down the names of your best 5 customers right now, without looking at the data. Then check the data. If your gut list does not match the revenue list, that tells you something important about how you perceive value.
- [ ] Calculate what percentage of total revenue this top 20% represents
- [ ] Now filter for profitability: are your highest-revenue customers also your most profitable? (Some high-revenue customers cost disproportionately more to serve.)

## Analyse Common Characteristics

### Demographics and Firmographics

- [ ] Company size (revenue, employee count)
- [ ] Industry or sector
- [ ] Geography or location
- [ ] Years in business
- [ ] Business stage (startup, growth, established, scaling)

### Behavioural Characteristics

- [ ] How did they find you? (Referral, organic search, advertising, networking, etc.)
- [ ] How long did the sales cycle take?
- [ ] What product or service did they buy first?
- [ ] How quickly did they expand or buy again?
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Look at your top 5 customers and note how they originally found you. If 3 out of 5 came from the same channel, that channel deserves more investment.

### Attitudinal Characteristics

- [ ] What problem were they trying to solve when they found you?
- [ ] How urgent was their need?
- [ ] Were they comparison shopping or ready to commit?
- [ ] Do they refer others? How actively?
- [ ] How engaged are they with your content, events, or community?

## Build Your High-Value Profile

- [ ] Summarise the 5-7 characteristics that your best customers have in common
- [ ] Create a one-page profile: "Our best customers tend to be [description]"
- [ ] Note any anti-patterns: characteristics that predict low-value or difficult customers
- [ ] Compare your high-value profile against your current Customer Avatar (1.5). Are they the same or different?
- [ ] If different, your marketing may be attracting the wrong audience

## Apply the Profile

- [ ] Share the profile with your marketing team or person. Targeting should favour high-value characteristics.
- [ ] Update your sales qualification criteria. Prospects matching the high-value profile should get priority.
- [ ] Design a premium retention programme for existing customers who match the profile
- [ ] Review your current customer base: which existing customers match the profile but are under-served?
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Identify 3 current customers who match your high-value profile and are not getting enough attention. Reach out to them this week with something valuable.
- [ ] Set a quarterly review to update the profile as your customer base evolves

---

## Expected Outcome

- Your top 20% of customers identified and their revenue contribution calculated
- Common characteristics identified across demographics, behaviour, and attitude
- A one-page high-value customer profile documented
- Marketing targeting adjusted to favour high-value characteristics
- Sales qualification criteria updated to prioritise high-value prospects
- A premium retention approach for high-value customers

## Coach Notes

This exercise often challenges assumptions. Clients think they know who their best customers are, but the data sometimes tells a different story. The most valuable insight is usually the acquisition channel pattern. If your best customers all came from referrals but you are spending most of your budget on paid ads, that is a misalignment worth fixing. The anti-pattern analysis is equally valuable. Knowing which customer characteristics predict low value or high hassle lets you qualify out poor-fit prospects earlier. This is not about being elitist. It is about focus. A business that serves everyone equally serves no one well. Connect this to the Customer Avatar (1.5) and the Customer Grade Analysis (1.5 Action 4) for a complete picture of who to pursue and who to let go.
