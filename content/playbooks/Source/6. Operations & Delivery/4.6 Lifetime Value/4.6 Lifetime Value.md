## **What This Is**

Lifetime value transforms how you think about marketing and customer acquisition. When you know exactly what each customer is worth over their entire relationship with you, spending money to acquire new customers becomes a strategic decision instead of a hope-and-pray gamble.

This playbook shifts your focus from transaction thinking to relationship thinking. Instead of asking "how much profit did we make this month?" you start asking "how much profit will this customer generate over the next two years?" That change in perspective unlocks marketing budgets, drives better retention strategies, and reshapes how you invest in growth.

This is not about complex financial modelling or customer analytics. It is about understanding the economics of your customer relationships so you can make smarter decisions about where to invest your time and money.

---

## **What It Looks Like**

### **🔴 When this is broken**

You have no idea what a customer is worth beyond their first purchase. Your marketing spend is based on gut feel or what you can afford right now, not what makes financial sense. You focus obsessively on acquisition cost while ignoring how much customers spend over time. Growth feels expensive because you are treating every customer like a one-off transaction instead of the start of a valuable relationship.

### **🟠 When this is OK**

You have a rough sense that some customers are more valuable than others but no clear numbers to guide your decisions. You track repeat purchases but have not calculated average lifespan or total value per customer. You know retention matters but you are not systematically investing in keeping customers longer or increasing what they spend.

### **🟢 When this is working**

You know exactly what each customer is worth over their lifetime relationship with you. You can confidently spend on marketing because you know the return you will get. Your retention strategies are deliberate and measured — you invest in keeping valuable customers because you know what losing them costs. Your pricing, product development, and marketing decisions are all informed by lifetime value, not just immediate profit.

## **Things to Think About**

How long do your customers actually stay with you? Most business owners guess at customer lifespan, but the real number is often very different from what you expect. The difference between an 18-month customer and a 3-year customer completely changes what you can afford to spend on acquisition.

Where are you leaving money on the table with existing customers? Your highest lifetime value often comes not from finding new customers but from getting existing customers to buy more often, spend more per transaction, or stay longer. Each of these levers multiplies the others.

Is your marketing spend economically sensible? If you spend £500 to acquire a customer worth £2,000 over two years, that is a great investment. If that same customer is only worth £400, it will bankrupt you. Without knowing lifetime value, you are making marketing decisions blind.

What makes some customers more valuable than others? Not all customers are created equal. The ones who stay longest, buy most frequently, or spend the most per purchase are your gold mine. Understanding what they have in common lets you find more like them.

Are you building loyalty or just processing transactions? A customer who buys once and disappears has very different value from one who becomes a regular. Your systems, communication, and follow-up should be designed to move people from the first category to the second.

## **The Plays Inside This Playbook**

When you open this playbook in the Profit System, you will find:

Play 1: Customer Lifespan Calculator — Calculate the average length of your customer relationships so you know how long your customers typically stay with you and can project future revenue.

Play 2: Lifetime Value Formula — Build a simple model that shows exactly what each customer is worth over their entire relationship, factoring in repeat purchases, average spend, and relationship duration.

Play 3: Value Maximiser Framework — Systematically increase lifetime value through three proven levers: frequency (how often they buy), monetary value (how much they spend), and retention (how long they stay).

Play 4: Acquisition Economics — Compare your cost to acquire customers against their lifetime value to determine what you can afford to spend on marketing and still be profitable.

Play 5: High-Value Customer Profile — Identify the characteristics of your most valuable customers so you can find more people like them and tailor your retention efforts to keep them longer.

## **Quick Wins**

This week: List your last 20 customers and mark how many times each one has bought from you. Calculate the percentage who have made repeat purchases — this is your starting point.

This week: Pick your three most valuable customers and write down what they have in common. Industry, size, buying behaviour, needs — find the patterns.

This week: Calculate your average customer spend and multiply it by your repeat purchase rate. Even this rough lifetime value number will change how you think about marketing investment.
