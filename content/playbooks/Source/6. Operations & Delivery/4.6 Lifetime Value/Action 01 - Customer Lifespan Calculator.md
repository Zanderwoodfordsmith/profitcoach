# Customer Lifespan Calculator

## What to Do

Calculate the average length of your customer relationships so you can project future revenue, identify retention problems early, and make smarter decisions about how much to invest in acquiring and keeping customers. You will analyse your existing customer data to determine how long customers stay, whether lifespan is improving or declining, and which factors predict longer or shorter relationships.

## Why It Matters

Most business owners think about revenue in monthly or annual snapshots. They rarely consider the full duration of a customer relationship. But the difference between a customer who stays 6 months and one who stays 3 years is enormous. A 3-year customer generates 6x the revenue of a 6-month customer, and the cost of serving them typically decreases over time because they need less hand-holding. Knowing your average customer lifespan changes every calculation in your business: how much you can spend to acquire a customer, how much you should invest in retention, and what your business is actually worth.

## How to Use This

You need access to your customer records showing start dates and, for customers who have left, end dates. A CRM, accounting software, or even a spreadsheet will work.

---

## Gather Your Customer Data

- [ ] List all customers who started in the last 2-3 years with their start date
- [ ] For customers who have left, note their end date (last purchase, cancellation date, or last active date)
- [ ] For customers still active, note their current tenure (they will be included differently in the calculation)
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: If your data is messy, start with your last 20 customers. Even a small sample gives useful directional insight.
- [ ] Remove any one-off or project-based customers that were never intended to be ongoing relationships

## Calculate Average Customer Lifespan

- [ ] For churned customers: calculate the average duration from start to end date (in months)
- [ ] For active customers: calculate their current average tenure (in months)
- [ ] Your blended average customer lifespan sits between these two numbers
- [ ] If you have subscription or retainer customers, calculate a churn rate instead: (Customers Lost in Period / Total Customers at Start of Period) x 100
- [ ] Convert churn rate to lifespan: Average Lifespan = 1 / Monthly Churn Rate (in months)

## Analyse Trends and Segments

- [ ] Compare lifespan by customer segment: industry, size, product purchased, acquisition channel
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Sort your customer list by tenure, longest to shortest. Look at the top 10 and bottom 10. What is different about them? The patterns are usually obvious once you look.
- [ ] Check whether lifespan is improving or declining over time (compare customers acquired this year vs. last year)
- [ ] Identify your "danger zone": the point in the relationship where most customers leave. This is where retention efforts should focus.
- [ ] Note any customers with unusually long lifespans. What makes them different?

## Calculate Lifespan Value

- [ ] Multiply your average lifespan by your average monthly revenue per customer
- [ ] This gives you a rough lifetime revenue figure (refined in Action 2: Lifetime Value Formula)
- [ ] Compare this number to your cost of acquisition. If lifespan revenue is less than 3x acquisition cost, you have a problem.
- [ ] Project: if you increased average lifespan by just 2 months, what would that mean in total revenue?

---

## Expected Outcome

- Average customer lifespan calculated in months
- Monthly churn rate calculated (if applicable)
- Lifespan analysed by segment to identify high-value and at-risk groups
- The "danger zone" identified where most churn occurs
- A rough lifetime revenue number per customer

## Coach Notes

This exercise often produces uncomfortable truths. Clients who believe their customers stay for years discover the average is much shorter. That is useful information, not bad news. The "danger zone" insight is particularly actionable. If most customers leave between months 3 and 6, that is where retention interventions should focus. Common data challenges: clients without clean records, businesses with mixed one-off and recurring customers, and seasonal businesses where "inactive" does not mean "gone." Help them define clear rules for what counts as an active vs. churned customer before running the numbers. Connect this directly to the Lifetime Value Formula (Action 2) and the Churn Analysis (3.6) for a complete retention picture.
