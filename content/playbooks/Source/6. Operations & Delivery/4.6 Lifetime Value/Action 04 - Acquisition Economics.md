# Acquisition Economics

## What to Do

Compare what it costs you to acquire a customer against what that customer is worth over their lifetime. This gives you a clear answer to the most important marketing question: how much can you afford to spend to win a customer? You will calculate your customer acquisition cost (CAC) by channel, compare it to your lifetime value (LTV), and use the ratio to make smarter spending decisions.

## Why It Matters

Most small businesses set marketing budgets based on what feels comfortable rather than what the maths supports. This leads to two equally damaging outcomes: underspending (missing growth because you are too cautious) or overspending (burning cash on channels that do not return enough). When you know your LTV:CAC ratio, marketing becomes a maths problem instead of a guessing game. If you know a customer is worth £10,000 and costs £2,000 to acquire, you can confidently invest in growth. If they cost £8,000 to acquire, you know to fix retention before scaling spend.

## How to Use This

You need your Lifetime Value Formula (Action 2) completed. You also need your marketing spend data by channel for the last 6-12 months.

---

## Calculate Your Customer Acquisition Cost

- [ ] List every marketing and sales expense from the last 6-12 months: advertising, content production, sales team costs, software, agency fees, events, sponsorships
- [ ] Count the total number of new customers acquired in the same period
- [ ] Divide total spend by total new customers. This is your blended CAC.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Even a rough CAC is better than no CAC. If your data is messy, use total marketing spend / new customers from last quarter. Refine later.
- [ ] Important: include sales team time and cost in CAC, not just marketing spend. Acquisition includes everything from first touch to signed deal.

## Calculate CAC by Channel

- [ ] Break out your marketing spend by channel: organic social, paid ads, referrals, content marketing, networking, cold outreach, partnerships, etc.
- [ ] For each channel, count the number of customers it generated
- [ ] Calculate CAC per channel: Channel Spend / Customers from Channel
- [ ] Rank channels from lowest to highest CAC
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Identify your lowest-CAC channel and ask: can I double my investment here before it hits diminishing returns? That is usually your fastest growth opportunity.
- [ ] Note: some channels (content marketing, SEO) have high upfront costs but declining CAC over time. Factor in the long-term trajectory.

## Calculate Your LTV:CAC Ratio

- [ ] Divide your LTV (from Action 2) by your blended CAC
- [ ] Benchmark: LTV:CAC of 3:1 or higher is healthy. Below 3:1 means you are spending too much relative to what customers are worth. Above 5:1 might mean you are underinvesting in growth.
- [ ] Calculate the ratio per channel to see which channels are most efficient
- [ ] Calculate your payback period: how many months until a customer's revenue covers their acquisition cost?
- [ ] A payback period under 12 months is ideal. Over 18 months creates cash flow pressure.

## Set Your Maximum CAC

- [ ] Based on your LTV:CAC analysis, set a maximum CAC you are willing to pay per customer
- [ ] Set a maximum CAC per channel (some channels can have higher CAC if they bring higher-LTV customers)
- [ ] Use these maximums as guardrails for all marketing investment decisions
- [ ] Review and adjust quarterly as your LTV data improves

## Make Spending Decisions

- [ ] For each active marketing channel, compare actual CAC against your maximum
- [ ] Increase investment in channels well below your CAC ceiling
- [ ] Reduce or pause channels above your CAC ceiling unless they bring disproportionately high-LTV customers
- [ ] Before starting any new marketing initiative, estimate the expected CAC and compare to your ceiling
- [ ] Track CAC trends monthly. Rising CAC is an early warning sign that a channel is saturating.

---

## Expected Outcome

- Blended CAC calculated for the whole business
- CAC calculated per marketing channel
- LTV:CAC ratio calculated (blended and per channel)
- Payback period calculated in months
- A maximum CAC set as a guardrail for marketing spend
- Clear decisions on which channels to scale, maintain, or reduce

## Coach Notes

Acquisition economics is where marketing stops being a cost centre and starts being an investment with measurable returns. For many clients, this is the first time they have ever calculated CAC. The reaction is usually surprise, either that they are spending far more than they realised per customer or, occasionally, that they are drastically underinvesting. The LTV:CAC ratio gives them a rational basis for marketing decisions rather than gut feel. Common pitfalls: ignoring organic and time-based costs (your time has a value even if you do not pay yourself for it), attributing all customers to a single channel when the journey was multi-touch, and comparing short-term CAC against long-term LTV without considering cash flow timing. Connect this to the Lead Generation playbook (2.5) for channel-specific tactics and the Cash Flow Forecast (1.4) to model the cash impact of acquisition investments.
