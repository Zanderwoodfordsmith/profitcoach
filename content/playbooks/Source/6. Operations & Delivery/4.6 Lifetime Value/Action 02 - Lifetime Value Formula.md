# Lifetime Value Formula

## What to Do

Build a simple model showing what each customer is worth over their entire relationship with your business. The formula combines three variables: how often they buy (frequency), how much they spend per transaction (monetary value), and how long they stay (lifespan). This single number becomes the foundation for every marketing, pricing, and retention decision you make.

## Why It Matters

Without knowing your customer lifetime value (LTV), you are guessing on every major business decision. How much can you spend to acquire a customer? How much should you invest in keeping them? Should you discount to win a deal? Should you fire a difficult customer? LTV answers all of these questions. It is the most important number in your business that you probably do not know. A business with a £500 LTV and a business with a £15,000 LTV require completely different strategies, even if they have the same monthly revenue.

## How to Use This

You need your Customer Lifespan Calculator (Action 1) completed and access to your revenue data by customer. Work through the sections below.

---

## Calculate Your Three Variables

- [ ] **Average Purchase Frequency**: How many times does a customer buy from you per year? (Total transactions / Total customers in the period)
- [ ] **Average Order Value (AOV)**: What is the average revenue per transaction? (Total revenue / Total transactions)
- [ ] **Average Customer Lifespan**: How long does a customer stay in years? (From Action 1)
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: If you have recurring revenue (retainers, subscriptions), simplify this. Monthly recurring revenue per customer x Average lifespan in months = LTV. Done.

## Build Your LTV Formula

- [ ] Simple LTV = Average Purchase Frequency x Average Order Value x Average Customer Lifespan
- [ ] Example: 4 purchases/year x £500/purchase x 3 years = £6,000 LTV
- [ ] Calculate this number and write it down. This is your headline LTV.
- [ ] If you have multiple products or services, calculate LTV per product line
- [ ] If you have distinct customer segments, calculate LTV per segment

## Add Gross Margin for Profit-Based LTV

- [ ] Calculate your gross margin percentage: (Revenue - Direct Costs) / Revenue
- [ ] Multiply your revenue-based LTV by your gross margin percentage
- [ ] This gives you your profit-based LTV, which is what each customer is actually worth after costs
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: If you do not know your exact gross margin, use 50% as a starting estimate for service businesses or 30% for product businesses. Refine later.
- [ ] Example: £6,000 revenue LTV x 60% gross margin = £3,600 profit LTV

## Validate and Sense-Check

- [ ] Compare your calculated LTV against your gut feeling. If it seems too high or too low, check the inputs.
- [ ] Look at your top 5 customers by total revenue. Does the average LTV feel right relative to them?
- [ ] Look at your bottom 5 customers. Are they dragging the average down significantly?
- [ ] Calculate the range: what is the LTV of your best customer vs. your worst? A wide range means segments matter more than averages.
- [ ] Recalculate LTV excluding your bottom 20% of customers. This is the LTV you should be targeting with your marketing.

## Document and Use

- [ ] Record your LTV number prominently (dashboard, wall, wherever you track key metrics)
- [ ] Set a maximum customer acquisition cost (CAC) at no more than one-third of your LTV
- [ ] Calculate your LTV:CAC ratio. Aim for 3:1 or better.
- [ ] Set a reminder to recalculate LTV quarterly as your data improves

---

## Expected Outcome

- Your headline LTV number calculated (revenue-based)
- Your profit-based LTV calculated (adjusted for gross margin)
- LTV calculated per segment or product line if applicable
- A maximum CAC derived from your LTV
- LTV:CAC ratio calculated and benchmarked
- LTV documented and integrated into decision-making

## Coach Notes

LTV is one of those numbers that changes how business owners think once they see it. A coaching client who learns their LTV is £12,000 suddenly sees a £1,500 acquisition cost differently. The number gives them permission to invest in marketing. Conversely, a client with a £2,000 LTV who is spending £1,800 to acquire customers needs to fix retention before scaling. Common mistakes: using revenue LTV when profit LTV is the real number, averaging across wildly different customer types (segment instead), and treating LTV as a fixed number rather than something to improve. Always connect this to the three levers in Action 3 (Value Maximiser Framework): frequency, monetary value, and retention. LTV is the outcome. Those three levers are how you improve it.
