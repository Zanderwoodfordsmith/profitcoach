# Onboarding Experience Design

## What to Do

Create a structured first-week experience for new clients that gets them a quick win, sets clear expectations, and makes them feel confident they made the right decision. The first 48 hours after someone becomes a client shapes their entire relationship with you. Design it deliberately.

## Why It Matters

Buyer's remorse peaks in the first 72 hours. The client has just committed money and is wondering "did I make the right choice?" If they hear nothing, feel confused about what happens next, or have to chase you for information, the doubt grows. A structured onboarding experience squashes that doubt immediately. It says: "You are in good hands. Here is exactly what happens next. And here is a quick result to prove this is already working."

## How to Use This

Design your onboarding sequence step by step, then implement it for every new client going forward.

---

## The First 24 Hours

- [ ] **Immediate confirmation:** Within 1 hour of sign-up, send a welcome email or message. Include: thank you, what happens next, and a specific timeline.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Write your welcome email right now. Template: "Welcome to [business]. Here is what happens next: [step 1 by day], [step 2 by day], [step 3 by day]. If you have any questions, reply to this email."
- [ ] **Set expectations:** Tell them exactly what to expect in the first week, who they will hear from, and what they need to prepare
- [ ] **Provide access:** Send logins, links, documents, or whatever they need to get started. Do not make them ask.

## The First Week

- [ ] **Day 1-2: Kickoff.** Schedule a kickoff call or meeting. Use it to: review their goals, explain how you work together, answer their questions, and identify their first quick win.
- [ ] **Day 2-3: Quick win.** Give them something valuable early. A useful template, a quick assessment, an insight from your kickoff conversation, or a simple action they can take today. The goal: make them feel "this is already worth it" before the first week is over.
- [ ] **Day 5-7: Check-in.** A brief message or call: "How is everything so far? Do you have any questions? Is there anything you need?" This shows you care and catches problems before they grow.

## Standardise the Process

- [ ] Write out your onboarding process as a simple checklist that anyone on your team could follow
- [ ] Create templates for each communication: welcome email, kickoff agenda, quick win delivery, week-one check-in
- [ ] Decide who owns each step (you, a team member, or automated)
- [ ] Time-stamp the sequence: what happens on Day 0, Day 1, Day 3, Day 5, Day 7
- [ ] Test the experience: run through it yourself as if you were the client. Does it feel good?

## Measure and Improve

- [ ] After onboarding the next 5 clients with the new process, ask each: "How was your first week experience? What could we improve?"
- [ ] Track early engagement: are onboarded clients more active and responsive than before the process existed?
- [ ] Track early issues: are fewer clients confused, frustrated, or silent in week 1?
- [ ] Refine the process quarterly based on feedback

---

## Expected Outcome

- A repeatable, structured onboarding process that runs the same way for every client
- New clients feeling confident, informed, and excited within the first week
- A quick win delivered before the first week is over
- Reduced buyer's remorse and fewer early cancellations
- Templates and checklists that make onboarding effortless for your team

## Coach Notes

Onboarding is where you protect your sale. A client who has a great first week is 3x more likely to stay for the full engagement. A client who feels ignored in week one is already mentally preparing to leave. The quick win is the most important element: it turns "I hope this works" into "this is already working." For coaching businesses, the quick win might be a specific insight from the first session. For service businesses, it might be a deliverable or a report. Whatever it is, make it tangible and fast. Hold clients accountable to building their onboarding process and then to using it consistently.
