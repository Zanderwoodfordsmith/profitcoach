# Client Feedback System

## What to Do

Build a simple way to collect, track, and act on client feedback so you fix problems before they become patterns and turn insights into improvements. This is not an annual survey. It is a continuous loop: ask, listen, act, repeat.

## Why It Matters

Most businesses hear about problems through complaints or cancellations - both of which come too late. For every client who complains, 10 more just leave quietly. A feedback system catches issues when they are small and fixable, surfaces patterns you would never spot from individual conversations, and shows clients that you care about improving. The businesses with the best retention are not the ones with the fewest problems - they are the ones that find and fix problems fastest.

## How to Use This

Design your feedback collection points, set up a simple tracking system, and commit to acting on what you learn.

---

## Collection Points

- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Send one message today to a current client: "What is one thing we could do differently to make working with us even better?" One question, one client, real feedback.
- [ ] Build feedback collection into your client journey at these natural moments:
  - **After onboarding** (week 2): "How was your first-week experience?"
  - **Mid-engagement** (monthly or at project midpoint): "What is working well? What could be better?"
  - **At completion** (project end or contract end): "What would you change about the experience?"
  - **After issues** (whenever something goes wrong): "We resolved [issue]. Was the resolution satisfactory?"
- [ ] Choose your method for each: quick email, short survey (3-5 questions max), phone call, or in-person conversation
- [ ] Keep it short. One powerful question beats a 20-question survey nobody completes.

## The Power Questions

Use one or two of these at each collection point:

- [ ] "On a scale of 1-10, how likely are you to recommend us to a colleague?" (NPS-style - tracks overall satisfaction over time)
- [ ] "What is the one thing we could improve?" (Identifies actionable issues)
- [ ] "What has been most valuable about working with us?" (Identifies what to protect and amplify)
- [ ] "Is there anything we promised that we have not delivered?" (Catches gaps before they become resentment)
- [ ] "Would you work with us again? Why or why not?" (Binary clarity on overall relationship health)

## Track and Act

- [ ] Create a simple feedback log: Date, Client, Feedback, Category (Service / Communication / Quality / Timing / Other), Action Taken
- [ ] Review feedback monthly: look for patterns. One complaint is feedback. Three similar complaints is a systemic issue.
- [ ] For every pattern you spot, create an improvement action and assign an owner
- [ ] Close the loop: when you fix something based on feedback, tell the client who raised it. "You mentioned X - we have now changed Y. Thank you for flagging it."
- [ ] Track your NPS score (or equivalent) quarterly. Is client satisfaction improving, flat, or declining?

## Make It Cultural

- [ ] Share feedback (anonymised if needed) with your team regularly
- [ ] Celebrate positive feedback publicly - it reinforces good behaviour
- [ ] Treat negative feedback as a gift, not a problem - it is free consulting on how to improve
- [ ] Set a team goal: respond to every piece of feedback within 48 hours, even if the fix takes longer

---

## Expected Outcome

- Feedback collection built into your client journey at natural moments
- A simple tracking system that captures feedback and actions taken
- Patterns identified and systemic issues fixed before they cost clients
- Clients who feel heard and valued because their feedback produces visible changes
- A continuous improvement loop that gets stronger over time

## Coach Notes

The biggest barrier is not collecting feedback - it is acting on it. Many businesses survey clients and then do nothing with the results, which is worse than not asking at all (it signals "we asked but do not care"). Push clients to close the loop: act on the top finding each month and tell clients what changed. The NPS-style question is useful for tracking trends, but the real value is in the qualitative responses. Watch for clients who stop giving feedback - silence is not satisfaction, it is often disengagement. If a previously vocal client goes quiet, reach out proactively.
