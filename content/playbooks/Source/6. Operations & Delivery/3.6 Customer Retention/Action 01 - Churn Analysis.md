# Map Your Churn - When and Why Customers Leave

## What to Do

Pull together every customer you have lost in the last 12 months and work out exactly when in the relationship they left and why. You are looking for patterns, not individual stories. Most businesses assume they know why customers leave. This exercise almost always reveals surprises.

## Why It Matters

You cannot fix retention if you do not know where the leaks are. A business losing customers at month 3 has a completely different problem than one losing them at month 12. One is an onboarding failure, the other is a value perception issue. Until you map the actual data, you are guessing. And guessing leads to expensive fixes aimed at the wrong problem.

## How to Use This

Gather your customer records, cancellations, and any exit feedback you have. Work through the analysis below. Be honest about the numbers, even if they are uncomfortable.

---

## Churn Data Collection

- [ ] **List every customer lost in the last 12 months** - Name, date they started, date they left, and revenue they represented.
- [ ] **Calculate your churn rate** - Number of customers lost divided by total customers at the start of the period. Write this number down. This is your baseline.
- [ ] **Record the reason each customer gave for leaving** - Use their words, not your interpretation. If they gave no reason, mark it as "no reason given."
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Sort lost customers by tenure** - Group them: left within 0-3 months, 3-6 months, 6-12 months, 12+ months. The biggest group tells you where your biggest leak is.
- [ ] **Calculate the revenue impact of each group** - Not all churn is equal. Losing ten small clients is different from losing two large ones.

## Pattern Analysis

- [ ] **Identify your top 3 stated reasons for leaving** - Look across all the reasons and group them. Price, service quality, changed circumstances, competitor, outgrew you, or something else.
- [ ] **Cross-reference reasons with tenure** - Do early leavers give different reasons than long-term leavers? They almost always do.
- [ ] **Check for seasonal patterns** - Is there a month or quarter where you lose more customers? Budget cycles, contract renewal dates, and industry cycles often drive this.
- [ ] **Look at who sold the account** - If one salesperson's clients churn at twice the rate, you have a promise-delivery gap, not a retention problem.
- [ ] **Identify your "silent churners"** - Customers who stopped buying or engaging but never formally cancelled. These are often the biggest hidden group.
- [ ] **Map the last 3 touchpoints before each departure** - What happened (or did not happen) in the weeks before they left? Look for patterns of disengagement.

## Action Planning

- [ ] **Rank your churn causes by revenue impact** - The biggest revenue leak gets fixed first, regardless of how many customers it affects.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Pick the single easiest pattern to fix** - There is usually one cause that is simple to address. Fix it this week.
- [ ] **Set a target churn rate for next quarter** - Based on what you have learned, what is a realistic improvement? Write it down and track it monthly.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A clear, data-backed picture of when and why customers leave
- Churn grouped by tenure, reason, and revenue impact
- Seasonal and salesperson patterns identified
- A prioritised list of retention problems to fix, ranked by revenue impact
- A baseline churn rate you can measure improvement against

## Coach Notes

Most business owners resist this exercise because they think they already know why people leave. Push past that. The data almost always tells a different story. Common surprise: the biggest churn driver is not price or service quality. It is neglect. Customers who feel forgotten leave quietly.

If the client does not have good records, start with whatever they have. Even a rough analysis of 10-15 lost customers will reveal patterns. Do not let "we don't have perfect data" become an excuse to skip this entirely.
