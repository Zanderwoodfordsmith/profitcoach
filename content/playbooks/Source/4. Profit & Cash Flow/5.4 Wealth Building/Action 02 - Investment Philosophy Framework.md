# Investment Philosophy Framework

## What to Do

Define your personal investment approach based on your timeline, risk tolerance, and specific financial goals - not on tips from friends, hot takes from social media, or whatever performed well last year. Build a written investment philosophy that guides every allocation decision so you are consistent over time rather than reactive to market noise.

Your philosophy answers: What do I believe about investing? What am I trying to achieve? What risks am I willing to accept? And what will I never do?

## Why It Matters

Howard Marks argues that having a clear investment philosophy is the single most important factor in long-term investment success. Without one, investors chase performance, panic during downturns, and make emotional decisions that destroy wealth. With one, they have a framework for staying disciplined when markets and friends are telling them to do something different.

Most business owners are excellent investors in their own business but terrible investors outside it. They take the same aggressive, concentrated approach that built the business and apply it to public markets or property speculation. What works as a founder - conviction, concentration, high risk tolerance - is usually the opposite of what works as an investor.

## How to Use This

Work through each section honestly. The goal is a written document - your investment policy statement - that you can reference before making any investment decision. Share it with your financial adviser if you have one.

## Define Your Investment Goals

- [ ] **Write down your financial independence number** - How much capital do you need to generate enough passive income to fund your life without working? See [[5.4 Wealth Building]] for the Financial Independence Calculator action.
- [ ] **Set a timeline** - When do you want to reach financial independence? 5 years? 10? 20? Your timeline determines how much risk you can afford to take.
- [ ] **List specific financial milestones** - Not just "get rich." Specific goals: children's education funded by X date. Mortgage paid by X date. Retirement income of X per year from X date.
- [ ] **Determine how much you can invest regularly** - What amount can you move from business to personal investments each month or quarter without endangering the business? Be conservative.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Write down the single most important financial goal you have outside the business** - This becomes the anchor for your entire philosophy.

## Understand Your Risk Profile

- [ ] **Honestly assess your risk tolerance** - Not what you think it should be. What you actually feel when investments drop 20%. If that keeps you awake at night, you need a more conservative approach than you probably think.
- [ ] **Separate risk tolerance from risk capacity** - Tolerance is emotional (how much loss can you stomach). Capacity is financial (how much loss can you absorb without it affecting your life). Both matter.
- [ ] **Define your "sleep test"** - What is the maximum portfolio drop you could experience without changing your behaviour? 10%? 20%? 30%? Build your portfolio to stay within that boundary.
- [ ] **Acknowledge what you do not know** - Most business owners overestimate their investment knowledge because they are successful in business. Different game, different rules. Intellectual humility is a competitive advantage here.

## Set Your Investment Beliefs

- [ ] **Write down 5 core beliefs about investing** - Examples: "I believe in diversification over concentration." "I believe costs matter more than picking winners." "I believe time in the market beats timing the market." Your beliefs should drive your strategy.
- [ ] **Define what you will never invest in** - Exclusions based on ethics, risk, or knowledge. "I will never invest in something I do not understand." "I will never put more than 10% in any single investment."
- [ ] **Decide your stance on active vs. passive investing** - Evidence consistently shows that passive index funds outperform most active managers after fees. Take a position based on evidence, not ego.
- [ ] **Set rules for when you will and will not act** - "I will rebalance annually." "I will not sell during a downturn unless my circumstances change." "I will review but not act on any investment tip for 30 days." Rules prevent emotional decisions.

## Build Your Investment Policy Statement

- [ ] **Write a one-page document combining goals, risk profile, beliefs, and rules** - This is your investment constitution. Every decision gets tested against it.
- [ ] **Share it with your financial adviser** - If they disagree with any part, have the conversation. If they ignore it, find a new adviser.
- [ ] **Review it annually** - Your goals, timeline, and risk tolerance will change over time. Update the document when they do.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Before your next investment decision, ask: "Does this align with my philosophy, or am I reacting to something?"** - This single question prevents most bad investment decisions.

## Expected Outcome

- Written investment philosophy covering goals, risk tolerance, beliefs, and rules
- Clear financial milestones with timelines
- Framework for making consistent investment decisions over time
- Protection against emotional, impulsive, or peer-influenced investing
- Foundation for working productively with a financial adviser

## Coach Notes

Many business owners resist this exercise because they believe investing should be intuitive, like business decisions. Help them see that the best investors in the world - Buffett, Marks, Dalio - all have written philosophies they follow religiously. Discipline is not boring. It is how wealth compounds.

Watch for the owner who wants to apply their business instincts to investing: concentrated bets, leverage, insider knowledge, moving fast. In business, these are advantages. In public markets, they usually lead to losses. The mindset shift from founder to investor is significant.

If the client does not have a financial adviser, this is a good moment to suggest finding one. An independent financial adviser who works on a fee basis (not commission) can be invaluable for business owners building wealth outside the business. The philosophy statement makes the adviser relationship more productive.
