# Asset Allocation Model

## What to Do

Build a diversified portfolio across asset classes that matches your life stage, risk profile, and financial goals. Asset allocation - how you split your money across different types of investments - determines roughly 90% of long-term investment returns. This is not about picking winners. It is about building a structure that grows steadily and survives downturns.

Use your Investment Philosophy Framework to guide the allocation. Then set a rebalancing schedule to maintain it over time.

## Why It Matters

Harry Markowitz called diversification "the only free lunch in investing." By spreading capital across asset classes that do not move together, you reduce overall risk without proportionally reducing returns. A diversified portfolio does not outperform a concentrated bet in a good year, but it dramatically outperforms over a full market cycle because it avoids the catastrophic losses that concentrated portfolios suffer.

For business owners specifically, asset allocation is critical because the business itself is already a concentrated, illiquid, high-risk asset. Everything outside the business should balance that risk. If your business is your "aggressive" investment, your portfolio should be your "conservative" one.

## How to Use This

Review the asset class descriptions, decide on your target allocation percentages, and implement the model. Set a calendar reminder to rebalance annually or when any asset class drifts more than 5% from its target.

## Understand the Asset Classes

- [ ] **Equities (stocks and shares)** - Highest long-term returns but highest short-term volatility. Index funds provide diversified equity exposure at low cost. Consider global diversification, not just UK or home-country bias.
- [ ] **Bonds and fixed income** - Lower returns but provide stability and income. Government bonds for safety, corporate bonds for higher yield with more risk. Essential ballast during equity downturns.
- [ ] **Property (beyond your primary residence)** - Rental income plus capital growth, but illiquid and management-intensive. REITs offer property exposure without the hassle of direct ownership.
- [ ] **Cash and near-cash** - Lowest returns but highest liquidity and zero volatility. Essential for short-term needs and as dry powder for opportunities. Not a long-term growth strategy.
- [ ] **Alternative investments** - Private equity, commodities, infrastructure, hedge funds, crypto. Higher complexity and often higher fees. Only appropriate if you genuinely understand the investment and it fits your philosophy.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Check your current allocation right now** - Add up what you have in each category. Most owners discover they are heavily overweight in business equity and property, with very little in diversified investments.

## Design Your Target Allocation

- [ ] **Choose an allocation model based on your risk profile and timeline** - Conservative (more bonds, less equity), balanced (roughly equal), or growth (more equity, less bonds). Your Investment Philosophy Framework guides this choice.
- [ ] **Set target percentages for each asset class** - Write them down. Example: 50% equities, 20% bonds, 15% property, 10% cash, 5% alternatives. Your numbers will differ based on your situation.
- [ ] **Factor in your business as an asset class** - If 60% of your net worth is in the business, your non-business portfolio should be more conservative than it would otherwise be. The business is your growth engine. The portfolio is your safety net.
- [ ] **Consider your income needs** - If you need investment income now, weight toward income-producing assets (bonds, dividend stocks, rental property). If you are building for the future, weight toward growth.
- [ ] **Set a rebalancing trigger** - Rebalance annually, or whenever any asset class drifts more than 5% from its target. This forces the discipline of selling what has grown and buying what has dropped - the opposite of what emotions suggest.

## Implementation

- [ ] **Choose your investment vehicles** - Index funds for equity and bond exposure (low cost, diversified). REITs or property funds for property exposure. High-interest accounts for cash. Keep it simple - complexity rarely adds value.
- [ ] **Set up regular contributions** - Automate monthly or quarterly transfers from business to personal investment accounts. Consistency matters more than timing.
- [ ] **Document your allocation model** - Write down your target percentages, chosen vehicles, contribution amounts, and rebalancing rules. This is the operational companion to your Investment Philosophy.
- [ ] **Review with a professional** - Share your model with an independent financial adviser. They may identify risks or opportunities you missed. Accept advice on the structure, not on specific tips.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Set up a standing order to move money from your business to a personal investment account** - Even a small amount. The habit of regular investment matters more than the amount.

## Expected Outcome

- Written target allocation across all major asset classes
- Clear rationale for each allocation decision tied to your goals and risk profile
- Automated contribution system moving money from business to investments
- Annual rebalancing rhythm that maintains target allocation over time
- Reduced dependence on business value for overall financial security

## Coach Notes

Most business owners find this action frustrating because it feels passive compared to running a business. They want to pick stocks, time markets, or make concentrated bets. The evidence is overwhelmingly against this approach for non-professional investors. Help them accept that boring, consistent allocation beats exciting, inconsistent speculation.

The biggest implementation barrier is usually moving money out of the business. Owners always find a reason to reinvest in the business instead: "I need it for growth," "Cash flow is tight this quarter," "There is a big opportunity coming." Challenge this. The business will always want more capital. The question is whether the owner's long-term financial security should be sacrificed for the business's short-term appetite.

If the client is uncomfortable designing their own allocation, that is fine. The action's value is in understanding the principles. The actual allocation can be designed with a financial adviser. But the owner should understand and agree with the logic, not just outsource the decision.
