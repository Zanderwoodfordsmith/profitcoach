# Cost Categorisation

## What to Do

Sort every business expense into fixed, variable, and semi-variable buckets so you understand what drives your cost base and how it behaves as revenue changes. This gives you control over your spending because you can see which costs are locked in, which move with sales, and which are discretionary.

## Why It Matters

Most business owners know their total expenses but do not understand the structure underneath. Fixed costs hit you every month regardless of revenue - they are your baseline burn. Variable costs scale with activity - they grow as you grow but also shrink if revenue drops. Semi-variable costs have a fixed base with a variable component. Understanding which is which lets you predict what happens to profit at different revenue levels, make smarter spending decisions, and know exactly where to cut if cash gets tight.

## How to Use This

Pull your last 3 months of business expenses from your accounting software or bank statements. Work through every line item.

---

## Expense Inventory

- [ ] Export or list every expense from the last 3 months
- [ ] Group them into natural categories: rent, salaries, software, marketing, supplies, professional services, etc.
- [ ] Calculate the monthly average for each category
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Highlight any expense you do not immediately recognise. Look it up today - you may find something you are paying for that you no longer use or need.

## Categorise Each Expense

### Fixed Costs (Stay the Same Regardless of Revenue)

- [ ] Identify all expenses that do not change whether you have a great month or a terrible one
- [ ] Common fixed costs: rent, salaries, loan repayments, insurance, core software subscriptions, accounting fees
- [ ] Total your monthly fixed costs - this is your baseline. You need this much revenue just to break even before anything else.

### Variable Costs (Move with Revenue or Activity)

- [ ] Identify expenses that increase when you sell more and decrease when you sell less
- [ ] Common variable costs: materials, commissions, transaction fees, shipping, subcontractor costs, per-unit costs
- [ ] Calculate variable cost as a percentage of revenue - this is your variable cost ratio
- [ ] Note: as revenue grows, your profit per sale is revenue minus variable cost per sale minus allocated fixed cost

### Semi-Variable Costs (Fixed Base + Variable Component)

- [ ] Identify expenses that have a base cost but increase with activity
- [ ] Common semi-variable costs: utilities, phone/internet (base plan + overages), marketing (base spend + per-campaign costs), part-time staff (minimum hours + overtime)
- [ ] Split these into their fixed base and variable component

### Discretionary Costs (Could Be Reduced or Eliminated)

- [ ] Review all expenses and flag anything that is a choice rather than a necessity
- [ ] Common discretionary costs: premium software tiers, office perks, conference attendance, subscriptions, professional development
- [ ] This is not about cutting them all - it is about knowing which costs you could reduce if needed

## Build Your Cost Map

- [ ] Create a simple summary:
  - Total fixed costs per month: £___
  - Total variable costs as % of revenue: ___%
  - Total semi-variable base: £___ (plus variable component)
  - Total discretionary spend per month: £___
- [ ] Calculate your break-even revenue: Fixed costs / (1 - variable cost %)
- [ ] Identify: if revenue dropped 20%, which costs could you reduce and by how much?

---

## Expected Outcome

- Every expense categorised as fixed, variable, semi-variable, or discretionary
- A clear picture of your monthly baseline burn
- Your variable cost ratio calculated
- Break-even revenue calculated
- A "contingency list" of costs you could cut if revenue dropped

## Coach Notes

This is foundational work that makes every other financial conversation more productive. Most clients have never separated their costs this way and the exercise itself creates insight. The break-even calculation is often surprising - many owners discover they need more revenue just to cover fixed costs than they realised. The contingency list is psychologically powerful: knowing you have a plan if things go wrong reduces anxiety about growth investments. Connect this to the Cash Flow Forecast (1.4) and the P&L (1.7) for a complete financial picture.
