# ROI Evaluation Matrix

## What to Do

Review your major expenses against measurable returns using a simple framework that separates essential costs from nice-to-haves and identifies what is delivering value versus what is consuming it. Every significant expense should be able to justify its existence with a clear return.

## Why It Matters

Businesses accumulate costs the way houses accumulate clutter - gradually, without anyone noticing. The consultant you hired 18 months ago who still sends monthly invoices. The premium software plan you upgraded to but only use 20% of. The marketing channel you keep funding because stopping feels like giving up. An ROI evaluation forces each expense to prove its value. Costs that cannot justify themselves are candidates for elimination, renegotiation, or downgrading.

## How to Use This

Pull your list of expenses from the Cost Categorisation (Action 1). Focus on your top 15-20 expenses by monthly cost. Work through the matrix below.

---

## Set Up the Matrix

- [ ] List your top 15-20 expenses by monthly cost (start with the biggest)
- [ ] For each expense, note the monthly cost and the annual cost
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Look at your subscriptions and software list. Cancel or downgrade anything you have not actively used in the last 30 days.

## Evaluate Each Expense

For each major expense, answer these questions:

- [ ] **What is the intended return?** (Revenue generated, time saved, risk reduced, quality improved, compliance met)
- [ ] **Can you measure the actual return?** (If yes, calculate it. If no, that is a warning sign.)
- [ ] **What would happen if you stopped this expense tomorrow?** (Nothing = candidate for cutting. Business impact = essential.)
- [ ] **Is there a cheaper alternative that delivers 80%+ of the value?**
- [ ] **When did you last review whether this expense still makes sense?**

## Categorise by ROI

Place each expense in one of four categories:

- [ ] **High ROI - Keep and potentially invest more:** Clear, measurable return that exceeds the cost significantly. These are your best-performing expenses.
- [ ] **Moderate ROI - Keep but review:** Returns are positive but could be better. Look for ways to get more value or reduce cost.
- [ ] **Low ROI - Reduce or renegotiate:** The return does not clearly justify the cost. Downgrade, renegotiate, or find a cheaper alternative.
- [ ] **No measurable ROI - Cut or justify:** Cannot demonstrate a return. Either find a way to measure the value or eliminate the cost.

## Action Plan

- [ ] For each "Low ROI" expense: set a deadline to renegotiate, downgrade, or cancel
- [ ] For each "No measurable ROI" expense: decide within 2 weeks - justify it or cut it
- [ ] Calculate the total monthly savings if you act on all Low and No ROI expenses
- [ ] Set a calendar reminder to re-run this evaluation every 6 months
- [ ] For "High ROI" expenses: is there an opportunity to invest more and get even better returns?

---

## Expected Outcome

- Every major expense evaluated against its actual return
- Expenses categorised by ROI into four clear groups
- Specific actions identified for underperforming costs
- Total potential savings calculated
- A regular review rhythm established to prevent cost creep

## Coach Notes

Business owners often protect expenses that feel important but cannot demonstrate returns. The "what would happen if you stopped this tomorrow?" question cuts through emotional attachment. If the honest answer is "probably nothing," that expense needs to go or be justified with data. Be particularly alert to marketing expenses where the owner "feels like it is working" but has no tracking in place. Also watch for the sunk cost trap: "We have already spent £X on this" is not a reason to keep spending. The only question is whether future spending will produce future returns.
