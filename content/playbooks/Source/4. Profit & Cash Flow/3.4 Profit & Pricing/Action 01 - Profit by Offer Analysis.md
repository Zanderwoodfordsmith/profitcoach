# Profit by Offer Analysis

## What to Do

Take every product or service you sell and calculate the true profitability of each one. Not just gross margin - full profitability including direct costs, indirect costs, and the opportunity cost of the time and attention each offer consumes. The goal is to see clearly which offers are genuinely profitable, which are break-even, and which are quietly losing you money.

Most business owners know their total profit but cannot tell you which specific offer makes the most money and which one drags the average down. This tool fixes that.

## Why It Matters

Michalowicz's Profit First methodology starts with a brutal truth: revenue is not profit, and busy is not profitable. A service that generates £200K in revenue but requires three full-time staff and constant firefighting might be less profitable than a £50K product that runs itself. Alex Hormozi makes the same point from the offer side - the best businesses have a small number of high-margin offers, not a long menu of mediocre ones. You cannot make good decisions about pricing, marketing, or product development until you know where profit actually comes from.

## How to Use This

You need your last 12 months of financial data, your price list, and your team structure. A spreadsheet is the best format for this work. Be prepared for surprises.

---

## Step 1: List Every Offer

- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Write down every product, service, and package you currently sell** - Include one-off projects, retainers, subscriptions, add-ons, and anything else that generates revenue. If you have more than 10, that is already useful information.
- [ ] **Record revenue per offer for the last 12 months** - Pull actual numbers, not estimates. If your accounting system cannot break this down by offer, that is a problem to fix after this exercise.
- [ ] **Note the number of clients or units for each offer** - This gives you average revenue per client per offer, which matters for pricing decisions later.

## Step 2: Calculate True Costs

- [ ] **Assign direct costs to each offer** - Materials, subcontractors, software licences, delivery costs. Anything that only exists because this offer exists.
- [ ] **Allocate indirect costs proportionally** - Rent, utilities, admin staff, insurance. Divide these across offers based on the share of time and resources each one consumes. This does not need to be precise to the penny. Directionally right is good enough.
- [ ] **Estimate the owner's time per offer** - How many hours per month do you personally spend on each offer? Value your time at a reasonable rate. This is often the hidden cost that turns a "profitable" offer into a break-even one.
- [ ] **Calculate profit per offer** - Revenue minus all costs (direct, indirect, and owner time). Express it as a pound amount and as a percentage margin.

## Step 3: Analyse and Decide

- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Rank offers by profit margin, highest to lowest** - The ranking alone is often a revelation. Your "best seller" by revenue may sit in the bottom half by margin.
- [ ] **Identify your top two profit generators** - These are the offers to protect, invest in, and grow. Every marketing pound should prioritise driving demand for these.
- [ ] **Identify any loss-making or break-even offers** - Ask honestly: does this offer need to exist? Is it a stepping stone to something profitable, or is it just legacy? If it does not serve a clear strategic purpose, consider killing it.
- [ ] **Check for complexity costs** - Offers that require custom scoping, bespoke delivery, or constant client management carry hidden costs that eat margin. Flag any offer where the delivery is never the same twice.
- [ ] **Document your findings** - One page: offer name, revenue, profit, margin, and a recommendation (grow, maintain, fix, or kill). Share this with your accountant or finance person for a reality check.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A clear, ranked picture of profitability by offer, not just total business profit
- Hidden loss-makers or break-even offers identified and flagged for decision
- Your top profit generators identified for investment and growth focus
- A foundation for the Pricing Architecture Review and Margin Protection work
- Data to support any decisions about retiring, simplifying, or restructuring offers

## Coach Notes

Expect resistance on the owner-time calculation. Many owners do not want to see how much of their time goes into low-margin offers because it forces an uncomfortable decision. Push through it. The number does not have to be exact, but ignoring it completely makes the analysis useless.

If the client does not have good financial data broken down by offer, do a rough version first based on estimates, then use it as motivation to fix their tracking. A rough Profit by Offer Analysis is still far better than none.

Watch for the "but it brings in referrals" defence of unprofitable offers. Ask them to quantify it. How many referrals? What revenue? If they cannot put a number on it, it is a feeling, not a strategy.
