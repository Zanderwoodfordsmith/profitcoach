# Pricing Architecture Review

## What to Do

Review your entire pricing structure to ensure it is logical, strategic, and easy to explain. Good pricing architecture means every offer sits at a clear price point, the relationship between offers makes sense, and clients can see the value progression from one tier to the next. Bad pricing architecture is a random collection of numbers that evolved over time with no design behind them.

This is about structure, not specific price points. You are designing the framework that prices sit within, not picking the exact numbers (that comes in the Value-Based Pricing action).

## Why It Matters

Allan Dib points out that most small businesses set prices by copying competitors or adding a margin to costs, then never revisit the decision. The result is pricing that does not reflect value, confuses buyers, and leaves money on the table. Hormozi's Value Equation makes this concrete: people do not buy based on cost. They buy based on perceived value minus perceived effort and risk. Your pricing architecture should make the high-value choice feel obvious and the comparison to alternatives feel unfair in your favour.

## How to Use This

Bring your current price list, your Profit by Offer Analysis results, and any competitor pricing you can find. Work through this step by step.

---

## Step 1: Audit Current Pricing

- [ ] **List every price point in your business** - Every offer, every tier, every add-on. Include discounted rates if you regularly discount. Include legacy pricing for existing clients if it differs from new-client pricing.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Check for pricing gaps and clusters** - Plot your prices on a number line. Are there big gaps where you have nothing? Are several offers clustered at similar prices, competing with each other? Both are problems.
- [ ] **Identify legacy pricing** - Offers where the price has not changed in over two years. Costs have gone up. Value has likely increased. The price should have moved too.
- [ ] **Map the client journey through your pricing** - What does a typical client buy first? What do they buy next? Is there a clear path from entry-level to premium, or do clients hit a dead end?

## Step 2: Design the Architecture

- [ ] **Define your pricing tiers** - Most businesses need 2-4 tiers: an entry point, a core offer, and a premium option. Some add a VIP or done-for-you tier at the top. Each tier should serve a different buyer: budget-conscious, standard, and high-value.
- [ ] **Set clear value gaps between tiers** - The jump from one tier to the next should feel justified by what the client gets. If your middle tier is £500/month and your premium is £550/month, the distinction is not strong enough. Typical multipliers are 2-3x between tiers.
- [ ] **Build in an anchor** - Your highest-priced offer makes your core offer feel reasonable by comparison. Even if few people buy the premium, it reframes the middle option as sensible rather than expensive.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Name your tiers strategically** - Names signal value. "Gold/Silver/Bronze" is generic. Names that reflect outcomes or access levels ("Foundations," "Growth," "Partnership") tell a story.
- [ ] **Simplify the menu** - If you cannot explain your pricing in under two minutes, it is too complex. Cut, combine, or restructure until a new prospect can understand their options quickly.

## Step 3: Pressure-Test the Architecture

- [ ] **Run the "explain it to a stranger" test** - Describe your pricing to someone unfamiliar with your business. If they look confused, simplify further.
- [ ] **Check alignment with profit data** - Does your architecture push clients toward your most profitable offers? If your highest-margin offer is buried in the middle with no spotlight, restructure.
- [ ] **Compare to 2-3 competitors** - Not to copy them, but to understand what your market expects. If you are 3x more expensive, you need a clear reason. If you are significantly cheaper, you may be signalling low value.
- [ ] **Plan the transition for existing clients** - If pricing is changing, decide how to handle current clients. Grandfather them, phase in changes, or renegotiate. Have a clear policy before you announce anything.

---

## Expected Outcome

- A clean, logical pricing architecture with defined tiers and clear value progression
- Legacy pricing identified and flagged for update
- A client journey that naturally leads from entry-level to higher-value offers
- Pricing that is simple enough to explain in under two minutes
- A plan for transitioning existing clients if prices are changing

## Coach Notes

Pricing is emotional. Clients will resist raising prices because they fear losing clients. Reframe: you are not raising prices, you are aligning prices to the value you deliver. If they have improved their service since they last set prices, the current price is actually wrong.

The biggest structural mistake is having too many offers at similar price points. It creates decision paralysis for buyers and internal competition between your own products. Push toward fewer, more distinct tiers.

If the client sells services (not products), watch for the trap of pricing by time instead of by outcome. Hourly rates punish efficiency and cap revenue. Guide them toward fixed-fee or value-based models where possible.
