# Value-Based Pricing Framework

## What to Do

Shift your pricing from "what does it cost me to deliver this" to "what is this worth to my client." Value-based pricing means setting prices based on the outcome the client receives, not the inputs you provide. This process walks you through identifying the value you create, quantifying it, and translating it into a price that feels fair to both sides.

This is not about charging as much as possible. It is about charging in proportion to the results you deliver, which usually means charging more than cost-plus pricing would suggest.

## Why It Matters

Hormozi's Value Equation is the clearest framework here: Value = (Dream Outcome x Perceived Likelihood of Achievement) divided by (Time Delay x Effort and Sacrifice). When you price based on cost, you ignore all four of those variables. A consultant who saves a client £200K per year in wasted spend should not be pricing based on the 40 hours the work took. The value is in the outcome, not the hours. Blair Enns makes the same argument in Pricing Creativity - the moment you sell time, you commoditise yourself and cap your income.

## How to Use This

You need your offer list, your client results data (case studies, testimonials, or even anecdotal evidence of outcomes), and your Pricing Architecture Review. Work through each offer.

---

## Step 1: Identify the Value You Create

- [ ] **List the tangible outcomes your clients get** - Revenue increases, cost savings, time saved, risks avoided, problems solved. Be specific and use numbers wherever possible.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Ask three recent clients what the engagement was worth to them** - Not what they paid. What it was worth. The gap between those two numbers is your pricing opportunity.
- [ ] **Identify intangible value** - Confidence, peace of mind, reduced stress, better decisions, stronger team. These are real and clients will pay for them, but you need to name them explicitly.
- [ ] **Calculate the ROI range** - If your service costs £10K and typically delivers £50K-£100K in value, that is a 5-10x return. This number becomes central to your pricing conversation.

## Step 2: Build the Pricing Model

- [ ] **Set a value-based price floor** - Your price should be no less than 10-20% of the value you create. If you save a client £100K, charging £5K is underpricing. Charging £10K-£20K is the starting point.
- [ ] **Create a pricing conversation framework** - Before quoting a price, ask questions that surface value: "What would solving this problem be worth to your business this year?" "What is it costing you to not fix this?" These questions set the context for your price.
- [ ] **Design outcome-linked pricing options** - Consider tying part of your fee to results. A base fee plus a performance bonus, or a higher fee with a guarantee. This signals confidence in your delivery.
- [ ] **Test price sensitivity** - Try the new price with your next three prospects. If all three say yes immediately, you are still too cheap. If all three balk, you have gone too far. One yes and two negotiations is usually about right.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: **Stop quoting hourly rates** - If you currently sell by the hour, switch to project fees or retainer fees immediately. Same total revenue, but it breaks the "time equals money" frame that caps your growth.

## Step 3: Support the Price with Proof

- [ ] **Build a results library** - Collect every case study, testimonial, and data point that shows the value you deliver. Organise by outcome type (revenue, cost saving, time, risk) so you can pull the right proof for the right prospect.
- [ ] **Quantify your track record** - "We have delivered £X in measurable results across Y clients" is more powerful than any individual case study. Calculate the aggregate.
- [ ] **Create before-and-after stories** - Specific narratives of client transformation are the most persuasive pricing support. Where were they before? What did you do? Where are they now?
- [ ] **Prepare for the "why so much?" question** - Do not defend the price. Reframe to value: "The investment is £15K. Based on what we have discussed, the likely return is £80K-£120K in the first year. The question is not whether you can afford it, but whether you can afford not to."

---

## Expected Outcome

- A clear understanding of the quantifiable and intangible value each offer creates
- Pricing that reflects outcomes delivered, not inputs consumed
- A conversation framework for surfacing value before discussing price
- A results library that supports premium pricing with evidence
- Confidence to quote higher prices backed by real data

## Coach Notes

The shift to value-based pricing is psychological as much as practical. Most clients undercharge because they anchor to their own costs and compare themselves to cheaper competitors. Your job is to reframe: the comparison is not "us versus the cheaper option" - it is "our fee versus the cost of the problem continuing."

Start with the client's highest-margin, highest-impact offer. Get value-based pricing working there first, then extend to other offers. Trying to change everything at once creates confusion.

If a client is deeply uncomfortable with this approach, start smaller. Raise prices by 20% on new clients only, keeping existing clients on current rates. Track whether win rates change. They almost never do, which builds the confidence to go further.
