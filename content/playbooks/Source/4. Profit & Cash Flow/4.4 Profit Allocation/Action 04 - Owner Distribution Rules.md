# Owner Distribution Rules

## What to Do

Establish clear, written rules for when and how much you distribute to yourself from the business. This covers both your regular owner pay and profit distributions. The goal is consistent, sustainable income that meets your personal needs without starving the business of cash. Once the rules are set, distributions happen automatically on schedule.

## Why It Matters

Owner pay is the area where most business owners either overpay themselves (draining the business) or underpay themselves (building resentment and burnout). Without rules, you end up making emotional decisions each month based on how the bank balance looks. Some months you take too much because it feels like a good month. Other months you take nothing because cash is tight. This inconsistency makes personal financial planning impossible and creates stress in the business. Clear rules eliminate the monthly negotiation with yourself.

## How to Use This

You need your Profit Allocation Framework (4.4 Action 1) completed first. You also need a clear picture of your personal financial requirements. Work through the steps below in order.

---

## Step 1: Define Your Personal Baseline

- [ ] Calculate your minimum monthly personal expenses (mortgage/rent, bills, food, insurance, school fees, essentials)
- [ ] Add a reasonable buffer for variable personal expenses (10-15% above essentials)
- [ ] This is your minimum owner pay requirement. The business must cover this or something is fundamentally wrong.
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: If you have never written down your personal minimum, do it now. Most owners are surprised by how low (or high) the actual number is.

## Step 2: Set Your Regular Pay Structure

- [ ] Choose between salary (fixed monthly amount) or percentage draw (fixed % of revenue)
- [ ] For stability, a fixed salary is usually better. Set it at a level the business can sustain even in a slow month.
- [ ] Set a pay schedule: weekly, fortnightly, or monthly. Stick to it like an employee payroll.
- [ ] If using a limited company, work with your accountant to optimise the salary vs. dividend split for tax efficiency
- [ ] Document the amount and schedule in writing

## Step 3: Set Profit Distribution Rules

- [ ] Determine how often profit distributions happen (quarterly is recommended, monthly is acceptable)
- [ ] Set a minimum profit account balance before distributions are allowed (e.g. must have at least £X before any distribution)
- [ ] Set a maximum distribution percentage (e.g. distribute 50% of accumulated profit, retain 50%)
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: If you have been taking irregular lump sums, switch to quarterly distributions immediately. The rhythm alone reduces financial anxiety.
- [ ] Define what happens when the business has a bad quarter: distributions pause, regular pay continues

## Step 4: Build Safety Mechanisms

- [ ] Create a "distribution gate" checklist that must pass before any profit distribution:
  - [ ] Tax reserves are fully funded for the period
  - [ ] Emergency reserves are at or above target
  - [ ] No overdue supplier payments
  - [ ] No upcoming large expenses in the next 30 days
- [ ] If any gate fails, the distribution is deferred, not cancelled
- [ ] Set an annual review date to adjust pay and distribution levels based on business performance

---

## Expected Outcome

- A written owner pay policy with fixed amount and schedule
- Clear rules for when profit distributions happen and how much
- A distribution gate checklist preventing withdrawals that would harm the business
- Separation between regular pay (predictable) and profit distributions (variable but structured)
- An annual review rhythm for adjusting levels

## Coach Notes

This is deeply personal territory. Many business owners feel guilty paying themselves well, while others feel entitled to every pound of profit. Neither extreme works. The goal is sustainability: pay that meets personal needs, distributions that reward risk and effort, and rules that protect the business from being hollowed out. If a client's personal minimum exceeds what the business can afford, that is a business model problem, not a distribution problem. Address it through revenue growth or cost reduction rather than increasing distributions.
