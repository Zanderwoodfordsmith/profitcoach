# Growth Investment Budget

## What to Do

Create a dedicated fund for strategic investments so you can say yes to the right opportunities without scrambling for cash or going into debt. You will set a fixed monthly contribution, define clear categories for what counts as a growth investment, and build a simple decision framework so choices become about priorities rather than affordability.

## Why It Matters

Most small businesses invest reactively. They see something shiny, check the bank balance, and either buy impulsively or say no because cash is tight. Neither approach is strategic. A dedicated growth budget changes the conversation entirely. Instead of "can we afford this?" the question becomes "is this the best use of our growth fund?" That shift in framing leads to dramatically better investment decisions over time. Businesses that invest consistently and strategically in capability, systems, and talent outperform those that spend erratically.

## How to Use This

You need your Profit Allocation Framework (4.4 Action 1) and your reserve fund status (4.4 Action 2). Growth investment comes after profit, tax, and essential reserves are funded. Work through the sections below to build your budget.

---

## Define Your Growth Categories

- [ ] List the types of investment that would genuinely move your business forward (not just keep it running)
- [ ] Common growth categories: new hires, training and development, marketing campaigns, technology and systems, product development, equipment
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Write down the single investment you have been putting off that would make the biggest difference. That is probably your first priority.
- [ ] Separate "maintenance" spending from "growth" spending. Replacing a broken laptop is maintenance. Implementing a new CRM is growth. Only growth goes in this budget.

## Set Your Monthly Growth Contribution

- [ ] Determine what percentage of revenue goes to growth investment (typically 5-15% depending on stage)
- [ ] Cross-check against your profit allocation framework to confirm this is sustainable
- [ ] If you cannot fund growth and reserves at the same time, prioritise reserves first until your emergency fund target is met
- [ ] Open a separate growth investment account or earmark funds within your accounting software
- [ ] Set up automatic monthly transfers into the growth fund

## Build Your Investment Decision Framework

- [ ] For each potential investment, require a written one-page case covering: what it is, what it costs, what outcome it produces, how you will measure success, and timeline to ROI
- [ ] Set a minimum threshold below which you can approve without a formal case (e.g. under £500)
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Create a simple scoring system. Rate each investment 1-5 on impact, urgency, and confidence in ROI. Only fund items scoring 10 or above.
- [ ] Maintain a running priority list so when funds are available, you already know what is next
- [ ] Review and reprioritise the list monthly

## Plan Your Investment Calendar

- [ ] Map out planned investments for the next 12 months
- [ ] Identify which investments are time-sensitive and which can wait
- [ ] Stagger large investments so you are not depleting the fund in a single month
- [ ] Build in a quarterly "investment review" to assess returns on previous investments

---

## Expected Outcome

- A funded growth investment account with regular contributions
- Clear categories defining what qualifies as growth investment
- A decision framework that evaluates investments on merit, not just affordability
- A prioritised investment pipeline for the next 12 months
- A review rhythm to assess returns on past investments

## Coach Notes

The biggest win here is psychological. Clients who create a growth fund stop feeling guilty about investing in their business because the money is already allocated for that purpose. Watch for two common traps: clients who never spend the growth fund (hoarding) and clients who spend it on the first thing that comes along (impulsiveness). The decision framework solves both. Push clients to actually write the one-page investment case, even for small items. The discipline of articulating expected ROI kills most bad ideas before they cost anything. Connect this to the Scalability Audit (2.2) and Strategic Project Prioritisation (3.3) for a complete investment picture.
