# Reserve Fund Targets

## What to Do

Calculate the right reserve levels for your business and create an automatic process to build and maintain them. You will set targets for an emergency fund (survival money), an opportunity fund (growth money), and a seasonal buffer (smoothing money). Then you will build a system that grows these reserves when times are good and protects them when they are not.

## Why It Matters

Businesses without reserves make fear-based decisions. Every unexpected expense becomes a crisis. Every slow month triggers panic. Every opportunity gets passed over because there is no cash to act on it. Reserves give you options. They let you survive a bad quarter without laying people off. They let you say yes to an opportunity without going into debt. They turn you from reactive to strategic. The difference between a business that survives a downturn and one that does not is almost always cash reserves.

## How to Use This

You need your monthly fixed costs number from the Cost Categorisation action (2.4) and your Profit Allocation Framework (4.4 Action 1). Work through each reserve type below.

---

## Calculate Your Emergency Reserve Target

- [ ] Look up your total monthly fixed costs (from Cost Categorisation or your P&L)
- [ ] Multiply by 3 for a minimum emergency reserve target (3 months of fixed costs)
- [ ] For businesses with variable or seasonal revenue, multiply by 4-6 months instead
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: If you currently have zero reserves, set a starter target of one month of fixed costs. Getting to one month changes your stress levels immediately.
- [ ] Write down your emergency reserve target: £___

## Calculate Your Opportunity Reserve Target

- [ ] Review your last 12 months and note any opportunity you missed because cash was not available
- [ ] Set an opportunity fund target based on the typical size of investments you want to make (new hire, equipment, marketing campaign, etc.)
- [ ] A good starting point is 5-10% of annual revenue
- [ ] Write down your opportunity reserve target: £___

## Calculate Your Seasonal Buffer

- [ ] Review monthly revenue for the past 12-24 months and identify your lowest revenue months
- [ ] Calculate the gap between your lowest month's revenue and your average monthly expenses
- [ ] Multiply that gap by the number of low months in your cycle
- [ ] If your business has no seasonal pattern, you can skip this or set a small buffer (one month of expenses)
- [ ] Write down your seasonal buffer target: £___

## Build the Reserve System

- [ ] Open a dedicated reserve account (separate from your operating account)
- [ ] Set a monthly contribution amount based on your profit allocation percentage
- [ ] ⚡ Quick Win: Set up an automatic transfer today, even if it is just £100/month. The habit matters more than the amount.
- [ ] Define rules for when reserves can be used (emergency only, opportunity with clear ROI, seasonal shortfall)
- [ ] Define a replenishment rule: after any withdrawal, increase monthly contributions until the reserve is rebuilt
- [ ] Set a quarterly review to check reserve levels against targets

---

## Expected Outcome

- Three clear reserve targets calculated: emergency, opportunity, and seasonal
- A dedicated reserve account opened and funded
- Automatic monthly contributions set up
- Clear rules for when reserves can and cannot be used
- A replenishment plan for rebuilding after withdrawals

## Coach Notes

Reserves are emotional for most business owners. Many have operated hand-to-mouth for years and the idea of "locking up" cash feels wasteful or even impossible. Reframe it: reserves are not idle money, they are insurance and freedom. Start small and build. The first milestone that matters psychologically is one month of fixed costs. Once a client hits that, they visibly relax and start making better decisions. Watch for clients who set aggressive targets but then raid the reserve at the first temptation. The rules for withdrawal need to be strict enough to create real friction.
