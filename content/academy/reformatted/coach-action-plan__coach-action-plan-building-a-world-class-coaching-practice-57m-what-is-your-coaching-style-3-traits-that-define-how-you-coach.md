## This Will Make You a More Confident Coach (Before You Even Sign a Client)

Confidence is one of the things I hear coaches talk about most, whether you're just starting out or you've been doing this for years.

And here's what I've come to believe: confidence doesn't come from doing more sessions, getting more qualifications, or waiting until you feel ready. Confidence comes from clarity. Specifically, clarity about who you are as a coach.

When you know your archetype, when you can name the kind of coach you actually are rather than the kind you think you **should** be, something shifts. You stop second-guessing your instincts. You stop apologising for your style. You start owning it. And when you own it, your clients feel it.

That's what this post is about.

---

## Three Traits That Define Your Coaching Style

After working with coaches across this community, I've found there are three dimensions that shape how you show up in a session. None of these is better or worse than any other. They're just different. And knowing where you sit changes everything.

### **1. Mentor to Coach** (1 = pure mentor, 5 = pure coach)

A mentor draws on their own experience. They've walked the path, they know what worked, and they share it. There's real power in that. Business owners often desperately need someone who's been there to just tell them what to do.

A pure coach believes the answer is already inside the client. Their job is to ask the question that helps the client hear themselves think. They're not there to give the answer. They're there to make the question unavoidable.

Ashley sits firmly at the mentor end. He's built businesses, he knows what works, and he'll tell you clearly. Mark is at the other extreme. He'll rarely share his own view in a session. He'll ask you questions until you arrive at the answer yourself, and when you do, it sticks in a way that being told never quite does. Pam sits somewhere in the middle, blending both approaches depending on what the client needs in the moment.

### **2. Growth-Focused to Lifestyle-Focused** (1 = growth-first, 5 = lifestyle-first)

Some coaches are wired to help clients scale. Revenue, team, market share. When they look at a business, they see opportunity and they want to chase it. That energy is infectious and it moves people.

Others believe the business exists to serve the life, not the other way around. They think holistically about the owner's health, relationships, and fulfilment, not just the P&L. They'll push back when a client is winning in business but losing in life.

Ashley is a 1 here too. He is focused on growth and he makes no apology for it. He only takes on clients who genuinely want to scale their business. When someone comes to him wanting primarily lifestyle balance, he refers them on, because he knows another coach will serve them far better. Pam sits at the other end of this scale, and there's a personal reason for that. She built a business that nearly cost her everything — her health, her relationships, the very life she was building the business for. That experience shaped her deeply. She still wants her clients to grow. But she will never let them lose sight of why they're building it. The business should serve the life. When it starts consuming the life instead, Pam will name it.

### **3. Tough Love to Nurturing** (1 = tough love, 5 = nurturing)

Some coaches challenge hard. They hold clients accountable with very little softening, call out the stories clients tell themselves, and create pressure that produces results. Clients who need a push thrive with them. Imagine Gordon Ramsay feedback, but a little less swearing!

Others lead with warmth. They create safety, they encourage, they meet the client exactly where they are, and from that place of trust, remarkable things happen. Clients who need permission to believe in themselves thrive with them.

Ashley brings tough love. He will hold your feet to the fire and he won't dress it up. Others lead with genuine warmth, challenging clients but encouraging them. They create a space where clients feel safe enough to be truly honest, and then they use that honesty to move them forward. Both get extraordinary results. The difference is the environment they create to get there.

## Why This Matters for Your Confidence

Here's what happens when you don't know your archetype: you try to be everything. You're tough when you think you should be tough and warm when you think you should be warm, and neither feels natural because you're performing a version of coaching rather than actually being a coach.

Here's what happens when you do know: you stop performing. You make decisions in the session from instinct rather than anxiety. A client pushes back and instead of wobbling, you hold your position, because you know why you do what you do.

Look at Ashley. He is completely clear on who he is and who he serves. That clarity means he never wastes energy trying to be something he's not. Look at Pam. Her experience gives her a perspective that no qualification could teach, and knowing that is part of what makes her so effective. Look at Mark. He trusts the process of pure coaching so completely that clients trust it too, even when it feels uncomfortable. That trust comes from his clarity about who he is.

Knowing your archetype also means knowing your ideal client. When you're clear on your style, you naturally attract the people who will thrive with you, and you can refer on the people who won't, with confidence rather than guilt.

**If you try to appeal to everyone, you appeal to no one!**
