### **What Is This?**

This lesson is about choosing your core client. It involves defining the specific group of clients you'll target with your automated cold outreach. It doesn't limit who you can sign as a client; it just helps focus your marketing efforts.

### **Why Is This Important?**

* **Maximises Value and Profitability:** By focusing on a specific group that aligns with your unique skills and experiences, you can provide more value.
* **Enhances Personalisation:** Automation works best when it feels personalised. Knowing your core client allows you to tailor your messages effectively.
* **Improves Marketing Efficiency:** Targeted efforts yield better results, saving time and resources.
* **Helps Identify Pain Points:** Understanding your core client's challenges allows you to address them more effectively.
* **Builds Credibility:** Using your relevant experience as proof boosts your credibility with potential clients.

### **When**

At the beginning of your business, especially before launching targeted automated marketing.

### **Who**

The coach needs to do this, as it is a key decision based on their career, passions and any outside influences like non-competes.

### **⌛ Time**

30 seconds to 3 days (only if really stuck).

## How To Do It — This Is Actually Really Simple

For 19 out of 20 coaches this should be super simple and take about 10 seconds. You just fill in the industry/business type in this sentence where you have the most experience and expertise and can add the most value:

> "My core client is {industry/business type} with 10-50 employees doing £1-£20 million revenue."

### **Coaches Overcomplicate This — Don't Do That!**

Think about the **people and businesses you can add the most value to** because of your experience, and choose them. For example:

### **Why Do You Need A Core Client?**

When we come to run automated cold outreach, there are a few critical parts to it being effective.

1. **Personalised** — We want to be able to say "Hi {First Name}, I see you run a UK engineering company." This helps overcome that initial disinterest from them feeling they are being spammed. **It feels relevant to them.**
2. **Pain/Problem** — No one is ever going to pay you £2,000/mo if you aren't helping improve something in their business/life. Having a core client related to your experience helps you have a better understanding of what those problems and pain points are. So the second part of the message will be something like: "Are you looking to win bigger contracts?" or "Are you looking to increase profits?" (relevant to your core client).
3. **Proof** — Once we have asked them about the pain or problem, you are going to have to back it up with proof that you can help them. This is why we leverage your experience from your career or past clients. Below are examples from coaches' messaging. This proof would come after identifying the prospect (e.g. "I see you run a law firm"), then using a hook (e.g. "are you looking to increase profit?"). These all worked way better than KPIs:
   * **Eileen Lancaster (career proof):** "I ask because at Dow I used a simple system to increase revenue from $2M → $40M in 5yrs (32% EBIT) by eliminating low-volume accounts to focus on money makers."
   * **James Baker (career proof):** "I ask because as an MD at JCB I used a system to grow the attachments business from £150K/yr profit to £9M in 4yrs by keeping the team accountable."
   * **John McCarthy (client proof):** "I ask because Stephen Ward, MD Clerksroom, grew his law firm's profit from £200k to £1M in 3 years & cut his working hours in half in 1 year by following my PROFIT system."

### **The 5 Criteria for Picking Your Core Client**

The proof is absolutely critical, which is why of the 5 core criteria the #1 is value.

1. **Most Value** — Who can you provide and demonstrate the most value to given your skillset & experience? You know the pains, know what it feels like, you will empathise with them & ultimately create a better product/service/coaching that will provide more value.
2. **Pain** — Is the market in pain? Do they have a desperate need for closing the gap? A nice-to-have is not something people are going to take their money out and buy. Most businesses always have issues they need to solve. Are they starving for the thing they are looking for?
3. **Growing** — Otherwise you're always fighting the trend.
4. **Easy To Find** — If you are marketing and you can't find your prospects, then you can't get your offer in front of them. You want to be able to easily locate them in LinkedIn Sales Navigator search, or if you have to, in associations, groups etc. They must be congregated in one place.
5. **Purchasing Power** — Do they have the money? This is why you want to avoid startups and charities.

You ideally want clients in the £1-20 million revenue range and 11-50 employees, but this is not set in stone and you should not limit yourself to businesses just this size. **Companies even below £1 million in revenue will pay coaches +£3,000/mo (ask Pam how she knows).**

---

### **How To Make The Decision**

So you want to make sure your core client hits all of the criteria, with the highest weight being given to #1. There are two strategies:

**Most Impressive:** What is most impressive from your career and background? Then pick the person who would be most impressed by it as your core client. For example, if you ran your own restaurant business for 10 years but then had to shut it down, but then grew a division of a major manufacturing company from £4M to £40M in 3 years, well, it's pretty clear you should pick the second one and manufacturing as your core client (example from one of our coaches).

**Most Confident:** If your whole family was going to die unless you massively improved the profit and performance of a business, and you could choose that one business, what type of business would you choose? Would it be an airline? Would it be a bakery? So if it's no to either of those, then you can try and find what it is yes to, or pick a bunch more businesses and keep eliminating the no's until you are left with the ones you have the most confidence with.

> "I realised I had greater value especially where my focused marketplace was."

### **Special Considerations**

Usually there are two categories of special circumstances where your core client has to be slightly adjusted from the best proof you have.

1. **Non-Competes and Mitigating Circumstances:** If you are not able to target that particular type of customer because of contacts, contracts and rules, then you need to pick a different core client. The best thing to do here is to figure out what businesses/industries have similar pain points to the industry you have proof in, and target that similar industry. For example, if you can't target care homes, you could target nurseries (also an example of a coach).
2. **Big Business!** Some industries are just prone to large companies, e.g. banking, pharma, airlines. In this case you probably aren't going to start coaching the CEO of one of these companies, so there are two options:
   * You target the MDs of divisions within these large companies, as they have "ownership" over that department. One of Pam's longest-standing clients was the MD of the fishing division of a multi-billion importer/exporter.
   * You find the smaller versions of the companies that your proof and experience would still be relevant to. See the Alternative Businesses for Large-Scale Industries table at the end of this page for examples.

### **Mistakes To Avoid**

There are several traps unwary coaches fall into when picking a core client. You need to avoid these if you don't want to waste time and effort.

**1 | Fear of Missing Out**

When coaches first think about a core client, they often fear missing out, because picking a core client / niching your marketing down to a small part of the market means you're excluding a massive number of the businesses you could work with. But you've got two choices:

1. Be a generic coach who is trying to appeal to "anyone" and "everyone", and in the process devalue your skills and experiences.
2. **Laser-focus your marketing towards a specific set of decision-makers who can see why your skills and experiences mean you are their best chance of overcoming a problem or situation — and are prepared to pay you handsomely because of it.**

**2 | Chasing The Money**

The second trap coaches fall into is picking a niche they believe is going to be highly profitable when they don't have experience in the niche or there's no obvious link to why they should be taken seriously in that market. **Chasing a profitable niche where you have no experience will make your life far harder than it needs to be, as most prospects will think "you can't help me as you don't understand me".**

Instead, serve a market you understand and have proven experience in, or one where your skills are transferable in a way that makes it obvious why you should be paid well for your guidance.

**3 | Fear of Making A Decision**

The third and most common trap is NOT making a decision for fear of making the wrong choice. Picking a niche is not a once-in-a-lifetime decision. If you pick a core client and then realise it's not for you, just change. And picking a core client type to focus on doesn't stop you working with anyone else if they directly ask for your help or are recommended to you. Instead of worrying between niche 1 or 2, **realise that by NOT choosing and not having focus you are actually choosing option 3** of not choosing, which is the only way to make a wrong decision.

**4 | Choosing an Unfindable or Non-Shared-Pain Core Client**

You have to be able to get them in a list and they have to have shared pain points. For example, it's virtually impossible to find a group of family-run businesses, and they likely don't share the same issues when they are in different industries. Another example is a group like military veteran business owners — you can probably find them, but once again they will not have shared pain points, so your marketing will have to be world-class to connect with them over the military link.

**5 | Changing Too Often**

Do not change your niche at the drop of a hat. If you keep changing, people will become confused and unsure as to what you do. Use this process to pick a suitable niche. When you become the best at serving clients is when you start to see the results. If you are changing every few weeks you can't become the best. Go all out on it for at least six months. **Then, if it's not for you, change it.**

**6 | Choosing Clients Who Can't Pay**

While it's noble to want to help charities or startups, it's crucial to focus on clients who have the financial means to afford your services. Working with clients who can't pay can drain your resources and limit your ability to grow your business. Ensure that your core clients can not only benefit from your expertise but also compensate you fairly for your time and effort. Even though it feels like you can help them, your fees will put a lot of burden on their cashflow, and within 3-4 months they won't be able to continue. Get yourself to £10-20k/mo first, and then if you want to help them to give back, you can.

---

## Conclusion

You could help any business. However, it's much better when you come to run automation to have a targeted list.

Do not overthink this! Choose the best proof from your career and the type of industry/business it is in, and choose that. If there are special circumstances, choose either an adjacent industry or a smaller similar type of firm.

---

### **FAQs**

**What if I choose the wrong core client?** Choosing a core client isn't a lifelong commitment. It's important to make a decision based on current insights and be open to pivoting as you gain more data and experience.

**How do I know if my core client is profitable?** Validate the core client by ensuring it meets the key criteria (who you can add value to, pain, growing, easy to find, purchasing power) and through initial marketing tests to gauge interest and willingness to pay. You can make virtually any market work, but it takes focus and commitment.
